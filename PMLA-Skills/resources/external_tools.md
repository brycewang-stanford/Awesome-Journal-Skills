# External Tools & Resources for PMLA-Style Literary & Language Scholarship

Sources, software, and references useful when preparing a *PMLA* (Publications of the Modern Language
Association) submission. PMLA is a **humanities generalist** journal: an essay is **argument, close
reading, theoretical framing, and engagement with the critical conversation**, formatted in **MLA
style**. There is **no dataset, no statistical analysis, and no replication package** — so this list
is about texts, archives, citation, and prose, not about estimation or code. Match tools to your
materials and method, check access terms, and verify any PMLA-specific policy in
[`official-source-map.md`](official-source-map.md).

## 1. Primary texts, editions & archives

| Source | Provider | Typical use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Scholarly / critical editions | University presses, MLA editions | Authoritative base text; collation and variants |
| EEBO / ECCO | ProQuest / Gale | Early modern and 18th-c. printed books (facsimile) |
| HathiTrust / Internet Archive / Google Books | Consortia | Out-of-copyright editions, periodicals, full text |
| Project Gutenberg / Wikisource | Open | Public-domain literary texts (verify against a scholarly edition) |
| Archive finding aids (ArchiveGrid, repository catalogs) | OCLC / repositories | Manuscripts, correspondence, marginalia for Little-known Documents |
| National / institutional digital libraries (e.g., Gallica, DPLA) | Various | Manuscripts, rare materials, non-English sources |

## 2. Bibliography & discovery

- **MLA International Bibliography** — the field's core index of scholarship on literatures, languages,
  linguistics, and folklore; use it to map the critical conversation (see `pmla-scholarly-positioning`).
- **JSTOR / Project MUSE** — full-text journals across the humanities; locate the debate and the
  closest prior essays.
- **WorldCat** — editions, translations, and holdings.
- **Library of Congress / national bibliographies** — authoritative bibliographic detail for citation.

## 3. Citation & MLA style

- **MLA Handbook (most recent edition)** — the authority for PMLA style: in-text parenthetical
  citations keyed to a **Works Cited** list built from the MLA **template of core elements**.
- **MLA Style Center** (style.mla.org) — worked examples, formatting Q&A, the template of core elements.
- Reference managers: **Zotero** (with an MLA style), **EndNote**, **BibTeX/BibLaTeX** with an MLA
  CSL/`biblatex-mla` style — keep one consistent style; verify every entry against the MLA Handbook.
- Quotation and translation: keep a record of editions used; for non-English sources, decide on a
  consistent policy for **original + translation** and cite both (translations are excluded from the
  word count).

## 4. Writing & manuscript preparation

- Typesetting: **Word** (PMLA's ScholarOne accepts Word files), or LaTeX exported to a compatible
  format — confirm the portal's accepted formats.
- Prepare an **anonymized** manuscript: no name in the text or notes, third-person references to your
  own prior work, and a separate **cover sheet** with name, address, and title.
- Style/readability aids (use with judgment, not as authority): a thesaurus, a good usage guide
  (e.g., a standard dictionary of usage), and prose-linting tools for wordiness — the argument and the
  voice are yours.
- Track the **word count** (6,000–9,000 for articles; discursive notes count; Works Cited and
  translations do not).

## 5. Theory & method references

- Field anthologies and companions (theory readers, Nortons, Blackwell/Cambridge Companions) to place
  a framework precisely rather than gesturing at "theory" in general.
- Keyword and concept references (e.g., *Keywords*-style lexicons, field encyclopedias) to define
  contested terms on first use.
- For digital-humanities or book-history methods: corpus/text tools (Voyant, AntConc) and bibliographic
  description guides — but in PMLA these **serve** an argument and close reading; they do not replace
  them.

## 6. Process & portal

| Item | Note |
|------|------|
| Submission portal | **PMLA's ScholarOne / Manuscript Central** (`mc.manuscriptcentral.com/pmla`) — confirm current URL |
| Owner / publisher | **Modern Language Association (MLA)** / **Cambridge University Press** |
| Eligibility | **MLA membership required** for the author (and all coauthors) |
| Review model | **Anonymous (blind)** — at least two reviewers, then the Editorial Board decides |
| Article length | **6,000–9,000 words** (discursive notes included; Works Cited and translations excluded) |
| Style | **MLA style** per the most recent **MLA Handbook** (in-text + Works Cited) |
| Special features | Theories and Methodologies · The Changing Profession · Criticism in Translation · Little-known Documents · special-topic issues |
| AI disclosure | Cite, at submission, all content created by an AI tool |

## 7. Cautions

1. **Verify volatile specifics** (editor, exact word caps, special-feature deadlines, membership and
   any fees, accepted features) on the official MLA / Cambridge pages — they change; unverified items
   are marked 待核实.
2. **This is not a data pack.** PMLA has no replication or statistics requirement; do not import a
   social-science template. The evidence is the **text**.
3. **Anonymize properly** — PMLA is blind-reviewed; strip your name from the manuscript and notes, use
   third-person self-reference, and keep identifying detail to the cover sheet.
4. **Cite to the MLA Handbook** — author-page in-text plus a Works Cited built from the template of
   core elements; one consistent style throughout.
5. **Write for the whole membership** — an essay must engage scholars and teachers of language and
   literature beyond your period, language, or theoretical school.
