# Worked Example: A PMLA-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **PMLA introduction arc** drawn from this pack's skills: by the end of the
introduction a generalist reader should know the **significant problem**, the **thesis**, the **critical
conversation** the essay enters, and **why the claim matters beyond the single text** — with the
intervention stated **plainly and early**, not buried, and the close reading kept in the foreground
(`pmla-structure-and-exposition`: "open on the problem and the claim"; `pmla-argument-development`:
"the intervention must be stated plainly, early"). Unlike a social-science introduction, there is **no
hypothesis, no data, and no method-first framing** — the evidence is the text, and the contribution is
an interpretive and theoretical one (`pmla-textual-evidence-and-close-reading`).

**Illustrative essay (fictional):** *"The Creature's Subjunctive: Conditional Grammar and Moral
Personhood in *Frankenstein*."* The (invented) argument: that the novel locates the creature's claim to
personhood not in his appearance or his deeds but in his command of the **subjunctive and conditional
mood** — the grammar of unrealized possibility — and that reading this grammar reframes a long critical
debate about sympathy in the novel.

---

## Before (buries the idea — typical first-draft intro)

> Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* (1818) has been the subject of an enormous body of criticism. Scholars
> have approached it through Romanticism, gender, science studies, race, and disability, among many
> other frameworks. In this essay I apply a linguistic and narratological approach to the novel,
> focusing on the speech of the creature. After reviewing the relevant criticism, I will conduct a
> close reading of several passages and then draw some conclusions about sympathy. This is an
> interesting and under-examined aspect of the text that deserves more attention than it has received.
> Section 1 surveys the criticism, section 2 analyzes the creature's narrative, and section 3 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `pmla-argument-development`, `pmla-scholarly-positioning`,
`pmla-structure-and-exposition`):**

- **No thesis.** "I apply a linguistic approach … focusing on the creature's speech" names a *topic and
  a method*, not a contestable claim. `pmla-argument-development`: a thesis is "one contestable
  sentence," "not 'the novel explores memory.'"
- **No significant problem and no stakes for a generalist.** "Interesting and under-examined … deserves
  more attention" is exactly the throat-clearing both `pmla-scholarly-positioning` and
  `pmla-topic-selection` flag ("not 'little attention has been paid'").
- **The conversation is a citation pile**, not a live disagreement the essay enters
  (`pmla-scholarly-positioning`: "engage the conversation, not a citation pile").
- **Method-first framing** and an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do
  (`pmla-structure-and-exposition` anti-patterns).
- **Stakes deferred to a conclusion** that has not been earned.

---

## After (PMLA arc — problem + thesis + conversation + stakes, intervention early)

> Few readers of *Frankenstein* forget the creature's plea, but criticism has long disagreed about why
> it moves us. One tradition locates his claim on us in the body — the novel teaches sympathy by forcing
> us past a face we are taught to loathe; another locates it in deeds, in the reciprocal obligations the
> creature invokes when he asks his maker for a companion. **I argue that the novel grounds the
> creature's personhood in neither his appearance nor his acts but in his grammar: specifically, in his
> fluency in the subjunctive and conditional — the moods of the unrealized — through which he lays claim
> not to what he is but to what he might have been.** *(significant problem + contestable thesis, by the
> end of the first paragraph; the intervention named against two specific prior readings.)*
>
> This matters because it relocates the novel's famous question — what do we owe a being we did not
> choose to make? — from the register of pity to the register of **possibility**. When the creature
> says, in effect, *"had you not abandoned me, I would not have become this,"* he reasons in a mood that
> assumes a self capable of having gone otherwise; to grant the conditional is already to grant the
> personhood the plot keeps refusing him. *(stakes drawn out early — what a reader must now think
> differently — using a paraphrased, non-quoted example. Cf. `pmla-argument-development`'s "so what"
> test: the claim must "reorganize something — a text, a genre, a method, a debate.")*
>
> The reading therefore speaks to a conversation larger than *Frankenstein*. Critics of the novel of
> sympathy have asked how narrative fiction trains moral feeling; I add that the **grammar of
> conditional possibility** is one of its instruments, and that attending to mood — not only to plot or
> to figure — lets us see how a text builds, or withholds, a character's standing as a person.
> *(positioning across a generalist conversation, per `pmla-scholarly-positioning`: a single-author
> reading made to bear on "the broader formal, generic, or historical question.")*
>
> My evidence is the creature's own narrative in the novel's central chapters, read closely for its
> verbs — where the conditional clusters, where it collapses into the indicative of resignation, and
> where Victor's flat declaratives refuse to answer it in kind. *(close reading kept in the foreground;
> theory frames, the reading carries — `pmla-theory-and-method`.)* I take up the strongest rival view —
> that the creature's eloquence is Shelley's irony, a borrowed rhetoric that underscores his exclusion
> rather than earning his inclusion — and show that the *pattern* of his moods, sustained and
> self-consistent across the narrative, is harder to read as mere ventriloquism than a single set
> speech would be. *(counter-reading named and answered, per `pmla-argument-development` and
> `pmla-scholarly-positioning`.)*
>
> The essay proceeds from the grammar of the creature's self-narration, to Victor's failure to answer it
> in the same mood, to what a grammar of the unrealized implies for how the novel of sympathy makes
> persons. *(brief roadmap — the argument, not an over-signposted table of contents.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the PMLA bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Significant problem + thesis by the end of the intro** — the contestable claim (personhood is
  grounded in *grammar*, the subjunctive/conditional) is stated plainly and early, against two named
  prior readings, not deferred to a conclusion (`pmla-structure-and-exposition`;
  `pmla-argument-development`).
- **Stakes for a generalist** — the "what must a reader now think differently" is explicit (pity →
  possibility), and reaches a scholar outside Romanticism via the broader question of how the novel of
  sympathy makes persons (`pmla-topic-selection` interest-beyond-the-specialty test;
  `pmla-scholarly-positioning` two-audiences demand).
- **Conversation engaged as a live disagreement**, not a citation pile — "one tradition … another …; I
  argue" is the "critics read X via assumption A; reading closely shows Y′" move from
  `pmla-scholarly-positioning`.
- **The move is named** — the essay *reframes* (relocates the ground of personhood), the verb of the
  contribution (`pmla-argument-development`: "re-reads / reframes / recovers / complicates / overturns").
- **Counter-reading pre-empted** — the strongest rival (eloquence as Shelleyan irony) is stated and
  answered by accounting for the same evidence better (pattern across the narrative vs. a single
  speech), not dismissed.
- **Close reading in the foreground; no data, no hypothesis** — the evidence is the creature's verbs in
  a reliable edition, read for mood; theory frames but the reading carries
  (`pmla-textual-evidence-and-close-reading`; `pmla-theory-and-method`).
- **Concise, readable, argument-forward prose** — claim before qualification, jargon ("subjunctive,"
  "conditional") earned and glossed, no throat-clearing (`pmla-writing-style`).
