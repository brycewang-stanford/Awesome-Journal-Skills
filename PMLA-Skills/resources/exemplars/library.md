## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **field × method** is closest to yours, then study how that essay makes a reading of
specific texts carry a **significant problem of interest across the membership** — the PMLA bar (see
[`../../skills/pmla-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pmla-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/pmla-scholarly-positioning/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pmla-scholarly-positioning/SKILL.md)).
For each, ask the self-check question before claiming your essay is "PMLA-shaped." Model your
introduction on [`../worked-examples/01-introduction.md`](../worked-examples/01-introduction.md).

## By field × method

### Close reading + form (early modern poetics, formalist method)

- **Colleen Ruth Rosenfeld, "The Contingency of Form in Renaissance Poetics," PMLA 2023, 138(5).**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/contingency-of-form-in-renaissance-poetics/161D7158A997A33BB2B135CF33329F79`; DOI `10.1632/S0030812923000974`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a humanist-schoolroom exercise (metaphrasis) is read as harboring a
    *theory of form* — formalist close reading made into a transferable critical claim, the kind of
    reading-into-argument move `pmla-argument-development` describes.
  - **Self-check:** does your close attention to form yield a claim a non-specialist in the period can
    see the stakes of, not just a finer description?

### Theory & method as intervention (queer theory; Theories-and-Methodologies–style forum)

- **Robert L. Caserio, Lee Edelman, Judith [Jack] Halberstam, José Esteban Muñoz & Tim Dean, "The
  Antisocial Thesis in Queer Theory," PMLA 2006, 121(3): 819–828.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/antisocial-thesis-in-queer-theory/ACEB5C7E2A41D52CE7A5FFCC1057E6C4`; DOI `10.1632/pmla.2006.121.3.819`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a multi-author forum stages a live theoretical disagreement *as* the
    contribution — the model for a `pmla-theory-and-method` intervention that addresses the membership,
    not only specialists, by making one timely debate legible.
  - **Self-check:** can you name the live disagreement your essay enters, and the specific concept (not
    just a "school") you put pressure on?

### Book history / paratext + close reading (race and the material text)

- **Beth A. McCoy, "Race and the (Para)Textual Condition," PMLA 2006, 121(1): 156–169.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/race-and-the-paratextual-condition/A477EFE0A5E17ACA6A637D0EE1030AA6`; DOI `10.1632/003081206X96168`. (Special topic: *The History of the Book and the Idea of Literature*.)
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns paratext (prefaces, apparatus, the fringes of the book) into
    evidence for an argument about how power is transacted in the material text — book history made to
    carry an interpretive claim, per `pmla-textual-evidence-and-close-reading`'s "material and book
    history … where the argument turns on them."
  - **Self-check:** does the material/bibliographic detail *do argumentative work*, or is it context you
    could cut without changing the claim?

### Comparative / theoretical framing across fields (comparative racialization)

- **Shu-mei Shih, "Comparative Racialization: An Introduction," PMLA 2008, 123(5): 1347–1362.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/comparative-racialization-an-introduction/16BF21FBF29BE840AC1A41D99B63E2E2`; DOI `10.1632/pmla.2008.123.5.1347`. (Special topic: *Comparative Racialization*.)
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** frames a comparative method that travels across national and linguistic
    fields — exactly the "reach the membership" move `pmla-scholarly-positioning` asks of period- or
    nation-specific work.
  - **Self-check:** does your frame let a scholar of a *different* literature see why the problem is
    theirs too?

### Digital humanities serving close reading (distant reading + critical race studies)

- **Richard Jean So & Edwin Roland, "Race and Distant Reading," PMLA 2020, 135(1): 59–73.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/race-and-distant-reading/6646170D88722BB195E40D4352BCDA1A`; DOI `10.1632/pmla.2020.135.1.59`. (Special topic: *Varieties of Digital Humanities*.)
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a computational method is made *answerable to* critical race studies and
    to the reading of specific fiction — the PMLA demand that method "serve an argument and close
    reading; [it does] not replace them" (`pmla-theory-and-method`; `external_tools.md` §5).
  - **Self-check:** if you use a digital or quantitative method, does it earn its keep by illuminating a
    text — or has the tool become the argument?

### Periodization as argument (critical race studies; long history / longue durée)

- **Geraldine Heng, "Why Do We Need a Long History of Race?," PMLA 2025, 140(5): 920–929.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla/article/abs/why-do-we-need-a-long-history-of-race/B65C92A42E6133996C65F7916FF65EBB`; DOI `10.1632/S0030812925101521`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** makes a *periodization* (the deep history of race) into a consequential
    claim for the whole field — a "significant problem … with implications drawn out" that reaches well
    beyond a single text or period (`pmla-topic-selection`; `pmla-argument-development`).
  - **Self-check:** does your essay reorganize how the field frames a problem, or only add one more case
    to an existing frame?

## Omitted for sibling-venue confusion or lack of full verification (do not attribute to PMLA)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these candidates were **excluded** after checking:

- **Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick, "Jane Austen and the Masturbating Girl" (1991)** — verified to be in
  ***Critical Inquiry*** 17(4), **not PMLA** (the MLA connection is only that an early version was given
  as an MLA-convention talk). Listed here purely as a guardrail.
- **Stephen Best & Sharon Marcus, "Surface Reading: An Introduction" (2009)** — verified to be in
  ***Representations*** 108(1), **not PMLA**. Guardrail only.
- **Franco Moretti, "Conjectures on World Literature" (2000)** — verified to be in ***New Left Review***
  (II/1), **not PMLA**. The "distant reading" formula is frequently mis-shelved as PMLA. Guardrail only.
- **"Prosodic Unrest in Shakespeare's Sonnets" (PMLA 2025, 140(1))** and **"Milton and the Invention of
  the Human in Paradise Lost" (PMLA 2025, 140(5))** — the *journal placement* is verified on Cambridge
  Core, but a **single authoritative author attribution could not be confirmed by independent web
  search** at access time, so they are omitted rather than risk a wrong byline. Re-verify the author on
  the article page before citing.

Before adding any essay to this library, confirm it on `cambridge.org/core/journals/pmla` (article page
with author, volume/issue, and `10.1632/...` DOI), check it is not actually one of the sibling venues
above, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
