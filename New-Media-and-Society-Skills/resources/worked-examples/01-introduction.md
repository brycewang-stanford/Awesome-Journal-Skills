# Worked Example: An NM&S-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **NM&S introduction arc** that the pack's own skills require. NM&S is the broad,
**interdisciplinary** digital-media-and-society journal, so the introduction must do four things by the
time it ends (`newms-writing-style` → "front-load the contribution"; `newms-topic-selection` → the
four-part fit test):

1. **The question**, stated plainly for a reader two fields away.
2. **The argument** — a named **concept or mechanism** that *travels* (`newms-theory-building`'s
   portability test), not just a platform finding.
3. **The evidence** — what method adjudicates the leading rival reading (`newms-research-design`'s
   adjudication sentence).
4. **Why it matters to new media and society broadly** — the interdisciplinary stakes, with **scope
   conditions**.

NM&S publishes qualitative, critical, content/discourse, computational, and mixed work and judges each on
its own terms. This example takes a **qualitative (digital ethnography + interviews)** angle with strong
conceptual framing — the combination the skills treat as a central NM&S target.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Datafied Control: How Opaque Ranking Disciplines Platform
Couriers."* Fictional setting: a multi-month digital ethnography of an online courier community plus 30
interviews, in a country where a food-delivery app ranks couriers by an undisclosed score.

---

## Before (platform-insider, buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Food-delivery platforms have received increasing attention in recent years. A number of studies have
> examined how gig workers experience algorithmic management, and debates continue about whether workers
> resist or comply. In this paper, we use interviews and online observation to examine couriers'
> experiences with the ranking algorithm on a popular delivery app. We find that couriers are very
> concerned about their scores and change their behavior in response. This topic is understudied for
> food-delivery couriers specifically. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the
> literature on gig work, Section 3 describes the methods, Section 4 presents the findings, and Section 5
> concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Platform-insider framing.** It speaks only to people already studying one app; a surveillance,
  labor, or media scholar sees no reason to read on (`newms-topic-selection`: "interdisciplinary reach";
  `newms-writing-style`: "reads as technical-only").
- **Descriptive, no concept.** "Couriers are concerned and change their behavior" is the named
  anti-pattern in `newms-theory-building` ("a finding with no mechanism"). No **concept** is offered for
  others to carry elsewhere → fails the **portability test**.
- **"Understudied for X" as the contribution** — exactly the `newms-topic-selection` anti-pattern.
- **No adjudication.** The method is named but we are never told what rival reading (e.g., "workers
  resist by gaming the system") it rules out (`newms-research-design`).
- **Contribution buried** and an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's job
  (`newms-writing-style`), eating the ~8,000-word budget.

---

## After (NM&S arc — question + concept + evidence + broad stakes, contribution front-loaded)

> **Why do workers discipline themselves to an algorithm they can neither see nor contest?** We argue
> that opaque platform ranking produces what we call *datafied control*: a mode of control exercised not
> through a visible manager but through continuous datafication and scoring, which workers internalize as
> anticipatory self-discipline. *(question + argument, in the first breath; the concept, "datafied
> control," and the mechanism, anticipatory self-discipline, are named immediately.)*
>
> The claim matters beyond food delivery. Scholarship on platforms, labor, and surveillance has debated
> whether algorithmic management produces resistance or compliance; we show a third path — workers who
> comply *before* any sanction, organizing their day, their bodies, and their sense of worth around a
> score whose rules they cannot know. If the mechanism holds, it reframes a range of contemporary
> settings — ride-hailing, content creation, warehouse work, even credit scoring — as instances of the
> *same* control process, legible to scholars of digital labor, surveillance, and platform power alike.
> *(why it matters across fields — the contribution is portable, stated against named rivals: not
> resistance, not post-hoc compliance, but anticipatory self-discipline.)*
>
> Distinguishing this from mere gaming-the-system is hard, because both produce score-conscious behavior.
> We combine a multi-month digital ethnography of a courier community with 30 interviews, and we look for
> *when* behavior changes relative to sanction. The adjudication is sharp: if couriers were simply
> resisting — gaming the metric — we would see workarounds *after* penalties; instead we see pre-emptive
> compliance and self-monitoring *before* any sanction, and persisting even when gaming would pay. *(the
> `newms-research-design` adjudication sentence: what we'd see if the rival were true vs. what we see —
> and a mixed-within-strand qualitative design matched to it.)*
>
> Our contribution is conceptual as well as empirical: we define *datafied control* independent of any
> one platform — control through opaque, consequential metrics — and we state its **scope conditions**:
> it operates where the score is *opaque* and tied to *material stakes*, and weakens where workers can
> see or contest the rule. *(contribution + scope conditions — `newms-theory-building`.)* Beyond couriers,
> the lesson is that **datafication can govern conduct without ever issuing an order** — a mechanism a
> researcher studying any metricized setting can import. *(broad lesson that travels.)* All forum data
> are paraphrased to resist reverse-search, informants are pseudonymized, and the study was ethics-board
> approved (see Methods). *(`newms-transparency-and-data`: online-data ethics signaled up front.)*
>
> The paper proceeds by developing the datafied-control argument and its observable implications, then
> presenting the ethnographic and interview evidence that separates anticipatory compliance from gaming,
> and closing with scope conditions and what datafied control implies for theories of platform power.
> *(brief roadmap — carries the argument, not a table of contents.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the NM&S bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded** — by the end of the intro the reader has the **question, the concept, the
  evidence, and the interdisciplinary stakes** (`newms-writing-style`).
- **Reads past the platform and the subfield** — framed for scholars of labor, surveillance, *and*
  platform power; jargon ("datafication") is named and immediately glossed (`newms-writing-style`;
  `newms-topic-selection` "interdisciplinary reach").
- **A concept that travels** — "datafied control" is defined independent of the app and offered for
  others to import, with explicit **scope conditions**; it passes the `newms-theory-building`
  **portability test** ("could a researcher in another context import this?").
- **Design adjudicates the leading rival** — the compliance-vs-gaming **adjudication sentence** is written
  out, and the timing-relative-to-sanction test is matched to it (`newms-research-design`).
- **Concept stated before the evidence** — avoids `newms-theory-building`'s HARKing; the observable
  implications (pre-sanction compliance, persistence despite gaming's payoff) are derived from the
  mechanism.
- **Online-data ethics signaled** — paraphrasing, pseudonymization, and ethics approval named up front
  (`newms-transparency-and-data`).
- **No "understudied-X" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named anti-patterns are
  avoided, and the lean intro respects the ~8,000-word target.

> One-sentence hook (fillable, as the skills imply): "We show that **opaque platform ranking disciplines
> couriers before any sanction** by producing **datafied control**, using **a digital ethnography +
> interviews** that separate anticipatory compliance from gaming the system."
