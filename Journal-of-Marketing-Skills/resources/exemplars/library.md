## By method

### Conceptual model that reframes a marketing construct (theory / framework)

- **Parasuraman, Zeithaml & Berry, "A Conceptual Model of Service Quality and Its Implications for Future Research," Journal of Marketing 1985, 49(4):41–50.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/002224298504900403`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** it took an under-defined phenomenon (quality in *services*, not goods) and
    gave the field a single, plain, managerially usable gaps model — substantive reframing whose
    practitioner value (where service quality breaks down) is immediate. The foundation later
    operationalized as SERVQUAL.
  - **Self-check:** does your framework give managers a new way to *see* a marketing phenomenon, not just
    a new label for a known one?

### Theory that builds and tests a mediating-mechanism model (SEM / survey field data)

- **Morgan & Hunt, "The Commitment-Trust Theory of Relationship Marketing," Journal of Marketing 1994, 58(3):20–38.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/002224299405800302`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** posits commitment and trust as the *key mediating variables* of successful
    relationship marketing, then tests the model (vs. a rival) on field survey data — theory development
    disciplined by data, with a clear managerial throughline about what relationships are built on.
  - **Self-check:** is your mediating mechanism the thing managers can actually move, and do you test it
    against a rival explanation a referee would raise?

### Construct conceptualization + measurement that managers can act on (brand strategy)

- **Keller, "Conceptualizing, Measuring, and Managing Customer-Based Brand Equity," Journal of Marketing 1993, 57(1):1–22.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/002224299305700101`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** defines brand equity from the *customer's* side (differential response to
    marketing driven by brand knowledge) and turns it into something a brand manager can build and audit —
    a substantive advance with a built-in managerial program.
  - **Self-check:** does your construct come with a way to *manage* it, or only to measure it?

### Strategy framework converting marketing into financial return (customer-equity modeling)

- **Rust, Lemon & Zeithaml, "Return on Marketing: Using Customer Equity to Focus Marketing Strategy," Journal of Marketing 2004, 68(1):109–127.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1509/jmkg.68.1.109.24030`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** makes competing marketing strategies tradeable on **projected financial
    return** (change in customer equity relative to spend) — the model exists to change a CMO's
    resource-allocation decision, the JM dual mandate executed in a modeling paper. Pairs with the
    CLV-style mechanism logic in [`../worked-examples/01-introduction.md`](../worked-examples/01-introduction.md).
  - **Self-check:** does your framework let a manager *choose* between options on a metric they are
    accountable for (return, CLV), not just describe an effect?

### Service quality made financially accountable (metrics / managerial decision model)

- **Rust, Zahorik & Keiningham, "Return on Quality (ROQ): Making Service Quality Financially Accountable," Journal of Marketing 1995, 59(2):58–70.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/002224299505900205`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reframes quality as an *investment* that can be overspent, and gives
    managers a way to tie quality outlays to financial return — substantive (quality has decreasing
    returns) and managerial (where to stop spending) in one move.
  - **Self-check:** does your contribution tell a manager not just *that* something works but *how much*
    to invest before it stops paying off?

### Discipline-reframing conceptual article (paradigm / dominant-logic)

- **Vargo & Lusch, "Evolving to a New Dominant Logic for Marketing," Journal of Marketing 2004, 68(1):1–17.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1509/jmkg.68.1.1.24036`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a "big idea" conceptual paper arguing the field's logic is shifting from
    goods-and-transactions to service-and-value-cocreation — JM does publish thought-leading conceptual
    work when the reframing is first-order and consequential for how managers think about exchange.
  - **Self-check:** if your paper is conceptual rather than empirical, is the reframe big enough that the
    field — and practice — would *act* differently, not merely cite it?

## Omitted to keep the list zero-hallucination

To protect the sibling-confusion guard, **only papers individually confirmed on the SAGE Journal of
Marketing article page are listed above.** Candidate landmark papers were *excluded* whenever web search
did not cleanly confirm a **Journal of Marketing** (not JMR / Marketing Science / JCR / JAMS / JCP)
placement with matching volume/issue. In particular:

- Highly cited marketing papers frequently sit in **JMR or Marketing Science**, whose titles and author
  teams overlap heavily with JM — none were added here on memory; each entry above was verified by its
  SAGE DOI resolving to the *Journal of Marketing* masthead.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `journals.sagepub.com` (article page on the
Journal of Marketing, with volume/issue) — **not** on an aggregator that may mislabel the venue — and
update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
