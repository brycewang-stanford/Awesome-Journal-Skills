# Annual Review of Sociology — Exemplar Library

Access date: **2026-06.** The entries below are **real *Annual Review of Sociology* articles**,
verified by DOI / publisher record at the access date. Re-confirm on the official archive before
relying on a citation. **Sibling-journal guard:** do not import ASR, AJS, *Sociological Theory*, or
sister-series (Annual Review of Psychology / Economics) papers as ARSoc exemplars — always check the
venue. Use these to study how an ARSoc review *organizes a subfield and ends with an agenda*, not as
content to copy.

## Verified ARSoc review articles

| Subfield / craft lesson | Article (verified) | Why it is instructive |
|-------------------------|--------------------|------------------------|
| **Stratification — evidence stocktake** | Killewald A, Pfeffer FT, Schachner JN. 2017. "Wealth Inequality and Accumulation." *Annu. Rev. Sociol.* 43:379–404. DOI 10.1146/annurev-soc-060116-053331 | A cross-method synthesis: defines wealth as a stratification construct, weighs measurement and data sources, then reviews trends, determinants, and consequences — comprehensiveness with an analytic spine. |
| **Culture — concept review / framework** | Patterson O. 2014. "Making Sense of Culture." *Annu. Rev. Sociol.* 40:1–30. DOI 10.1146/annurev-soc-071913-043123 | Imposes an integrative framework on a fragmented, multi-school literature rather than listing approaches — a model of the "spine over annotated bibliography" mandate. |
| **Networks — theory consolidation** | Lin N. 1999. "Social Networks and Status Attainment." *Annu. Rev. Sociol.* 25:467–87. DOI 10.1146/annurev.soc.25.1.467 | Traces social-resources theory across two decades and organizes findings around a propositional structure — synthesis prose making studies talk to each other. |
| **Education / inequality — mechanism framing** | Domina T, Penner AM, Penner EK. 2017. "Categorical Inequality: Schools as Sorting Machines." *Annu. Rev. Sociol.* 43:311–30. DOI 10.1146/annurev-soc-060116-053354 | Uses a single organizing metaphor/mechanism ("sorting machines") as the spine that reconciles disparate work on schooling and stratification — accessible across subfields. |
