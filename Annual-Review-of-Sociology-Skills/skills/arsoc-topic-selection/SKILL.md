# Topic Selection for an ARSoc Review (arsoc-topic-selection)

## When to trigger

- You have a candidate sociology literature and need to know if it is "ARSoc-shaped"
- A topic feels either too narrow (a single-study debate) or too sprawling (a whole field)
- You are deciding between ARSoc and a sibling outlet (ASR/AJS empirical paper, *Sociological Theory*, a handbook chapter)
- The literature is young/fast-moving and you suspect it is not yet ready for an annual-volume review

## What ARSoc is for

ARSoc is the **Annual Reviews** sociology survey series: a yearly volume of **invited, authoritative review articles** that are **accessible to sociologists outside the subfield**, not only to its specialists. The reader is a competent sociologist — a culture scholar reading the networks review, a demographer reading the race/ethnicity review — who wants to enter, teach, or locate the open questions of an area they do not currently work in. That cross-subfield reader profile, plus the annual-volume cadence, sets the fit bar. ARSoc covers the breadth of the discipline: stratification, culture, organizations, networks, race/ethnicity, gender, family, demography, political and economic sociology, and more.

## The four ARSoc-fit tests

A literature belongs in ARSoc when it passes all four. If it fails one, route accordingly.

1. **Maturity.** There is a *body* of research — multiple research lines, accumulated evidence, recognizable theoretical debates — not a handful of recent papers. A subfield still being born produces a *premature* review that dates within a volume cycle. (Fails → too young; wait.)
2. **Broad importance.** The questions matter to sociologists generally, not only to a dozen specialists. ARSoc serves the **whole discipline**; a review only its own subfield could love is a specialist-journal review, not an ARSoc article. (Fails → too niche.)
3. **Need for synthesis.** A newcomer cannot currently get oriented without a guide, *and* existing entry points (a recent ARSoc review, a handbook chapter) have not already done the job — a too-recent prior ARSoc review on the same area is a hard barrier. The value is the **map**, not the **list**. (Fails → already synthesized.)
4. **Tractable for the ARSoc envelope.** ARSoc reviews are substantial but bounded; Annual Reviews does not publish a fixed public word limit, so length is set per commissioned article — confirm with your editor (待核实, do not assume a number). One author/team must read and weigh the literature within that envelope. (Fails → carve a coherent axis, or it is a handbook chapter.)

## Decision table

| Situation | Verdict | Action |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Mature, broadly important, needs synthesis, fits the ARSoc envelope | ARSoc-shaped | → `arsoc-proposal-and-commissioning` |
| Important but immature | Not yet | revisit in a volume or two |
| Mature but narrow / specialist-only | Wrong venue | a specialty journal's review section |
| Mature, important, but exhaustive and book-length | Wrong venue | handbook chapter (deeper, longer) |
| You have original data/findings to report | Wrong venue | ASR / AJS / *Sociological Science* / *Demography* |
| A pure theoretical-development argument (not a survey of evidence) | Wrong venue | *Sociological Theory* |

## Framing the animating question

An ARSoc review is organized by a **question about the subfield**, not by a topic label. Before approaching the Committee, write one sentence of each:

- **State-of-knowledge question:** "What do we now know about X, and how confident should we be?"
- **Why now:** what makes a synthesis valuable *for this volume* — a wave of new evidence, a methodological turn (e.g. computational/administrative-data methods), a policy or public debate, convergence or fracture of findings.
- **Reader payoff:** what a sociologist from an adjacent subfield can *do* after reading (enter the area, teach it, locate the open questions and the forward agenda).

## Checklist

- [ ] The subfield passes all four fit tests (maturity, broad importance, need, envelope-tractability)
- [ ] The animating *question about the subfield* is written in one sentence
- [ ] The "why now" is concrete (new evidence / method turn / public-policy salience), not "no one has reviewed this"
- [ ] The intended reader is the **cross-subfield sociologist**, and the payoff is stated
- [ ] You can name the 4–7 research lines / theoretical traditions the review must cover (a coverage skeleton)
- [ ] Checked no too-recent prior ARSoc review covers the same ground
- [ ] Checked it is not an ASR/AJS empirical paper, a *Sociological Theory* argument, or a handbook chapter
- [ ] You are *not* the subfield's only contributor — the review will not become a self-retrospective

## Anti-patterns

- "No one has reviewed X" used as the *sole* justification — absence is not importance
- Reviewing your own research program under a neutral subfield title (self-promotion; ARSoc punishes this)
- Picking an area so new the review freezes a moving target before the volume prints
- A scope so broad it becomes a handbook chapter; so narrow it is a specialist-journal review
- Confusing "topic" (a label like "social networks") with "animating question" (what the review argues about the area)
- Planning to submit cold — ARSoc does not take unsolicited manuscripts (see `arsoc-proposal-and-commissioning`)

## Output format

```text
【Subfield】<the literature / area>
【Four tests】maturity / broad-importance / need-for-synthesis / envelope-tractable — pass or fail each
【Animating question】"What do we know about ___, and how sure are we?"
【Why now】<new evidence / method turn / policy or public salience>
【Reader payoff】<what a cross-subfield sociologist can do after reading>
【Coverage skeleton】<4–7 research lines / theoretical traditions the review must cover>
【Verdict】ARSoc-shaped / rescope / wrong-venue (→ ASR/AJS / Sociological Theory / handbook)
【Source status】verified URL / 待核实 / not asserted
【Next step】→ arsoc-proposal-and-commissioning (get the topic to the Editorial Committee)
```
