# American Anthropologist — Worked Introduction

This is a fictional example for structure only. Do not cite it as evidence.

## Before

This article studies how residents of a coastal town respond to rebuilding after storms. It uses interviews and observation to describe disagreements among households, local officials, and heritage workers. The case is important because storms are increasing and communities need better planning.

## After

Coastal rebuilding is usually narrated as a technical problem of risk, infrastructure, and adaptation. In the town examined here, however, rebuilding became a dispute over kinship obligations, ancestral land, remembered labor, and the moral status of leaving. Drawing on eighteen months of ethnographic fieldwork, archival traces of earlier relocation debates, and collaborative review with community interlocutors, the article shows how residents translated climate risk into a four-field anthropological problem: What counts as evidence for remaining, and who is authorized to decide?

The argument is not that local attachment simply blocks adaptation. Instead, rebuilding work made visible a portable concept, **obligated place**, through which households balanced safety, intergenerational care, material remains, and public accountability. The case contributes to environmental anthropology, heritage studies, and public anthropology by showing how adaptation policy can misrecognize the ethical relations that make relocation imaginable or unacceptable.

## Why It Works

- It starts with a broad anthropological problem, not only a site description.
- It names the evidence base and accountability relation early.
- It turns the case into a portable concept that can travel beyond one community.
- It avoids overstating causality where the design is interpretive and ethnographic.
