# Worked Example: An AA-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **AA introduction bar** from `amanthro-writing-style` and
`amanthro-topic-selection`: **by the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the
argument, the evidence, the author's position in the field, and why it matters to anthropology
*broadly*** — not just to the home subfield. The arc here is **question → four-field significance →
ethnographic setting & access (with positionality) → the grounded finding and its mechanism → the
portable concept (contribution) → brief roadmap**, with AA's distinctive demands that the contribution be
*portable across the four fields*, that *reflexivity* be visible, and that *ethics* be woven in.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Lending Faces: How a Rotating Credit Society Turns Debt into
Kinship in a Coastal Market Town."* Fictional setting: a market town where traders run a rotating
savings-and-credit association ("the circle"). (Subfield: sociocultural anthropology; mode: ethnographic
fieldwork — participant observation + interviews. Chosen because debt and obligation are four-field
stakes — they connect to exchange theory, the archaeology of value, and the language of moral
obligation.)

---

## Before (subfield-insider, descriptive, no portable concept — typical first draft)

> The anthropology of money and finance is a large field. Many scholars have studied rotating
> savings-and-credit associations (ROSCAs) in various societies. In this paper, based on twelve months
> of fieldwork, I describe how members of a rotating credit society in a market town organize their
> contributions and payouts. I conducted participant observation and interviews with members. I find that
> the society is important to its members and helps them manage money. Section 2 reviews the ROSCA
> literature, Section 3 describes my methods, Section 4 describes the society, Section 5 discusses my
> findings, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `amanthro-writing-style` / `amanthro-topic-selection`):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It speaks only to economic-anthropology specialists; an archaeologist,
  linguist, or bioanthropologist is given no reason to care. AA's bar is **four-field significance** — a
  named anti-pattern in `amanthro-topic-selection` ("subfield-only").
- **Description, not argument.** "The society is important to its members" is not a contribution; nothing
  here a scholar in another subfield could *import* (`amanthro-theory-building`'s portability test fails).
- **No positionality.** We learn nothing about the author's relationship to the circle or how access
  shaped what was seen — a gap AA reviewers notice (`amanthro-theory-building`: reflexivity).
- **No ethics in view.** Debt and money are sensitive; consent and anonymization are invisible
  (`amanthro-transparency-and-data`).
- **Over-signposted six-section roadmap** doing the work the argument should do, and padding the word
  count AA caps (`amanthro-writing-style`: notes/sections count toward the limit).

---

## After (AA arc — four-field significance + reflexive argument + portable concept)

> **When traders in the market town hand money to the circle each week, are they saving — or are they
> making kin?** I argue that the rotating credit society does not merely pool savings; it *converts a
> financial obligation into a kinship one*, so that defaulting becomes not a breach of contract but a
> betrayal of relatedness. *(question + the portable claim in the first breath — this is about how debt
> becomes kinship, not a description of a savings club.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond the anthropology of finance: debt and obligation sit at the center of
> anthropology's oldest questions — the gift and reciprocity in social theory, the materialization of
> value that archaeologists read in exchanged objects, and the moral idioms in which obligation is
> *spoken*. If a credit institution can manufacture kinship out of money, it speaks to how economic forms
> become social forms across human life. *(four-field significance, framed so an archaeologist of
> exchange and a linguistic anthropologist of moral talk both see the stake — `amanthro-topic-selection`
> four-field test.)*
>
> I came to the circle not as a neutral observer but as a borrower: to be trusted with fieldwork, I was
> expected to *join*, and my own turn in the rotation taught me what default would have cost me socially.
> My account is shaped by that position, and I read the members' words alongside my own obligation.
> *(positionality made part of the argument's credibility — `amanthro-theory-building` reflexivity, and an
> ethics-of-care stance.)*
>
> Over a year of participant observation and interviews — with consent renewed as the project changed,
> and with all members and the town given pseudonyms because debt relations can expose people to harm — I
> traced how the circle's talk, feasting, and naming practices reclassify a creditor as a kinsperson.
> *(setting, design, and ethics in one breath; consent and anonymization are stated, per
> `amanthro-research-design` and `amanthro-transparency-and-data`.)*
>
> The mechanism is **not** simply trust or social pressure, the usual explanations: members describe, and
> enact, a *transformation of category* — the language of lending gives way to the language of kinship at
> a specific ceremonial moment, after which a missed payment is mourned rather than litigated. *(the
> finding restated with its mechanism and a ruled-out rival reading — the `amanthro-research-design`
> adjudication move.)*
>
> My contribution is a portable concept I call **obligational conversion**: the process by which one kind
> of bond (here, financial) is ritually and linguistically reclassified as another (here, kinship). This
> reframes ROSCAs — long treated as economic risk-pooling — as *kinship-making institutions*, and offers
> a concept an archaeologist of exchanged valuables or a linguist of address terms could carry into their
> own material. *(contribution stated early and as a *portable* argument that travels across the four
> fields — `amanthro-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> The article proceeds in three movements: the institution and the moment of conversion; the language and
> ritual through which it happens; and the scope conditions under which money becomes kin. *(brief
> roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the AA bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Four-field significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names stakes a theorist of
  exchange, an archaeologist of value, and a linguistic anthropologist all recognize
  (`amanthro-topic-selection`: "a scholar in a different subfield should see why it matters").
- **A portable concept, not a description** — "obligational conversion" is offered for others to use,
  passing the `amanthro-theory-building` portability test; the finding is attached to a mechanism with
  scope conditions, not left as "the society is important."
- **Reflexivity is visible** — the author's position as a borrower is part of the evidence, not hidden
  (`amanthro-theory-building`: "name how your position bears on the interpretation").
- **Ethics is woven in** — ongoing consent and pseudonymization appear in the introduction because debt
  can endanger people (`amanthro-transparency-and-data`: protect interlocutors; ethics designed in).
- **The strongest rival reading is adjudicated** — "not simply trust or social pressure… instead a
  transformation of category" (the `amanthro-research-design` adjudication sentence).
- **Reads across the four fields and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and glossed, the roadmap is
  three movements not six sections, and the framing is legible to a generalist within AA's word limit
  (`amanthro-writing-style`: Research Articles ≤ 8,000 words, counts include figures/tables/notes).
