## How to use this library

AA is anthropology's **four-field flagship**: each paper below earns its place by making a result in one
subfield legible and useful to the *whole* discipline (see
[`../../skills/amanthro-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amanthro-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/amanthro-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amanthro-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick
the row whose **subfield × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) makes a four-field claim,
and (2) defends its evidence on its own methodological terms (see
[`../../skills/amanthro-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amanthro-research-design/SKILL.md)).

## By subfield × method

### Sociocultural ethnography (interpretive)

- **Geertz, "Ritual and Social Change: A Javanese Example," AA 1957, 59(1):32–54.**
  Verified: AnthroSource DOI `10.1525/aa.1957.59.1.02a00040`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a single failed funeral ritual becomes a *portable* theoretical claim —
    that culture and social structure can change at different rates and pull against each other. The
    ethnographic particular is made to carry a concept other subfields can import (see
    `amanthro-theory-building`: "portability test").
  - **Self-check:** does your case yield a concept another anthropologist could use, not just a vivid
    description of one event?

### Reflexive / public anthropology (satire-as-critique)

- **Miner, "Body Ritual among the Nacirema," AA 1956, 58(3):503–507.**
  Verified: AnthroSource DOI `10.1525/aa.1956.58.3.02a00080`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** by describing "the Nacirema" (American spelled backward) in exoticizing
    ethnographic prose, Miner turns the discipline's gaze back on itself — a four-field-legible argument
    about how ethnographic framing constructs the "exotic." A model of reflexivity as method.
  - **Self-check:** does your framing make the reader notice the *anthropologist's* position, not only
    the people described (see `amanthro-theory-building`: positionality)?

### Linguistic anthropology (discourse-centered)

- **Sherzer, "A Discourse-Centered Approach to Language and Culture," AA 1987, 89(2):295–309.**
  Verified: AnthroSource (Wiley) AA article record; widely cited as AA 89:295–309.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** argues that the *discourse* (actual situated speech) is the locus where
    language and culture meet — a programmatic claim that reorients how all four fields treat language as
    evidence, not just a linguistics-internal point.
  - **Self-check:** does your language analysis show what the discourse *indexes* about culture, beyond
    its form (see `amanthro-data-analysis`: linguistic row)?

### Biological / physical anthropology (evolutionary + cultural)

- **Livingstone, "Anthropological Implications of Sickle Cell Gene Distribution in West Africa," AA 1958, 60(3):533–562.**
  Verified: AnthroSource DOI `10.1525/aa.1958.60.3.02a00110`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a four-field classic linking a *biological* distribution (the sickle-cell
    gene) to *cultural* practice (agriculture creating malarial mosquito habitats) — biology made legible
    to sociocultural readers, and culture made consequential for human variation.
  - **Self-check:** does your biological finding connect to social or cultural meaning, rather than
    standing as acultural fact (see `amanthro-research-design`: biological mode)?

### Archaeology (archaeological theory / inference)

- **Watson & Fotiadis, "The Razor's Edge: Symbolic-Structuralist Archaeology and the Expansion of Archaeological Inference," AA 1990, 92(3):613–629.**
  Verified: AnthroSource DOI `10.1525/aa.1990.92.3.02a00040`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** debates how far archaeological *inference* can reach toward symbolic and
    structural meaning — a methodological argument about evidence and interpretation that speaks to the
    interpretive and material wings of the discipline at once.
  - **Self-check:** are you explicit about how far your material evidence licenses interpretive claims
    (see `amanthro-research-design`: the adjudication test)?

## Omitted for lack of AA verification (do NOT attribute to AA without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* venue, not AA. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Binford, "Archaeology as Anthropology" (1962)** — verified to be in *American Antiquity* 28(2):217–225,
  **not AA**. A common misattribution because of its "archaeology as anthropology" title.
- **Wolf, "Closed Corporate Peasant Communities in Mesoamerica and Central Java" (1957)** — verified to be
  in the *Southwestern Journal of Anthropology* 13(1):1–18, **not AA**.
- **Wolf, "Kinship, Friendship, and Patron-Client Relations in Complex Societies" (1966)** — a chapter in
  M. Banton, ed., *The Social Anthropology of Complex Societies*, **not an AA article**.
- **Sahlins, "Poor Man, Rich Man, Big-Man, Chief" (1963)** — verified to be in *Comparative Studies in
  Society and History* 5(3):285–303, **not AA**.
- **Geertz, "Religion as a Cultural System" (1966)** — a chapter (Banton, ed.; later *The Interpretation
  of Cultures*), **not an AA article**. Note: a *different* Geertz paper (Javanese ritual, 1957) *is* AA
  and is listed above — do not conflate the two.
- **Appadurai, "Putting Hierarchy in Its Place" (1988)** — verified to be in *Cultural Anthropology*
  3(1):36–49, **not AA**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on AnthroSource / Wiley
(`anthrosource.onlinelibrary.wiley.com`) with volume/issue/pages and the `10.1525/aa.*` or `10.1111/aman.*`
DOI, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit. For background reporting hygiene on the
*quantitative* (biological/archaeological) subfields only, see
[`../../../shared-resources/empirical-methods/reporting-standards.md`](../../../shared-resources/empirical-methods/reporting-standards.md).
