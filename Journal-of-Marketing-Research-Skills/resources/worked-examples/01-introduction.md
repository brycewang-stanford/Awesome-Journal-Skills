# Worked Example: A JMR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **front-loaded research argument** from `jmr-writing-style` and the **dual bar**
from `jmr-contribution-framing` and `jmr-topic-selection`: a JMR introduction must open with the
**marketing phenomenon and research question**, then the gap/tension, then what you do and find — with
**both** contributions visible on page one: the **substantive/theoretical** insight *and* the
**methodological credibility** that makes it believable. It must stay a **research** argument, not a
managerial pitch (that drift signals a *Journal of Marketing* paper, per the sister-journal triage in
`jmr-topic-selection`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When 'Only a Few Left' Backfires: Scarcity Cues, Inferred Stockouts,
and Cross-Category Substitution."* Fictional setting: a hybrid **lab-establishes-mechanism + field-shows-it-in-market**
design — converging online experiments plus a randomized field experiment on a retailer's product pages.
This is a **behavioral** paper in the genre taxonomy of `jmr-topic-selection`, so it leads with the
substantive insight and the *tested mechanism*.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Scarcity appeals are widely used in marketing and have been studied in many contexts. Prior work
> generally finds that scarcity increases purchase intentions. In this paper we run several experiments
> and a field study using a mixed-effects logit model with random intercepts for participant and product
> to examine how low-stock messages affect consumer behavior. Scarcity messaging is an important tactic
> for managers, who should consider when to deploy it to lift conversion. Our results have clear
> implications for retail practice. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 presents Study 1,
> Section 4 presents Study 2, Section 5 presents the field experiment, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `jmr-writing-style`, `jmr-contribution-framing`, `jmr-theory-development`):**

- **No research question and no answer on page one** — a managerial reminder ("scarcity is an important
  tactic") replaces the substantive insight (`jmr-writing-style`: open with the phenomenon and question,
  contribution visible without the appendix).
- **Leads with / foregrounds the estimator** ("a mixed-effects logit model with random intercepts…")
  before the reader knows the question — the demote-the-method rule in `jmr-writing-style`.
- **No mechanism.** It states a directional effect with no *process* — the "black-box effect" anti-pattern
  in `jmr-theory-development`; JMR reviewers expect the mechanism to be tested, not asserted.
- **Only one bar attempted.** It gestures at managerial payoff but never states a substantive *research*
  contribution or why the design is *credible* — the canonical JMR reject in `jmr-contribution-framing`.
- **Managerial-pitch framing** ("managers should consider," "implications for retail practice") as the
  lead — the explicit *Journal of Marketing* drift warned against in `jmr-topic-selection`.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.
- **Numbers/format problems waiting to happen:** no effect sizes or exact statistics previewed; the prose
  must later carry **exact p-values to three digits, no leading zero** (`jmr-writing-style`).

---

## After (JMR arc — phenomenon → question → tension → mechanism → result → dual contribution)

> Retailers increasingly tag products with low-stock cues — "Only 3 left," "Almost gone." The premise is
> that scarcity makes an item more attractive. **But when does a scarcity cue on one product change what
> shoppers buy in a *different* category — and why?** *(marketing phenomenon + research question, in the
> first breath.)*
>
> The standard account predicts a uniform demand lift, yet it is silent on spillovers: a low-stock tag is
> not only a desirability signal but also an **availability** signal, and a shopper who infers the item
> may be unavailable when they return must decide what to do *now*. Whether scarcity helps or backfires
> should therefore depend on which inference the cue triggers. *(the gap/tension — why the existing
> prediction is incomplete.)*
>
> We argue the cue operates through **inferred stockout risk**: a low-stock tag raises the perceived
> chance the item will be gone later, which drives consumers to **substitute toward an available
> alternative**, sometimes in an adjacent category, rather than buy the cued item. This makes
> inferred-stockout risk the **mediator** and the credibility of the cue the **moderator** — when the tag
> is not believed (e.g., a retailer known to overuse scarcity claims), the inference, and the effect,
> disappear. *(the tested psychological process — mediator and moderator named a priori, per
> `jmr-theory-development`.)*
>
> Across three preregistered online experiments and a randomized field experiment on a retailer's live
> product pages, a low-stock cue **reduced** purchase of the cued item by 6.1 percentage points while
> **raising** purchase of an available substitute by 4.3 points (field experiment; *p* = .004).
> Manipulating the mediator directly — reassuring shoppers the item would be restocked — eliminated the
> substitution, and the effect held only when the cue was credible. *(headline result with units and exact
> statistics, plus the mechanism evidence — measured-and-manipulated mediation, moderation-of-process.)*
>
> Our **substantive** contribution is to show that scarcity cues can **backfire for the cued product** by
> reallocating demand across categories through an availability inference — overturning the uniform-lift
> account and identifying *when* scarcity helps versus hurts. *(substantive bar — a belief changed, stated
> against a specific prior view.)* This is **credible** because the mechanism is established by converging
> experiments that both measure and manipulate the mediator, and then shown to move real purchases in a
> field experiment — lab establishes the process, field shows it in the market. *(methodological-credibility
> bar — `jmr-contribution-framing`'s second contribution, stated explicitly.)* The effect is bounded to
> cues a shopper finds **believable**; where scarcity claims are routinely inflated, it does not arise.
> *(honest boundary, which JMR rewards over over-claiming.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the inferred-stockout account; Section 3 reports the
> experiments establishing the mechanism; Section 4 reports the field experiment. *(brief roadmap — no
> over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JMR bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Front-loaded research argument** — opens with the phenomenon and question, not the method or a
  managerial reminder; the contribution is visible on page one (`jmr-writing-style`).
- **Dual bar cleared explicitly** — a one-sentence **substantive** claim ("scarcity can backfire via an
  availability inference") *and* a one-sentence **credibility** claim ("converging mediation experiments +
  a field experiment"), exactly the two sentences `jmr-contribution-framing` requires, plus a **boundary**
  sentence (believable cues only).
- **Mechanism is tested, not asserted** — mediator (inferred stockout risk) and moderator (cue
  credibility) are named *a priori* with a measured-and-manipulated-mediation plan, avoiding the
  "black-box effect" and HARKing anti-patterns in `jmr-theory-development`.
- **Genre-appropriate lead** — as a behavioral paper it leads with the substantive insight and process,
  with the lab→field structure carrying external validity (`jmr-topic-selection`, `jmr-methods`).
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the estimator is not the lead; the design is described in plain terms
  before any technical detail (`jmr-writing-style`).
- **Research-first, not managerial** — no "managers should" lead; managerial use would belong in the
  discussion as a consequence, pre-empting the "why isn't this a *Journal of Marketing* paper?" objection
  (`jmr-topic-selection` sister-journal triage).
- **Exact-statistics discipline previewed** — the headline reports effect sizes with an exact p-value, no
  leading zero (`p` = .004), as `jmr-writing-style` mandates for the prose.
