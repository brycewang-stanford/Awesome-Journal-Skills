# Worked Example: A *Social Forces*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **Social Forces introduction arc** the pack's own skills require. Social Forces is
a **general social-science** flagship that prizes rigor *and* a theoretically meaningful result, and it
enforces a **reference-inclusive 10,000-word cap** — so the introduction must, by the time it ends, lead
with a **theoretical puzzle**, name a **portable mechanism**, ground it in a **data/design**, deliver the
**headline finding**, and state a **contribution a general reader can carry** — all **stated
economically**, because every word in the intro is a word not spent on analysis (`sf-writing-style`:
front-load the contribution; `sf-theory-building`: the portability test + concision check;
`sf-topic-selection`: general-audience significance).

Social Forces publishes quantitative, demographic, comparative-historical, network, and computational
work and judges each on its own terms. This example takes a **multilevel / cross-context quantitative**
angle with strong theoretical framing — the combination the skills treat as a central Social Forces
target.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Thin Ties, Thick Places: How Neighborhood Organizational Density
Conditions the Payoff of Weak Social Ties for Job Finding."* Fictional setting: a multilevel design
linking individuals' tie portfolios to the organizational density of the neighborhoods they live in.

---

## Before (subfield-insider, buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> The role of social networks in the labor market has been studied for decades. Many studies have
> examined how social ties help people find jobs, and there is debate about whether weak ties or strong
> ties matter more. In this paper, we use survey data to examine the relationship between individuals'
> social ties and their job-finding outcomes across different neighborhoods. We estimate multilevel
> models and find that the effect of ties varies across neighborhoods. This question has not been
> examined with this kind of data before. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the
> literature on networks and labor markets, Section 3 describes the data and measures, Section 4 presents
> the multilevel models, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It addresses only network-and-labor-market specialists; a sociologist of
  organizations, neighborhoods, or inequality sees no reason to read on (`sf-topic-selection`:
  general-audience significance; `sf-writing-style`: "reads past the subfield").
- **No mechanism, only "the effect varies."** "The effect of ties varies across neighborhoods" is the
  named anti-pattern in `sf-theory-building` ("mechanisms named but never made observable"; a finding
  with the theory left implicit) → fails the **portability test**.
- **"Has not been examined with this data before" as the contribution** — the `sf-topic-selection`
  anti-pattern (novelty of setting/data is not a contribution).
- **No adjudication.** Multilevel models are named but we are never told what rival account the
  cross-level interaction rules out (`sf-research-design`).
- **Wordy and contribution-buried** — costly under the **reference-inclusive 10,000-word cap**
  (`sf-writing-style`: concision; `sf-theory-building` concision check).

---

## After (*Social Forces* arc — puzzle + mechanism + design + finding + general contribution, economical)

> **Why do weak social ties pay off in the job market for some people but not others — and is the answer
> about the ties, or about the places people live?** We argue the payoff is *conditional on
> organizational infrastructure*: weak ties carry job information only where a neighborhood's churches,
> clubs, and local associations give those ties somewhere to circulate, so it is **organizational
> density — not tie strength alone — that converts a thin connection into a job lead**. *(theoretical
> puzzle + mechanism, in the first two sentences; "organizational density" is named and glossed for a
> general reader.)*
>
> The claim matters beyond job search. A long line of work treats social capital as a property of
> individuals; we show it is **co-produced by the organizational context** a person is embedded in —
> reframing neighborhood effects, civic infrastructure, and network payoffs as instances of the *same*
> contextual-activation process, legible to sociologists of organizations, neighborhoods, and inequality
> alike. *(why it matters to a general audience — the contribution is portable, stated against a specific
> prior view: social capital as contextual, not purely individual.)*
>
> Identifying this is hard because people sort into neighborhoods: those with the most useful ties may
> also choose the densest places, so a naive comparison conflates **context activation** with
> **selection**. We use a multilevel design linking individuals' tie portfolios to the measured
> organizational density of their neighborhoods, model the **cross-level interaction** of tie strength
> with density, and adjudicate sharply: if selection drove our results, weak ties would pay off wherever
> the well-connected cluster regardless of local organizations; instead, **the weak-tie payoff appears
> only where organizational density is high, and disappears where it is low** — net of individual
> resources and neighborhood composition. *(data and design in one tight paragraph, with the
> `sf-research-design` adjudication sentence: what we'd see if the rival were true vs. what we see.)*
>
> The contribution is to specify **social capital as activated by organizational context**, with **scope
> conditions** — it holds where local organizations broker information among weak ties, not where job
> information flows through formal channels that bypass them. *(contribution + scope conditions, stated in
> one sentence — `sf-theory-building`.)* Beyond job finding, the lesson is that **the value of a network
> tie is set by the organizational environment it sits in** — a mechanism a researcher studying any
> resource that flows through ties can import. *(portable, general-audience lesson — the Social Forces
> payoff, stated without crowding the word budget.)*
>
> We develop the activation argument and its observable implications, then present the multilevel design
> and the density-by-tie interaction that separates activation from selection, and close with scope
> conditions for theories of social capital. *(brief roadmap — carries the argument, not a table of
> contents, and spends few of the capped words.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Social Forces* bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded and economical** — by the end of the intro the reader has the **puzzle, the
  mechanism, the design, the finding, and the general stakes**, with no throat-clearing
  (`sf-writing-style`: front-load + concision; the reference-inclusive cap rewards this).
- **Reads past the subfield** — framed for sociologists of organizations, neighborhoods, *and*
  inequality; jargon ("organizational density") is named and glossed (`sf-topic-selection`:
  general-audience significance).
- **A mechanism and concept that travel** — "social capital activated by organizational context" is
  offered for others to import, with explicit **scope conditions**; it passes the `sf-theory-building`
  **portability test** ("could a researcher in another subfield import this?").
- **Design adjudicates the leading rival** — the activation-vs-selection **adjudication sentence** is
  written out and matched to the cross-level interaction (`sf-research-design`: nest individuals in
  contexts; put the contextual level to theoretical work, not treat it as a control).
- **Theory stated before the tests** — avoids the `sf-theory-building` "hypothesizing after results are
  known" anti-pattern; the observable implications (payoff only where density is high) are derived from
  the mechanism.
- **No "not-examined-with-this-data" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named
  `sf-topic-selection` / `sf-writing-style` anti-patterns are avoided, and the method is demoted to a
  tool rather than led with.

> One-sentence hook (fillable, as the skills imply): "We show that **weak ties find jobs only where
> neighborhood organizations let them circulate**, using **a cross-level interaction of tie strength with
> organizational density** to separate contextual activation from selection."
