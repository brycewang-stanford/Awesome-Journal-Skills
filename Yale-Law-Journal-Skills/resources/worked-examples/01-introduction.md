# Worked Example — A YLJ Article Introduction (before → after)

> **FICTIONAL ILLUSTRATION.** The Article, author, holdings, and citations below are **invented** for
> teaching. The case names, statutory sections, and footnotes are **not real** and must not be cited.
> The point is to show *form and altitude* in The Yale Law Journal house style, not to convey legal
> content. For the venue facts, see [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

A hypothetical Article: *"The Forgotten Forum: Administrative Adjudication and the Vanishing Right to a
Hearing."* It argues that agencies have quietly displaced statutory hearing rights through informal
adjudication, and that courts should revive a procedural-due-process floor for these proceedings.

---

## Before (subfield-pitched, footnote-led, no claim up front)

> This Article examines informal adjudication in federal administrative agencies. Part I surveys the
> Administrative Procedure Act's adjudication provisions and the case law interpreting them.¹ Part II
> discusses the rise of informal adjudication across several agencies. Part III considers due process.
> Part IV offers some thoughts on reform. As many scholars have noted, informal adjudication is an
> important and understudied topic.²

Why a YLJ student editor stalls:

- **No claim.** "This Article examines… surveys… discusses… considers… offers some thoughts" is a
  *topic*, not a contestable thesis (see [`../../skills/ylj-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md)).
- **No stakes a generalist feels.** "Important and understudied" is a placeholder, not a reason a
  reader outside administrative law should care.
- **Roadmap of topics, not moves.** Each Part "surveys / discusses / considers" rather than *argues*
  something (see [`../../skills/ylj-argument-structure/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-argument-structure/SKILL.md)).
- **The argument is hiding in the footnotes.** The real contribution is signaled by "as many scholars
  have noted" plus a string cite — the text carries none of it (see [`../../skills/ylj-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-writing-style/SKILL.md)).

---

## After (claim first, generalist stakes, roadmap as moves)

> Every year, federal agencies decide millions of disputes that determine whether a person keeps a
> benefit, a license, or a livelihood — and they decide most of them **without a hearing**.³ The
> Administrative Procedure Act promised a hearing for adjudications "required by statute to be
> determined on the record."⁴ But agencies have learned to route the same decisions through *informal*
> channels the Act never contemplated, and courts have let them.⁵
>
> This Article argues that **informal adjudication has become a hearing-free shadow docket, and that the
> Due Process Clause — not the APA — supplies the floor courts have abandoned.** The claim cuts against
> the prevailing view that informal adjudication is a pragmatic necessity beyond meaningful judicial
> supervision.⁶ It matters beyond administrative law: the same move that lets an agency avoid a hearing
> lets it avoid the record on which judicial review depends, putting a generalist question — when may the
> state decide without listening? — at the center of a doctrinal one.
>
> The argument proceeds in four steps. **Part I** shows that the APA's on-the-record trigger has been
> read so narrowly that it no longer reaches the adjudications that matter most. **Part II** argues that
> this is not drift but design: agencies and reviewing courts share an interest in informality that the
> APA cannot check. **Part III** contends that procedural due process supplies an independent,
> enforceable floor, and specifies its content. **Part IV** answers the strongest objection — that a due
> process floor would ossify agency practice — and bounds the claim to adjudications affecting protected
> interests.

Why this reads as YLJ-shaped:

- **The claim is in bold-faced substance, up front** — contestable, and a generalist ("when may the
  state decide without listening?") can restate it.
- **Stakes are concrete** ("a benefit, a license, or a livelihood") and reach past administrative law.
- **The roadmap is four *moves*** ("shows," "argues," "contends," "answers"), each a claim, with the
  counterargument given its own Part.
- **Footnotes support; the text argues.** The footnotes (³–⁶) hold the authority; the reasoning lives
  above the line and is followable without them.

---

## Checklist this example passes

- [ ] Contestable, generalist, one-sentence thesis stated in the first page
- [ ] Stakes a non-specialist legal reader feels
- [ ] Roadmap written as claims, not "discusses/surveys"
- [ ] Strongest counterargument flagged and assigned a Part
- [ ] Argument legible from text alone; footnotes carry support, not the argument
