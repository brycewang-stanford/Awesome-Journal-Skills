## How to use this library

YLJ is the discipline's **generalist flagship**: each piece below earns its place by making a claim that
reaches the *whole* legal profession, not one doctrinal niche (see
[`../../skills/ylj-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/ylj-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md)).
Pick the row whose **contribution type** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) states a contestable,
generalist claim and (2) carries the argument in the text while the apparatus carries the support (see
[`../../skills/ylj-argument-structure/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ylj-argument-structure/SKILL.md)).

## By contribution type

### Conceptual / analytical framework (jurisprudence)

- **Wesley N. Hohfeld, "Some Fundamental Legal Conceptions as Applied in Judicial Reasoning," 23 Yale
  L.J. 16 (1913).** Verified: JSTOR Vol. 23, No. 1 record (pp. 16–59) + Wikipedia author entry.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** introduces a portable analytical vocabulary (right/duty,
    privilege/no-right, power/liability, immunity/disability) that reorganizes how lawyers reason across
    *every* field — the prototype of a framework contribution whose value is its generality.
  - **Self-check:** does your framework give readers in unrelated doctrines a tool they can reuse, not
    just a relabeling of one area?

### Theoretical reconception of a doctrine (property / administrative state)

- **Charles A. Reich, "The New Property," 73 Yale L.J. 733 (1964).** Verified: YLJ Vol. 73, No. 5
  (April 1964) + the article's own caption page.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reconceives government largess (benefits, licenses, franchises) as a form
    of "property" deserving due-process protection — a single conceptual move with massive downstream
    doctrinal stakes, later influential in the Supreme Court's procedural-due-process cases.
  - **Self-check:** does your reconception change what *courts* must now do, not just how scholars talk?

### Doctrinal critique of a landmark decision (constitutional law)

- **John Hart Ely, "The Wages of Crying Wolf: A Comment on Roe v. Wade," 82 Yale L.J. 920 (1973).**
  Verified: Vol. 82, No. 5 (pp. 920–949) + Wikipedia author entry + openyls record.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a pointed, generalist critique of a single high-profile opinion's
    *reasoning* (distinct from its result) that became a touchstone for constitutional-method debate — a
    model of the focused, high-stakes doctrinal comment (a natural Essay-scale move).
  - **Self-check:** is your critique aimed at the *reasoning* in a way that travels beyond agreement or
    disagreement with the outcome?

### Structural constitutional theory (federalism)

- **Akhil Reed Amar, "Of Sovereignty and Federalism," 96 Yale L.J. 1425 (1987).** Verified: 96 Yale
  L.J. 1425 (1987) + JSTOR Vol. 96 record + Wikipedia author entry.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** advances a structural account of popular sovereignty and federalism
    (the "converse-1983" model) that reframes a foundational constitutional question — an Article that
    reorganizes a field rather than resolving one case.
  - **Self-check:** does your structural argument give a *new organizing principle* a generalist
    constitutional reader can carry into other problems?

### Theoretical foundation for a field (torts / law and economics)

- **Guido Calabresi, "Some Thoughts on Risk Distribution and the Law of Torts," 70 Yale L.J. 499
  (1961).** Verified: 70 Yale L.J. 499 (1961) + YLJ author page.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an early, foundational economic analysis of accident law that helped
    launch a whole research program — a theory contribution whose payoff is the *field* it opens, not a
    single doctrinal fix.
  - **Self-check:** could your theoretical move seed further work by others, or does it end at your
    own application?

## Omitted for lack of YLJ verification (do NOT attribute to YLJ without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** because they
were verified to a *sibling* venue or could not be confirmed in YLJ. Listed only as guardrails:

- **Warren & Brandeis, "The Right to Privacy" (1890)** — verified to be in the **Harvard Law Review**
  (4 Harv. L. Rev. 193), **not YLJ**. A very common misattribution in privacy-law searches.
- **Oliver Wendell Holmes, "The Path of the Law" (1897)** — verified to be in the **Harvard Law
  Review** (10 Harv. L. Rev. 457), **not YLJ**.
- **Ronald Coase, "The Problem of Social Cost" (1960)** — verified to be in the **Journal of Law and
  Economics** (3 J.L. & Econ. 1), **not YLJ**. (The task seed flagged this trap explicitly.)
- **Herbert Wechsler, "Toward Neutral Principles of Constitutional Law" (1959)** — verified to be in the
  **Harvard Law Review** (73 Harv. L. Rev. 1), **not YLJ**.
- Pieces in **Yale's other student journals** (Yale Law & Policy Review, Yale Journal on Regulation, Yale
  Journal of Law & Technology, Yale Journal of International Law) — these are **separate** journals with
  their own submission systems; do not list them as YLJ.

Before adding any new piece to this library, confirm it on the Yale Law Journal site / JSTOR with
volume, starting page, and year; cross-check the author entry; and update the access-date header. When in
doubt, omit.
