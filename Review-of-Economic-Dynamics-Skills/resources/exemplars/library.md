# RED Exemplars Library (method × topic)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06-08.** Every paper below was checked to confirm it
> actually appeared in the **Review of Economic Dynamics** (Elsevier, for the **Society for Economic
> Dynamics** / SED), with year and volume/issue, against the journal's RePEc/EconPapers `red:issued`
> records and the Elsevier ScienceDirect article pages. Candidates outside the confirmed RED record —
> including papers that turned out to be in a **sibling** outlet — were **omitted** and documented below. This is
> deliberately a short, clean, verified list rather than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Sibling-confusion guardrail.** RED is *not* the *Journal of Monetary Economics*, *AEJ:
> Macroeconomics*, the *Journal of Economic Theory*, or *Macroeconomic Dynamics*, and it is *not* the
> *Review of Economic Studies* or the *Journal of Political Economy*. Several canonical
> heterogeneous-agent papers (Aiyagari; Krusell–Smith) live in those outlets, **not** RED — see the
> omissions list.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only**. It does not
> reproduce or invent calibrated magnitudes, coefficients, or specific findings — read the original on
> ScienceDirect / the authors' pages before citing any number. For RED-specific scope, abstract limits,
> and house facts, see [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × question** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a *dynamic*
mechanism the core contribution and carries it with a *dynamic model* — the RED bar (see
[`../../skills/red-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/red-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/red-contribution-framing/SKILL.md`](../../skills/red-contribution-framing/SKILL.md), and
[`../../skills/red-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/red-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each, ask the
self-check question before claiming your paper is "RED-shaped."

## By method

### Heterogeneous-agent OLG quantitative policy model (computational macro)

- **Conesa & Krueger, "Social Security Reform with Heterogeneous Agents," RED 1999, 2(4):757–795.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:v2y1999i4p757-795`; ScienceDirect `S1094202598900394`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a calibrated overlapping-generations incomplete-markets economy where
    idiosyncratic risk gives a pay-as-you-go system an insurance/redistribution role — the dynamics
    (life-cycle uncertainty) *are* the contribution, the canonical RED "quantitative heterogeneous-agent
    policy" paper. Pairs with the SED readership's lens (`red-topic-selection`).
  - **Self-check:** does idiosyncratic risk over the life cycle drive your result, so a representative-agent
    version would miss the mechanism entirely?

### Quantitative growth / misallocation model + microdata calibration (development macro)

- **Restuccia & Rogerson, "Policy Distortions and Aggregate Productivity with Heterogeneous Establishments," RED 2008, 11(4):707–720.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:07-48`; ScienceDirect `S1094202508000203`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent growth model with heterogeneous producers turns
    producer-level distortions into an aggregate-TFP counterfactual — model-disciplined quantitative work
    delivering one memorable magnitude, the prototype "new quantitative result" contribution in
    `red-contribution-framing`.
  - **Self-check:** does your model map cleanly to producer-level heterogeneity and deliver one headline
    aggregate counterfactual, not just a calibration exercise?

### Real-business-cycle model confronted with financial data (business cycles)

- **Gomme, Ravikumar & Rupert, "The Return to Capital and the Business Cycle," RED 2011, 14(2):262–278.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:08-123`; ScienceDirect `S1094202510000591`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** constructs a measured return to business capital and confronts the
    neoclassical growth model with the cyclical behavior of financial returns — model-disciplining
    evidence that pins down a model element, exactly the fourth contribution type in
    `red-contribution-framing`.
  - **Self-check:** does your evidence discipline (confirm or refute) a specific element of a dynamic
    model the field already argues about?

### General-equilibrium search/supply labor-market model (labor dynamics)

- **Krusell, Mukoyama, Rogerson & Sahin, "Gross Worker Flows and Fluctuations in the Aggregate Labor Market," RED 2020.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:20-206`; ScienceDirect `S109420252030048X`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a three-state general-equilibrium model unifying labor-supply forces and
    matching frictions to match gross worker flows over the cycle — a *new mechanism* (frictions vs.
    choice) shown by what turns it on, squarely the SED labor-dynamics readership.
  - **Self-check:** does your model decompose a cyclical fact into competing dynamic forces and show which
    one the data selects?

### Special-issue cross-country quantitative facts (inequality / data for macro)

- **Krueger, Perri, Pistaferri & Violante, "Cross-Sectional Facts for Macroeconomists," RED 2010, 13(1):1–14.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:10-0`; ScienceDirect `S1094202509000726`; the lead article of the RED
  Vol. 13(1) special issue.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** assembles, for nine countries, the level and evolution of inequality in
    wages, earnings, income, consumption, and wealth — the facts a quantitative dynamic model must match.
    Shows RED publishes *model-disciplining empirical dynamics*, not only solved models.
  - **Self-check:** do your facts state, over time and over the life cycle, the moments a structural
    dynamic model would be calibrated to hit?

### Multi-survey empirical inequality dynamics (applied macro, descriptive)

- **Heathcote, Perri & Violante, "Unequal We Stand: An Empirical Analysis of Economic Inequality in the United States, 1967–2006," RED 2010, 13(1):15–51.**
  Verified: RePEc `red:issued:09-214`; same RED Vol. 13(1) special issue; NBER w15483 working version.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** traces inequality through the full mapping from individual wages to
    earnings to disposable income to consumption and wealth, integrating CPS, PSID, CEX, and SCF — the
    template for the empirical-dynamics facts a heterogeneous-agent model is built to reproduce.
  - **Self-check:** does your empirical work follow a *dynamic* object (a distribution evolving over time
    and the life cycle) that a structural macro model must explain?

## Omitted for lack of full RED verification (do not attribute to RED without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking — each is a
sibling-confusion trap that web search resolved against RED:

- **Aiyagari, "Uninsured Idiosyncratic Risk and Aggregate Saving" (1994)** — verified to be in the
  *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 109(3):659–684, **not RED**. Listed only as a guardrail.
- **Krusell & Smith, "Income and Wealth Heterogeneity in the Macroeconomy" (1998)** — verified to be in
  the *Journal of Political Economy* 106(5):867–896, **not RED**.
- **Krueger & Perri, "Does Income Inequality Lead to Consumption Inequality?" (2006)** — verified to be in
  the *Review of Economic Studies* 73(1):163–193, **not RED** (despite the same authors appearing in the
  RED 13(1) special issue above — a direct sibling-confusion trap).
- **Den Haan, "Comparison of solutions to the incomplete markets model with aggregate uncertainty"
  (2010)** and the Den Haan–Judd–Juillard computational suite — verified to be in the *Journal of Economic
  Dynamics and Control* 34(1), **not RED** (a different "dynamics" journal at the same publisher).
- **Maliar & Maliar, envelope-condition / numerical-methods papers** — appear in *Economics Letters* and
  the *Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control*; **no RED placement confirmed** by web search, so omitted.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on the RED article record (RePEc `red:issued:...`
and/or the Elsevier ScienceDirect RED page with volume/issue) and update the access-date header. When in
doubt — especially for heterogeneous-agent or "dynamics"-titled papers that often land in JME, JEDC, RES,
or JPE — **omit**.
