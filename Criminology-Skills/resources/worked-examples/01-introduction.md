# Worked Example: A *Criminology*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the introduction arc the skills in this pack ask for. *Criminology* is the
**interdisciplinary flagship of the American Society of Criminology** (Wiley), so the bar is a
**general criminological contribution** — a mechanism the field can reuse — not a dataset-specific
correlation. The arc here follows `crim-writing-style` and `crim-theory-building`:

**criminological question → why it's hard (the rival explanation) → theory & mechanism → design &
variation → headline result tied to the mechanism → contribution & portability → brief roadmap** —
with the house rules that the intro **leads with the mechanism, not the dataset**, **front-loads the
contribution** (question + theory + design + "so what" by the end of the intro), spells out acronyms,
and uses **bias-free, person-first language** for justice-involved people.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Steady Work and the Within-Person Turn Away from Crime: Evidence
from a Subsidized-Employment Lottery."* Fictional setting: an oversubscribed transitional-jobs program
that admitted people leaving incarceration **by lottery**, with self-reported and official re-offending
tracked for three years.

---

## Before (leads with the dataset — typical first-draft intro)

> Recidivism among formerly incarcerated offenders is a widely studied topic in the criminological
> literature. Many studies have used administrative data to examine predictors of re-arrest. In this
> paper, we analyze a panel dataset of program participants and estimate fixed-effects and survival
> models of recidivism, using data from a transitional-jobs program. We include controls for age, prior
> record, and demographics. Employment is an important factor in reentry. Section 2 reviews the
> literature, Section 3 describes the data, Section 4 presents the methods, Section 5 reports results,
> and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `crim-writing-style`, `crim-topic-selection`, `crim-theory-building`):**

- **Leads with the dataset and the estimator** ("we analyze a panel dataset… estimate fixed-effects and
  survival models") instead of the criminological question — the named anti-pattern in
  `crim-writing-style` ("an intro that describes a dataset before stating the criminological question").
- **No theory and no mechanism.** It never says *why* steady work would change offending, so the result
  would be "just a finding" (`crim-theory-building` / `crim-topic-selection` atheoretical flag).
- **No within- vs. between-person distinction** — fatal for a life-course turning-point claim, where the
  theory lives or dies on within-individual change (`crim-research-design`, `crim-theory-building`).
- **Stigmatizing labels** ("offenders") rather than person-first language (`crim-writing-style`).
- **No headline result, no contribution, no scope/portability**; an over-signposted roadmap doing the
  argument's work.
- **Reads as a program evaluation** — drifting toward the sister policy journal *Criminology & Public
  Policy* rather than a field-level *Criminology* contribution (`crim-topic-selection`).

---

## After (*Criminology* arc — mechanism-led, theory-forward, contribution up front)

> **Does landing steady work actually turn people away from crime, or do the people who find work
> differ in ways that would have lowered their offending anyway?** This is the central puzzle of
> life-course criminology — whether employment is a genuine *turning point* that produces
> **within-person** change, or merely a marker of pre-existing differences between people. We show that
> a transitional job causes a within-individual decline in offending: among people leaving
> incarceration, random assignment to subsidized work cut the three-year re-offending rate by 9
> percentage points (from 46% to 37%). *(criminological question + the rival selection story + headline
> answer, in the first breath — and within-person stated explicitly.)*
>
> Answering this cleanly is hard because work and desistance are jointly produced: people already
> moving away from crime are the ones who seek and keep jobs, so an ordinary comparison of employed and
> unemployed people confounds the job with the turn that preceded it. *(why it is hard — the selection
> confound a referee raises first.)*
>
> Our mechanism follows age-graded informal social control: a job restructures **routine activities and
> social bonds** — it fills unstructured time, attaches a person to coworkers and a supervisor, and
> raises the stakes of a new arrest — so offending should fall *within the same individual* once steady
> work begins, and the drop should be largest where the job most reorganizes daily life. *(theory &
> mechanism — a reusable causal story, not a coefficient.)*
>
> We exploit an oversubscribed program that admitted applicants **by lottery**, generating random
> variation in access to a transitional job among people with similar reentry circumstances. We track
> both self-reported offending and official re-arrest, and read the two against each other to bound the
> dark figure of unrecorded crime. Random assignment lets us compare otherwise-similar people, and the
> panel structure lets us isolate within-person change rather than stable between-person differences.
> *(design & variation — identification stated, crime measure and the dark figure named.)*
>
> Assignment to a transitional job lowers three-year re-offending by **9 percentage points**, with the
> decline concentrated in the months a person is actively working and largest among those who entered
> with the most unstructured time. The effect operates within individuals: the same person offends less
> while employed than before, ruling out the selection story that more job-ready people simply offend
> less. *(headline result tied back to the mechanism; the rival is adjudicated.)*
>
> Our contribution is to identify **employment as a within-person turning point, not a between-person
> trait** — the reform changes no person's history, yet reorganizes the daily routines and bonds through
> which desistance operates. This adjudicates a long-running ambiguity in life-course criminology
> between "work causes desistance" and "desisters find work." *(contribution stated early, relative to a
> specific prior debate.)* The mechanism is **portable**: any institution that restructures routine
> activities and social bonds — schooling, military service, residential stability — should produce
> within-person change in offending, with the same scope condition that the effect holds while the bond
> is active and may fade when it lapses. *(portability + scope condition — a field-level, not
> sample-specific, lesson.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the turning-point argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 describes the lottery, the sample, and the offending measures; Section 4
> presents the within-person estimates and adjudicates the selection rival; Section 5 examines scope
> conditions and mechanism. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Criminology* bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Leads with the mechanism, not the dataset** — the opening is a criminological question and a
  turning-point mechanism; the data and lottery are demoted to evidence (`crim-writing-style`: "lead
  with the mechanism, not the dataset").
- **Front-loaded contribution** — question, theory, design, and "so what" are all present by the end of
  the intro (`crim-writing-style` reach-the-field rule).
- **Theory-forward, not a bare correlation** — an explicit mechanism (routine activities + social bonds),
  observable implications (within-person decline, concentrated while working), and a scope condition
  (holds while the bond is active) — the `crim-theory-building` spine.
- **Within- vs. between-person made explicit** and used to **adjudicate the rival** ("work causes
  desistance" vs. "desisters find work") — the `crim-research-design` adjudication test and the
  `crim-theory-building` between/within warning.
- **Crime-measure validity surfaced** — self-report read against official re-arrest, with the **dark
  figure** named (`crim-research-design` crime-data validity).
- **Bias-free, person-first language** throughout ("people leaving incarceration," "people with similar
  reentry circumstances"), never "offenders/felons" as identities (`crim-writing-style`).
- **General-flagship fit, not a policy memo** — the lesson is a portable criminological mechanism, not a
  verdict on one program, keeping it in *Criminology* rather than *Criminology & Public Policy*
  (`crim-topic-selection`).
- **Acronym discipline** — no unspelled jargon; any official-data acronym (UCR/NIBRS/NCVS) would be
  spelled on first use (`crim-writing-style`).
