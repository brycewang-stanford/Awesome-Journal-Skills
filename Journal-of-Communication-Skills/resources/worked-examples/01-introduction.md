# Worked Example: A Journal-of-Communication-Style Introduction (before → after)

JoC is the **field-wide flagship of the International Communication Association** — a multi-paradigm
communication-science journal. The introduction is not judged by platform novelty but by whether a
communication researcher *outside the home subfield* can see the question, the argument, the evidence,
and **why it matters to communication broadly** by the end of the intro. This example demonstrates that
arc from this pack's skills:

**general question → why it's a field-level problem → construct & mechanism → setting & design → headline
finding → portable contribution → scope conditions → brief roadmap** — with the JoC rules that the
**contribution is front-loaded**, **subfield jargon is minimized or defined**, prose is **argument-first**,
and the abstract stays **≤ 150 words** (`joc-writing-style`). The bar is *general significance*, not
"new on this platform" (`joc-topic-selection`), and a bare effect is not a contribution until it is
attached to a **portable mechanism** (`joc-theory-building`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Corrective Cues: How Visible Peer Fact-Checking Reshapes the
Spread of Misinformation Across Platforms."* Fictional setting: a multi-wave online experiment plus a
matched observational corpus in which ordinary users append public corrections to misleading posts.

---

## Before (subfield-insider draft — buries the contribution)

> Misinformation on social media has been studied extensively, and scholars have proposed many
> interventions. In this paper we focus on peer fact-checking on Platform X. We ran an experiment and
> also collected a large dataset of posts, which we analyzed using a fine-tuned transformer classifier
> with supervised machine learning to detect corrections. We find that corrections are associated with
> changes in sharing. Misinformation is an important problem for society and platforms. Section 2 reviews
> the literature on fact-checking, Section 3 describes our experimental procedure, Section 4 describes the
> computational pipeline, Section 5 reports the results, and Section 6 discusses implications.

**What's wrong (against `joc-writing-style`, `joc-topic-selection`, `joc-theory-building`):**

- **Platform-bound and method-led.** It leads with "Platform X" and a "fine-tuned transformer classifier"
  before stating any communication question — a `joc-topic-selection` anti-pattern ("new on this platform"
  as the contribution) and a `joc-research-design` warning (method for its own sake).
- **No general significance.** A health-comm or interpersonal-comm reader cannot tell why this matters
  beyond one platform; the stakes for communication *broadly* are never stated.
- **No construct, no mechanism, no contribution.** "Associated with changes in sharing" is a bare
  effect; `joc-theory-building` requires the communication process and a portable argument.
- **Throat-clearing and a literature dump** ("studied extensively," "important problem") that engages no
  specific debate.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** that mirrors the methods sections instead of advancing the argument; it
  also spends scarce words against the **35-page** limit.

---

## After (JoC arc — contribution front-loaded, reads for the whole field)

> **When ordinary users publicly correct a misleading post, does the correction travel — and does it slow
> the misinformation, or merely add noise the original audience ignores?** We show that *visible* peer
> fact-checking reduces onward sharing of the false claim by 27% relative to an identical post left
> uncorrected, and that the effect operates not by changing what the original poster believes but by
> reshaping what **bystanders** infer the community will sanction. *(general question + headline answer +
> why it matters, in the first breath — the construct named, units included.)*
>
> This is a field-level problem, not a platform quirk: every communication subfield — health, political,
> organizational, risk — now asks how *audiences*, not just sources, police the accuracy of shared
> messages. Yet most evidence treats fact-checking as a message aimed at the *misinformed individual*,
> leaving open whether its real work is **social-normative and audience-directed**. *(why it is a
> general-significance problem; engages a specific prior view rather than the whole literature.)*
>
> We define a **corrective cue** as a publicly visible, peer-authored signal that a claim is contested,
> and theorize its mechanism as **perceived injunctive norm activation**: the cue tells onlookers that
> sharing the claim risks social disapproval, independent of whether anyone's factual belief changes.
> This makes the construct portable — the same logic should govern any visible peer signal about message
> acceptability, across platforms and topics. *(construct defined and distinguished from "belief
> correction"; mechanism made explicit and portable — the `joc-theory-building` core.)*
>
> We test this with a preregistered, three-wave online experiment that randomizes the *visibility* of an
> identical correction (n = 3,200), and triangulate with a matched observational corpus of 48,000 posts
> in which automated correction-detection is **validated against a human-coded gold-standard sample**
> (Krippendorff's α = .84). The design lets us separate the audience-norm account from the
> belief-change account: if corrections worked through belief, hidden and visible corrections would move
> sharing equally; if through norms, only *visible* ones would. *(setting & design in one paragraph; the
> adjudication test from `joc-research-design` stated plainly — and the computational measure is
> human-validated, not treated as ground truth.)*
>
> Visible corrections cut onward sharing by **27%** (off a 14% baseline), while otherwise-identical
> hidden corrections do not move it, and the gap is **largest among bystanders who never engaged with the
> original post** — exactly the pattern the norm account predicts and the belief account does not.
> *(headline finding restated with the discriminating pattern; the rival is ruled out, not just
> mentioned.)*
>
> Our contribution is to reframe peer fact-checking as **audience-directed normative communication**
> rather than source-directed belief correction: the correction changes the *spread* of a message by
> changing what onlookers think the community will tolerate, not by persuading the misinformed. *(portable
> contribution stated early, relative to a specific prior view.)* The argument is bounded — we expect it
> to hold where corrections are publicly visible and communities have legible sharing norms, and to weaken
> in private channels or norm-ambiguous spaces. *(scope conditions: portability ≠ universality.)* Beyond
> this study, the corrective-cue logic gives health, political, and organizational communication a common
> handle on a question each faces separately: **how visible audience behavior, not just source content,
> governs which messages travel.** *(general lesson that reaches the whole field.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the corrective-cue argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 reports the preregistered experiment; Section 4 triangulates with the validated
> observational corpus. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## A ≤ 150-word abstract for the same paper (`joc-writing-style`)

> Does visible peer fact-checking slow misinformation, and through what process? We argue that publicly
> visible, peer-authored corrections — **corrective cues** — reduce the *spread* of false claims not by
> changing the misinformed source's beliefs but by activating perceived injunctive norms among bystanders
> about which messages the community will sanction. A preregistered three-wave experiment (n = 3,200)
> randomizes correction *visibility*, triangulated with a matched corpus of 48,000 posts using
> human-validated automated correction-detection (α = .84). Visible corrections cut onward sharing 27%
> relative to identical uncorrected posts; otherwise-identical hidden corrections do not, and the effect
> concentrates among non-engaging bystanders — distinguishing an audience-norm mechanism from belief
> change. We reframe peer fact-checking as audience-directed normative communication, bounded to settings
> with visible corrections and legible sharing norms. The account gives health, political, and
> organizational communication a shared handle on how audience behavior, not just source content, governs
> message diffusion. *(≈148 words.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JoC bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded; reads for the whole field** — the question, mechanism, and the "27%"
  headline appear immediately, framed so a non-specialist grasps the stakes; the construct ("corrective
  cue") is defined and the one piece of jargon ("injunctive norm") is given in plain terms
  (`joc-writing-style`: front-load the contribution, minimize/define subfield jargon, argument-first prose).
- **General significance, not platform novelty** — the lesson is stated for health, political, and
  organizational communication, not "new on Platform X" (`joc-topic-selection` fit test, anti-pattern
  avoided).
- **Portable mechanism, not a bare effect** — the communication process (audience-directed norm
  activation) is explicit, the construct is distinguished from a neighbor (belief correction), and the
  argument is stated as importable by other subfields (`joc-theory-building` portability test), with
  **scope conditions** so portability ≠ universality.
- **Design adjudicates the rival** — the visible-vs-hidden contrast is the one-sentence adjudication test
  from `joc-research-design`, and the **computational measure is validated against a human gold standard**
  with reported reliability rather than trusted as ground truth.
- **Preregistration noted; theory stated before tests** — avoids HARKing (`joc-theory-building`), and
  preregistration is the kind of thing to flag in the cover letter (`joc-research-design`).
- **Format-disciplined** — no throat-clearing or literature dump, a lean roadmap, and a **≤ 150-word**
  abstract that still states question, approach, and finding — respecting the **35-page** main-document
  limit (`joc-writing-style`).
