# Worked Example: A JPE-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JPE introduction arc** from `jpe-literature-positioning`
(**hook/question → what we know → the gap/tension → this paper → findings + contribution**) written in the
**JPE voice** from `jpe-writing-style`: **spare, economics-first, unhedged where the evidence warrants,
the mechanism stated in words before any method**. It also applies the `jpe-topic-selection` fit test —
a mechanism-driven question with an explicit theoretical prediction and a portable lesson, not an
atheoretical correlation — and JPE's hard house convention: **Chicago author-date citations**, never
numbered.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Licenses as Entry Costs: How Occupational Permits Raise Prices by
Thinning Competition."* Fictional setting: a country whose states phased in occupational-licensing
requirements for a service trade at different times.

---

## Before (buries the economics under procedure — typical first-draft intro)

> It is important to note that the relationship between occupational licensing and market outcomes has
> been studied extensively. Many papers have examined wages, employment, and prices. In this paper, we
> will run a difference-in-differences regression of service prices on a licensing dummy, using a
> two-way fixed-effects specification with state and year fixed effects and standard errors clustered at
> the state level [1]. We also estimate an event study and conduct several robustness checks. We find
> that licensing appears to be associated with a potentially meaningful increase in prices. These
> findings may have important implications for policymakers. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3
> describes the data, Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, Section 5 reports results, Section 6
> discusses robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against the JPE skills):**

- **Procedure before economics.** It opens with the regression specification, not the economic question —
  the lead anti-pattern in both `jpe-writing-style` ("method-first writing") and
  `jpe-literature-positioning` ("explains *what you did* before establishing *why economics needed it*").
- **No mechanism.** "Licensing is associated with prices" is the atheoretical-correlation pitch
  `jpe-topic-selection` rejects — there is no economic channel and no theoretical prediction being tested.
- **Hedged to the point of saying nothing** ("appears to be associated with a potentially meaningful
  increase") — the exact softening `jpe-writing-style` says to cut.
- **Throat-clearing** ("It is important to note that...", "In this paper, we will...") that the style
  skill names explicitly.
- **Wrong citation house style:** a numbered `[1]` reference. JPE is **Chicago author-date** — a hard
  University of Chicago Press convention and a frequent mis-template flag.
- **No portable lesson and no contribution**; an over-signposted seven-section roadmap doing the
  argument's job.

---

## After (JPE arc — economics first, mechanism stated, contribution early)

> **Occupational licensing is usually debated as a labor-market policy — does it raise practitioners'
> wages? We ask instead what it does to the product market, and show that licensing raises the price of
> licensed services by 6.8% primarily by thinning the number of competing providers, not by raising any
> single provider's costs.** *(hook/question + headline answer with units, economics first — the JPE
> first-move.)*
>
> Standard price theory is unambiguous about the channel: a binding entry requirement is an entry cost,
> and in a market with free entry an entry cost reduces the equilibrium number of firms and raises the
> markup (Stigler 1961; Becker 1968 on how regulation reshapes incentives). What the licensing literature
> has largely measured, however, is the *wage* effect on incumbents — the rent — while the *price* effect
> that the same entry-cost logic predicts has been treated as a by-product rather than tested as the
> mechanism. *(what we know + the canonical theory the result speaks to, in Chicago author-date — and the
> precise gap, a conflated channel, stated as an economic shortcoming.)*
>
> We test the entry-cost mechanism directly. Theory predicts that if licensing works through entry, its
> price effect should operate through provider counts: ∂(price)/∂(license) > 0 *and* ∂(providers)/∂(license)
> < 0, with little change in incumbents' marginal cost. We exploit the staggered phase-in of licensing
> across states, comparing each state to not-yet-licensed states, and we verify no differential pre-trends
> in prices before a state adopts. *(this paper — mechanism first, with the falsifiable theoretical
> prediction, then the identifying variation in two sentences.)*
>
> Licensing raises service prices by **6.8%** and reduces the number of active providers by **9.1%**,
> while incumbents' input costs and service quality are unchanged — the price rises because the market
> grows more concentrated, not because serving a customer becomes more expensive. The effect appears only
> after adoption and grows over three years. *(findings, stated plainly with magnitudes and the mechanism
> evidence previewed — not just the headline.)*
>
> Our contribution is to identify **the product-market consequences of an entry restriction as distinct
> from its labor-market rents**: prior work credited licensing's price effect to higher costs or quality;
> we show it is an equilibrium thinning of competition that the entry-cost model predicts and the wage
> literature missed. *(contribution stated early and relative to a specific prior view, as
> `jpe-topic-selection` requires — an economic claim, not a data-novelty claim.)* The portable lesson is
> that **any policy that raises the fixed cost of entering a market — licensing, certification, minimum
> capital requirements — should be evaluated by its effect on the number of competitors, not only on the
> incomes of those already inside.** *(general lesson that travels beyond the setting.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 lays out the entry-cost model and its testable predictions;
> Section 3 describes the licensing phase-in and data; Section 4 presents the design and results; Section
> 5 isolates the provider-count mechanism. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JPE bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Economics before method** — the first sentence is the economic question and the channel; the
  difference-in-differences design is demoted to a tool introduced only where the design is discussed
  (`jpe-writing-style`: "Lead with the economics, then the method").
- **A real mechanism, with a theoretical prediction the paper tests** — the entry-cost channel is stated
  in one sentence and made falsifiable (signs on price *and* provider count), satisfying the
  `jpe-topic-selection` mechanism and theory-linkage filters rather than reporting a net correlation.
- **The canonical theory is engaged, in JPE's house style** — the result is positioned against price
  theory with **Chicago author-date** citations (Stigler 1961; Becker 1968), addressing the
  `jpe-literature-positioning` rule to engage the foundational theory and not only the recent applied
  frontier, and never using numbered references.
- **Results stated plainly with magnitudes** (6.8%, 9.1%), hedging removed — the `jpe-writing-style`
  unhedged-where-warranted rule.
- **Contribution is an economic claim stated early and against a specific prior view** ("product-market
  thinning, not cost or quality"), not "we are the first to use this data."
- **Equilibrium reasoning is explicit** — the price effect is an equilibrium change in the number of
  firms, the kind of market-clearing logic `jpe-topic-selection` says a Chicago referee will demand.
- **A portable lesson** generalizes to certification and capital requirements, meeting the JPE generality
  premium.
