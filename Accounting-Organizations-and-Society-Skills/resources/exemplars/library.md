# AOS Exemplars Library (tradition × topic)

> **Curation principle (zero hallucination).** Every entry below is a famous, canonical *Accounting,
> Organizations and Society* paper — the kind cited hundreds or thousands of times and taught in
> doctoral seminars — listed here from that settled public record (checked 2026-07-16). Volume/issue
> details are given where they are part of the standard citation; **re-verify exact pagination on
> ScienceDirect (`sciencedirect.com`, ISSN 0361-3682) before citing** in a manuscript. This file gives
> **design and positioning guidance only**: it reproduces no findings, quotations, or numbers — read the
> originals. For journal policy (portal, anonymization, OA), see
> [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).
>
> **Sibling-confusion guard.** AOS is not *Management Accounting Research*, *AAAJ*, *Critical
> Perspectives on Accounting*, or *The Accounting Review* — several publish on identical topics. Each
> entry below belongs to the AOS canon specifically; when adding new entries, confirm the journal on the
> ScienceDirect article page, not by topic resemblance.

## How to use this library

Pick the row closest to your **tradition × question**, then study how that paper earns its place in a
journal whose bar is *conceptual insight into accounting's social operation* (see
[`aos-topic-selection`](../../skills/aos-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`aos-contribution-framing`](../../skills/aos-contribution-framing/SKILL.md)). Each entry carries a
self-check question to run against your own draft.

## Foundational statements

- **Hopwood (1976), "Editorial: The path ahead," AOS 1(1).** The founding manifesto: accounting must be
  studied in its behavioral, organizational and social contexts.
  *Self-check:* could your paper's motivation paragraph have been written for a capital-markets journal?
  If yes, it fails Hopwood's founding test.
- **Burchell, Clubb, Hopwood, Hughes & Nahapiet (1980), "The roles of accounting in organizations and
  society," AOS 5(1).** The programmatic statement that accounting acquires roles — rationalization,
  legitimation, ammunition — beyond its official ones.
  *Self-check:* which unofficial role of accounting does your paper document or theorize?
- **Hopwood (1983), "On trying to study accounting in the contexts in which it operates," AOS 8(2–3).**
  The methodological charter for field-based accounting research.
  *Self-check:* does your design actually observe accounting *operating in context*, or only people
  talking about it?

## Qualitative field studies

- **Roberts & Scapens (1985), "Accounting systems and systems of accountability," AOS 10(4).**
  Structurationist account of how accounting systems shape everyday accountability.
  *Self-check:* does your paper theorize the recursive loop between system and use, or just describe use?
- **Covaleski & Dirsmith (1986), "The budgetary process of power and politics," AOS 11(3).** Budgeting as
  an arena of institutional power — a template for institutional-theory fieldwork.
  *Self-check:* whose interests does the accounting serve at your site, and does the theory name the
  mechanism?
- **Dent (1991), "Accounting and organizational cultures: a field study of the emergence of a new
  organizational reality," AOS 16(8).** The classic longitudinal study of accounting remaking an
  organization's culture.
  *Self-check:* can you show change over time, or only a cross-sectional snapshot?
- **Ahrens & Chapman (2006), "Doing qualitative field research in management accounting: positioning data
  to contribute to theory," AOS 31(8).** The journal's own statement of the qualitative rigor bar.
  *Self-check:* is your theory–data movement continuous and inspectable, per this paper's standard?

## Sociology-of-calculation / critical-theoretic

- **Miller & O'Leary (1987), "Accounting and the construction of the governable person," AOS 12(3).**
  Governmentality applied to standard costing — the exemplar of Foucauldian accounting research.
  *Self-check:* do you specify both the programme (ideal) and the technology (calculative instrument)?
- **Hines (1988), "Financial accounting: in communicating reality, we construct reality," AOS 13(3).**
  The constructivist provocation on accounting's world-making.
  *Self-check:* if you claim accounting "constructs" something, do you show the construction work?
- **Robson (1992), "Accounting numbers as 'inscription': action at a distance and the development of
  accounting," AOS 17(7).** Actor-network theory's inscription concept brought to accounting numbers.
  *Self-check:* do you trace how the number travels and enrolls allies, or merely relabel it an inscription?
- **Chua (1995), "Experts, networks and inscriptions in the fabrication of accounting images," AOS
  20(2–3).** ANT fieldwork on how accounting expertise is assembled in hospitals.
  *Self-check:* is expertise in your account an achievement to explain, or an assumed input?
- **Power (2009), "The risk management of nothing," AOS 34(6–7).** Risk-management practice as a
  socially organized production of auditability.
  *Self-check:* does your paper on risk/audit practices ask what the apparatus *does*, not whether it "works"?

## Management control & strategy

- **Simons (1990), "The role of management control systems in creating competitive advantage: new
  perspectives," AOS 15(1–2).** Interactive vs diagnostic control — field-grounded conceptual innovation.
  *Self-check:* does your control-systems paper deliver a concept managers' behavior confirms, or a typology
  imposed on it?

## Experimental / behavioral

- **Libby, Bloomfield & Nelson (2002), "Experimental research in financial accounting," AOS 27(8).** The
  synthesis that defines what experiments contribute to financial-accounting knowledge — and the
  predictive-validity discipline ("Libby boxes") behavioral work is held to.
  *Self-check:* does each link of your conceptual-to-operational chain hold, and does the experiment answer
  something archival data cannot?
