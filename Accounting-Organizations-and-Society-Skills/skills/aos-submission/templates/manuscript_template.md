# AOS Manuscript Skeletons

> Structural templates for an Accounting, Organizations and Society (AOS) submission. Two skeletons are given — the qualitative field study (the modal AOS genre) and the behavioral experiment — because their architectures differ fundamentally. Verify current formatting, artwork, and declaration requirements on the official Guide for Authors; keep the manuscript file fully anonymized (identity lives on the separate title page only).

## Title Page (separate, identified file — never inside the manuscript)

> Title (names the accounting practice and the conceptual claim, not just the setting)

> 1. Affiliation (school, university, country)
> 2. Affiliation

## Skeleton A — Qualitative Field Study

### Abstract
One self-contained paragraph: the puzzle about accounting's organizational/social operation; the site and method (anonymized); the conceptual answer; what it changes in the AOS conversation. No citations.

**Keywords:** accounting practice; theoretical tradition; phenomenon; method; setting-type

### 1. Introduction
- The phenomenon, concretely, and its organizational/societal stake
- The AOS conversation and its settled view; the crack (anomaly, silence, contradiction)
- The question, the theoretical lens, and why this site can answer it
- Preview of the conceptual answer and the contribution (stated, not teased)

### 2. Theoretical framing
- The tradition and its primary sources, as appropriated in accounting research
- The specific concepts the analysis will use (each must reappear in Section 5)
- What the lens leads us to expect — and what would count as surprising

### 3. Research setting
- The organization (pseudonymous), its field, and the accounting practice at stake
- Why this case: extreme / revelatory / transparently typical — the theoretical case logic

### 4. Method
- Access and engagement period; researcher role and reflexivity
- Data: interviews (n, roles, durations), observations, documents → **Table 1: data inventory**
- Analysis: coding approach, iteration between theory and data, how anomalies were handled
- Ethics: approval, consent, anonymity commitments

### 5. Findings
- Organized by conceptual theme (not chronology): each subsection interleaves field excerpts and interpretation
- **Table 2+: evidence tables** linking themes to representative material
- Counter-evidence and how the interpretation survives it
- Optional: timeline/process figure for change studies

### 6. Discussion
- The answer restated conceptually; what each nearest prior paper must now concede
- What the imported theory gains from the case (the give-back)
- Scope conditions and transferability logic; limitations as analysis
- Implications for practice/policy, sized to the evidence

### 7. Conclusion
Brief: the claim, its consequence, the questions it opens.

### References — author–date, per the current guide
### Appendices — interview guide (as consent allows), coding structure, supplementary exhibits

## Skeleton B — Behavioral Experiment

### Abstract
Question and why the process matters for accounting; theory; design (participants, manipulation); key result including the process evidence; implication.

**Keywords:** judgment and decision making; accounting task; process; experiment

### 1. Introduction
- The accounting judgment at stake and why the field's current account is incomplete
- The psychological process theorized; preview of predictions and findings
- Contribution: what the process evidence changes for the accounting conversation

### 2. Theory and hypotheses
- The process account, built from primary psychology sources and their accounting uses
- H1 (effect), H2 (process/mediation), H3 (moderation/boundary) — each with its "because" clause
- Figure 1: predicted pattern (interaction) or process chain

### 3. Method
- Participants: pool and its justification (professional expertise vs general process); n; compensation
- Design: factors and levels; randomization; task and materials (accounting-realistic)
- Manipulations and manipulation checks; process measure; attention screens
- Pre-registration and exclusion rules; ethics approval and consent

### 4. Results
- Manipulation checks
- **Table: cell means, SDs, n per condition**; ANOVA/contrasts; effect sizes
- Process tests: mediation with bootstrap CIs; simple effects for the interaction
- Figure: the key interaction with error bars
- Supplementary analyses honestly labeled as such

### 5. Discussion
- What the process evidence establishes that archival correlations could not
- Boundary conditions; where the effect should and should not generalize
- Implications for the accounting literature first; for practice/standard-setting second

### References — author–date, per the current guide
### Appendices — experimental instrument (full), supplementary analyses

## Cross-cutting rules (both skeletons)

- One name per concept, everywhere: text, exhibits, appendices.
- Every exhibit self-contained and passing the one-sentence test.
- Nothing in the manuscript file identifies authors or site; metadata scrubbed.
- Declarations (competing interests, AI, ethics) assembled per `checklist.md` before upload.
