# Topic Selection & AOS Fit (aos-topic-selection)

## When to trigger

- You have a phenomenon, a field site, or a dataset but no question AOS would recognize as its own
- You cannot decide between AOS and a sibling outlet (JAR, JAE, TAR, CAR, MAR, AAAJ, CPA)
- A coauthor asks "isn't this really a capital-markets paper?"
- You are choosing between studying an accounting practice in the field, in the lab, or in the archive

## What AOS was built to publish

Anthony Hopwood launched AOS in 1976 precisely because the leading journals of the day treated accounting as a technical input to markets. AOS instead asks how accounting **shapes and is shaped by** human behavior, organizational processes, and social and political institutions. Its home turf: how budgets exercise power inside organizations (Covaleski & Dirsmith 1986); how costing regimes construct "governable persons" (Miller & O'Leary 1987); how accounting gains roles no textbook assigned it (Burchell et al. 1980); how preparers, auditors and investors actually judge and decide (Libby, Bloomfield & Nelson 2002); how audit and risk practices order social life (Power 2009). The core fit test: **would the paper still matter if security prices never appeared in it?** If yes, AOS is plausible. If the payoff is an asset-pricing or earnings-quality coefficient, you are writing for JAR/JAE/TAR.

## The desk-reject genre — know it before you submit

The most common AOS misfire is a competent archival-financial-economics paper — Compustat/CRSP panel, fixed effects, identification appendix — with no organizational or social theorizing. Method is not the problem; AOS publishes archival work when the *question* is institutional or organizational and the theory is real. The problem is importing JAR's contribution logic into a journal whose founding editorial rejected it. If your draft's motivation section could open a JAE paper unchanged, stop and rethink before spending months on polish.

## Pick the tradition the question demands

| If the question concerns…                                              | Lean toward…                          |
|-------------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| How accounting is practiced, resisted, or given meaning inside organizations | Qualitative field study (interviews, observation, documents) |
| How individuals judge, decide, or are influenced by accounting information   | Experiment (behavioral / JDM)        |
| Attitudes, roles, or practices across many organizations                     | Survey (validated instrument)        |
| How an accounting practice or institution came to exist and with what effects | Historical / genealogical archival work |
| An institutional or regulatory shift with organizational consequences        | Archival design **plus** social/organizational theory |

## Sharpen the question

- Write it in one sentence naming the **accounting practice**, the **actors**, and the **organizational or societal stake**.
- Prefer a genuine puzzle (accounting doing something unexpected, or failing to do what it promises) over an unstudied empirical cell.
- Test early feasibility: field access and site anonymity for qualitative work; participant pool and IRB for experiments; archive availability for historical work.
- Check that the question rewards a theoretical answer — AOS pays for insight into accounting's social operation, not for novel data alone.

## Sibling-venue triage

- **MAR** (Management Accounting Research): narrower — management accounting and control specifically.
- **AAAJ / CPA**: kindred interpretive/critical venues; AOS expects a stricter theory-evidence discipline and broader interdisciplinary reach.
- **CAR / TAR**: take AOS-style behavioral experiments too, but frame contribution to *accounting literature* rather than to social theory.
- **BRIA**: behavioral accounting with a lower theoretical-novelty bar.
If a sibling owns the audience your answer serves, reroute now — before the framing hardens.

## Checklist

- [ ] The question is about accounting's social/organizational/behavioral/institutional operation, not its pricing consequences
- [ ] The paper would survive the "no security prices" test
- [ ] A tradition is chosen because the question needs it, not because the data were convenient
- [ ] A puzzle or tension is named, not just an unstudied setting
- [ ] Access / participants / archives / ethics look feasible
- [ ] No sibling venue has a stronger claim on the intended audience

## Anti-patterns

- **JAR-in-disguise**: capital-markets question submitted to AOS because the sample is unusual.
- **Setting-as-contribution**: "no one has studied accounting in [exotic context]" with nothing at stake theoretically.
- **Method-first**: running an experiment on a question that is really about situated organizational practice (or vice versa).
- **Theory shopping after data collection** — pick the intellectual tradition before the design locks in.
