# Worked Example: A GCB-Style Abstract + Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **GCB arc** distilled from `gcb-writing-style` and `gcb-topic-selection`:
**global-change driver → biological response → mechanism**, stated up front, quantified, with
**global-change significance early**, honest scope/scale, and plain prose legible to a broad ecology /
biogeochemistry / Earth-system reader. The GCB rules applied here:

- The **abstract** states aim, approach, **key quantitative result (effect size + interval + units)**,
  and conclusion — mechanism and significance are not buried in the discussion (`gcb-writing-style`).
- The work passes the **GCB fit test**: a real global-change driver, a biological response/feedback, a
  **mechanistic** link, and **broad relevance beyond one site/species** (`gcb-topic-selection`).
- **Scale and inference are matched**, with extrapolation limits stated (`gcb-study-design`); effect
  sizes carry intervals and ecological meaning (`gcb-data-analysis`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Warming reallocates the soil-carbon response of montane grasslands
from microbial gain to root loss."* Fictional design: a five-year infrared-warming experiment (+2.0 °C)
replicated across an elevational gradient, paired with a soil-carbon model evaluated against the plots.

---

## Before (buries driver, response, and mechanism — typical first-draft abstract)

> Soil carbon is important for the global carbon cycle and has been studied extensively. Here we set up
> a warming experiment in montane grassland and measured soil respiration, root biomass, and microbial
> biomass over several years. We also ran a soil-carbon model. We used mixed-effects models to analyze
> the data. Warming had significant effects on several variables. Our results have implications for
> understanding how grasslands respond to climate change. Further work is needed.

**What's wrong (against `gcb-writing-style` / `gcb-topic-selection` / `gcb-data-analysis`):**

- **No mechanism and no global-change significance up front** — the driver→response→mechanism chain is
  missing; the reader cannot tell what was learned (`gcb-writing-style` anti-pattern: "describes methods
  but never states the quantitative result or mechanism").
- **"Significant" with no effect size, interval, or unit** — a named anti-pattern in both
  `gcb-writing-style` and `gcb-data-analysis`.
- **Throat-clearing** ("is important," "studied extensively," "further work is needed") with vague stakes.
- **Method-first, result-never**: the analysis is listed but the finding is not.
- **No scope/scale statement** — no acknowledgement of plot-to-biome extrapolation limits
  (`gcb-study-design`).

---

## After (GCB arc — driver → response → mechanism, quantified, significance early)

> **Abstract.** Whether warming makes grassland soils a carbon source or sink depends on which process
> responds faster — microbial carbon use or root carbon loss — yet the two are rarely separated in the
> same system. *(aim: the mechanistic question, framed for a broad reader.)* We exposed montane
> grassland to **+2.0 °C** infrared warming for five years, replicated across a 900 m elevational
> gradient, and partitioned the soil-carbon response into microbial and root pathways, evaluating a
> soil-carbon model against the plots. *(approach + scale, in one breath.)* Warming **reduced topsoil
> organic carbon by 7.4% (95% CI 4.1–10.6) over five years**, because a transient gain in microbial
> carbon-use efficiency was outweighed by a sustained **31% (95% CI 19–44) increase in root-derived
> carbon loss**; the model reproduced the net loss only when both pathways were represented.
> *(key quantitative result with effect sizes, intervals, and units — and the mechanism.)* Because the
> root-loss pathway strengthens with warming while the microbial gain saturates, **montane grasslands
> are likely to shift from neutral toward a net soil-carbon source as warming proceeds** — a feedback
> current single-pathway models underestimate. *(conclusion = global-change significance, stated as a
> feedback that travels beyond the site.)*

> **Introduction (first paragraph).** Grassland soils hold a large share of global soil carbon, so
> whether they gain or lose carbon under warming is a first-order control on the land-carbon feedback.
> **We ask whether +2 °C warming turns montane grassland soil from a carbon sink into a source, and
> through which process** — faster microbial carbon use, or faster root-driven loss. *(driver → response
> → mechanism, in the first breath; significance is the global feedback, not a local description.)* This
> is hard to resolve because the two pathways move soil carbon in opposite directions and are usually
> measured only in aggregate, so a net flux can hide offsetting processes that diverge as warming
> continues. *(why it is hard — the mechanistic confound.)* We separate them with a warming experiment
> replicated **across an elevational gradient**, which lets us test whether the response is consistent
> across background temperature rather than peculiar to one site, and we use a process model to ask
> whether representing both pathways is necessary to predict the net direction. *(design + scale + the
> scope of the claim.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the GCB bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Mechanism and significance up front** — the abstract and the first intro paragraph both state
  driver (+2 °C warming) → response (soil-carbon loss) → mechanism (root loss outpacing microbial gain),
  not buried in the discussion (`gcb-writing-style`: "mechanism up front," "global-change significance
  early").
- **Quantified with intervals and units** — −7.4% (95% CI 4.1–10.6) net carbon; +31% (95% CI 19–44)
  root loss — replacing "significant effects" (`gcb-data-analysis`: "effect sizes with intervals, not
  just p-values").
- **Passes the GCB fit test** — a global-change driver (warming), a biological response/feedback (soil
  carbon), a **mechanistic** explanation, and **broad relevance** (a land-carbon feedback models
  underestimate), not a local description or a conservation plan (`gcb-topic-selection`).
- **Scale matched to claim** — the elevational replication and the explicit framing keep the inference
  at the level the design supports; generality is claimed only as far as the gradient reaches
  (`gcb-study-design`: "match scale to claim").
- **Model evaluated against observations** — the model earns its conclusion only because it reproduces
  the plot-level net loss with both pathways represented (`gcb-data-analysis`: "evaluate models against
  observations").
- **One-sentence fit is fillable:** "We show that [+2 °C warming shifts montane grassland soil toward a
  carbon source] via [root loss outpacing a saturating microbial gain]" — both brackets filled, as
  `gcb-topic-selection` requires.
