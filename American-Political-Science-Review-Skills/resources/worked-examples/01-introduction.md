# Worked Example: An APSR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **APSR introduction bar** from `apsr-writing-style` and `apsr-topic-selection`:
**by the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the argument, the evidence, and why it
matters to political science *broadly*** — not just to the home subfield. The funnel here is
**question → general significance (the discipline-level stake) → why it is hard to identify → setting &
design → headline finding with the mechanism → contribution (portable argument) → brief roadmap**, with
APSR's distinctive demand that the contribution be *general* (a comparativist result a behavioralist or
theorist can use) and the design be *defended on its own terms*.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Drawn Together: How Compulsory-Voting Enforcement Reshapes the
Class Skew of the Electorate."* Fictional setting: a federation whose constituent states began enforcing
a long-dormant compulsory-voting rule in **different years**, creating staggered within-country
variation. (Subfield: comparative politics / political behavior; method: staggered difference-in-
differences. Chosen because turnout inequality is a discipline-wide stake — see the Lijphart exemplar in
[`../exemplars/library.md`](../exemplars/library.md).)

---

## Before (subfield-insider, buries the contribution — typical first draft)

> The literature on voter turnout is vast. Scholars have long debated the effects of compulsory voting,
> and many studies examine its correlates across countries. In this paper, we use a staggered
> difference-in-differences design with the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator to study the effects
> of compulsory-voting *enforcement* on turnout in a federation where enforcement varies by state. We
> assemble an individual-level panel and estimate event-study specifications. Turnout is an important
> topic in the study of elections. Section 2 reviews the literature on compulsory voting, Section 3
> describes our data, Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, Section 5 reports results, Section 6
> discusses robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `apsr-writing-style` / `apsr-topic-selection`):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It speaks only to turnout specialists; a theorist, Americanist, or IR
  reader is given no reason to care. APSR's whole bar is **general significance** — a named anti-pattern
  in `apsr-topic-selection` ("subfield-only").
- **Leads with method** ("we use a staggered DID design with the Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator") instead
  of the question and argument — the `apsr-writing-style` anti-pattern of burying the contribution.
- **No argument and no finding on page one.** The reader does not learn what was found, the mechanism, or
  the stake. "Turnout is an important topic" is throat-clearing with vague stakes.
- **No portable contribution.** Nothing a scholar in another subfield could *import* (`apsr-theory-
  building`'s portability test fails).
- **Over-signposted seven-section roadmap** doing the work the argument should do — and padding the word
  count that APSR caps (`apsr-writing-style`: footnotes/sections count toward the limit).

---

## After (APSR arc — general significance + argument + finding, contribution stated early)

> **Does forcing citizens to the polls make the electorate look more like the public — or does it simply
> add more of the same voters?** We show that when states begin *enforcing* compulsory voting, turnout
> among low-income citizens rises far more than among high-income citizens, compressing the class skew of
> the active electorate by roughly **40%** and pulling the median voter measurably closer to the median
> citizen. *(question + general significance + headline finding in the first breath — this is a claim
> about representation and political equality, not just a turnout number.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond the study of elections: a central worry across political science — from
> theories of democratic legitimacy to models of redistribution and to the study of unequal
> responsiveness — is that the *people who vote* are systematically richer than the *people who are
> governed*. If a feasible institutional rule closes that gap, it speaks directly to whether unequal
> participation is a fixed feature of mass democracy or a movable one. *(the discipline-level stake,
> framed for a theorist and a comparativist alike — `apsr-topic-selection` general-significance test.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because states do not begin enforcing the rule at random: a state may
> crack down on non-voting precisely when a mobilized lower-income electorate is already emerging, so a
> simple before-after comparison would confound the rule with the mobilization that prompted it. *(why it
> is hard — the identification problem stated plainly, per `apsr-research-design`.)*
>
> We exploit a federation in which constituent states activated a long-dormant enforcement provision in
> **different years**, for administrative reasons unrelated to local turnout trends. The staggered timing
> lets us compare each state to not-yet-enforcing states using a heterogeneity-robust difference-in-
> differences estimator, and we verify no differential pre-trends in the class composition of voters
> before enforcement begins. *(setting & design in one paragraph; the modern staggered estimator is named
> only as a tool, not as the lead.)*
>
> Enforcement raises low-income turnout by **18 percentage points** against **5 points** for high-income
> citizens, and the gap closes only after enforcement — never before. The mechanism is **not** persuasion
> or mobilization by parties: the newly compelled voters are disproportionately the previously
> disengaged poor, and their entry shifts the electorate's revealed preferences toward redistributive
> positions. *(headline finding restated with the mechanism and a ruled-out rival — the
> `apsr-research-design` adjudication move.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that the **class skew of the electorate is an institutional artifact, not a
> fixed disposition**: the same citizens who "choose" not to vote turn out when the cost of abstention
> rises, and their entry changes whom the electorate represents. This reframes turnout inequality — long
> treated as a stubborn behavioral fact — as a **design parameter** that institutions set. *(contribution
> stated early and as a *portable* argument: a comparativist studying any participation rule, an
> Americanist studying registration, and a theorist of political equality can all use it —
> `apsr-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the institutional argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 presents the design and the class-skew results; Section 4 examines the
> compositional mechanism and scope conditions. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the APSR bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **General significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names the discipline-wide stake
  (legitimacy, redistribution, unequal responsiveness) so a theorist or IR reader sees why it matters
  (`apsr-topic-selection`: "a scholar in a different subfield should see why it matters").
- **Argument-first, contribution early** — the claim ("class skew is an institutional artifact, not a
  fixed disposition") is stated plainly and early, not buried in the middle (`apsr-writing-style`:
  "front-load the contribution").
- **Portable argument, not a bare finding** — the contribution is framed so another subfield can import
  it, passing the `apsr-theory-building` portability test; the finding is attached to a mechanism with
  scope conditions, not left as "we estimate an effect."
- **Identification problem named before the design**, and the **single strongest rival is adjudicated**
  ("the mechanism is *not* persuasion or party mobilization… instead…") — the `apsr-research-design`
  adjudication sentence, with no-differential-pre-trends stated plainly.
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the staggered, heterogeneity-robust estimator appears only where the
  design is discussed, never as the lead (avoids the `apsr-writing-style` "leading with the estimator"
  anti-pattern; the estimator choice itself follows the modern-staggered-DiD guidance referenced in
  [`../code/`](../code/)).
- **Reads past the subfield and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and defined, the roadmap is three
  sections not seven, and the framing is legible to a generalist within the APSR word limit
  (`apsr-writing-style`: < 11,000 words for Articles, counts include footnotes/tables).
