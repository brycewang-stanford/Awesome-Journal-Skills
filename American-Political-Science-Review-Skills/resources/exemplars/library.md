## How to use this library

APSR is the discipline's **generalist flagship**: each paper below earns its place by making a result in
one subfield legible and useful to the *whole* discipline (see
[`../../skills/apsr-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/apsr-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/apsr-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/apsr-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **subfield × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) states general significance, and
(2) defends its design on its own methodological terms (see
[`../../skills/apsr-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/apsr-research-design/SKILL.md)). Run the
self-check before claiming your paper is "APSR-shaped."

## By method

### Formal / game-theoretic model (international relations)

- **Fearon, "Domestic Political Audiences and the Escalation of International Disputes," APSR 1994, 88(3):577–592.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v88y1994i03p577-592_09.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent war-of-attrition model yields one portable concept —
    *audience costs* — that travels far beyond the modeled crisis to comparative institutions and
    domestic politics. The model earns its keep by delivering a non-obvious, testable comparative static
    (see `apsr-theory-building`: "say what the model buys").
  - **Self-check:** does your model produce a *named, portable mechanism* another subfield can import,
    not just an equilibrium specific to your setup?

### Survey / framing experiment (American politics, public opinion)

- **Chong & Druckman, "Framing Public Opinion in Competitive Democracies," APSR 2007, 101(4):637–655.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v101y2007i04p637-655_07.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** moves framing research from one-sided lab demonstrations to *competitive*
    framing that mirrors real democratic discourse — a measurement-and-design advance with stakes for
    the whole discipline's account of how elites move opinion. Pairs with the experimental-design
    guidance in [`../../skills/apsr-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/apsr-research-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** does your experiment recreate the *contested* environment the theory is about, rather
    than a clean one-sided treatment that over-generalizes?

### Survey experiment for a macro-level claim (international relations)

- **Tomz & Weeks, "Public Opinion and the Democratic Peace," APSR 2013, 107(4):849–865.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v107y2013i04p849-865_00.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** tests a *macro* IR regularity (the democratic peace) at the *micro*
    individual level with parallel experiments in two countries — micro-foundations done so cleanly that
    a comparativist or Americanist immediately sees the mechanism. A model of linking individual evidence
    to a system-level claim.
  - **Self-check:** if your finding is about states or systems, can you show the individual-level
    mechanism that must hold for the macro claim to be true?

### Natural experiment / paired cross-border comparison (comparative politics)

- **Posner, "The Political Salience of Cultural Difference: Why Chewas and Tumbukas Are Allies in Zambia and Adversaries in Malawi," APSR 2004, 98(4):529–545.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v98y2004i04p529-545_04.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the same two ethnic groups split by a colonial border give a natural
    experiment that holds *cultural* difference fixed while varying *relative group size* — the design
    *is* the identification of why cleavages become politically salient (see `apsr-research-design`:
    "say what the case is a case *of*").
  - **Self-check:** does your comparison hold the would-be confound fixed by design, so the variation
    that remains is the one your argument is about?

### Observational + institutional argument, presidential address (comparative / political behavior)

- **Lijphart, "Unequal Participation: Democracy's Unresolved Dilemma" (APSA Presidential Address, 1996), APSR 1997, 91(1):1–14.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v91y1997i01p1-14_23.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** marshals cross-national turnout evidence into a *normative-institutional*
    argument that low, class-skewed turnout is a first-order democratic problem — the prototype of a
    discipline-wide synthesis that moves between empirical regularity and institutional prescription.
  - **Self-check:** does your empirical regularity connect to a stake (representation, power, justice)
    that a scholar in any subfield recognizes as fundamental?

### Interpretive / conceptual reconstruction (political theory / American political development)

- **Smith, "Beyond Tocqueville, Myrdal, and Hartz: The Multiple Traditions in America," APSR 1993, 87(3):549–566.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + `ideas.repec.org/a/cup/apsrev/v87y1993i03p549-566_10.html`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reconstructs a foundational concept (the "liberal" character of American
    political culture) using historical-interpretive evidence, replacing a hegemonic story with a
    "multiple traditions" account — an APSR theory contribution that reorders how empirical Americanists
    read their own field (see `apsr-theory-building`: interpretive/normative mode).
  - **Self-check:** does your conceptual move change what *empirical* scholars in the field can see or
    must now account for, not just rename an existing debate?

## Omitted for lack of APSR verification (do NOT attribute to APSR without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not APSR. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Acemoglu & Robinson, "A Theory of Political Transitions" (2001)** — verified to be in the *American
  Economic Review* 91(4):938–963, **not APSR**. A common cross-discipline misattribution.
- **Fearon, "Rationalist Explanations for War" (1995)** — verified to be in *International Organization*
  49(3):379–414, **not APSR**. Note: a *different* Fearon paper (audience costs, 1994) *is* APSR and is
  listed above — do not conflate the two.
- **Mansfield & Snyder, "Democratization and the Danger of War" (1995)** — verified to be in
  *International Security* 20(1):5–38, **not APSR**.
- **Gerring, Thacker & Alfaro, "Democracy and Human Development" (2012)** — verified to be in the
  *Journal of Politics* 74(1):1–17, **not APSR**.
- **Ross, "Is Democracy Good for the Poor?" (2006)** — verified to be in the *American Journal of
  Political Science* 50(4):860–874, **not APSR (AJPS)**.
- **Pateman, *Participation and Democratic Theory* (1970)** — a Cambridge University Press *book*, not an
  APSR article. Listed because it surfaces in "political theory + APSR" searches.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on Cambridge Core
(`cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review`) with volume/issue/pages, cross-check
the RePEc `cup:apsrev` record, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
