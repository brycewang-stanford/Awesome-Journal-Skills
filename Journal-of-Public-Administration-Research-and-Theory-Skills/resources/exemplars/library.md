## How to use this library

JPART is the **theory-and-research flagship of public administration**: each paper below earns its place by
making a contribution to a **public-management theory** (PSM, red tape, representative bureaucracy,
performance, behavioral PA), not by reporting a stand-alone finding (see
[`../../skills/jpart-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jpart-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/jpart-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jpart-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **theory × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) states a theory contribution, and (2)
defends its design — increasingly experimental and causal — on its own terms (see
[`../../skills/jpart-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jpart-research-design/SKILL.md)).

## By PA theory × method

### Public service motivation — measurement / construct development

- **Perry, "Measuring Public Service Motivation: An Assessment of Construct Reliability and Validity," JPART 1996, 6(1):5–22.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/6/1/5/923013`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** builds and validates the PSM construct that anchors a whole research program —
    a measurement contribution whose payoff is *theoretical* (it makes PSM testable), exactly the "construct
    defined and validated" demand in `jpart-theory-building`.
  - **Self-check:** does your measure follow from a clearly defined construct, distinguished from neighbors,
    with reported reliability/validity?

### Public service motivation — theory construction

- **Perry, "Bringing Society In: Toward a Theory of Public-Service Motivation," JPART 2000, 10(2):471–488.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article/10/2/471/987765`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a theory paper that incorporates social and institutional variables to fix
    "anomalies in dominant theories of motivation" — a pure public-management theory move (`jpart-theory-building`).
  - **Self-check:** does your paper *extend or repair* a theory, or just report that motivated employees do better?

### Red tape — foundational theory

- **Bozeman, "A Theory of Government 'Red Tape'," JPART 1993, 3(3):273–304.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/3/3/273/949923`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** defines red tape as a distinct construct and theorizes its origins — the model
    of a JPART theory contribution that later empirical work tests (`jpart-theory-building`: define and
    distinguish the construct).
  - **Self-check:** is your key construct distinguished from its neighbors (red tape vs. administrative burden),
    not used loosely?

### Representative bureaucracy — survey experiment

- **Riccucci, Van Ryzin & Lavena, "Representative Bureaucracy in Policing: Does It Increase Perceived Legitimacy?" JPART 2014, 24(3):537–551.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/24/3/537/900329`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** brings an **experiment** to a representative-bureaucracy claim — moving the
    theory from observational correlation to a designed test of symbolic representation, the experimental turn
    JPART rewards (`jpart-research-design`: experiments on citizens/employees).
  - **Self-check:** does your experiment *test the mechanism* the theory names, on the right population, rather
    than demonstrate an effect in the abstract?

### Behavioral PA — large-scale survey experiment (public vs. private)

- **van den Bekerom, van der Voet & Christensen, "Are Citizens More Negative About Failing Service Delivery by Public Than Private Organizations? Evidence From a Large-Scale Survey Experiment," JPART 2021, 31(1):128–149.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article/31/1/128/5871646`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a behavioral-PA survey experiment that tests an "anti-public-sector bias"
    mechanism cleanly — micro-foundations for citizen attitudes toward government (`jpart-data-analysis`:
    pre-registered, designed inference).
  - **Self-check:** does your experiment recreate the contrast the theory is about, with an outcome that
    isolates the mechanism rather than self-reported attitude alone?

### Performance information — discrete-choice experiment

- **van der Voet & Lerusse, "Performance Information and Issue Prioritization by Political and Managerial Decision-Makers: A Discrete Choice Experiment," JPART 2024, 34(4):582–597.**
  Verified: Oxford Academic article page (`academic.oup.com/jpart/article/34/4/582/7659633`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a modern conjoint/discrete-choice design that tests how decision-makers weigh
    performance information — performance-management theory tested with a trade-off-revealing experiment
    (`jpart-research-design`: conjoint/factorial designs; `jpart-tables-figures`: AMCE/marginal-means plots).
  - **Self-check:** if your theory is about trade-offs, does your design *reveal* them (conjoint/DCE) rather
    than ask about one factor at a time?

## Omitted for lack of JPART verification (do NOT attribute to JPART without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** because web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not JPART. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Grimmelikhuijsen, Jilke, Olsen & Tummers, "Behavioral Public Administration" (2017)** — verified to be in
  *Public Administration Review* 77(1):45–56, **not JPART**. The field-defining behavioral-PA agenda piece is
  a PAR article.
- **Meier & Nicholson-Crotty, "Gender, Representative Bureaucracy, and Law Enforcement" (2006)** — verified to
  be in *Public Administration Review* 66(6):850–860, **not JPART**.
- **Bradbury & Kellough, "Representative Bureaucracy: Assessing the Evidence on Active Representation" (2011)**
  — verified to be in the *American Review of Public Administration* 41(2):157–167, **not JPART**.
- **Tummers, Bekkers, Vink & Musheno, "Coping During Public Service Delivery" (2015)** — frequently cited in
  street-level-bureaucracy searches; confirm the venue (it appeared in *JPART* per some indexes, but verify
  the volume/issue/pages on Oxford Academic before citing). 待核实 — omitted pending exact-page verification.
- **Bozeman & Feeney, *Rules and Red Tape* (2011)** — a Routledge *book*, not a JPART article. Listed because
  it surfaces in "red tape + JPART" searches.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on Oxford Academic
(`academic.oup.com/jpart`) with volume/issue/pages and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
