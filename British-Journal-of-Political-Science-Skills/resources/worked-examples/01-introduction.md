# Worked Example: A BJPS-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **BJPS introduction bar** from `bjps-writing-style` and `bjps-topic-selection`:
**by the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the argument, the evidence, and why it
matters to political science *broadly* and *beyond the single case*** — not just to the home subfield or
the studied country. The funnel here is **question → wide/international significance → why it is hard to
identify → setting & design → headline finding with the mechanism → contribution (portable argument) →
brief roadmap**, with BJPS's distinctive demand that a single-country result be framed as a test of a
*general* mechanism a comparativist or behaviouralist elsewhere can use, and that the design be
*defended on its own terms*.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Compelled to Count: How Local Audit Mandates Reshape Citizens'
Trust in Government."* Fictional setting: a federation whose constituent regions adopted a mandatory
independent-audit requirement for municipal budgets in **different years**, creating staggered
within-country variation. (Subfield: comparative politics / political behaviour; method: staggered
difference-in-differences. Chosen because political trust is a discipline-wide, international stake —
see the Scruggs and Li & Reuveny exemplars in [`../exemplars/library.md`](../exemplars/library.md).)

---

## Before (subfield-insider, single-country framing, buries the contribution — typical first draft)

> The literature on political trust is vast. Scholars have long debated the determinants of trust in
> government, and many studies examine its correlates within particular countries. In this paper, we use
> a staggered difference-in-differences design with the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator to study
> the effect of municipal audit mandates on political trust in [Country], where the mandate was adopted
> by regions in different years. We assemble an individual-level panel and estimate event-study
> specifications. Trust is an important topic in the study of [Country]'s politics. Section 2 reviews the
> literature, Section 3 describes our data, Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, Section 5 reports
> results, Section 6 discusses robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `bjps-writing-style` / `bjps-topic-selection`):**

- **Single-country, subfield-insider framing.** It speaks only to specialists on one country's trust;
  a comparativist, an IR scholar, or a reader of another democracy is given no reason to care. BJPS's
  bar is **wide, international interest** — a named anti-pattern ("single-country," "subfield-only").
- **Leads with method** ("we use a staggered DID design with the Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator") instead
  of the question and argument — the `bjps-writing-style` anti-pattern of burying the contribution.
- **No argument and no finding on page one.** The reader does not learn what was found, the mechanism, or
  the stake. "Trust is an important topic" is throat-clearing with vague, parochial stakes.
- **No portable contribution.** Nothing a scholar studying another country or institution could
  *import* (`bjps-theory-building`'s portability test fails).
- **Over-signposted seven-section roadmap** doing the work the argument should do — and padding the word
  count BJPS caps.

---

## After (BJPS arc — wide significance + argument + finding, contribution stated early)

> **Does making government accountable to citizens actually make citizens trust it — or do audits simply
> publicize failures and erode trust further?** We show that when regions begin requiring independent
> audits of municipal budgets, political trust among ordinary citizens rises by roughly **8 percentage
> points**, and the gain is concentrated among the previously most distrustful — precisely the citizens
> reform advocates most hope to reach. *(question + wide significance + headline finding in the first
> breath — this is a claim about the sources of legitimacy, not just one country's trust scores.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond a single country: a central question across political science — from theories
> of democratic legitimacy to the comparative study of state capacity and to work on accountability
> institutions — is whether *transparency* mechanisms build diffuse support for government or merely
> expose its flaws. If a concrete, transferable institutional rule moves trust, it speaks to whether
> declining confidence in government is a fixed cultural fact or a movable, institutionally-shaped one —
> a debate comparativists, behaviouralists, and political economists all recognize. *(the international,
> cross-subfield stake — `bjps-topic-selection` wide-interest test.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because regions do not adopt audit mandates at random: a region may
> legislate transparency precisely when a reform-minded, already-trusting public is ascendant, so a
> simple before-after comparison would confound the mandate with the political mood that produced it.
> *(why it is hard — the identification problem stated plainly, per `bjps-research-design`.)*
>
> We exploit a federation in which regions activated the audit requirement in **different years**, for
> administrative and fiscal-calendar reasons unrelated to local trust trends. The staggered timing lets
> us compare each region to not-yet-adopting regions using a heterogeneity-robust difference-in-
> differences estimator, and we verify no differential pre-trends in trust before adoption. *(setting &
> design in one paragraph; the modern staggered estimator is named only as a tool, not as the lead.)*
>
> Trust rises by **8 points** on average and by **14 points** among the initially least trusting, and the
> gain appears only after adoption — never before. The mechanism is **not** a general improvement in
> services: spending and service quality do not move; what changes is citizens' *perceived* probability
> that misconduct would be caught. *(headline finding restated with the mechanism and a ruled-out rival —
> the `bjps-research-design` adjudication move.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that **diffuse political trust responds to credible monitoring, not only to
> performance**: citizens update their confidence when the *expected cost of malfeasance* rises, even
> absent any change in what government delivers. This reframes trust — often treated as a slow-moving
> cultural disposition — as partly a **design parameter** that accountability institutions set, a logic a
> scholar studying any transparency reform in any democracy can carry to their own case. *(contribution
> stated early and as a *portable, international* argument — `bjps-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the monitoring argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 presents the design and the trust results; Section 4 examines the
> perceived-accountability mechanism and scope conditions. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the BJPS bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Wide, international significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names the
  cross-national, cross-subfield stake (legitimacy, state capacity, accountability) so a comparativist or
  behaviouralist elsewhere sees why it matters (`bjps-topic-selection`: "a scholar studying a different
  country or subfield should see why it matters").
- **Argument-first, contribution early** — the claim ("trust responds to credible monitoring, not only
  performance") is stated plainly and early, not buried (`bjps-writing-style`: "front-load the
  contribution").
- **Portable argument, not a bare finding** — the contribution is framed so a scholar of another country
  or reform can import it, passing the `bjps-theory-building` portability test; the finding is attached to
  a mechanism with scope conditions.
- **Identification problem named before the design**, and the **single strongest rival is adjudicated**
  ("the mechanism is *not* a general improvement in services… instead…") — the `bjps-research-design`
  adjudication sentence, with no-differential-pre-trends stated plainly, plus the generalization sentence.
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the staggered, heterogeneity-robust estimator appears only where the
  design is discussed, never as the lead (the estimator choice follows the modern-staggered-DiD guidance
  referenced in [`../code/`](../code/)).
- **Reads past the subfield and the country, and respects the cap** — country-specific institutions are
  glossed, the roadmap is three sections not seven, and the framing is legible to a generalist within the
  BJPS word limit (`bjps-writing-style`: Research Articles ~10,000 words, Letters ~4,000).
