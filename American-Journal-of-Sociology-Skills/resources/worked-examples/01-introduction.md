# Worked Example: An *AJS*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **AJS introduction arc** the pack's own skills require. AJS is sociology's
**oldest and most theory-forward** generalist flagship — founded (1895) to "build up a fund of social
theory" — so the introduction must, by the time it ends, lead with a **theoretical puzzle**, name a
**portable mechanism**, ground it in a **data/design**, deliver the **headline finding**, and state a
**contribution to sociological theory** that another subfield can carry (`ajs-writing-style`:
"front-load the contribution"; `ajs-topic-selection`: dialogue with current sociology + portable
theoretical payoff; `ajs-theory-building`: the portability test).

AJS judges quantitative, comparative-historical, ethnographic, network, and formal work each on its own
terms, but it *prejects* papers that are not original sociological research in dialogue with the field.
This example takes a **comparative-historical** angle — the tradition most associated with AJS — with an
explicit portable mechanism, because that is the combination the skills treat as the central AJS target.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Brokered Closure: How Guild Re-Licensing Reorganized Access to
Skilled Trades in Two Industrializing Cities, 1860–1910."* Fictional setting: a paired comparison of two
cities whose craft guilds adopted formal licensing boards at different moments during industrialization.

---

## Before (subfield-insider, buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> The history of occupational guilds has long interested historical sociologists. A number of studies
> have examined how guilds responded to industrialization, and debates continue about whether guilds
> declined or adapted. In this paper, we use archival data from two cities to examine the relationship
> between guild licensing reforms and access to skilled trades. We conduct a comparative-historical
> analysis and find that licensing was associated with changes in who entered the trades. This case has
> not been studied before. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature on
> guilds, Section 3 describes the archival sources, Section 4 presents the comparison, Section 5 reports
> findings, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It addresses only people who already study guilds; a sociologist of
  organizations, professions, or inequality sees no reason to read on (`ajs-topic-selection`: general
  significance; `ajs-writing-style`: "reads past the subfield").
- **No theoretical puzzle, no mechanism.** "Was associated with changes" is the named anti-pattern in
  `ajs-theory-building` ("mechanisms named but never made observable"; "a finding with theory left
  implicit"). No **concept** is offered for others to carry → fails the **portability test**.
- **"Has not been studied before" as the contribution** — exactly the `ajs-topic-selection` anti-pattern
  ("'never studied in setting X' as the whole contribution"), the kind of paper AJS *prejects*.
- **No adjudication.** The method is named but we are never told what rival historical account the paired
  comparison rules out (`ajs-research-design`).
- **Contribution buried** under an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's job
  (`ajs-writing-style`).

---

## After (*AJS* arc — puzzle + mechanism + design + finding + theoretical contribution, front-loaded)

> **How do the boundaries that ration access to good work get redrawn — and why do they harden in some
> moments of economic upheaval but dissolve in others?** We argue that one engine is *brokered closure*:
> when an occupation converts an informal guild into a **licensing board**, a small set of incumbents
> becomes the broker between aspirants and the trade, and it is that brokerage position — not the skill
> the work requires — that lets closure be tightened or relaxed at will. *(theoretical puzzle +
> mechanism, in the first breath; the concept, "brokered closure," is named immediately and glossed for
> a reader outside historical sociology.)*
>
> The claim matters beyond guilds. Sociologists of professions, organizations, and inequality have long
> asked how durable boundaries between insiders and outsiders are built and sustained; here we watch a
> boundary being **engineered in real time**, through an organizational act (chartering a licensing
> board) that installs brokers, rather than through any change in human capital. If the mechanism holds,
> it reframes professionalization, credentialing, and the gatekeeping of skilled work as instances of the
> *same* brokerage process — legible to scholars of work, organizations, and stratification alike.
> *(why it matters to the discipline broadly — the contribution is portable, stated against a specific
> prior view: closure as brokered, not skill-driven.)*
>
> Identifying this is hard because cities did not charter boards at random: a guild may have licensed
> precisely as its trade grew more technically demanding, so a naive comparison conflates **closure** with
> **upskilling** — the leading rival account. We exploit the **staggered** chartering of licensing boards
> across matched trades in two industrializing cities and read serial apprenticeship rolls, board minutes,
> and entry petitions across each threshold, comparing aspirants seeking the *same* trade just before and
> just after it licensed, with not-yet-licensing trades as the comparison. The adjudication is sharp: if
> upskilling drove our results, the measured task content and training length of entrants would rise at
> chartering; instead, **task content is flat across the threshold while who is admitted changes** —
> admission tilts toward incumbents' kin and clients. *(data and design in one paragraph, with the
> `ajs-research-design` adjudication sentence: what we'd see if the rival were true vs. what we see.)*
>
> Across both cities, **chartering a board reorganized entry within a single apprenticeship cohort**,
> concentrating admission among those already tied to the brokers, and the effect appears only where the
> board controlled entry rather than merely certifying incumbents — its **scope condition**. *(headline
> finding restated with the cohort signature and the boundary of the claim — the observable implications
> from `ajs-theory-building`.)*
>
> Our contribution is conceptual as well as empirical: we specify **closure as a brokerage position with
> a charter date**, which lets the mechanism be observed rather than inferred, and we state where it
> operates — wherever a gate to a valued good is administered by incumbents who broker access, and where
> chartering is not itself a response to rising task demands. *(contribution + scope conditions —
> `ajs-theory-building`.)* Beyond guilds, the lesson is that **inequality between positions can be
> manufactured by installing brokers at a boundary, leaving the work and the workers' skills untouched** —
> a mechanism a sociologist studying any gated good, from medical licensure to platform access, can
> import. *(portable theoretical lesson that travels — the AJS payoff.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. We develop the brokered-closure argument and its observable implications,
> then present the paired-city design and the task-content test that separates closure from upskilling,
> and close with scope conditions and what brokered closure implies for theories of durable inequality.
> *(brief roadmap — carries the argument, not a table of contents.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *AJS* bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded** — by the end of the intro the reader has the **puzzle, the mechanism, the
  design, the finding, and the theoretical stakes** (`ajs-writing-style`: front-load the contribution so
  a sociologist outside the subfield knows the argument and why it matters).
- **Reads past the subfield and is in dialogue with current sociology** — framed for sociologists of
  professions, organizations, *and* inequality; jargon ("brokered closure") is named and immediately
  glossed (`ajs-topic-selection`: general significance; the AJS "preject" bar for dialogue with the
  field).
- **A mechanism and concept that travel** — "brokered closure" is offered for others to import, with
  explicit **scope conditions**; it passes the `ajs-theory-building` **portability test** ("could a
  sociologist in another area import this?").
- **Design adjudicates the leading rival** — the closure-vs-upskilling **adjudication sentence** is
  written out and matched to the staggered, paired-city comparison (`ajs-research-design`,
  comparative-historical track: specify the causal form and the counterfactual).
- **Theory stated before the tests** — avoids the `ajs-theory-building` "hypothesizing after results are
  known" anti-pattern; the observable implications (flat task content, kin-tilted entry) are derived from
  the mechanism.
- **No "never-studied-X" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named `ajs-topic-selection`
  / `ajs-writing-style` anti-patterns are avoided, and the method is demoted to a tool rather than led
  with.

> One-sentence hook (fillable, as the skills imply): "We show that **chartering a licensing board
> reorganizes who gets into a trade** by **installing incumbent brokers at the gate**, using **the
> staggered chartering of boards across two cities** to separate closure from upskilling."
