# Worked Example: A *Critical Inquiry*-Style Opening (before → after)

This demonstrates the **CI arc** from `ci-structure-and-exposition`:
**opening that earns attention (scene/object/problem) → stakes and intervention up front → the
conversation entered → the build → the turn → an opening (not summarizing) close** — long-form
humanistic prose, **not IMRaD**. It is paired with the intervention test from
`ci-argument-and-intervention` (reconceive / overturn / cross / name / historicize), the
conversation-not-literature rule from `ci-scholarly-positioning`, and the clarity-and-word-cap
discipline of `ci-writing-style`.

A CI essay is **not** an empirical report: the "evidence" is **objects read closely through theory**,
never data, statistics, or code (see `ci-evidence-and-objects`). So the opening's job is rhetorical —
make a cross-field reader feel the problem, then stake an ambitious conceptual claim quickly.

**Illustrative essay (fictional):** *"The Unfinished Frame: On the Aesthetics of the Provisional."*
Fictional objects: a single unfinished Cézanne-style canvas, a passage from a fictional modernist
novel left mid-sentence, and a building permanently "under renovation." (All invented for teaching.)

---

## Before (buries the claim — a competent but inert first draft)

> This essay examines the concept of the unfinished in modern art and literature. The unfinished has
> been discussed by many critics and theorists across a range of disciplines, and it remains an
> important topic in aesthetics. In what follows I will consider several examples — a painting, a
> novel, and a work of architecture — and explore how each can be understood as unfinished. I draw on
> theories of form, temporality, and the fragment. The first section reviews the existing literature
> on the fragment; the second section analyzes my examples; the third section offers some concluding
> reflections on what the unfinished might mean for criticism today.

**What's wrong (against the pack's skills):**

- **"This essay examines / will consider / explore"** — the named anti-pattern in
  `ci-argument-and-intervention`: it promises a *survey*, not a *claim*. There is no thesis in a
  sentence, and no intervention type declared.
- **No stakes up front, and no cross-field "so what."** A philosopher or art historian cannot tell why
  the field needs this essay now (`ci-structure-and-exposition`, `ci-scholarly-positioning`).
- **"Literature review → analysis → conclusion" is IMRaD in disguise** — exactly the template
  `ci-structure-and-exposition` says not to force onto an essay.
- **A pile of three thin examples** announced as a catalog, violating
  `ci-evidence-and-objects` ("few and deep over many and thin"); none is shown to *test* the claim.
- **Bureaucratic signposting** ("the first section… the second section…") doing the work the argument
  should do.
- **Throat-clearing** ("an important topic," "remains important") and clichés `ci-writing-style` says
  to cut.

---

## After (CI arc — object → stakes → intervention, conversation, then the turn)

> A canvas in the Courtauld stops mid-gesture: one corner is dense oil, the next bare primed linen, and
> the seam between them is not a flaw the painter ran out of time to repair but the part of the picture
> that holds your eye. *(opening on a single object a non-specialist can see — the scene earns
> attention before any theory.)*
>
> Criticism has a habit of treating such works as **deficient** — as finished works minus something,
> to be completed in the mind, redeemed by the fragment's promise of a whole. I want to argue the
> opposite: that the **provisional is a positive aesthetic form**, not a lack but a way of making time
> visible inside an object, and that once we see it as such, a set of works we have filed under
> "incomplete" turn out to be doing their most precise work exactly where they stop. *(thesis in two
> sentences; the **intervention is to reconceive** a category — `ci-argument-and-intervention` type
> "reconceive" + "overturn a critical commonplace.")*
>
> The stakes cross fields. If the provisional is a form, then the criteria by which art history dates
> and "completes" a work, the way literary criticism reads a broken-off sentence as failure or
> intention, and the philosophical opposition between the made and the merely begun all need
> rethinking together. *(stakes up front, and they genuinely cross art history, literary criticism,
> and aesthetics — `ci-structure-and-exposition`, `ci-scholarly-positioning`.)*
>
> The conversation I enter runs from the Romantic cult of the fragment to recent theories of form that
> treat closure as the mark of the aesthetic. The standing view — shared across these otherwise
> opposed camps — is that finish is the default and the unfinished the exception that must be
> explained. I press on that shared assumption. *(the **conversation, not a literature review**: a
> debate is named and a single pressure point identified — `ci-scholarly-positioning`. Real principals
> would be named and cited in Chicago footnotes; omitted here because this is fictional.)*
>
> To earn the claim I read **one** object to the hilt — the stalled canvas — and let it resist me: its
> bare linen is not neutral ground but a decision, and I show where that decision refuses my own
> argument before it confirms it. A mid-sentence passage and a building held in permanent renovation
> then test whether the form travels across media. *(few-and-deep objects that *test*, not illustrate,
> the claim, including counter-evidence — `ci-evidence-and-objects`.)*
>
> The turn comes when the provisional stops being a property of objects and becomes a **demand on the
> viewer's time**: to stand before the unfinished is to be made to hold a work open, to refuse the
> closure that completion would grant. *(the unmistakable pivot from setup to claim —
> `ci-structure-and-exposition` "the turn.")*
>
> If that is right, then "finish" is not the natural endpoint of art but one historically specific
> resolution among others — and the unfinished is less a wound in the work than a different,
> answerable form of attention. What else have we mistaken for incompleteness because we could only
> imagine value as closure? *(close that **opens** rather than summarizes — names what is now
> thinkable and a new question — `ci-structure-and-exposition` "the close.")*

## Why the "after" meets the CI bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Opens on an object a cross-field reader can feel**, then surfaces the question and stakes quickly —
  the CI arc, not an IMRaD skeleton (`ci-structure-and-exposition`).
- **Thesis in a sentence, with a declared intervention type** ("reconceive" / "overturn a
  commonplace"): the provisional *is a form*, not a lack — `ci-argument-and-intervention`'s core test,
  and it says what is no longer tenable once the essay lands.
- **Stakes are interdisciplinary and stated early** — art history, literary criticism, and aesthetics
  all have to move — meeting `ci-scholarly-positioning`'s "conversation, not literature" and "name the
  pressure point."
- **Evidence is objects read closely, never data** — one object read deep, made to *resist* the claim,
  then two more to test travel across media (`ci-evidence-and-objects`: "few and deep," "let the object
  talk back").
- **Prose is clear and ambitious, signposting by argument not bureaucracy** — no "Section 2 will…,"
  difficulty in the idea not the sentence, clichés cut (`ci-writing-style`).
- **One-sentence intervention is fillable:** "I argue that [the provisional is a positive aesthetic
  form] by reading [a stalled canvas, a broken sentence, a building in permanent renovation], which
  changes how [art history, literary criticism, and aesthetics] treat finish" — the
  `ci-argument-and-intervention` self-test passes.
