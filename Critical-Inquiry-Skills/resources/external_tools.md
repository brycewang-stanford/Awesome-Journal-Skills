# External Tools & Resources for Critical Inquiry-Style Criticism and Theory

Sources, software, and references useful when preparing a *Critical Inquiry* (CI) essay. CI is an
**interdisciplinary** journal of **criticism and theory** in the **arts and humanities** — a
submission may read literary texts, artworks, films, photographs, buildings, performances,
philosophical arguments, political concepts, or historical events **through theory**. This is **not**
a data-and-statistics venue: there are no datasets to clean, no models to fit, no replication
packages. The "tools" below are the apparatus of humanistic scholarship — archives, image and text
sources, theory libraries, and the craft software for footnotes and figures. Verify any CI-specific
policy in [`official-source-map.md`](official-source-map.md).

## 1. Primary objects & where to find them

### Texts (literary, philosophical, political, historical)
| Source | Provider | Typical use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Archive holdings / special collections | University & national libraries | Manuscripts, letters, marginalia, primary texts |
| HathiTrust / Internet Archive | Consortium / nonprofit | Out-of-copyright editions, historical print |
| Project Gutenberg / Perseus | Volunteer / Tufts | Public-domain literary and classical texts |
| EEBO / ECCO / Gallica | ProQuest / BnF | Early modern and 18th-c. print; French holdings |
| Online editions & scholarly databases (JSTOR, Project MUSE) | Various | Located, citable primary and critical sources |

### Images, art & visual culture
| Source | Provider | Typical use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Museum open-access collections (Met, Rijksmuseum, NGA, Getty) | Museums | High-resolution artwork images, provenance |
| Artstor / JSTOR images | ITHAKA | Licensed art and architecture images for scholarship |
| Wikimedia Commons / Europeana | Nonprofit / EU | Public-domain or freely licensed images |
| Rights holders / artists' estates / agencies (ARS, DACS) | Various | **Permissions** for in-copyright images |

### Film, media & performance
| Source | Provider | Typical use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Film archives & restorations (Criterion, national archives) | Various | Stills, frame enlargements, version history |
| Media repositories / streaming with citation metadata | Various | Locating and citing time-coded sequences |

> CI requires **author-secured permissions** for published images and images supplied as **separate
> 300 ppi JPEG/TIFF** files (see `ci-evidence-and-objects` and `ci-submission`).

## 2. Theory & criticism reference shelf

- **Movements / lenses** to know and cite precisely: hermeneutics and phenomenology; structuralism and
  poststructuralism (Saussure, Barthes, Derrida, Foucault); psychoanalysis (Freud, Lacan, Klein);
  Marxism and critical theory (Frankfurt School, Jameson); deconstruction; new historicism;
  postcolonial and decolonial thought (Said, Spivak, Fanon); critical race theory and Black study;
  feminist and queer theory (Butler, Sedgwick); affect theory; media and visual-culture theory;
  ecocriticism and the environmental humanities; science and technology studies; thing theory and
  new materialisms; aesthetics and philosophy of art.
- **Use theory as an instrument, not décor.** Cite the primary theoretical text, render the concept
  in your own words, and show what work it does on your object (see `ci-theory-and-method`).

## 3. Writing, footnotes & references
- **Footnotes follow The Chicago Manual of Style, 17th ed.** (as stated by CI), numbered consecutively;
  **no separate works-cited list** — full bibliographical information lives in the notes.
- Reference managers: **Zotero**, **EndNote**, or BibTeX/BibLaTeX with a **Chicago notes** style;
  keep one consistent style and let notes carry complete citations.
- Typesetting: most CI essays are prepared in **Microsoft Word** (the required submission format);
  LaTeX users should export clean Word or follow the journal's file requirements.
- The word limit (**≤ 9,500** for articles) **counts discursive notes and all bibliographical
  information** — discipline the notes, not just the body (see `ci-writing-style`).

## 4. Figures & image preparation
- Supply images as **separate files**, **300 ppi**, **JPEG or TIFF** — do not embed in the Word file.
- Provide a numbered **figure list with captions and full credit lines**; keep a permissions log.
- Prefer images that the argument actually reads; a figure in CI is an object of analysis, not
  decoration (see `ci-evidence-and-objects`).

## 6. Cautions
1. **Verify volatile specifics** (editors and term, caps, OA terms, image-permission wording,
   accepted formats) on the official CI / University of Chicago Press pages immediately before upload.
2. **This is theory, not data.** Do not import a social-science apparatus (hypotheses, datasets,
   models, replication) into a CI essay; the "evidence" is texts, images, artworks, and cases read
   closely (see `ci-evidence-and-objects`).
3. **Make the intervention, not a survey.** CI rewards an argument that reorients a conversation, not
   a literature review or a competent reading with no stakes (see `ci-argument-and-intervention`).
4. **Interdisciplinary by design.** A CI essay should speak past one department; theory and objects
   are put in dialogue across fields (see `ci-scholarly-positioning`).
5. **Clear permissions early.** Image rights take time and money; budget for them before acceptance.
