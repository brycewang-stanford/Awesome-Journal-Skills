## How to use this library

CPS is the **comparative-politics specialist**: each paper below earns its place by making a claim with
**comparative leverage** (across countries, subnational units, or time) and a mechanism that **travels**
(see [`../../skills/cps-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/cps-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/cps-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/cps-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **subfield × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) sets up the comparison, and
(2) defends its design on its own terms (see
[`../../skills/cps-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/cps-research-design/SKILL.md)). Run the
self-check before claiming your paper is "CPS-shaped."

## By method

### Typological / comparative theory (parties, behavior, political economy)

- **Kitschelt, "Linkages between Citizens and Politicians in Democratic Polities," CPS 2000, 33(6–7):845–879.**
  Verified: SAGE article page, DOI 10.1177/001041400003300607.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** builds a portable typology of citizen–politician linkages (programmatic,
    charismatic, clientelistic) that travels across regions and party systems — a framework other
    comparativists import rather than a one-country description (see `cps-theory-building`: portable
    mechanism with scope conditions).
  - **Self-check:** does your argument yield a *named, portable* distinction another comparativist can
    carry to a new party system, not just a label for your cases?

### Cross-national pooled time-series (ethnic politics & conflict)

- **Saideman, Lanoue, Campenni & Stanton, "Democratization, Political Institutions, and Ethnic Conflict: A Pooled Time-Series Analysis, 1985–1998," CPS 2002, 35(1):103–129.**
  Verified: SAGE article page, DOI 10.1177/001041400203500108.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** tests institutional theories of the ethnic security dilemma across all
    Minorities-at-Risk groups over time — the comparative leverage is the cross-group, over-time
    variation in institutions, not a single country (see `cps-research-design`: comparative-causal panel).
  - **Self-check:** does your panel exploit institutional variation *across* cases and time, with
    inference honest about the clustered structure?

### Cross-national observational + institutional argument (institutions / governance)

- **Gerring, Thacker & Moreno, "Are Parliamentary Systems Better?," CPS 2009, 42(3):327–359.**
  Verified: SAGE article page, DOI 10.1177/0010414008325573.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a global cross-national test of a sharp institutional claim
    (parliamentarism vs. presidentialism) across development outcomes — the comparison is the
    identification of the institutional effect, with measurement defended across countries.
  - **Self-check:** is your institutional contrast measured comparably across countries, and do you
    confront the obvious confounds (development, history) rather than asserting them away?

### Subnational within-country comparison, mixed-method (clientelism / political economy)

- **Berenschot, "The Political Economy of Clientelism: A Comparative Study of Indonesia's Patronage Democracy," CPS 2018, 51(12):1563–1593.**
  Verified: SAGE article page, DOI 10.1177/0010414018758756.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses *within-country* (subnational) variation plus ethnography and an
    expert survey to explain why clientelism varies across regions — intra-national comparison is fully
    CPS, and the multi-method design ties the cross-regional pattern to a mechanism (see
    `cps-research-design`: case-based + multi-method).
  - **Self-check:** if your comparison is subnational, does the cross-unit variation identify the claim,
    and does your qualitative evidence carry the *mechanism* the quantitative pattern only describes?

### Cross-national survey experiment (contentious politics / behavior)

- **Dahlum, Pinckney & Wig, "Moral Logics of Support for Nonviolent Resistance: Evidence From a Cross-National Survey Experiment," CPS 2023, 56(3):326–362.**
  Verified: SAGE article page, DOI 10.1177/00104140221100198.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a **preregistered** survey experiment fielded across many countries tests
    competing micro-logics for a macro phenomenon (support for nonviolent resistance) — comparative
    experimental leverage with instrument equivalence across contexts (see `cps-research-design`:
    experiments fielded comparatively; `cps-transparency-and-data`: optional pre-analysis plan).
  - **Self-check:** does your cross-country experiment keep the instrument equivalent across contexts,
    and does the comparative contrast license the generalization you claim?

## Omitted for lack of CPS verification (do NOT attribute to CPS without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not CPS. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Stokes, "Perverse Accountability: A Formal Model of Machine Politics with Evidence from Argentina"
  (2005)** — verified to be in the *American Political Science Review* 99(3):315–325, **not CPS**. A
  classic clientelism paper that surfaces in CPS-adjacent searches.
- **Lijphart, "Consociational Democracy" (1969)** — verified to be in *World Politics* 21(2):207–225,
  **not CPS**. Foundational comparative-politics piece, frequently misplaced.
- **Gerring, Thacker & Moreno, "Centripetal Democratic Governance: A Theory and Global Inquiry" (2005)**
  — verified to be in the *American Political Science Review* 99(4):567–581, **not CPS**. Note: a
  *different* Gerring–Thacker–Moreno paper ("Are Parliamentary Systems Better?", 2009) *is* CPS and is
  listed above — do not conflate the two.
- **Hainmueller & Hangartner, "Who Gets a Swiss Passport? A Natural Experiment in Immigrant
  Discrimination" (2013)** — verified to be in the *American Political Science Review* 107(1):159–187,
  **not CPS**.
- **Pierson, "Increasing Returns, Path Dependence, and the Study of Politics" (2000)** — verified to be
  in the *American Political Science Review* 94(2):251–267, **not CPS**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on SAGE
(`journals.sagepub.com/home/cps`) with volume/issue/pages and the `10.1177/0010414...` DOI stem, and
update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
