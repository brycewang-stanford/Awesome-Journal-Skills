# Worked Example: A CPS-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **CPS introduction bar** from `cps-writing-style` and `cps-topic-selection`: by the
end of the introduction the reader knows the **comparative puzzle**, the **argument**, the **evidence**,
and why it matters to **comparative politics** — with a mechanism that **travels** beyond the cases
studied. The funnel is **comparative puzzle → stake → why it is hard to identify → setting & design →
headline finding with the mechanism → portable contribution → brief roadmap**, with CPS's distinctive
demand that the contribution be *comparative* (variation across cases or time) and the design be
*defended on its own terms*.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Quotas Without Power? When Gender Quotas Reshape Legislative
Behavior and When They Don't."* Fictional setting: a set of democracies that adopted legislative gender
quotas in **different years and under different party-system conditions**, creating staggered,
cross-nationally varied adoption. (Subfield: parties & elections / institutions; method: cross-national
staggered difference-in-differences with a paired-case mechanism check. Chosen because the conditions
under which a reform "bites" is a discipline-spanning comparative question — see the Kitschelt and
Saideman exemplars in [`../exemplars/library.md`](../exemplars/library.md).)

---

## Before (single-case, descriptive, no comparative leverage — typical first draft)

> The literature on gender quotas is large. Many scholars have studied the adoption of quotas, and case
> studies describe their effects in individual countries. In this paper, we study Country X, which adopted
> a legislative gender quota in 2010. We collect data on legislative speeches and votes before and after
> the reform and describe how women's participation changed. Quotas are an important topic in the study of
> representation. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes our case, Section 4 presents the
> data, Section 5 reports results, Section 6 discusses robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `cps-writing-style` / `cps-topic-selection`):**

- **No comparative leverage.** It studies one country before and after one reform — the central CPS test
  (variation *across* cases or time that identifies the claim) is absent. This is an area-studies case
  study, the named anti-pattern in `cps-topic-selection`.
- **No travel.** Nothing here a comparativist of another region could import; the claim is about Country X
  only (`cps-theory-building`'s portability test fails).
- **Descriptive, not explanatory.** "We describe how participation changed" — there is no mechanism and
  no rival to adjudicate.
- **Leads with the case, not a puzzle.** "Quotas are an important topic" is throat-clearing with vague
  stakes; no finding appears on page one.
- **Over-signposted seven-section roadmap** padding the prose against the 11,000-word cap.

---

## After (CPS arc — comparative puzzle + portable mechanism, contribution stated early)

> **Why do gender quotas transform legislative behavior in some democracies but leave it almost unchanged
> in others?** Across democracies that adopted legislative quotas, we show that the reform reshapes whose
> voice is heard on the floor *only where parties cannot circumvent it* — where candidate selection is
> centralized and lists are closed — and barely moves behavior where local party brokers control who
> actually wins. *(comparative puzzle + headline finding in the first breath — a claim about when an
> institution binds, not a description of one country.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond the study of quotas: a recurring question across comparative politics — from
> theories of representation to work on party organization and institutional change — is when a formal
> rule actually changes behavior versus when it is absorbed and neutralized by the organizations it
> targets. If the same reform "bites" under one party system and is hollowed out under another, then the
> effect of a reform is a property of the *interaction* between the rule and party structure, not of the
> rule alone. *(the comparative-politics stake, framed for a scholar of institutions or parties in any
> region — `cps-topic-selection` comparative-significance test.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because countries do not adopt quotas at random or under random party
> systems: a country may legislate a quota precisely when its parties are already moving toward greater
> internal openness, so a simple before-after comparison in one country would confound the rule with the
> organizational change that prompted it. *(why it is hard — the identification problem stated plainly,
> per `cps-research-design`.)*
>
> We exploit the **staggered, cross-nationally varied adoption** of quotas: democracies activated quota
> laws in different years and under different pre-existing party-system conditions. The staggered timing
> lets us compare each adopter to not-yet-adopters with a heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences
> estimator, clustering on country and verifying no differential pre-trends in legislative behavior; a
> **paired most-similar comparison** of two adopters that differ only in candidate-selection structure
> then traces the mechanism. *(setting & design in one paragraph; the modern staggered estimator and the
> paired case are named as tools, not as the lead — comparative leverage made explicit.)*
>
> Where selection is centralized, quotas raise women's floor participation sharply; where local brokers
> control selection, the same law produces near-zero change — and the paired cases show party gatekeepers
> reallocating safe seats to neutralize the quota. The mechanism is **not** a difference in voter demand
> or in women's qualifications across countries: it is **party control over candidate selection**.
> *(headline finding restated with the mechanism and a ruled-out rival — the `cps-research-design`
> adjudication move.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that **the effect of an electoral reform is conditional on party
> organization**: the same rule is transformative or inert depending on who controls selection. This
> reframes quota effects — often reported as a single average — as an *interaction* a comparativist can
> carry to any reform that runs through party gatekeepers, from candidate diversity rules to
> anti-defection laws. *(contribution stated early and as a *portable* mechanism with scope conditions:
> a scholar of parties in another region can import it — `cps-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the rule-meets-organization argument and its
> observable implications; Section 3 presents the cross-national design and the conditional effects;
> Section 4 traces the gatekeeping mechanism in the paired cases and states the scope conditions.
> *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the CPS bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Comparative leverage is explicit** — the design exploits variation *across countries and over time*
  (staggered, cross-nationally varied adoption), not one before-after case (`cps-research-design`:
  comparative leverage; `cps-topic-selection`: comparative puzzle).
- **Portable mechanism, not a bare finding** — the contribution ("reform effects are conditional on party
  organization") is framed so another comparativist can carry it to a different reform, passing the
  `cps-theory-building` portability test, with scope conditions stated.
- **Identification problem named before the design**, and the **single strongest rival is adjudicated**
  ("not voter demand or qualifications… it is party control over selection") — the `cps-research-design`
  adjudication sentence, with no-differential-pre-trends and country-level clustering stated plainly.
- **Multi-method, each method doing real work** — the panel gives the conditional average effect; the
  paired most-similar cases carry the *mechanism* (`cps-research-design`: multi-method linkage).
- **Reads for comparativists and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and defined, the roadmap is three
  sections not seven, references/tables/figures do not count toward the 11,000-word limit, and the
  framing is legible to an institutions or parties scholar in any region (`cps-writing-style`).
