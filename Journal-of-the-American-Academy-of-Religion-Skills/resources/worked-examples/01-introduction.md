# Worked Example: A JAAR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JAAR introduction move** drawn from the pack's own skills: an article must have
a **contestable point** (`jaar-argument-development`), reach **broad and fundamental interest to the
study of religion** beyond its subfield (`jaar-topic-selection`), be **method-conscious, reflexive, and
non-confessional** (`jaar-theory-and-method`), ground claims in **primary sources** read closely
(`jaar-sources-and-evidence`), and use **in-text author-date citations — not footnote citations**
(`jaar-citation-and-style`). This is humanities scholarship: there is **no dataset, no hypothesis test,
and no headline number** — the work is interpretive.

**Illustrative article (fictional):** *"Naming the Threshold: A Doorway Rite and the Category of
'Domestic Religion.'"* Fictional case: a household blessing performed at the threshold of a new home in
a fictional community, read against scholarship on the category of "domestic" or "lived" religion.

---

## Before (a survey with no point — typical first-draft intro)

> This article examines the threshold blessing as practiced in the community under study. The ritual has
> received little scholarly attention. Scholars of religion have long been interested in ritual, and many
> have written on domestic practice.¹ After describing the rite's history and its main elements, I will
> show how it is performed today. The blessing is an important part of the community's religious life and
> deserves to be better known. This study fills a gap in the literature on this tradition.
>
> ¹ See the extensive literature on ritual and domestic religion, e.g. the foundational studies and
>   numerous case treatments cited throughout this article.

**What's wrong (against the pack's skills):**

- **No point — it is a "this article examines" topic, not a claim** (`jaar-argument-development`: the #1
  JAAR rejection trigger is description that never becomes an argument).
- **Subfield-bound** — "deserves to be better known," "fills a gap in the literature on this tradition"
  signals interest only to specialists in this community (`jaar-topic-selection`: a piece that matters to
  one subfield is **returned for reframing before review**).
- **Bibliographic citation in a footnote** — JAAR uses **in-text author-date**; footnotes are for
  substance only (`jaar-citation-and-style`).
- **No theory or method named, no reflexivity** (`jaar-theory-and-method`).
- **"Important part of the community's religious life"** drifts toward an appreciative, confessional
  register rather than an analytic one (`jaar-writing-style`: analytic, not hagiographic).

---

## After (a contestable point of broad interest, method-conscious, in-text citations)

> A household blessing performed at the threshold of a new home looks like a paradigm case of "domestic
> religion" — private, informal, and centered on the home. I argue the opposite: the rite is legible only
> as a **boundary practice that publicly renegotiates who belongs to the household**, and reading it as
> "domestic" imports precisely the public/private split that the category was built to escape. *(A
> contestable thesis stated early — the standard reading is named and reversed.)*
>
> The stakes reach past this one community. The category of **"domestic" or "lived" religion** was
> introduced to recover practices that text- and institution-centered scholarship had ignored (Vance
> 2019: 14–17). But if a signature "domestic" rite turns out to do **boundary work** — sorting insider
> from outsider at the literal threshold — then the category may quietly re-inscribe the very
> public/private opposition it set out to dissolve. How we draw that line shapes what counts as religion
> at all, a question that travels well beyond any single tradition (cf. Okonkwo 2021: 88). *(Broad and
> fundamental interest: the claim is about a category the whole field uses, named for scholars outside
> the case.)*
>
> My evidence is the rite itself. I read the blessing's spoken formula — which addresses not the
> household but those *outside* the door — against three recorded performances I observed, and against the
> liturgical text the celebrant follows (Riala 2008). The wording is load-bearing: the threshold term
> *liminna* (translated here as "the named edge," italicized and glossed on first use) marks the doorway
> as a site of decision, not of interiority. *(Primary sources read closely; foreign term italicized,
> translated, and made to carry the argument — `jaar-sources-and-evidence`, `jaar-writing-style`.)*
>
> Methodologically, I read the rite through **ritual-studies and spatial theory**, treating the method as
> argument rather than decoration: it is the spatial reading that exposes the boundary work a "domestic"
> framing hides (Delacroix 1994). I describe the celebrant's claims about the rite faithfully without
> adopting them as my conclusions, and I note that my own presence at the threshold as an observer was
> itself read by participants as a kind of crossing — a fact I treat as evidence, not noise. *(Framework
> named and load-bearing; reflexive and non-confessional — `jaar-theory-and-method`.)*
>
> The contribution is therefore not a first account of one community's blessing but a **caution about a
> category**: "domestic religion" can smuggle back the public/private divide it was meant to retire, and
> a boundary-practice lens recovers what the domestic framing obscures. *(Contribution stated as a move
> on the field's conversation, not a gap-filling — `jaar-scholarly-positioning`.)* I develop this in three
> steps: the rite and its text, the spatial reading, and the consequences for how we use "lived religion."
> *(Brief roadmap, no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the JAAR bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **It has a point** — a single contestable thesis ("a boundary practice, not domestic religion")
  reverses the standard reading and could be disputed (`jaar-argument-development`).
- **Broad and fundamental interest** — the argument is about the **category** "domestic / lived
  religion," so scholars of other traditions and methods have a stake; the piece is reframed past the
  subfield, which JAAR requires before review (`jaar-topic-selection`, `jaar-scholarly-positioning`).
- **Method-conscious, reflexive, non-confessional** — a named framework (ritual-studies + spatial theory)
  *produces* the reading; the scholar's own position at the threshold is acknowledged; believers' claims
  are described, not endorsed (`jaar-theory-and-method`).
- **Primary sources do the work** — the spoken formula and liturgical text are read closely, with the
  key term italicized, translated, and glossed on first use (`jaar-sources-and-evidence`,
  `jaar-writing-style`).
- **In-text author-date citations** — `(Vance 2019: 14–17)`, `(Riala 2008)`, `(Delacroix 1994)` appear
  **in the text, not in footnotes**; the one footnote in the "before" version (a bibliographic dump) is
  exactly the error `jaar-citation-and-style` warns against.
- **No data, no hypothesis, no number** — the payoff is interpretive: it changes how a category is used,
  which is the humanities form of a JAAR contribution.
