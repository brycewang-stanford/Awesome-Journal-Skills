## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × question** is closest to yours, then study how that paper grounds an
accounting phenomenon in an **economic friction** (agency, information asymmetry, contracting cost,
political cost) and tests it on large-sample archival data — the JAE bar (see
[`../../skills/jae-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jae-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/jae-theory-development/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jae-theory-development/SKILL.md), and
[`../../skills/jae-contribution-framing/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jae-contribution-framing/SKILL.md)). For
each, ask the self-check question before claiming your paper is "JAE-shaped."

## By method

### Compensation contracting — managerial incentives and accounting choice (positive accounting theory)

- **Healy, "The Effect of Bonus Schemes on Accounting Decisions," JAE 1985, 7(1–3):85–107.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v7y1985i1-3p85-107.html` (Elsevier, *Journal of
  Accounting and Economics*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the canonical Positive-Accounting-Theory test — bonus-plan parameters
    (bogey/cap) give managers an *economic incentive* to shift accruals, and the paper derives and tests
    the directional prediction from the contract itself rather than from intuition. Pairs with the
    contracting framing in [`../../skills/jae-theory-development/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jae-theory-development/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** does your prediction follow from a contract's payoff structure (an *a priori*
    incentive), not a reverse-engineered correlation?

### Conditional conservatism — asymmetric timeliness as an efficient-contracting outcome (capital-markets measurement)

- **Basu, "The Conservatism Principle and the Asymmetric Timeliness of Earnings," JAE 1997, 24(1):3–37.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v24y1997i1p3-37.html` (Elsevier, December 1997).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** operationalizes a long-standing accounting principle (conservatism) as a
    *measurable* asymmetry — earnings reflect bad news faster than good news — and ties it to the
    contracting/verifiability demand for accounting, spawning the most-used conservatism measure in the
    literature.
  - **Self-check:** can you turn the qualitative construct you care about into a regression coefficient
    with an *a priori* sign grounded in an economic friction?

### Auditor monitoring — analytical model of audit quality (contracting / monitoring theory)

- **DeAngelo, "Auditor Size and Audit Quality," JAE 1981, 3(3):183–199.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v3y1981i3p183-199.html` (Elsevier, December 1981).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a compact economic argument — quasi-rents from incumbency make larger
    auditors less willing to compromise independence — showing JAE publishes tightly-reasoned analytical
    contracting/monitoring work, not only large-sample archival tests.
  - **Self-check:** is your monitoring claim derived from agents' objective functions and the constraint
    they face, with comparative statics, rather than asserted?

### Capital-markets information content — review that frames a field (empirical capital markets)

- **Kothari, "Capital Markets Research in Accounting," JAE 2001, 31(1–3):105–231.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v31y2001i1-3p105-231.html` (Elsevier, 2001).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the field-defining synthesis of the value-relevance / market-efficiency /
    contracting demand for accounting numbers — use it to locate which capital-markets conversation your
    paper joins (see [`../../skills/jae-literature-positioning/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jae-literature-positioning/SKILL.md)).
  - **Self-check:** can you name the specific information-content stream you advance, and the economic
    demand (valuation, efficiency tests, contracting) that motivates it?

### Financial-accounting role in governance — contracting & monitoring synthesis (agency theory)

- **Bushman & Smith, "Financial Accounting Information and Corporate Governance," JAE 2001, 32(1–3):237–333.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v32y2001i1-3p237-333.html` (Elsevier, December 2001).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** maps how accounting information enters governance — compensation,
    monitoring, the disciplining of managers — squarely in the Jensen-Meckling agency tradition that JAE
    is built on; a template for framing accounting as an input to contracts.
  - **Self-check:** does your paper connect an accounting feature to a *governance/contracting* economic
    mechanism, not just a market correlation?

### Earnings quality — accruals and the measurement of firm performance (capital-markets archival)

- **Dechow, "Accounting Earnings and Cash Flows as Measures of Firm Performance: The Role of Accounting Accruals," JAE 1994, 18(1):3–42.**
  Verified: RePEc `ideas.repec.org/a/eee/jaecon/v18y1994i1p3-42.html` (Elsevier, 1994).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an economic account of *why* accruals make earnings a better performance
    measure than cash flows (they mitigate timing and matching problems), with cross-sectional predictions
    tied to where that friction bites — model behavior for a JAE measurement paper.
  - **Self-check:** do your cross-sectional partitions tie the effect's magnitude to the severity of the
    underlying friction, as JAE prizes?

## Omitted for sibling-journal confusion (do NOT attribute to JAE)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following landmark accounting papers were **checked and
excluded** — they are foundational but were published in a *different* journal. They are listed here only
as guardrails against the JAE-vs-sibling trap:

- **Ball & Brown, "An Empirical Evaluation of Accounting Income Numbers" (1968)** — *Journal of Accounting
  Research* 6(2):159–178, **not JAE** (JAE was founded in 1979).
- **Watts & Zimmerman, "Towards a Positive Theory of the Determination of Accounting Standards" (1978)** —
  *The Accounting Review* 53(1):112–134, **not JAE** (TAR). The PAT founders, but this article is TAR.
- **Sloan, "Do Stock Prices Fully Reflect Information in Accruals and Cash Flows About Future Earnings?"
  (1996)** — *The Accounting Review* 71(3):289–315, **not JAE** (TAR).
- **Dechow, Sloan & Sweeney, "Detecting Earnings Management" (1995)** — *The Accounting Review*
  70(2):193–225, **not JAE** (TAR).
- **Watts, "Conservatism in Accounting — Part I" (2003)** — *Accounting Horizons* 17:207–221, **not JAE**.
- **Lang & Lundholm, "Corporate Disclosure Policy and Analyst Behavior" (1996)** — *The Accounting Review*
  71:467–492, **not JAE** (TAR).
- **Leuz & Verrecchia, "The Economic Consequences of Increased Disclosure" (2000)** — *Journal of
  Accounting Research* 38 (Supplement):91–124, **not JAE** (JAR).

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on ScienceDirect (`sciencedirect.com`, ISSN
0165-4101) or RePEc series `eee/jaecon` with volume/issue, and update the access-date header. When in
doubt — especially for canonical accruals/disclosure/conservatism papers that are easy to misremember as
JAE — **omit**.
