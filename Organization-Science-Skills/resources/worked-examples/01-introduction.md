# Worked Example: An *Organization Science*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the introduction arc *Organization Science* rewards (from `orgsci-writing-style`,
`orgsci-topic-selection`, and `orgsci-contribution-framing`): **organizational puzzle / theoretical
tension → the construct and the setting → why prior theory cannot resolve it → the organizational
mechanism and level(s) → the contribution to organization theory → how the design / identification
supports the claim → brief roadmap.** The governing rules: **front-load the contribution** (by the end of
the intro the reader knows the phenomenon, the conversation, the mechanism, and what organization research
learns), write for an **interdisciplinary micro-to-macro audience** (define constructs and name the level
on first use), and lead with **overall contribution, not novelty** — "theoretical novelty is neither
necessary nor sufficient."

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Holding the Line or Letting Go? How Frontline Routine Improvisation
Becomes Organizational Capability During Demand Shocks."* Fictional setting: a single multi-site
service-delivery organization observed across an unexpected, plausibly exogenous surge in demand, combining
an inductive field study of three sites with archival operating data on all sites.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Organizational routines and capabilities have been studied extensively in organization theory, strategy,
> and operations. Many scholars have examined how routines change. In this paper, we conduct a qualitative
> study at a service organization and also analyze archival data using a fixed-effects regression. We
> collected interviews and observations and coded them. Our results show several interesting patterns
> related to improvisation and capability. Understanding routines is important for managers and
> organizations. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes the methods, Section 4 presents
> findings, and Section 5 discusses implications.

**What's wrong (against `orgsci-writing-style` / `orgsci-topic-selection` / `orgsci-contribution-framing`):**

- **Leads with "routines have been studied extensively" and with method** ("a qualitative study … a
  fixed-effects regression") rather than the organizational puzzle — the named anti-pattern in
  `orgsci-writing-style` (a first page that withholds the contribution).
- **No theoretical tension and no contribution on page one.** A macro reader cannot tell what we did not
  understand about organizations before, and now do — it fails the
  `orgsci-contribution-framing` test and reads as a "novelty"/gap pitch, which the journal explicitly does
  not reward.
- **Level of analysis is unnamed.** Is the claim about individual improvisation, the site, or the
  organization? `orgsci-theory-development` flags level conflation as a reject signal.
- **No organizational mechanism.** "Patterns related to improvisation and capability" is a relationship,
  not an engine.
- **Method-first framing with no design logic** — why these three sites, why the archival panel, and what
  the shock buys for the argument are all missing.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (*Organization Science* arc — tension + construct + mechanism + contribution, design in support)

> **When a sudden demand shock overwhelms an organization's established routines, frontline workers
> improvise — but does that improvisation erode the organization's capability or become the raw material
> for a new one?** Capability theory gives two opposed answers: an inertia view treats off-script
> improvisation as drift that degrades reliability, while a dynamic-capabilities view treats it as the
> microfoundation of adaptation. We show that the answer turns on an organizational mechanism the existing
> debate leaves implicit — **whether local improvisations are *articulated and re-embedded* into the
> organization's routines, or remain private workarounds** — and that this articulation process, not the
> improvisation itself, is what converts frontline action at the *individual* level into capability at the
> *organizational* level. *(Organizational puzzle + the opposed theoretical positions + the construct
> [articulation/re-embedding] + the cross-level claim, in the first breath; "improvisation" and
> "re-embedding" are defined in place for a reader from either a micro or a macro tradition.)*
>
> Adjudicating these views is hard because improvisation and capability are entangled: in any single
> period, a site that is quietly drifting looks much like a site that is productively adapting, and sites
> that improvise most may also be the ones already under the most strain — so a raw correlation between
> improvisation and later performance confounds the mechanism with selection into improvisation. *(Why it
> is hard — the identification problem, stated in organizational terms, per `orgsci-data-analysis`.)*
>
> We study a multi-site service-delivery organization through an unexpected, plausibly exogenous surge in
> demand that hit all sites at once but to differing degrees. We pair an inductive field study of three
> sites — observation and interviews that let us build the articulation-and-re-embedding process model —
> with an archival panel of all sites that lets us ask whether sites where improvisations were articulated
> into shared routines subsequently sustained higher reliability than sites where they stayed private,
> using the differential exposure to the shock and site fixed effects to separate the mechanism from
> standing differences across sites. *(Setting and mixed design in one paragraph; the inductive arm builds
> the mechanism and the archival arm disciplines the cross-level claim, per `orgsci-theory-development`
> and `orgsci-methods` — neither method leads.)*
>
> The process model has three phases — local improvisation under strain, collective articulation of what
> worked, and re-embedding into the standard routine — and the panel shows that the capability gain appears
> **only** where the second and third phases occurred; sites that improvised without articulating returned
> to baseline once the shock passed. The pattern is concentrated where frontline workers had the standing
> to make their workarounds legible to the rest of the site, and it does *not* appear from improvisation
> volume alone. *(Findings restated as the observable implications of the mechanism — the move
> `orgsci-theory-development` calls building, not describing.)*
>
> Our contribution is to organization theory: we identify **articulation-and-re-embedding as the mechanism
> that turns individual improvisation into organizational capability**, reconciling the inertia and
> dynamic-capabilities readings of the same behavior rather than choosing a side. This is a *mechanism* and
> *new-process-model* contribution in the sense `orgsci-contribution-framing` privileges — what we did not
> understand before is *why* improvisation sometimes builds capability and sometimes only masks drift — and
> it travels: any organization facing a shock to its routines can ask whether local adaptations are being
> re-embedded or merely tolerated. *(Contribution stated early, tied to a named conversation and a specific
> contribution source — mechanism / new theory — not to novelty; boundary condition [worker standing]
> stated as theory.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the articulation-and-re-embedding model against the
> inertia and dynamic-capabilities literatures; Section 3 describes the field and archival design and the
> shock; Section 4 presents the process model and the panel test of its cross-level implication.
> *(Brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Organization Science* bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded for a cross-level audience** — by the end of the first paragraph the reader
  has the organizational puzzle, the opposed theories, the construct, and the level-bridging claim
  (`orgsci-writing-style`: make the phenomenon, the conversation joined, and the contribution unmistakable
  on the opening pages; define constructs and name the level on first use).
- **Leads with the theoretical tension, not the method** — the inertia-vs-dynamic-capabilities tension
  opens the paper; the field study and the panel appear only where the design is described
  (`orgsci-writing-style` anti-pattern of leading with method, avoided).
- **A named organizational mechanism, not a relationship** — *articulation-and-re-embedding* is the engine
  that does the theoretical work, exactly the `orgsci-theory-development` requirement to build the
  mechanism and not just "A relates to B."
- **The cross-level bridge is explicit** — individual improvisation → collective articulation →
  organizational capability, with the bridging logic stated rather than asserted from micro data
  (`orgsci-theory-development` level-conflation guard).
- **Contribution source is explicit and is not "novelty"** — the paper wins on *mechanism* and a *new
  process model* that **reconciles a contradiction** in the literature, the framing
  `orgsci-contribution-framing` and `orgsci-literature-positioning` reward; "theoretical novelty is neither
  necessary nor sufficient."
- **Identification / selection is handled, not ignored** — the differential, plausibly exogenous shock and
  site fixed effects separate the mechanism from selection into improvisation, satisfying the
  `orgsci-data-analysis` design-logic standard (valued even though the journal holds that causal inference
  "is not necessary and often impossible").
- **Boundary condition stated as theory** — worker standing is part of the mechanism's scope, not a
  limitations afterthought.
