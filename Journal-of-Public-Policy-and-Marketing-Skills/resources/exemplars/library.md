# JPP&M Exemplar Library

Well-known papers in the marketing-and-public-policy tradition to study for framing, evidence style, and policy translation. Compiled 2026-07-16. Citation details were written from field knowledge, not re-pulled from DOI pages in this pass — **confirm volume/pages/DOI in the SAGE archive before citing**; entries marked *adjacent* appeared in sibling outlets but anchor conversations JPP&M runs.

| # | Exemplar | Venue | Why it matters for a JPP&M author |
|---|----------|-------|-----------------------------------|
| 1 | Bettman, Payne, and Staelin (1986), "Cognitive Considerations in Designing Effective Labels for Presenting Risk Information" | JPP&M | The foundational statement that label *design* is a behavioral-science problem regulators must solve; the template for instrument-design theorizing |
| 2 | Stewart and Martin (1994), "Intended and Unintended Consequences of Warning Messages: A Review and Synthesis of Empirical Research" | JPP&M | Canonical synthesis showing warnings can fail or backfire — the reason unintended-consequence hypotheses are expected here |
| 3 | Moorman (1996), quasi-experimental assessment of nutrition-information processing under the Nutrition Labeling and Education Act | JPP&M | A model policy evaluation: a federal labeling law studied with explicit before/after-plus-comparison logic |
| 4 | Culnan (2000), "Protecting Privacy Online: Is Self-Regulation Working?" | JPP&M | The reference point for privacy self-regulation debates; shows how to evaluate an enforcement regime rather than a stimulus |
| 5 | Andreasen (2002), "Marketing Social Marketing in the Social Change Marketplace" | JPP&M | Defines social marketing's institutional position; essential for public-health-marketing framing |
| 6 | Seiders and Petty (2004), "Obesity and the Role of Food Marketing: A Policy Analysis of Issues and Remedies" | JPP&M | Exemplary mapping from marketing practice to a menu of policy remedies with named actors |
| 7 | Andrews, Netemeyer, and Burton's research program on warning labels and nutrition claims (1990s–2000s) | JPP&M / JM (program) | The sustained experimental program on mandated disclosures — how realistic stimuli and regulator-relevant contrasts are done; cite specific papers after verifying venue |
| 8 | Pechmann and colleagues' antismoking-advertising program for adolescents (e.g., the 2003 Journal of Marketing piece on protection-motivation message themes) | JM / related JPP&M work (program, *adjacent*) | The model for theory-driven public-health message testing feeding directly into campaign and policy design |
| 9 | Grier and Bryant (2005), "Social Marketing in Public Health" | Annual Review of Public Health (*adjacent*) | The cross-disciplinary bridge JPP&M authors use to speak to health agencies |
| 10 | Baker, Gentry, and Rittenburg (2005), "Building Understanding of the Domain of Consumer Vulnerability" | Journal of Macromarketing (*adjacent*) | The conceptual anchor for vulnerability claims — read before hypothesizing about protected groups |
| 11 | Mick, Pettigrew, Pechmann, and Ozanne, eds. (2012), *Transformative Consumer Research for Personal and Collective Well-Being* | Routledge volume (*adjacent*) | The TCR movement that shapes JPP&M's welfare orientation and many of its special issues |
| 12 | Martin and Murphy (2017), "The Role of Data Privacy in Marketing" | JAMS (*adjacent*) | The privacy-scholarship map to position against when writing for the data-policy conversation |

## Guardrails

- Venue discipline: entries 8–12 are *adjacent* — cite them as context, but position the manuscript against the JPP&M stream itself (entries 1–7).
- When citing a "program" row, pick a specific verified paper; do not cite the program as if it were one article.
- The regulatory record (FTC staff reports, FDA rulemakings, CFPB studies) counts as citable prior work at this journal — pair exemplars with the current rule text.
