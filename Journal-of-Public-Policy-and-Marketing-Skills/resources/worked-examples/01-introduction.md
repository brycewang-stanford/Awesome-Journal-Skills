# Worked Example: A JPP&M-Style Introduction, Annotated

A fictional introduction for an imagined paper — "Front-of-Package Sugar Warnings and Household Beverage Purchases" — showing the beat structure `jppm-writing-style` prescribes. No real study is described; the annotations are the point.

## Beat 1 — The policy problem, concretely

> Sugar-sweetened beverages remain a leading dietary source of added sugar for U.S. households, and front-of-package warning labels — already mandatory in several Latin American markets — are now under active consideration by U.S. regulators and state legislatures. Yet the central design question remains unsettled: whether a graphic warning changes actual purchases, or only stated intentions.

**Why this works:** the opening names a marketplace harm, a live instrument, and jurisdictions where it is real. The reader knows within two sentences which policy conversation this is. No "in recent years, scholars have become increasingly interested…".

## Beat 2 — The decision-relevant unknown

> Two decades of labeling research establish that evaluative formats outperform numeric panels in lab comprehension tasks, and the warning-label literature has long cautioned that intended effects can coexist with unintended ones. What this evidence cannot tell an agency drafting a rule is how mandated warnings perform in noisy retail environments, over time, among the low-income households whose consumption the policy most aims to shift.

**Why this works:** positions against *both* the academic stream and the agency's decision. The gap is what a rule-writer needs and lacks — not "little research has examined."

## Beat 3 — What this paper does, in plain language

> We exploit the staggered adoption of warning mandates across [jurisdictions], comparing household beverage purchases in adopting markets to matched non-adopting markets before and after implementation, using retail scanner data. A companion experiment tests the two label formats named in the current draft rule.

**Why this works:** the counterfactual is visible in one sentence — a reader can already audit the design's logic. The experiment's conditions map to the regulator's actual choice set, signaling mandatable stimuli.

## Beat 4 — What was found, with magnitude

> Warning adoption reduces sugary-beverage volume by roughly [X] percentage points from a [Y]% baseline share, with the decline concentrated in households below median income and no detectable substitution toward untaxed high-calorie alternatives. The graphic format outperforms the text format by [Z] points in the experiment.

**Why this works:** decision units (percentage points off a stated base), the welfare-relevant subgroup, and the unintended-consequence check are all in the headline — not deferred to page 30.

## Beat 5 — Who should do what

> The findings indicate that an FDA front-of-package rule adopting the graphic format would deliver measurable purchase reductions where they are most intended, and give beverage marketers a concrete benchmark for reformulation thresholds that avoid the warning.

**Why this works:** two stakeholders, each with an action inside its authority — the agency's format choice, the firm's reformulation decision. This sentence is the introduction's version of the Policy Contribution Statement.

## What this introduction deliberately avoids

- A theory recital before the reader knows the decision at stake (theory arrives in Section 2, harnessed to the instrument).
- Advocacy language — the label "works" only as far as the estimate and its CI.
- Mechanism-first framing that would signal a JCP paper wearing a policy costume.
