# Review Process (jppm-review-process)

## When to trigger

- A decision letter arrived and you must extract what the editors actually require
- Estimating desk-reject risk before submitting
- Reviews pull in different directions (methods reviewer vs. policy reviewer) and you need a strategy
- Deciding whether an R&R is worth the added data work, or whether to reroute
- Wondering how the editor transition affects a paper submitted under the old team

## How JPP&M review works

Submissions enter SageTrack and go first to the **joint Editors in Chief** (Melissa Bublitz and Stacey Finkelstein for submissions from April 1, 2026; verify the current masthead), who desk-screen — the Policy Contribution Statement and abstract carry most of that decision — and assign surviving papers to an **associate editor** from the journal's AE board, who recruits reviewers under **double-anonymized** review. The reviewer pool is distinctive: alongside marketing academics, JPP&M draws scholars with law, policy, and public-health backgrounds and board members with agency or practice experience. Plan for the paper to be read simultaneously by someone auditing the estimator and someone auditing the policy realism; a paper that satisfies only one camp usually returns as a split verdict the AE must resolve.

## The desk-reject patterns to pre-empt

| Pattern | What the editors see |
|---------|----------------------|
| Bolted-on policy | a competent consumer study whose policy content lives in one closing paragraph |
| Non-actionable implications | recommendations no identifiable stakeholder could implement |
| Advocacy without evidence | conclusions whose strength comes from conviction, not from the design |
| Missing counterfactual | an "evaluation" with no credible comparison — before/after trends alone |
| Wrong-outlet fit | firm-performance or pure-mechanism papers better suited to JM or JCP |
| Scope mismatch | no marketing content at all — straight policy analysis |

If two or more of these describe the draft, fix upstream (`jppm-topic-selection`, `jppm-contribution-framing`) before submitting; a desk reject here is usually a fit verdict, not bad luck.

## Reading the decision letter

- **Weight the AE/editor synthesis** over any single review; it defines the revision contract.
- **"Strengthen the policy contribution"** — the most common JPP&M R&R theme. It rarely means more words; it means sharper stakeholder mapping, decision-unit magnitudes, or evidence on the contested margin.
- **"Identification concerns"** — for evaluations, expect requests for modern DiD estimators, pre-trend sensitivity, placebo tests, or an added design leg.
- **"Consider firm response / unintended effects"** — a request to close the loop on strategic reactions or backfire outcomes, often satisfiable with supplementary data or bounded analysis.
- **"Tone down the advocacy"** — recalibrate claim verbs and acknowledge trade-offs; this is cheap to fix and expensive to ignore.
- **Split reviews** — when the methods and policy reviewers disagree, the AE's letter tells you whose concern is binding; answer both, prioritize the binding one.

## Decisions and what they mean

| Decision | Reading | Move |
|----------|---------|------|
| Desk reject | fit failure — usually one of the six patterns above | rework fit or reroute; do not resubmit cosmetics |
| Reject after review | counterfactual or contribution not credible to either camp | major rebuild; consider JCA/Macromarketing if the lever was the weak part |
| Major revision | the policy question is valued; evidence or framing must tighten | budget realistically — new analyses are common, new data sometimes |
| Minor revision | substance accepted | comply precisely; keep the page cap in mind as you add |
| Conditional accept | editorial polish | execute; verify statements still current at resubmission |

Timelines vary with the reviewer mix and editor transition; expect an experimental-plus-policy pool to take longer than a single-discipline one, and re-check current turnaround expectations with the editorial office rather than folklore.

## Checklist

- [ ] Desk-screen self-audit run against the six patterns before submission
- [ ] The AE/editor synthesis identified as the binding revision contract
- [ ] Each major comment mapped to a feasible fix (analysis, data, framing, tone)
- [ ] Split-review conflicts resolved by the AE's stated priority, not by picking favorites
- [ ] Revision effort budgeted against the realistic value of the R&R
- [ ] Post-transition policies (formats, statements) re-checked before resubmission

## Anti-patterns

- **Resubmitting a desk reject** with a rewritten discussion and the same fit problem
- **Answering the loud reviewer** while the AE's stated concern goes unaddressed
- **Treating "strengthen policy contribution" as a word-count task** rather than an evidence/framing task
- **Ignoring the practitioner reviewer** because their comments lack citations — their realism check often decides the AE
- **Assuming old-team norms** carry over across an editorial transition unverified

## Output format

```text
【Decision】desk reject / reject / major R&R / minor / conditional accept
【Binding contract】the AE-or-editor concern the revision must satisfy
【Camp map】methods-reviewer asks vs. policy-reviewer asks, and the AE's priority
【Feasibility】new analysis / new data / reframing — cost vs. value of the R&R
【Go / no-go】revise / reroute (where) / withdraw
【Next skill】jppm-rebuttal
```
