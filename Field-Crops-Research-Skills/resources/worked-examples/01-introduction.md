# Worked Example: An FCR-Style Abstract + Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **FCR framing arc** assembled from this pack's own skills:

- **`fcr-topic-selection`** — clear the four-part scope gate (field-based, multi-season/multi-environment,
  a field crop, general significance — not local/descriptive).
- **`fcr-literature-positioning`** — build to a sharp objective; name the gap in **general** terms
  (a biophysical process / management response that is unresolved across environments), not "first in
  my region"; connect to **yield formation**.
- **`fcr-experimental-design`** — show a **multi-environment (≥ 2 seasons and/or multiple sites)**
  design and the **G×E** structure that lets the result generalise.
- **`fcr-data-analysis`** — report **estimated marginal means with SED/LSD**, effect sizes with
  uncertainty, and G×E done properly (not pooled plots, no a/b/c letters on a quantitative dose).
- **`fcr-writing-style`** — abstract ≤ 400 words stating the **quantitative** finding in **SI units**;
  a discussion that **interprets rather than repeats**; highlights that are findings, not topics.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Closing the rainfed wheat yield gap with delayed nitrogen
splitting: a genotype × environment × management evaluation across nine site-years."* Fictional setting:
three rainfed sites across a Mediterranean-climate wheatbelt, three seasons, four cultivars, two N-timing
strategies. All quantities below are invented.

---

## Before (buries the agronomic point — typical first-draft abstract + intro)

> **Abstract.** Nitrogen management is important for wheat production. A field experiment was conducted to
> study the effect of nitrogen timing on wheat. Treatments were arranged in plots and data were analysed
> by ANOVA. Means were compared and some differences were significant (P < 0.05). Delayed nitrogen
> tended to give higher yield in our trial. The results are useful for local farmers and agree with
> previous studies. Further research is needed.
>
> **Introduction.** Wheat is one of the most important crops in the world. Nitrogen is one of the most
> important inputs. Many studies have looked at nitrogen and wheat. However, no study has looked at
> nitrogen timing at our research station. Therefore, the objective of this study was to test the effect
> of nitrogen timing on wheat yield in our region.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Fails the FCR scope gate** (`fcr-topic-selection`): novelty framed as **"not done at our station / in
  our region"** — FCR treats this as local/descriptive and desk-rejects it.
- **No quantitative finding** in the abstract (`fcr-writing-style`: the abstract must state the principal
  quantitative result with units); "tended to give higher yield" hides the number.
- **Wrong analysis signalled** (`fcr-data-analysis`): "arranged in plots… ANOVA… means were compared"
  ignores blocks/split-plot/environment structure and offers stars without effect size or SED/LSD.
- **No multi-environment design stated** (`fcr-experimental-design`): no seasons, no sites, no G×E — the
  single most important FCR-specific expectation is invisible.
- **"Agree with previous studies"** is corroborative, the exact framing
  `fcr-literature-positioning` warns against; the gap is not named in general terms.
- **No link to yield formation or a transferable mechanism**, so the "so what" is unanswered.

---

## After (FCR arc — general gap → multi-environment design → quantified G×E result → interpretation)

> **Abstract.** Delayed nitrogen can lift rainfed wheat yield, but whether the benefit is **consistent**
> or **environment-dependent** is unresolved, which limits its use as a general recommendation. We
> evaluated two nitrogen-timing strategies — a conventional sowing-plus-tillering split versus a
> **delayed split that shifts 40% of N to stem elongation** — for four cultivars across **nine
> site-years** (three rainfed Mediterranean-climate sites × three seasons) spanning **280–520 mm**
> in-season rainfall. Treatments were a split-plot within a randomised complete block design (cultivar as
> main plot, N timing as sub-plot, four replicates), analysed with a **linear mixed model** (environment,
> block, and genotype-within-environment as random effects); we report **estimated marginal means with
> SED**, and partition genotype × environment × management with a Finlay–Wilkinson stability analysis.
> Averaged over environments, delayed splitting raised grain yield by **0.46 t ha⁻¹** (from 4.18 to 4.64
> t ha⁻¹; SED 0.11) and grain protein by **0.7 percentage points**, but the response was **strongly
> environment-dependent**: it added **0.9–1.2 t ha⁻¹** in the three wettest site-years and was
> **near zero (and once −0.2 t ha⁻¹)** below ~340 mm in-season rainfall, where late N arrived after the
> canopy had set grain number. The benefit tracked **post-anthesis water availability**, not site
> identity, and one slow-senescing cultivar captured most of the upside. We conclude that delayed N
> splitting is a **conditional**, water-mediated tool — worth deploying where stored soil water or spring
> rainfall supports grain filling, and avoided in drier environments — and that recommendations should be
> indexed to expected post-anthesis water rather than issued as a blanket practice. *(≈ purpose →
> multi-environment methods → quantified, units-bearing, G×E-aware result → agronomic conclusion.)*
>
> **Introduction (opening).** Whether the yield benefit of **delayed nitrogen** in rainfed wheat is a
> general gain or an environment-contingent one is unresolved, and the answer determines whether it can be
> recommended across a wheatbelt or only in specific seasons. *(the gap, stated in general terms — not
> "untested in our region.")* Prior work attributes the response to better **post-anthesis N supply** for
> grain filling, yet trials disagree because they span different water environments without separating the
> water effect from the timing effect — so the **mechanism**, not just the site list, is what is
> contested. *(positions against a mechanism, per `fcr-literature-positioning`.)* Because late-applied N
> can only raise yield if the crop still has the water to translate it into grain, we hypothesised that
> the benefit scales with **post-anthesis water availability** and is therefore conditional. *(connects
> the gap to yield formation and a transferable biophysical process.)* We tested this across **nine
> site-years** chosen to span the rainfall range of the target population of environments, so the result
> is expected to hold across rainfed Mediterranean-climate wheat systems within that water envelope — and
> explicitly not beyond it. *(the generalisation test from `fcr-experimental-design`, stated up front.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the FCR bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Scope gate cleared** (`fcr-topic-selection`): field-based, **nine site-years** (multi-season +
  multi-site), a field crop (wheat), and a **general** insight (a water-mediated *condition* on the
  practice) — not a local confirmation.
- **Gap named generally and tied to yield formation** (`fcr-literature-positioning`): the unresolved
  issue is *consistency vs. environment-dependence* of a mechanism (post-anthesis N for grain filling),
  engaging the physiology rather than only same-region papers.
- **Multi-environment design and G×E made explicit** (`fcr-experimental-design`): seasons, sites,
  split-plot layout, replication, and a stated **generalisation sentence** ("these environments represent
  ___, so the result holds for ___ and not for ___").
- **Analysis matches the design** (`fcr-data-analysis`): a **linear mixed model** with the right random
  effects, **estimated marginal means with SED**, effect sizes with uncertainty (0.46 t ha⁻¹; SED 0.11),
  and **G×E reported, not hidden by pooling** — no significance stars standing in for magnitude.
- **Abstract carries the quantitative finding in SI units** (`fcr-writing-style`); the closing sentence
  **interprets** (a conditional, water-indexed recommendation) rather than restating the numbers, and
  invents **no policy** — it scopes an agronomic recommendation to the evidence.
- **Highlights would be findings, not topics**, e.g. *"Delayed N split added 0.46 t ha⁻¹ on average
  across nine site-years"* and *"Benefit reached 0.9–1.2 t ha⁻¹ when wet, ~0 below 340 mm"* — each
  concrete and within the ≤ 85-character limit.
