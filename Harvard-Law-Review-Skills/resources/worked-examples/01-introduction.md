# Worked Example: An HLR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **HLR introduction bar** from `hlr-writing-style` and `hlr-thesis-and-contribution`:
by the end of the introduction the reader knows the **live problem, the thesis, the contribution, and the
payoff** — and can see the **doctrine → theory → prescription** arc the body will follow. In law-review
prose, the **argument lives in the text** and the **support lives in footnotes**; the introduction
front-loads a contestable normative claim rather than warming up with a doctrinal survey.

**Illustrative Article (fictional):** *"Borrowed Authority: Why Agencies May Not Subdelegate the
Sanctions Power."* Fictional setting: a (made-up) circuit split over whether a federal agency may delegate
its statutory power to impose civil penalties to a private self-regulatory body. (Field: administrative /
constitutional law; type: doctrinal-normative Article. Chosen because subdelegation touches structural
commitments — accountability and the nondelegation principle — that a generalist editor recognizes.)

---

## Before (doctrinal warm-up, buries the claim — typical first draft)

> The law of administrative delegation is vast and much debated. Courts have long grappled with how much
> authority Congress may vest in agencies, and scholars have written extensively on the nondelegation
> doctrine. This Article examines whether agencies may further delegate their enforcement powers to private
> entities. Part I reviews the nondelegation doctrine and its history. Part II surveys the circuit courts'
> treatment of subdelegation. Part III discusses the relevant statutes. Part IV offers some thoughts on
> how courts might approach the question. The issue is an important one that deserves attention.

**What's wrong (against `hlr-writing-style` / `hlr-thesis-and-contribution`):**

- **No thesis.** "This Article examines… and offers some thoughts" describes a topic, not a claim. A
  generalist editor cannot tell what is being argued (`hlr-thesis-and-contribution`: the survey trap).
- **Buries the point behind a doctrinal warm-up.** Pages of "the law is vast and much debated" before any
  argument — the `hlr-writing-style` throat-clearing anti-pattern.
- **No payoff.** Nothing tells the reader who acts differently if the author is right.
- **No preemption signal.** "Scholars have written extensively" without distinguishing the nearest work —
  exactly what `hlr-preemption-check` exists to prevent.
- **Over-signposted four-part roadmap** doing the work the argument should do, with no sense of the arc.

---

## After (HLR arc — live problem + thesis + contribution + payoff, claim front-loaded)

> **May a federal agency hand its power to punish to a private club?** A widening split among the circuits
> now permits exactly that: three courts have upheld agency rules that let private self-regulatory bodies
> impose civil penalties, while two have held that the sanctions power cannot be subdelegated outside the
> government at all.¹ *(the live problem and the split, stated concretely in the first breath — footnote 1
> would pincite the conflicting decisions, per `hlr-sources-and-bluebook`.)*
>
> This Article argues that the **sanctions power is constitutionally non-subdelegable to private actors**,
> and that the permissive circuits err by treating subdelegation as an ordinary efficiency question rather
> than a structural one. *(the thesis — one contestable, declarative sentence, on page one, per
> `hlr-thesis-and-contribution`.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond the doctrinal corner it occupies: subdelegation to private hands severs the chain
> of political accountability that justifies agency power in the first place. When a private body wields the
> state's coercive authority, no elected official answers for the penalty — the very harm the nondelegation
> principle and the Appointments Clause guard against. The question is therefore not about administrative
> convenience but about who may legitimately exercise the sovereign power to punish. *(the generalist
> stake — framed so a constitutional-law editor and an administrative-law editor both see why it matters,
> per `hlr-thesis-and-contribution`'s generalist-stake test.)*
>
> Existing scholarship has analyzed *intra*-governmental subdelegation and the private-nondelegation
> doctrine in the licensing context, but has not addressed the **sanctions** power specifically, nor
> reconciled the split this Article identifies.² This Article fills that gap. *(the contribution, staked
> precisely against the nearest prior work rather than "scholars have written extensively" — the
> `hlr-preemption-check` discipline; footnote 2 distinguishes the nearest articles.)*
>
> The argument proceeds in three steps. Part I maps the subdelegation doctrine and the circuit split as it
> actually stands. Part II shows *why* private subdelegation of the sanctions power betrays the
> accountability principle that legitimates agency authority — the theory the prescription rests on.
> Part III argues that courts should adopt a bright-line bar on private subdelegation of coercive penalties
> and applies it to the hardest case, where the private body acts under close agency supervision. *(the
> roadmap previews the doctrine → theory → prescription arc, with the hardest case named — per
> `hlr-argument-structure`; short, not padded signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the HLR bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **A contestable thesis, stated early** — "the sanctions power is constitutionally non-subdelegable to
  private actors" is declarative and on page one, not buried (`hlr-thesis-and-contribution`).
- **Generalist stake named** — accountability, the nondelegation principle, the Appointments Clause — so a
  reader outside administrative law sees why it matters (`hlr-thesis-and-contribution` generalist test).
- **Contribution staked against the nearest work** — it names what prior scholarship did *not* address
  (the sanctions power, the split) rather than gesturing at a literature, reflecting a real preemption
  search (`hlr-preemption-check`).
- **The arc is previewed** — doctrine (Part I) → theory/diagnosis (Part II) → prescription tested on the
  hardest case (Part III), exactly the structure `hlr-argument-structure` rewards.
- **Argument in the text, support in footnotes** — the split, the distinguishing of prior work, and the
  authorities are carried by footnotes 1-2, keeping the text a clean argument
  (`hlr-writing-style` text/footnote division; `hlr-footnotes-and-cite-check` pull-readiness).
- **No throat-clearing** — the "the law is vast and much debated" warm-up is gone; the roadmap is three
  steps that track the argument, not four topics.
