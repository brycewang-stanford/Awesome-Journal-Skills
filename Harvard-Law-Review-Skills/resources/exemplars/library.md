## How to use this library

HLR is a **generalist, student-edited** flagship. Each piece below earns its place by making an
**original, normative legal claim** of broad importance and supporting it with a disciplined argument and
a heavy footnote apparatus (see [`../../skills/hlr-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md`](../../skills/hlr-thesis-and-contribution/SKILL.md)
and [`../../skills/hlr-argument-structure/SKILL.md`](../../skills/hlr-argument-structure/SKILL.md)). Pick
the row closest to your **field × type**, then study how it (1) states a contestable claim early and
(2) builds doctrine → theory → prescription. Run the self-check before calling your piece "HLR-shaped."

## By field × type

### Privacy / common-law innovation (the founding example of a normative claim)

- **Warren & Brandeis, "The Right to Privacy," Harvard Law Review, Vol. 4, No. 5 (Dec. 15, 1890), pp. 193-220.**
  Verified: JSTOR (`jstor.org/stable/1321160`); corroborated by multiple archives. Often called one of the
  most influential essays in American law.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** it does not survey existing privacy law — there was none. It argues, from
    scattered common-law precedents, that the law *should* recognize a "right to be let alone." A textbook
    **normative reconstruction**: doctrine (scattered precedents) → theory (an underlying principle of
    inviolate personality) → prescription (recognize the new right).
  - **Self-check:** does your piece argue for an outcome the law has not yet reached, built up from
    authority, rather than merely cataloging the current rule?

### Constitutional theory (the discipline-defining article)

- **Wechsler, "Toward Neutral Principles of Constitutional Law," Harvard Law Review, Vol. 73, No. 1 (Nov. 1959), p. 1.**
  Verified: JSTOR (`jstor.org/stable/i257346`, Vol. 73 No. 1, Nov. 1959); widely cited as among the most
  influential constitutional-law articles ever written. (An expanded version of Wechsler's Holmes Lecture.)
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a **normative-theoretical** claim — that constitutional adjudication must
    rest on principles that transcend the immediate result — pressed against the hardest cases. The
    argument is the whole piece; the doctrine serves the theory, not the reverse.
  - **Self-check:** does your theory *generate* a standard others can apply, and do you test it on the case
    that most threatens it?

### The Supreme Court Foreword (the flagship's signature genre)

- **Bridges, "The Supreme Court, 2021 Term — Foreword: Race in the Roberts Court," Harvard Law Review, Vol. 136, No. 1 (Nov. 2022), p. 23.**
  Verified: Harvard Law Review site (`harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-136/foreword-race-in-the-roberts-court/`)
  and the official PDF (`136-Harv.-L.-Rev.-23`); corroborated by Berkeley Law and Yale Law coverage.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the **invited** November Foreword — a unifying theory of a Supreme Court
    Term, anchored on a keystone case (here, *Dobbs*). A very long, heavily footnoted piece that models how
    the Foreword turns a Term's decisions into a single argument. (Invited; do not pitch the Foreword as a
    regular submission.)
  - **Self-check (for an invited Term piece):** does your reading unify the Term around one defensible
    theory rather than annotating each case separately?

## Omitted for lack of HLR verification (do NOT attribute to HLR without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** because they
were placed in a *sibling* journal, not HLR. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Warren & Brandeis, "The Right to Privacy" — the 1890 piece *is* HLR (listed above).** Do not confuse it
  with Brandeis's later judicial writing or with William Prosser's **"Privacy," 48 Cal. L. Rev. 383 (1960)**,
  which appeared in the **California Law Review**, not HLR.
- **Hart, "The Power of Congress to Limit the Jurisdiction of Federal Courts: An Exercise in Dialectic" (1953)**
  — verify carefully; commonly cited HLR jurisdiction scholarship exists, but confirm volume/page before
  attributing any specific Hart piece to HLR rather than to a Hart-Wechsler casebook or another venue. 待核实.
- **"The Path of the Law" (Holmes, 1897)** — appeared in the **Harvard Law Review** *as a published address*
  in some reprints, but is frequently cited from collected works; confirm the HLR citation (10 Harv. L. Rev.
  457) before relying on it, and do not conflate the address with later anthology reprints. 待核实.
- **Bork, "Neutral Principles and Some First Amendment Problems" (1971)** — verified to be in the **Indiana
  Law Journal** 47(1):1, **not HLR** — a common confusion with Wechsler's HLR "Neutral Principles" piece
  listed above. Do not conflate the two.
- **"The Forms and Limits of Adjudication" (Fuller)** — appeared in **Harvard Law Review** (92 Harv. L.
  Rev. 353, 1978) as a posthumously published piece; verify the exact citation before relying on it. 待核实.

Before adding any new piece to this library, confirm it on `harvardlawreview.org` or JSTOR with
volume/issue/page, cross-check a second source, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
Never assert a current HLR editor's name — the masthead turns over annually (see `../official-source-map.md`).
