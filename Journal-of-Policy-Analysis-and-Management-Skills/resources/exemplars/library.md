## How to use this library

JPAM is the **cross-disciplinary policy-analysis flagship**: each paper below earns its place by (1)
credibly identifying the effect of a real policy or program and (2) drawing a decision-relevant
implication legible across economics, political science, and public management (see
[`../../skills/jpam-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jpam-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/jpam-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jpam-research-design/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **field × method** is closest to yours, then study how it defends identification and states the
policy takeaway. Run the self-check before claiming your paper is "JPAM-shaped."

## By method

### Regression discontinuity (education — teacher policy)

- **Dee & Wyckoff, "Incentives, Selection, and Teacher Performance: Evidence from IMPACT," JPAM 2015, 34(2):267–297.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v34y2015i2p267-297.html` + Wiley (doi:10.1002/pam.21818).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses the performance-rating thresholds of a real teacher-evaluation system
    (DCPS IMPACT) as a regression discontinuity to identify how dismissal threats and retention incentives
    change teacher behavior — the design *is* the policy lever. A model of evaluating an administrative
    program on its own thresholds (see `jpam-research-design`: estimand at the cutoff).
  - **Self-check:** does your RD exploit a *real* policy threshold, with density and continuity tests, and
    do you state that the estimate is local to that threshold?

### Regression kink design (labor & family policy)

- **Bana, Bedard & Rossin-Slater, "The Impacts of Paid Family Leave Benefits: Regression Kink Evidence from California Administrative Data," JPAM 2020, 39(4):888–929.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v39y2020i4p888-929.html` + Wiley (doi:10.1002/pam.22242).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses the kink in California's benefit formula and linked administrative data
    to identify how benefit *generosity* affects leave-taking and later labor-market outcomes — a
    policy-parameter question answered with a credible quasi-experimental design on restricted data.
  - **Self-check:** if your policy varies at a kink in a benefit/tax formula, do you use the kink for
    identification and document the administrative data and its access?

### Difference-in-differences (health & labor — minimum wage)

- **Wehby, Dave & Kaestner, "Effects of the Minimum Wage on Infant Health," JPAM 2020, 39(2):411–443.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v39y2020i2p411-443.html` + Wiley (doi:10.1002/pam.22174).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** evaluates a high-salience labor policy (the minimum wage) on a downstream
    health outcome (infant health) using variation in state minimum wages — bridging labor and health
    policy, exactly the cross-field reach JPAM rewards (see `jpam-literature-positioning`).
  - **Self-check:** does your DiD connect a policy in one field to an outcome a *different* policy audience
    cares about, with pre-trends shown?

### Difference-in-differences (health & crime — Medicaid)

- **Vogler, "Access to Healthcare and Criminal Behavior: Evidence from the ACA Medicaid Expansions," JPAM 2020, 39(4):1166–1213.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v39y2020i4p1166-1213.html` + Wiley.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses staggered state ACA Medicaid expansions to identify a spillover of a
    *health* policy onto *crime* — a textbook cross-field policy-evaluation question with a clear decision
    relevance (the budgetary and public-safety case for expansion).
  - **Self-check:** does your evaluation surface a policy-relevant *spillover* an evaluator focused on the
    direct outcome would miss?

### Within-study comparison / methods for policy analysis

- **Cook, Shadish & Wong, "Three Conditions Under Which Experiments and Observational Studies Produce Comparable Causal Estimates: New Findings from Within-Study Comparisons," JPAM 2008, 27(4):724–750.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v27y2008i4p724-750.html` + Wiley.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a *Methods for Policy Analysis*–style contribution that asks when
    non-experimental program evaluations recover the experimental benchmark — directly relevant to every
    policy analyst weighing a quasi-experimental design (see `jpam-research-design`).
  - **Self-check:** if your contribution is methodological, is it framed around what it changes for
    *policy evaluators*, not only for econometricians?

### Meta-analysis of a design (methods)

- **Calonico, Cattaneo & Farrell, "The Internal and External Validity of the Regression Discontinuity Design: A Meta-Analysis of 15 Within-Study Comparisons," JPAM 2018, 37(2):403–429.**
  Verified: `ideas.repec.org/a/wly/jpamgt/v37y2018i2p403-429.html` + Wiley.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** synthesizes within-study comparisons to assess when the RD design — a
    workhorse of policy evaluation — recovers experimental estimates; a methods contribution pitched at the
    policy-analysis community's most-used quasi-experimental tool.
  - **Self-check:** does your methods paper speak to the validity of a design policy analysts already rely
    on, with practical guidance?

## Omitted for lack of JPAM verification (do NOT attribute to JPAM without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible, famous policy papers were **excluded** after
web search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not JPAM. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Ludwig & Miller, "Does Head Start Improve Children's Life Chances? Evidence from a Regression
  Discontinuity Design" (2007)** — verified to be in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 122(1):159–208,
  **not JPAM**. A very common policy-evaluation misattribution.
- **Dobbie & Fryer, "Are High-Quality Schools Enough to Increase Achievement Among the Poor? Evidence from
  the Harlem Children's Zone" (2011)** — verified to be in *AEJ: Applied Economics* 3(3):158–187,
  **not JPAM**.
- **Hoynes & Schanzenbach, "Consumption Responses to In-Kind Transfers: Evidence from the Introduction of
  the Food Stamp Program" (2009)** — verified to be in *AEJ: Applied Economics* 1(4):109–139, **not JPAM**.
- **Almond, Hoynes & Schanzenbach, "Inside the War on Poverty: The Impact of Food Stamps on Birth
  Outcomes" (2011)** — verified to be in the *Review of Economics and Statistics* 93(2):387–403,
  **not JPAM**.
- **Neumark & Wascher minimum-wage/EITC interaction work** — much of it appears in economics field outlets
  (e.g., *ILR Review*, NBER) rather than JPAM; confirm the venue before citing as JPAM.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on Wiley Online Library
(`onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15206688`) with volume/issue/pages, cross-check the RePEc `wly:jpamgt`
record, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
