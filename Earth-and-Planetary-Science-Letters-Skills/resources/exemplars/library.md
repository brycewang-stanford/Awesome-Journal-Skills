## How to use this library

Find the row nearest your sub-field and contribution type, read how that paper converts a
measurement, calibration, or model into a **portable, process-level claim** — the EPSL bar (see
[`../../skills/epsl-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/epsl-topic-selection/SKILL.md)) — then ask
its self-check question of your own draft.

## Geochemistry & petrology

- **Watson & Harrison (1983), "Zircon saturation revisited: temperature and composition effects in a
  variety of crustal magma types," EPSL 64, 295–304.** DOI 10.1016/0012-821X(83)90211-X (verified).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a compact experimental calibration that became a universal tool — the
    zircon-saturation thermometer. The experiments are narrow; the claim (any crustal melt's zircon
    behavior is predictable from T and composition) is field-wide.
  - **Self-check:** does your calibration/measurement give *other people* a tool, or only describe
    your own samples?

- **Hofmann (1988), "Chemical differentiation of the Earth: the relationship between mantle,
  continental crust, and oceanic crust," EPSL 90, 297–314.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** turns trace-element systematics into a framework for how the silicate Earth
    differentiated — a synthesis whose organizing quantities (compatibility ordering, reservoir
    complementarity) the field still uses.
  - **Self-check:** does your interpretation give the community a *framework*, or restate reservoir
    labels?

- **Blichert-Toft & Albarède (1997), "The Lu-Hf isotope geochemistry of chondrites and the evolution
  of the mantle-crust system," EPSL 148, 243–258.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** nails a reference parameter (the chondritic Lu-Hf frame) that every εHf value depends
    on — infrastructure-grade geochemistry with journal-wide consequences.
  - **Self-check:** if your paper revises a reference value, do you show what downstream
    interpretations change?

- **Bouvier, Vervoort & Patchett (2008), "The Lu–Hf and Sm–Nd isotopic composition of CHUR:
  constraints from unequilibrated chondrites…," EPSL 273, 48–57.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** the modern re-anchoring of the same reference frame — a model of how re-measuring a
    "known" constant with better traceability is letters-worthy when the constant underpins a field.
  - **Self-check:** is your improved precision *decisive* for some interpretation, or just smaller
    error bars?

## Geochronology & surface processes

- **Lal (1991), "Cosmic ray labeling of erosion surfaces: in situ nuclide production rates and
  erosion models," EPSL 104, 424–439.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** the theoretical foundation that made in-situ cosmogenic nuclides a dating and
    erosion-rate tool — method-establishing physics with an explicit user manual for a community.
  - **Self-check:** does your methods advance come with the framework others need to apply it?

- **von Blanckenburg (2005), "The control mechanisms of erosion and weathering at basin scale from
  cosmogenic nuclides in river sediment," EPSL 237, 462–479.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** a synthesis that converts a decade of scattered basin studies into process-level rules
    for what sets erosion rates — the shape of a strong (invited-review-style) EPSL synthesis.
  - **Self-check:** does your synthesis extract *controls*, or catalogue rates?

- **Cheng, Edwards, Shen et al. (2013), "Improvements in 230Th dating, 230Th and 234U half-life
  values, and U–Th isotopic measurements by multi-collector ICP-MS," EPSL 371–372, 82–91.** (verified)
  - **Why:** re-determines half-lives and tightens the U-Th clock used across paleoclimate — the
    exemplar of EPSL's methods-transparency culture: constants, standards, and uncertainties treated
    as first-class results.
  - **Self-check:** are your constants and calibrations reported so your ages remain portable when
    values shift?

## Geophysics & geodynamics

- **Hirth & Kohlstedt (1996), "Water in the oceanic upper mantle: implications for rheology, melt
  extraction and the evolution of the lithosphere," EPSL 144, 93–108.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** couples experimental rheology to a planetary-scale consequence (how dehydration
    stiffens the oceanic plate) — the classic EPSL move of one mechanism reorganizing several
    observations.
  - **Self-check:** does your mechanism explain observations from *outside* the dataset that
    motivated it?

- **Dziewonski, Lekic & Romanowicz (2010), "Mantle Anchor Structure: an argument for bottom-up
  tectonics," EPSL 299, 69–79.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** reads global tomography for its lowest-order, longest-lived structure and proposes a
    directional hypothesis for mantle dynamics — interpretation bold enough to be testable.
  - **Self-check:** does your structural result end in a hypothesis someone can falsify?

## Planetary science & cosmochemistry

- **Zhong & Zuber (2001), "Degree-1 mantle convection and the crustal dichotomy on Mars," EPSL 189,
  75–84.** (verified)
  - **Why:** a focused dynamical calculation offered as the mechanism for a first-order planetary
    observable (the Martian hemispheric dichotomy) — planetary scope executed in letters format.
  - **Self-check:** does your model target a specific, named observable of the body?

- **Marty (2012), "The origins and concentrations of water, carbon, nitrogen and noble gases on
  Earth," EPSL 313–314, 56–66.** (confirm on ScienceDirect)
  - **Why:** budgets Earth's volatiles from isotopic systematics into a coherent delivery story —
    cosmochemistry, mantle geochemistry, and atmosphere science made mutually accountable.
  - **Self-check:** do your budget's inputs and uncertainties trace to stated reservoir values?
