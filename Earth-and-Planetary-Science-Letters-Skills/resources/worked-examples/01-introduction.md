# Worked Example: An EPSL-Style Abstract + Introduction (before → after)

The EPSL house style this rewrite targets: a **process-forward** opening (the question belongs to the
planet, not the field area), the **headline number stated early with a defined uncertainty**,
**cross-disciplinary legibility** (a seismologist can follow the geochemistry and vice versa), and
**letters-format economy** — the traceability chain is signaled, then delegated to the supplement.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Sediment transit time sets a 5-Myr resolution limit on
detrital records of mountain building."* Fictional system: a small orogen–foreland pair
("the K system") where bedrock thermochronology and basin deposition can be dated in the same
routing system.

---

## Before (regional, inventory-first — a typical first draft)

> The K Basin is a foreland basin located adjacent to the K Mountains. Its sedimentary fill has been
> the subject of several previous studies. In this study, we present 612 new detrital zircon U-Pb
> ages from six stratigraphic sections of the basin fill, together with 24 new apatite (U-Th)/He
> bedrock cooling ages from the adjacent range. The data were collected by LA-ICP-MS and analyzed
> using standard techniques. Age spectra were compared between sections. The implications of the new
> data for the exhumation history of the K Mountains and the provenance of the basin are discussed.

**What fails (against `epsl-writing-style` / `epsl-topic-selection`):**

- **Opens on the place, not a process** — the title-and-first-sentence pattern editors read as a
  regional case study, EPSL's canonical desk decline.
- **Inventory instead of a question** — grain counts and methods appear before any hypothesis;
  "implications are discussed" withholds the result the abstract exists to deliver.
- **No number, no uncertainty** — nothing here could be cited.
- **No portability** — a reader working on another orogen (or another planet's sediment routing) is
  given no reason to continue.
- **No rigor signals** — nothing about standards, replication, or how the two chronometers are made
  comparable (`epsl-data-analysis`).

---

## After (EPSL arc — process first, number early, rigor signaled)

> **Detrital mineral archives are read worldwide as if erosion were recorded in sediments
> instantly, yet the transit time from bedrock to basin has never been measured directly in a single
> routing system.** We date both ends of one conveyor — bedrock cooling in the source (apatite
> (U-Th)/He, n = 24) and the arrival of the matching age population downstream (612 detrital zircon
> U-Pb ages through six dated sections) — in the K system, where routing is confined and the
> stratigraphy is independently anchored. *(the process question + the discriminating design, in two
> sentences.)*
>
> The exhumation signal appears in the basin **5.0 ± 1.2 Myr (2σ)** after it is set in the bedrock.
> The lag is resolved against the sections' astrochronologically tied age model and is robust to how
> the detrital populations are unmixed (three methods agree within 0.4 Myr; full comparisons in the
> Supplementary Material). *(headline number with defined uncertainty; robustness signaled and
> delegated — letters economy.)*
>
> A transit time of this magnitude sets a resolution floor: tectonic events shorter than ~5 Myr
> cannot be read from detrital archives in systems of this scale, and apparent "delays" between
> collision and sedimentary response need not require tectonic explanations at all. Detrital records
> — on Earth, and in any planetary surface archive built by sediment routing — are low-pass filters
> with a measurable, correctable time constant. *(the portable consequence — every basin worker, and
> the planetary reader, must respond.)*

**Introduction close (the discriminating-test sentence, per `epsl-writing-style`):**

> Here we test whether sediment routing delays the detrital record by a resolvable interval, by
> measuring source cooling and basin arrival on the same age populations; a lag of zero (instant
> transfer) and a lag exceeding the orogenic cycle (archive decoupling) predict distinct age–height
> patterns at our ±30 kyr (illustrative) bed-level precision.

---

## Why the "after" clears the EPSL bar

- **Process-forward** — the question ("is the detrital record delayed?") is posed for all routing
  systems; the K system is the laboratory, not the subject (`epsl-topic-selection`: process, not place).
- **Headline number early, uncertainty defined** — `5.0 ± 1.2 Myr (2σ)` is citable from the abstract;
  σ-level stated per `epsl-data-analysis`.
- **Rigor signaled compactly** — n for both chronometers, the independent stratigraphic anchor, and
  the three-way unmixing robustness check are all named in one clause each, with detail routed to the
  supplement — the letters-format division of labor
  (`epsl-reporting-and-reproducibility`).
- **Cross-disciplinary legibility** — no unglossed lab jargon; the physical picture (a conveyor with
  a time constant, a low-pass filter) carries readers from geochronology to geodynamics to planetary
  surfaces.
- **A falsifiable close** — the introduction ends on the two hypotheses and the precision that
  separates them, the structure `epsl-review-process` predicts referees will audit.
- **Exhibit pairing** — the natural Figure 1 is a two-panel: the routing-system map with sections and
  bedrock sites (context), and the age–height plot with the lag interval shaded and 2σ envelopes
  drawn (`epsl-figures-and-tables`).
