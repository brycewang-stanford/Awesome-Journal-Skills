# Worked Example: An SMJ-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **SMJ empirical-paper introduction arc** that the three skills jointly require:
**name the conversation → state the theoretical gap → make the "our move" claim → preview design and
finding → land the contribution to *strategy theory*** — with the SMJ rule from `smj-writing-style` that
**a reader should know your contribution to strategy theory (not generic management) by the end of page
two**, framed for strategy scholars, with the advance — not the method — leading.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Borrowed Hands: How Alliance-Sourced Capabilities Bound the
Performance Value of In-House R&D."* Fictional setting: a panel of 612 medical-device firms (2008–2020)
in which some firms acquired a key manufacturing capability through alliances rather than building it
internally.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> The relationship between R&D and firm performance has been studied extensively in the strategic
> management literature. Many scholars have examined how firms build capabilities, and others have looked
> at alliances. In this paper, we assemble a panel of 612 medical-device firms and use a staggered
> difference-in-differences design with the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator, supplemented by
> two-stage least squares to address endogeneity, to study how in-house R&D and alliance activity relate
> to performance. We find several statistically significant effects. R&D is an important topic for
> managers and scholars alike. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes the data, Section 4
> presents the methods, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against the three skills):**

- **No named conversation.** "The literature has studied R&D extensively" is an annotated-bibliography
  opening — the exact anti-pattern in `smj-literature-positioning`. The reader cannot tell *whose* live
  debate this enters.
- **Leads with the estimator**, selling the method as if it were the substance — a named anti-pattern in
  both `smj-contribution-framing` and `smj-writing-style`.
- **Contribution is a list of findings** ("several statistically significant effects"), not a statement of
  what strategy scholars should now believe differently (`smj-contribution-framing`).
- **No strategy stakes.** Nothing ties the claim to competitive advantage or performance heterogeneity, so
  a reviewer can dismiss it as generic management — a core SMJ rejection trigger.
- **Relies on significance, not an economically meaningful magnitude** — SMJ disapproves of treating a
  significant coefficient as a contribution.
- **Throat-clearing + over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do
  (`smj-writing-style`).

---

## After (SMJ arc — conversation → gap → our move → design → contribution to strategy theory)

> Does building a capability in-house create more durable competitive advantage than *borrowing* the same
> capability through an alliance? The **resource-based and dynamic-capabilities conversation** has long
> held that internally developed resources are harder to imitate and therefore more performance-relevant
> than externally sourced ones. Yet recent work in this stream disagrees on whether alliance-sourced
> capabilities **substitute for** or **erode** the value of a firm's own R&D — and the field has largely
> assumed the two channels operate independently. *(names the conversation and the live disagreement —
> `smj-literature-positioning`.)*
>
> We argue this independence assumption is where the debate is stuck. Our move is to show that
> alliance-sourced capabilities are not merely a separate input but a **contingency that bounds** the
> performance value of in-house R&D: in-house R&D raises performance, but only when the firm has *not*
> outsourced the complementary capability, because outsourcing severs the integration through which R&D is
> converted into advantage. *(the theoretical gap is a contingency/boundary condition — the strong end of
> the gap-quality ladder — and the "our move" sentence is one line; `smj-literature-positioning` +
> `smj-contribution-framing`.)*
>
> We test this in a panel of 612 medical-device firms (2008–2020), using staggered differences in when
> firms moved a key manufacturing capability outside the firm boundary, with not-yet-outsourced firms as
> controls and an instrument for the outsourcing decision; we verify no differential pre-trends. *(design
> previewed in one paragraph — method is a tool, demoted from the lead; `smj-writing-style`.)*
>
> In-house R&D raises return on assets by **1.8 percentage points** among firms that kept the
> complementary capability internal, but its effect is **statistically and economically indistinguishable
> from zero** among firms that outsourced it — a difference too large to attribute to noise and robust to
> the endogeneity of the outsourcing choice. *(headline finding with an economically meaningful magnitude
> and units, not just significance; `smj-contribution-framing`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to **re-specify when in-house R&D is a source of advantage**: the theory should not
> predict an R&D–performance link in general, but only conditional on the firm retaining the complementary
> capabilities needed to integrate it. This bounds the prevailing resource-based claim and reconciles the
> stream's conflicting findings by identifying outsourcing as the moderator that separates them. *(states
> what strategy scholars should now believe differently — a boundary-condition + reconciliation
> contribution to *strategy theory*, tied to competitive advantage; `smj-contribution-framing`.)* For
> managers, the implication is concrete: outsourcing a capability can quietly strip the strategic value out
> of R&D the firm continues to fund. *(a real practitioner takeaway that feeds the separate managerial
> summary — it does not replace the theory claim.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the boundary-condition argument; Section 3 describes
> the design and data; Section 4 reports the results and robustness; Section 5 discusses implications for
> the capabilities literature. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the SMJ bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Names the conversation and the live disagreement** in the first paragraph, organizing prior work by
  *position* (substitute vs. erode), not chronology (`smj-literature-positioning`: conversation principle;
  prior work grouped by finding).
- **Gap is theoretical, at the strong end of the ladder** — a contingency/boundary condition that
  reconciles inconsistent findings — not "not yet studied in medical devices"
  (`smj-literature-positioning` gap-quality ladder).
- **Contribution leads with the theoretical advance, stated as what scholars should now believe
  differently** ("re-specify *when* in-house R&D is a source of advantage"), tied explicitly to
  competitive advantage, and right-sized — it *bounds* rather than "overturns" RBV
  (`smj-contribution-framing` framing rules 1, 4, 5).
- **Rests on an economically meaningful magnitude** (1.8 pp vs. ≈0), not a bare significance claim
  (`smj-contribution-framing`; consistent with SMJ's stated disapproval of treating significance as a
  contribution — verify the policy in `official-source-map.md`).
- **Method demoted to a tool**, introduced only where the design is, never as the lead — avoids the shared
  "selling the estimator" anti-pattern (`smj-contribution-framing` + `smj-writing-style`).
- **Contribution visible by page two**, argument-first sentences, proportionate hedging, and a managerial
  takeaway that feeds — but does not substitute for — the separate **managerial summary** SMJ requires
  alongside the research summary (`smj-writing-style` house conventions; confirm the dual-summary and
  page-limit rules in `official-source-map.md`).
- **Consistency hook:** the same boundary-condition claim must reappear, unchanged, in the theory section
  and the discussion — drift between intro and discussion is a common reviewer complaint
  (`smj-contribution-framing`: intro = theory = discussion).
