# Worked Example: Reframing an Introduction for the Annual Review of Psychology

This is a **fictional** example written in the ARPsych house style. It does not describe a
real paper, author, or finding. It shows how an invited review's opening should read.

## Before (reads like a primary paper / annotated bibliography)

> Self-control has been studied for decades. Mischel ran the marshmallow studies; Baumeister
> proposed ego depletion; later work questioned whether depletion replicates. Many studies
> find effects and some do not. In this review we summarize this large literature and discuss
> several theories, presenting studies in roughly chronological order.

## Diagnosis

- **No organizing question** — it names a topic ("self-control"), not the question the review answers.
- **No spine** — "roughly chronological order" is a list, not a framework (see `arpsych-organizing-framework`).
- **Vote-counting** — "many find effects and some do not" instead of weighing by credibility and replication (see `arpsych-comprehensiveness-and-balance`).
- **No forward agenda** — promises a summary, not an argument or open questions.
- **Wrong register** — reads like a primary-paper intro, not an authoritative survey for a broad readership.

## After (ARPsych voice: organizing question → spine → contribution → forward)

> Why do measures of self-control predict life outcomes while laboratory manipulations of it
> replicate so unevenly? We argue this apparent paradox dissolves once self-control is treated
> not as a single resource but as **three separable processes** — attention deployment,
> goal valuation, and response inhibition — each with its own measures, neural signatures, and
> evidentiary status. Organizing the literature around these processes reconciles the durable
> predictive findings (largely attention and valuation) with the fragile depletion effects
> (largely inhibition), and it exposes where the field's evidence is strong, contested, or
> absent. We weigh studies by design and replication rather than by count, and we close with
> the process-level questions — and the diverse-sample, preregistered designs — most likely to
> move the field next.

## Why the "after" works

- States an **organizing question**, then the **three-process spine** that answers it.
- The spine is **reconciling** (explains why predictive and experimental results diverge) and **generative** (points to open process-level questions).
- Confidence is **calibrated** ("strong, contested, or absent") rather than vote-counted.
- Closes **forward**, as ARPsych reviews are expected to.
- Reads for a **broad psychology readership**, not just self-control insiders.
