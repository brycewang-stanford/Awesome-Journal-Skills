# CSCW Writing Style

A CSCW paper is judged by readers from several intellectual traditions at once —
ethnographers, statisticians, system builders, social theorists — inside a journal
process where the same reviewers may read the paper twice. The prose must therefore
do two unusual things: make each tradition's part legible to the others, and hold up
under a *second*, more skeptical read during Revise and Resubmit.

## Length: no cap, but a scale

CSCW imposes no formal page limit. The 2026-cycle CFP instead put weight on a band:
papers under roughly **5,000 words** or over roughly **12,000 words** attract extra
scrutiny, and a paper whose length is incommensurate with its contribution can be
desk rejected. Treat length as a claim you are making: every additional section
asserts that the contribution needs it. Practical calibration:

- Write the paper the contribution needs, then defend the word count in one
  sentence. If you cannot, cut until you can.
- Long ≠ thorough. A 14,000-word manuscript signals unedited fieldnotes more often
  than it signals depth; move protocol detail to appendices.
- Short ≠ crisp. Under ~5,000 words, reviewers will ask whether the study is a
  workshop paper wearing a journal costume.

## The group-level sentence

The single most common style failure at this venue is drift back to the individual
user. Audit every claim sentence:

| Drifted (individual) | Anchored (collective) |
| --- | --- |
| "Users struggled to trust the bot's suggestions" | "Teams routed the bot's suggestions through a senior member before acting on them" |
| "Participants felt burnout" | "The moderation team rotated exposure to harmful content to distribute burnout" |
| "People adopted the feature" | "The community renegotiated its norms around what the feature made visible" |
| "The tool improved efficiency" | "The tool redistributed articulation work from editors to contributors" |

If a finding cannot be written in the right-hand register, either the analysis
stopped too early or the paper belongs at a different venue (`cscw-topic-selection`).

## Concepts, theory, and participant voice

- **Name your concept once, define it, then use it consistently.** CSCW's canon is
  full of load-bearing vocabulary (awareness, articulation work, place vs. space).
  A new concept must be defined against — not merely alongside — the existing terms.
- **Theory must produce consequences.** Every theoretical frame invoked should
  change either the analysis or the design discussion. A framework cited in the
  introduction and never touched again reads as ornament and will be flagged.
- **Quote people as evidence, not decoration.** Each participant quote should carry
  analytic weight the surrounding prose then does; a quote that merely restates the
  preceding sentence is filler. For sensitive communities, paraphrase or lightly
  alter quotes when verbatim text is reverse-searchable, and say that you did so.
- **Report your positionality where it matters** — in fieldwork with communities
  the authors belong to or hold power over, a sentence about the researchers'
  relationship to the setting is analytic information, not confession.

## Calibration under re-review

Because R&R returns to the same reviewers, overclaiming is a double cost: it draws
the objection in round one and the *check* in round two. Write claims that a hostile
re-reader can verify against your own data sections:

- Scope every generalization: "in the six communities we studied," "among
  moderators of crisis-support forums."
- Separate what participants said, what traces show, and what you interpret — with
  different verbs ("reported," "exhibited," "we read this as").
- Put limitations where they belong analytically, not as a ritual final paragraph.

## First-page audit

```text
[Protagonist]  Is a group/community/organization the subject of the first
               claim sentence? yes / no
[Stake]        Does ¶2 say what breaks if the field keeps misunderstanding
               this — or just that "little is known"?
[Concept]      Is there one named, reusable idea? Can a reviewer paraphrase
               it in a sentence?
[Methods+ethics] Are counts, settings, consent posture, and data-sensitivity
               handling visible by end of the introduction?
[Length claim] Word count: <n>. One-sentence defense if outside 5,000-12,000.
```

Run the audit again on the revised manuscript before any R&R resubmission — the
introduction is where reviewers first test whether the revision was real.
