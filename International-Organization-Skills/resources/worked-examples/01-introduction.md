# Worked Example: An IO-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **IO introduction arc** assembled from this pack's skills:
**international question → live IR debate the paper enters → generalizable theory + international mechanism
→ design that adjudicates the strongest rival → headline evidence → portable contribution & scope
conditions → brief roadmap**.

The IO-specific bar layered on top of a generic empirical intro:

- The **international (cross-border) phenomenon must be load-bearing** — strip it and the paper collapses
  (`io-topic-selection`).
- By the **end of the introduction** the reader knows the international question, the **generalizable IR
  theory**, the evidence, and why it matters for international politics, **written to read across
  paradigms** (rationalist / constructivist / IPE / security) with jargon and acronyms defined
  (`io-writing-style`).
- The contribution is staked as a **move in a recognizable IR debate**, not a citation pile
  (`io-literature-positioning`), and the design names the **single strongest rival international
  explanation** and how it is ruled out (`io-research-design`).
- Because IO is **double-blind**, the author's own prior work is referred to in the **third person**.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Designed to Bind? Dispute-Settlement Teeth and Compliance in
Preferential Trade Agreements."* Fictional setting: a (made-up) dataset of 180 preferential trade
agreements (PTAs) signed 1990–2020, some with binding third-party dispute settlement and some without.

---

## Before (buries the IR contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> The study of international institutions has generated a large literature. Many scholars have examined
> trade agreements, and there is ongoing debate about whether they matter. In this paper, we assemble a
> new dataset of 180 preferential trade agreements and use a staggered difference-in-differences design
> with two-way fixed effects to estimate the effect of dispute-settlement provisions on compliance. We
> also run instrumental-variables and matching specifications as robustness checks. As we showed in our
> earlier work, institutional design varies widely across agreements. Trade cooperation is an important
> topic. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes the data, Section 4 presents the empirical
> strategy, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against the IO skills):**

- **No international question and no IR theory on page one.** A reader from another tradition cannot tell
  what general claim about international politics is at stake (`io-writing-style`: contribution stated by
  end of intro; `io-topic-selection`: generalizable theory).
- **Leads with method and a robustness inventory** ("staggered DiD with TWFE… IV and matching") instead
  of the argument — and **naive TWFE on staggered treatment** is a named design anti-pattern
  (`io-research-design`).
- **"As we showed in our earlier work"** breaks **double-blind anonymity** (`io-writing-style`: refer to
  own work in the third person).
- **No live IR debate named** — "ongoing debate about whether they matter" is a literature dump, not a
  position in a contest (`io-literature-positioning`).
- **No strongest-rival explanation** named, so the design's identifying leverage is invisible
  (`io-research-design` adjudication test).
- **Throat-clearing** ("important topic") and an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's work.
- **Acronym (PTA) never spelled out** on first use (`io-writing-style`: spell out IGO/dataset acronyms).

---

## After (IO arc — international question + IR theory + adjudicating design, contribution early)

> **When does the legal design of an international institution actually change state behavior, rather
> than merely ratify what states intended to do anyway?** We show that preferential trade agreements
> (PTAs) equipped with *binding third-party dispute settlement* raise members' compliance with their
> trade commitments by 11 percentage points, while PTAs that merely add consultative bodies do not — and
> we argue this is because binding teeth let governments generate a **credible, externally visible
> commitment** their domestic and foreign audiences can hold them to. *(international question +
> generalizable claim + headline magnitude, in the first breath; acronym spelled out.)*
>
> This speaks directly to a live IR debate: do international institutions **constrain** states, or do
> they merely **screen** — selecting in those already inclined to cooperate? *(the debate the paper
> enters, legible across rationalist and institutionalist traditions.)* The screening view predicts that
> design "teeth" should be epiphenomenal, a label states accept only when they already plan to comply.
> Our contribution is to identify a **design feature that constrains over and above selection**, which
> reframes institutional teeth as a commitment technology rather than a sorting signal. *(contribution
> stated early, as a move against the named rival account.)*
>
> The mechanism is general and travels beyond trade: a state that delegates adjudication to a binding
> third party raises the **political and reputational cost of defection** for its leaders, making the
> commitment self-enforcing where a non-binding pledge is cheap talk. *(international mechanism — who,
> why, under what constraint — portable to investment treaties, arms-control regimes, and human-rights
> bodies.)* The argument holds where leaders are accountable to audiences that observe rulings; it should
> weaken where rulings are opaque or audiences are absent. *(scope conditions — portability, not
> universality.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because design is **chosen**: states that expect to comply may be
> exactly the ones that accept binding settlement, which is the screening story a referee raises first.
> We exploit the **staggered roll-out** of a model treaty template that diffused binding clauses across
> agreements for reasons tied to a regional secretariat's drafting calendar rather than to any single
> dyad's compliance plans, and estimate the effect with a heterogeneity-robust staggered
> difference-in-differences estimator, clustering on both members of each dyad. *(design built to
> adjudicate the rival, with the dyadic-dependence trap handled.)* **If screening rather than constraint
> drove our result, compliance should jump *before* binding clauses take effect and should not vary with
> whether rulings are publicly observable; instead, it rises only *after* adoption and only where rulings
> are public.** *(the adjudication sentence — what the data would look like under the rival vs. under our
> argument.)*
>
> Compliance rises by **11 percentage points** (off a base of 68%) in the three years after a binding
> mechanism activates, with no differential pre-trend, and the effect is concentrated where rulings are
> publicly reported — consistent with the audience-cost mechanism and not with selection. *(headline
> evidence restated with the mechanism test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the commitment theory and its observable
> implications; Section 3 describes the PTA data and the staggered design; Section 4 reports the
> compliance results and the public-rulings mechanism test; Section 5 discusses scope and portability to
> other institutions. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the IO bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **International question + generalizable IR theory stated by end of intro** — the commitment-vs-screening
  claim is portable across institutions, and the headline magnitude (11 pp) appears immediately with
  units (`io-topic-selection` fit test; `io-writing-style` IR-contribution-by-end-of-intro).
- **Reads across traditions** — the question is legible to an institutionalist, a rationalist, and an IPE
  scholar; "PTA" is spelled out on first use (`io-writing-style`: define jargon, spell out acronyms).
- **Positioned as a move in a live IR debate** ("constrain vs. screen"), engaging the rival account on
  its terms rather than piling cites (`io-literature-positioning`).
- **The international phenomenon is load-bearing** — the mechanism runs through delegation to a *binding
  international third party*; strip the international institution and there is no paper
  (`io-topic-selection`: international level a major cause, not a backdrop).
- **Design adjudicates the strongest rival** — the explicit "if the rival were true… instead…" sentence,
  plus a **heterogeneity-robust staggered estimator** (not naive TWFE) and **clustering on both dyad
  members** to handle dyadic non-independence (`io-research-design`).
- **Mechanism made observable internationally** — the public-rulings test ties the audience-cost story to
  something the data can show or fail to show (`io-theory-building`: observable implications).
- **Scope conditions stated** — accountable leaders + observable rulings — so the claim is portable, not
  universal (`io-theory-building`).
- **Method demoted to a tool** and self-citation removed, preserving **double-blind anonymity**
  (`io-writing-style`).
