## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × question** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes an
international phenomenon load-bearing and attaches it to a **generalizable IR theory** — the IO bar (see
[`../../skills/io-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md), and
[`../../skills/io-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each, ask the
self-check question before claiming your paper is "IO-shaped."

## By method

### Constructivist theory that reframes the field (IR theory)

- **Wendt, "Anarchy Is What States Make of It: The Social Construction of Power Politics," IO 1992, 46(2):391–425.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + RePEc `cup/intorg/v46y1992i02p391-425`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a single conceptual move — that anarchy's effects are constructed through
    interaction, not fixed by structure — reorganized how the field argues about identity and power
    politics. The international system *is* the object; the claim is maximally portable across cases. A
    model of the constructivist mode in [`../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** does your conceptual claim about world politics travel beyond any one case, and does
    it engage the rationalist baseline it improves on?

### Formal / rationalist theory of conflict (IR theory)

- **Fearon, "Rationalist Explanations for War," IO 1995, 49(3):379–414.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + RePEc `cup/intorg/v49y1995i03p379-414`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent bargaining argument isolating three general mechanisms
    (private information, commitment problems, issue indivisibility) under which rational states still go
    to war — portable comparative-statics intuition that the whole conflict field now uses. The canonical
    "small model, enormous lesson" formal IO paper; pairs with the formal mode in
    [`../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-theory-building/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** can your formal mechanism be stated plainly, motivated by a real IR puzzle, and
    recognized across many dyads — not tuned to one case?

### Theory of norm change (constructivist, with observable implications)

- **Finnemore & Sikkink, "International Norm Dynamics and Political Change," IO 1998, 52(4):887–917.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core record + RePEc `cup/intorg/v52y1998i04p887-917`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns a fuzzy idea (norms matter) into a structured, *observable* theory —
    a three-stage life cycle (emergence → cascade → internalization) with distinct mechanisms — so a
    constructivist claim becomes testable against the rationalist baseline.
  - **Self-check:** have you made your ideational mechanism observable at the international level, with
    what we should and should *not* see if it operates?

### Rational-design theory of institutions (institutionalist / IPE)

- **Koremenos, Lipson & Snidal, "The Rational Design of International Institutions," IO 2001, 55(4):761–799.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core record + RePEc `cup/intorg` (vol. 55, 2001).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a generalizable framework — five design dimensions (membership, scope,
    centralization, control, flexibility) explained by four features of the underlying cooperation
    problem — that gives the field a portable vocabulary for *why institutions look the way they do*,
    rather than a history of one IGO. (Spell out IGO = intergovernmental organization on first use.)
  - **Self-check:** does your account of institutional form generalize across issue areas, or only
    describe a single institution?

### Quantitative IPE with dyadic data (causal-ish, regime type × cooperation)

- **Mansfield, Milner & Rosendorff, "Why Democracies Cooperate More: Electoral Control and International Trade Agreements," IO 2002, 56(3):477–513.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + RePEc `cup/intorg/v56y2002i03p477-513`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an explicit domestic-to-international mechanism (electoral accountability →
    credibility → trade agreements) tested on dyadic data, where the international outcome (forming a
    preferential trade agreement) is the thing explained. Shows IO publishing quantitative IPE when the
    theory is general and the international level is load-bearing. Mind dyadic non-independence — see
    [`../../skills/io-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-research-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** is your unit (dyad / directed dyad / state-year) matched to the theory, and is the
    international outcome — not a domestic one — what you actually explain?

### Quantitative measurement / scaling of an international institution (descriptive + inferential)

- **Voeten, "Clashes in the Assembly," IO 2000, 54(2):185–215.**
  Verified: Cambridge Core article page + RePEc `cup/intorg/v54y2000i02p185-215`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses scaling of UN General Assembly roll-call votes to *measure* the
    structure of global conflict and show it is largely one-dimensional and stable — a measurement
    exercise that earns its place by answering a general question about post-Cold-War international
    alignment, not by being "the first to code the data."
  - **Self-check:** does your measurement adjudicate a contested IR question, or is it a data exercise
    without an IR payoff (the named anti-pattern in [`../../skills/io-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/io-topic-selection/SKILL.md))?

## Omitted for sibling-venue confusion (do NOT attribute to IO)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after web search placed them in a
**different journal** — IO's siblings are the most common misattribution risk:

- **Fearon, "Domestic Political Audiences and the Escalation of International Disputes" (1994)** — verified
  in the *American Political Science Review* 88(3):577–592, **not IO**. (Fearon 1995 above *is* IO; do not
  conflate the two.)
- **Mansfield, Milner & Rosendorff, "Free to Trade: Democracies, Autocracies, and International Trade" (2000)**
  — verified in the *American Political Science Review* 94(2):305–321, **not IO**. (Their 2002 "Why
  Democracies Cooperate More" above *is* IO — same authors, different venues.)
- **Tomz & Weeks, "Public Opinion and the Democratic Peace" (2013)** — verified in the *American Political
  Science Review* 107(4):849–865, **not IO**.
- **Simmons & Hopkins, "The Constraining Power of International Treaties: Theory and Methods" (2005)** —
  verified in the *American Political Science Review* 99(4):623–631, **not IO**.
- **Hyde, "Catch Us If You Can: Election Monitoring and International Norm Diffusion" (2011)** — verified
  in the *American Journal of Political Science* 55(2), **not IO**; her natural-experiment "Observer
  Effect" (2007) is in *World Politics*, **not IO**.
- **Hafner-Burton & Montgomery, "Power Positions: International Organizations, Social Networks, and
  Conflict" (2006)** — verified in the *Journal of Conflict Resolution* 50(1):3–27, **not IO**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on
`cambridge.org/core/journals/international-organization` (article page with volume/issue) and update the
access-date header. **IO is NOT** *International Security*, *World Politics*, *International Studies
Quarterly*, *APSR*, or *AJPS* — when in doubt, omit.
