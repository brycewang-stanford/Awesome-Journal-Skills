# Worked Example: An ASR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **ASR introduction arc** that the pack's own skills require. ASR is sociology's
**general** flagship, so the introduction must do four things by the time it ends
(`asr-writing-style` → "front-load the contribution"; `asr-topic-selection` → the four-part fit test):

1. **The question**, stated plainly for a sociologist outside the subfield.
2. **The argument** — a named **mechanism and concept** that *travels* (`asr-theory-building`'s
   portability test), not just an association.
3. **The evidence** — what design adjudicates the leading rival account (`asr-research-design`'s
   adjudication sentence).
4. **Why it matters to sociology broadly** — the discipline-level stakes, with **scope conditions**.

ASR publishes quantitative, demographic, comparative-historical, ethnographic, network, and
computational work and judges each on its own terms. This example takes a **quantitative / demographic**
angle with strong theoretical framing — the combination the skills treat as the central ASR target.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Credential Gatekeeping and the Reproduction of Occupational
Closure: A Cohort Analysis of Licensing's Spread Across Mid-Skill Work."* Fictional setting: a panel
following several birth cohorts as an expanding set of mid-skill occupations adopts entry credentials.

---

## Before (subfield-insider, buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Occupational licensing has received increasing attention in the stratification literature in recent
> years. A number of studies have examined wage premia associated with licensed occupations, and
> debates continue about whether licensing raises wages. In this paper, we use data from a large
> longitudinal survey to examine the relationship between the spread of credential requirements and the
> labor-market outcomes of workers across several cohorts. We estimate a series of fixed-effects models
> and find that credential requirements are associated with differences in occupational attainment. This
> topic is understudied for mid-skill occupations specifically. The paper is organized as follows.
> Section 2 reviews the literature on licensing, Section 3 describes the data and variables, Section 4
> presents the models, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It speaks only to people already studying licensing; a cultural,
  organizational, or demographic sociologist sees no reason to read on (`asr-topic-selection`:
  "broad significance"; `asr-writing-style`: "reads past the subfield").
- **Descriptive, no mechanism.** "Is associated with differences" is the named anti-pattern in
  `asr-theory-building` ("a finding with no mechanism — 'X is associated with Y' and stop"). No
  **concept** is offered for other sociologists to carry elsewhere → fails the **portability test**.
- **"Understudied for X" as the contribution** — exactly the `asr-topic-selection` anti-pattern
  ("'not yet studied in setting X' as the whole contribution").
- **No adjudication.** The design is named ("fixed-effects models") but we are never told what rival
  account it rules out (`asr-research-design`).
- **Contribution buried** and an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's job
  (`asr-writing-style`).

---

## After (ASR arc — question + argument + evidence + broad stakes, contribution front-loaded)

> **Why does inequality between occupations harden even when the work itself, and the people doing it,
> change little?** We argue that one engine is *credential gatekeeping*: when an occupation adopts an
> entry credential, it converts a once-open job into a site of **social closure**, and that closure —
> not any change in the skill the work requires — is what reallocates opportunity and reproduces
> advantage across cohorts. *(question + argument, in the first breath; the concept, "credential
> gatekeeping," and the mechanism, social closure, are named immediately.)*
>
> The claim matters beyond licensing. Stratification scholarship has long asked how durable boundaries
> between groups are built and maintained; here we show a boundary being **manufactured in real time**,
> through an organizational act (adopting a credential) rather than through differences in human capital.
> If the mechanism holds, it reframes a range of contemporary changes — professionalization of
> mid-skill work, the rise of certification, the bundling of schooling with job access — as instances of
> the *same* closure process, legible to sociologists of work, education, organizations, and inequality
> alike. *(why it matters to the discipline broadly — the contribution is portable, stated against a
> specific prior view: closure as constructed, not human-capital-driven.)*
>
> Identifying this is hard because credentials are not adopted at random: occupations may add
> requirements precisely as their tasks grow more complex, so a naive comparison conflates **closure**
> with **upskilling** — the leading rival account. We exploit the **staggered** adoption of entry
> credentials across mid-skill occupations and follow successive birth cohorts through it, comparing
> workers entering the *same* occupation just before and just after it closes, with not-yet-closing
> occupations as the comparison. The adjudication is sharp: if upskilling drove our results, the task
> content and measured skill of entrants would shift at adoption; instead, **task content is flat across
> the threshold while who gets in changes** — entry tilts toward the already-advantaged. *(evidence and
> the `asr-research-design` adjudication sentence: what we'd see if the rival were true vs. what we see.)*
>
> Our contribution is conceptual as well as empirical: we specify **closure as an organizational event
> with a date**, which lets the mechanism be observed rather than inferred, and we state its **scope
> conditions** — it operates where credentials are gateways to entry rather than signals among incumbents,
> and where adoption is not itself a response to changing task demands. *(contribution + scope conditions
> — `asr-theory-building`.)* Beyond credentials, the lesson is that **inequality between positions can be
> produced by acts of institutional boundary-drawing that leave the work, and the workers' skills,
> untouched** — a mechanism a sociologist studying any gated good can import. *(broad lesson that
> travels.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. We develop the closure argument and its observable implications, then
> present the cohort design and the task-content test that separates closure from upskilling, and close
> with scope conditions and what credential gatekeeping implies for theories of durable inequality.
> *(brief roadmap — carries the argument, not a table of contents.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the ASR bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded** — by the end of the intro the reader has the **question, the argument,
  the evidence, and the disciplinary stakes** (`asr-writing-style`: "by the end of the introduction the
  reader knows the question, the argument, the evidence, and why it matters to sociology broadly").
- **Reads past the subfield** — framed for sociologists of work, education, organizations, *and*
  inequality; jargon ("social closure") is named and immediately glossed (`asr-writing-style`;
  `asr-topic-selection` "broad significance").
- **A mechanism and concept that travel** — "credential gatekeeping" / closure-as-organizational-event
  is offered for others to import, with explicit **scope conditions**; it passes the
  `asr-theory-building` **portability test** ("could a sociologist in another subfield import this?").
- **Design adjudicates the leading rival** — the closure-vs-upskilling **adjudication sentence** is
  written out, and the staggered cohort comparison is matched to it (`asr-research-design`; note the
  skill's warning against **naive TWFE on staggered timing** — the design uses a cohort/before-after
  comparison with not-yet-treated occupations).
- **Theory stated before the tests** — avoids `asr-theory-building`'s "hypothesizing after results are
  known"; the observable implications (flat task content, tilted entry) are derived from the mechanism.
- **No "understudied-X" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named
  `asr-topic-selection` / `asr-writing-style` anti-patterns are avoided.

> One-sentence hook (fillable, as the skills imply): "We show that **closing an occupation with a
> credential reproduces advantage across cohorts** by **manufacturing social closure**, using **the
> staggered adoption of entry credentials** to separate closure from upskilling."
