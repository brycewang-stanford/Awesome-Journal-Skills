# Worked Example: A JEL-Style Survey Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JEL survey-opening arc** from
[`jel-writing-style`](../../skills/jel-writing-style/SKILL.md):
**frame the field → why a synthesis is needed now → the organizing question and the spine → what the
survey concludes and the open questions → brief roadmap.**

Two JEL facts drive the rewrite and distinguish it from a primary-research journal:

- JEL is the **American Economic Association's survey-of-record** journal (founded 1969), read by
  **non-specialist economists**. A JEL article **synthesizes** a literature; it reports **no original
  estimates of its own** ([`jel-topic-selection`](../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md)). It is not
  *JEP* (shorter, non-technical, broad audience) and not *AER* (primary research).
- A JEL survey's contribution is an **organizing framework** that turns a scattered literature into an
  argument about the field — not an annotated bibliography
  ([`jel-organizing-framework`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md)). The bottom line is stated
  **early**, and rival camps are **steelmanned**
  ([`jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`](../../skills/jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance/SKILL.md)).

**Illustrative survey (fictional):** *"What We Know About the Employment Effects of Local Minimum-Wage
Ordinances: A Survey."* Fictional field: a (made-up) literature on city-level minimum-wage laws, with
invented schools and debates. Every characterization of "the literature" is invented for teaching.

---

## Before (an annotated bibliography — typical first-draft survey intro)

> The literature on minimum wages is large. Card and Krueger started the modern debate. Many papers
> have since estimated employment effects using various methods. Some studies find negative effects,
> others find no effect, and a few find positive effects. Neumark and co-authors have argued for
> negative effects, while others disagree. In this survey we review the main contributions. Section 2
> covers the early studies, Section 3 covers the panel studies, Section 4 covers the border-discontinuity
> studies, Section 5 covers recent work, and Section 6 concludes. We discuss each paper in turn and
> summarize what they find.

**What is wrong (against `jel-writing-style` / `jel-organizing-framework` / `jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`):**

- **No organizing framework.** Sections are "early / panel / border / recent" — a chronological list, not
  an analytical spine. This is the cardinal JEL sin: an **annotated bibliography**.
- **Vote-counting.** "Some find negative, others no effect" tallies results instead of *reconciling* them
  by credibility and by what each study actually estimates.
- **No bottom line.** A non-specialist finishes the paragraph not knowing what the field has *settled* or
  why a synthesis is needed *now*.
- **Camps named, not steelmanned.** "Neumark argues X, others disagree" caricatures a live debate rather
  than stating each side at its strongest.
- **"We discuss each paper in turn"** announces the summary-not-synthesis failure outright.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (JEL arc — frame the field, impose a spine, state the bottom line, steelman the debate)

> **When a city raises its own minimum wage above the federal floor, what happens to employment — and why
> do careful studies of seemingly similar policies still disagree?** Local minimum-wage ordinances have
> spread across dozens of US cities, generating a large empirical literature whose headline estimates
> range from clearly negative to indistinguishable from zero. *(frame the field + the animating question
> in the first breath — for a non-specialist economist.)*
>
> A synthesis is needed now because the disagreement is **no longer mainly about data** — it is about
> **design**: the recent wave of border-discontinuity, synthetic-control, and bunching studies has made
> clear that different credible methods are, in effect, **estimating different objects** (short- vs.
> long-run, extensive- vs. intensive-margin, tradable- vs. local-sector employment). Reading the studies
> as rival answers to one question is the source of the apparent contradiction. *(why a synthesis is
> needed now — a method shift has reframed the debate;
> [`jel-topic-selection`](../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md).)*
>
> We organize the literature not by chronology but by a **simple taxonomy of what is being identified**:
> we classify each study by (i) the **margin** of employment it measures, (ii) the **time horizon** of its
> estimand, and (iii) the **credibility of its counterfactual**. This spine turns the scattered estimates
> into a coherent map: studies that looked contradictory often occupy **different cells** and are, on
> inspection, **mutually consistent**. *(the organizing question and the spine — the survey's contribution;
> [`jel-organizing-framework`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md).)*
>
> Read through this taxonomy, the literature is more settled than the headline range suggests:
> well-identified short-run, local-sector estimates **cluster in a narrow, modest range**, while the wide
> dispersion comes almost entirely from studies that differ in margin, horizon, or counterfactual
> credibility — not from genuine disagreement about the same parameter. The genuinely **open questions**
> are about **long-run** adjustment, **firm-entry and price** responses, and **spillovers** to nearby
> jurisdictions, where credible evidence remains thin. *(what the survey concludes + the open questions,
> stated up front;
> [`jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`](../../skills/jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance/SKILL.md).)*
>
> Throughout, we state each research program's **strongest case before its limits**: the monopsony-based
> view that modest minimums need not cost jobs, and the competitive view that they do, are each given the
> evidence that most favors them, and we are explicit about where our own reading sits and what evidence
> would change it. *(steelman + labelled author's read, not consensus-by-assertion.)*
>
> The survey proceeds as follows. Section 2 sets out the taxonomy; Section 3 maps the well-identified
> short-run estimates into it; Section 4 turns to long-run and general-equilibrium responses; Section 5
> collects the open questions. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JEL bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **The opening follows the survey arc** — frame the field → why-now → organizing question + spine →
  conclusions + open questions → roadmap — and states the **bottom line early**
  ([`jel-writing-style`](../../skills/jel-writing-style/SKILL.md): "bottom-line state of knowledge stated
  early, not withheld to the end").
- **There is a real spine, not a list** — a three-axis taxonomy (margin × horizon × counterfactual
  credibility) that **reconciles an apparent contradiction** (studies sit in different cells), exactly the
  [`jel-organizing-framework`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md) test (MECE, reconciling,
  generative — the thin cells become the open questions).
- **Conflicting findings are weighed, not tallied** — the survey explains the dispersion by *estimand and
  credibility*, the [`jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`](../../skills/jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance/SKILL.md)
  standard ("reconciled by credibility + estimand, not vote-count"), and each camp is **steelmanned**.
- **It is written for the non-specialist** — no notation in the intro; the method shift is explained by the
  *question it answers*, the JEL voice ([`jel-writing-style`](../../skills/jel-writing-style/SKILL.md)).
- **Sibling check passes:** this is a technical, comprehensive synthesis for the survey-of-record — not a
  short non-technical *JEP* essay, and not an original-estimate *AER* paper. It reports **no new estimates
  of its own**; it appraises others'.
