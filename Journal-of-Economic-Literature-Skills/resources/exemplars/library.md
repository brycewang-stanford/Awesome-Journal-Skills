# JEL Exemplars Library (survey type × topic)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06.** Every survey below was checked to confirm it actually
> appeared in the ***Journal of Economic Literature*** (the American Economic Association's survey-of-record
> journal) and carries a **`10.1257/jel…` DOI stem** — the marker that confirms JEL specifically. Year,
> volume(issue), and pages were read off the AEA article pages (aeaweb.org). Surveys that could not be fully
> verified as JEL were **omitted** — this is deliberately a short, clean list rather than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Sibling-confusion guard (do not misattribute).** JEL is **not** the *Journal of Economic Perspectives*
> (JEP, `10.1257/jep…` — shorter, non-technical, broad-audience essays) and **not** the *American Economic
> Review* (AER, `10.1257/aer…` — original research). The AEA shares the `10.1257/` prefix across its
> journals, so only the **`10.1257/jel`** stem confirms JEL. An omission on exactly this ground is recorded
> below.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only** — it shows how a great
> JEL survey is *built* (its spine, its balance, its conclusions). It does **not** reproduce or invent the
> surveys' specific findings — read the original before citing any claim. For JEL-specific policy, scope, and
> house rules, see [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **survey type × question** is closest to yours, then study how that survey makes a
*comprehensive* reading of a literature into an *argument about the field* for a *non-specialist economist* —
the JEL bar (see [`../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md)). For each,
ask the self-check question before claiming your draft is "JEL-shaped." Remember JEL's identity: a survey
**appraises** the literature even-handedly — it reports **no original estimates of its own**
([`jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`](../../skills/jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance/SKILL.md)).

## By survey type

### Empirical-evidence stock-take (what does the accumulated evidence say?)

- **Atkinson, Piketty & Saez, "Top Incomes in the Long Run of History," JEL 2011, 49(1):3–71.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.49.1.3` (DOI `10.1257/jel.49.1.3`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** synthesizes a sprawling cross-country body of evidence on top income shares
    into a coherent, comparable account, with an explicit organizing structure (data construction → facts →
    interpretations). A model of comprehensiveness *plus* a spine. Routes to
    [`../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** does your survey turn a scattered evidence base into a *comparable* set of facts and
    competing interpretations, rather than a country-by-country list?

- **DellaVigna, "Psychology and Economics: Evidence from the Field," JEL 2009, 47(2):315–72.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.47.2.315` (DOI `10.1257/jel.47.2.315`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** imposes a clean **taxonomy** (nonstandard preferences / nonstandard beliefs /
    nonstandard decision-making) on a fast-growing behavioral field, so every study has a home and the gaps
    are visible. The taxonomy *is* the contribution — the
    [`jel-organizing-framework`](../../skills/jel-organizing-framework/SKILL.md) ideal.
  - **Self-check:** can a non-specialist restate your taxonomy after one read and slot a new paper into it?

### Methods / tool survey (teach a method family to non-specialists)

- **Lee & Lemieux, "Regression Discontinuity Designs in Economics," JEL 2010, 48(2):281–355.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.48.2.281` (DOI `10.1257/jel.48.2.281`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the canonical *accessible* methods survey — motivates RDD by the question it
    answers, builds intuition before formalism, and gives practitioners a usable guide. The
    [`jel-writing-style`](../../skills/jel-writing-style/SKILL.md) "intuition-first, notation-second" bar.
  - **Self-check:** could a non-specialist economist *use* the method after reading your survey, not just
    recognize its name?

- **Gentzkow, Kelly & Taddy, "Text as Data," JEL 2019, 57(3):535–74.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20181020` (DOI `10.1257/jel.20181020`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** surveys a new toolkit (text-as-data methods) for an economics audience,
    organizing techniques by the *economic problems they solve* rather than by computer-science lineage —
    accessibility plus a spine for a fast-moving methods area.
  - **Self-check:** is your methods survey organized by the *questions economists ask*, not by the tools'
    technical genealogy?

### Field-defining synthesis (orient a whole subfield)

- **Keane, "Labor Supply and Taxes: A Survey," JEL 2011, 49(4):961–1075.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.49.4.961` (DOI `10.1257/jel.49.4.961`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a comprehensive synthesis that *weighs* a large, conflicting estimate base by
    methodology and estimand — explaining why estimates differ rather than vote-counting — the
    [`jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance`](../../skills/jel-comprehensiveness-and-balance/SKILL.md) standard.
  - **Self-check:** do you reconcile conflicting estimates by *what each measures and how credible it is*,
    rather than tallying signs?

- **Lusardi & Mitchell, "The Economic Importance of Financial Literacy: Theory and Evidence," JEL 2014, 52(1):5–44.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.52.1.5` (DOI `10.1257/jel.52.1.5`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** integrates *theory and evidence* on a policy-salient field into one account,
    with a clear statement of what is known and where evidence is thin — the survey-with-a-bottom-line the
    JEL reader wants. Connects to
    [`../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jel-topic-selection/SKILL.md) ("why now" =
    policy salience).
  - **Self-check:** does your survey state plainly what the field has *settled* and where the open questions
    are, instead of ending in noncommittal summary?

### Climate / environment evidence synthesis

- **Dell, Jones & Olken, "What Do We Learn from the Weather? The New Climate-Economy Literature," JEL 2014, 52(3):740–98.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.52.3.740` (DOI `10.1257/jel.52.3.740`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** organizes a young-but-important literature around a methodological frame (what
    the weather/panel approach can and cannot identify), making the field's contribution *and* its limits
    legible — comprehensiveness with an analytical spine for an emerging area.
  - **Self-check:** for an emerging field, does your survey make clear both what the new approach establishes
    and what it cannot, rather than overstating maturity?

## Omitted for lack of full verification, or to avoid sibling-confusion (do not attribute to JEL without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking:

- **Any `10.1257/jep…` "perspectives"-style piece** — the *Journal of Economic Perspectives* shares the AEA
  `10.1257/` prefix and publishes short, non-technical surveys for a broad audience. A JEP essay is **not** a
  JEL survey; only the **`10.1257/jel`** stem confirms JEL. Listed here only as a sibling-confusion
  guardrail: check the stem before attributing any AEA survey-like article to JEL.
- Several frequently-cited economics surveys that *sound* like JEL pieces were left off because their **exact
  JEL volume/issue/pages and `10.1257/jel` DOI** could not be confirmed to the same standard in one pass; add
  them only after reading the AEA article page and confirming the DOI stem.

Before adding any new survey to this library, confirm it on `aeaweb.org` (article page with volume/issue/pages)
**and** that the DOI begins **`10.1257/jel`** (not `jep` or `aer`), then update the access-date header. When in
doubt, omit.
