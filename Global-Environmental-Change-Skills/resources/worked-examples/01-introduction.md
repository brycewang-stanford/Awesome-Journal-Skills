# Worked Example: A GEC-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **GEC introduction arc** assembled from the pack's own skills: a paper on the
**human and policy dimensions** of environmental change must, on the first page, state the
**human/policy problem, the gap across at least two literatures, the conceptual framework, and the
contribution** — front-loaded, legible to an interdisciplinary reader, and connected to scale and stakes
without overclaiming (`gec-writing-style`, `gec-topic-selection`). The framework must *do work*: it
generates the question, defines contested terms, and dictates what evidence would confirm or disconfirm
it (`gec-conceptual-framework`), and the design must isolate the claim at a stated scale
(`gec-research-design`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When the Well Runs Dry: Social Vulnerability and Adaptive Capacity
under Staggered Groundwater-Rationing Reforms."* Fictional setting: a country where 30 rural districts
introduced groundwater-extraction quotas in different years; the paper couples a **household
social-vulnerability** measure with district **governance capacity** to ask who adapts and who is left
exposed.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Groundwater depletion is a serious environmental problem that has attracted growing attention in the
> literature. Many studies have examined aquifer decline, irrigation efficiency, and water policy. In
> this paper, we assemble a household panel across 30 districts and estimate a staggered
> difference-in-differences model of the effect of groundwater-rationing quotas on rural household
> outcomes, with an event-study specification and robustness checks. Water is essential to livelihoods
> and is an important topic for policymakers. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes the
> data, Section 4 presents the method, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against the pack's skills):**

- **Leads with the topic and then the method**, not the human/policy question — fails the
  `gec-writing-style` rule to *front-load the contribution* on the first page.
- **No framework.** Nothing names *vulnerability*, *adaptive capacity*, or *governance*, so the paper
  reads as "atheoretical / descriptive" — the exact anti-pattern in `gec-conceptual-framework`.
- **Single literature.** It engages only the water/hydrology domain, ignoring the human-dimensions side
  (vulnerability, governance) — off-fit per `gec-literature-positioning`.
- **No social-science contribution and no stated stakes** beyond "water is important" — throat-clearing
  that fails the `gec-topic-selection` fit test (significant social-science component; societal
  significance).
- **No scale.** The claim's scale (household? district?) is never stated — a scale-mismatch risk flagged
  in `gec-research-design`.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (GEC arc — problem + framework + contribution, front-loaded, multi-scale)

> **When a shared aquifer is rationed, who manages to adapt and who is pushed into deeper exposure — and
> does the answer depend on how local institutions govern the rationing?** We show that staggered
> groundwater quotas reduce water stress on average, but that the relief is captured by households with
> higher *adaptive capacity*, while the most *socially vulnerable* households become **more** exposed
> where district governance is weak — so a single environmental instrument produces opposite human
> outcomes depending on the social and institutional context. *(human/policy question + headline answer +
> why it matters, in the first breath.)*
>
> Answering this requires more than measuring water: it requires a framework that separates **exposure**
> to the rationing from the **social vulnerability** and **adaptive capacity** that determine who can
> absorb it. We define *vulnerability* as susceptibility to harm from a stress combined with limited
> capacity to adapt, and *adaptive capacity* as the household and institutional resources that convert a
> shock into a manageable adjustment; *governance capacity* is the district's ability to implement and
> enforce the quota equitably. The framework asserts a clear relationship — the human outcome of an
> environmental policy is **exposure filtered through vulnerability and mediated by governance** — and it
> tells us what evidence would disconfirm it: if outcomes tracked the quota alone, regardless of
> vulnerability or governance, the framework would be wrong. *(conceptual framework that generates the
> question, defines contested terms once, and is falsifiable — `gec-conceptual-framework`.)*
>
> This question sits at the intersection of two literatures GEC readers hold together but that are often
> kept apart: the **water / resource-governance** literature on how rationing reallocates a common-pool
> resource, and the **social-vulnerability and adaptation** literature on who bears environmental risk.
> The dominant framing treats rationing as a technical allocation problem; we show it is also a
> **distributive** one, and that whether it widens or narrows vulnerability is an institutional outcome,
> not a technical given. *(engages a domain + a human-dimensions literature; states the gap as a
> contribution, not a hole — `gec-literature-positioning`.)*
>
> We exploit the **staggered timing** of quota adoption across 30 districts — adopted in different years
> for administrative, not livelihood-driven, reasons — to compare each district to not-yet-rationed
> districts, and we couple the household panel with district governance measures so the design can test
> the framework's *mediation* claim rather than just an average effect. We state the scale plainly: the
> estimand is the **household-level** change in exposure and adaptive outcomes, identified at the
> **district** level of treatment, and we ask whether it aggregates to a **regional** pattern of
> differentiated vulnerability. *(design tied to the framework's mechanism, at a stated multi-scale —
> `gec-research-design`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to reframe resource rationing as **adaptation policy with distributional stakes**:
> the same instrument can build adaptive capacity for some and manufacture vulnerability for others, and
> which it does is governed locally. *(contribution stated early, relative to a specific prior view.)*
> The broader lesson travels beyond groundwater: **any environmental instrument that rations a shared
> resource — emissions permits, fishing quotas, land-use restrictions — should be evaluated not only by
> its aggregate environmental effect but by how exposure is distributed across socially differentiated
> groups and mediated by the institutions that implement it.** *(broad, scale-spanning lesson, stated as
> an evaluation principle — no specific real-world policy is recommended.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the vulnerability–capacity–governance framework and
> the data; Section 3 presents the staggered design and the average and mediated effects; Section 4
> draws the distributional and governance implications. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the GEC bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded on page one** — the human/policy question, the answer, and the stakes
  appear immediately, legible to a geographer, a governance scholar, *and* an environmental economist
  (`gec-writing-style`: "front-load the contribution"; `gec-topic-selection`: societal significance).
- **A framework that does work** — it generates the question, defines *vulnerability* / *adaptive
  capacity* / *governance* once and uses them consistently, asserts a mechanism (exposure × vulnerability,
  mediated by governance), and states what evidence would disconfirm it (`gec-conceptual-framework`).
- **At least two literatures engaged** — resource governance × social vulnerability, meeting on the
  question, with the gap framed as a reframing rather than a hole (`gec-literature-positioning`).
- **Significant social-science component** — the paper is about *who* is made vulnerable and *which
  institutions* mediate it, not a hydrological result with a policy sentence attached
  (`gec-topic-selection` fit test).
- **Design tied to the mechanism, at a stated scale** — staggered adoption identifies the effect, the
  household/district/regional scales are named, and the design tests the framework's *mediation* claim,
  avoiding the local-data/global-claim scale mismatch (`gec-research-design`).
- **Implications are distributive and honest** — the lesson is stated as an *evaluation principle* across
  rationing instruments, grounded in the framework, with **no invented policy or overclaim**
  (`gec-policy-relevance-and-implications`).
