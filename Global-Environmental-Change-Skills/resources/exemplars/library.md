# GEC Exemplars Library (method × topic)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06-08.** Every paper below was checked to confirm it
> actually appeared in *Global Environmental Change* (Elsevier; full title *Global Environmental Change:
> Human and Policy Dimensions*), with a **`10.1016/...gloenvcha`** (or legacy **`10.1016/S0959-3780`**)
> DOI and an Elsevier ScienceDirect article page (`sciencedirect.com/.../pii/S0959378...`). Papers that
> could not be fully verified as GEC — or that turned out to be in a **sibling journal** — were
> **omitted** and documented at the bottom. It is deliberately a short, clean list (6 verified) rather
> than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Sibling-confusion guard.** GEC is **not** *Environmental Science & Policy*, *Climatic Change*,
> *Ecological Economics*, *Nature Climate Change*, or *Global Environmental Change Part B: Environmental
> Hazards* (a separate, discontinued title). Several famous human-dimensions papers live in those
> siblings, not in GEC; see the omissions list.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only**. It does not
> reproduce or invent findings, coefficients, or numbers — read the original on ScienceDirect before
> citing anything. For GEC-specific scope, caps, and policy, see
> [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × question** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a
human-dimensions result carry an **interdisciplinary, policy-relevant** lesson — the GEC bar (see
[`../../skills/gec-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/gec-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/gec-conceptual-framework/SKILL.md`](../../skills/gec-conceptual-framework/SKILL.md)). For
each, ask the self-check question before claiming your paper is "GEC-shaped."

## By method

### Conceptual synthesis that defines a contested term (vulnerability / adaptation)

- **Adger, "Vulnerability," Global Environmental Change 2006, 16(3):268–281.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2006.02.006`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378006000422`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** takes the field's most contested term and gives it a usable definition —
    susceptibility to harm plus limited capacity to adapt — integrating the vulnerability, resilience, and
    adaptation traditions. The canonical "the framework *is* the contribution" GEC paper
    ([`gec-conceptual-framework`](../../skills/gec-conceptual-framework/SKILL.md)).
  - **Self-check:** does your paper define its contested concept once and use it to do analytic work,
    rather than invoking "vulnerability/resilience" loosely?

### A framework that couples two global processes (conceptual, human–environment)

- **O'Brien & Leichenko, "Double exposure: assessing the impacts of climate change within the context of economic globalization," Global Environmental Change 2000, 10(3):221–232.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/S0959-3780(00)00021-2`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378000000212`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** introduces a framework ("double exposure") that couples a **biophysical**
    process (climate change) with a **socio-economic** one (globalization) to explain differentiated
    winners and losers — interdisciplinary *by construction*, exactly what
    [`gec-literature-positioning`](../../skills/gec-literature-positioning/SKILL.md) asks for.
  - **Self-check:** does your framework connect a domain process to a social-science process so it speaks
    across GEC's literatures, rather than living in one silo?

### Governance theory for a collective-action problem (institutions / commons)

- **Ostrom, "Polycentric systems for coping with collective action and global environmental change," Global Environmental Change 2010, 20(4):550–557.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2010.07.004`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378010000634`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reframes a "global" problem (emissions) as the aggregate of multi-scale
    actions, and argues for **polycentric** governance — a governance argument that explains *outcomes*,
    not just describes institutions (the watch-out in
    [`gec-conceptual-framework`](../../skills/gec-conceptual-framework/SKILL.md)).
  - **Self-check:** does your governance claim connect institutional form to an outcome across scales,
    rather than narrating one institution's story?

### Critical appraisal that reorganizes a research field (transitions)

- **Geels, McMeekin, Mylan & Southerton, "A critical appraisal of Sustainable Consumption and Production research: The reformist, revolutionary and reconfiguration positions," Global Environmental Change 2015, 34:1–12.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2015.04.013`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378015000813`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** maps a fragmented field into three positions and proposes a synthesizing
    "reconfiguration" lens — an agenda-setting contribution that *changes how the field frames* a problem
    ([`gec-topic-selection`](../../skills/gec-topic-selection/SKILL.md): contribution = reconceptualization).
  - **Self-check:** does your synthesis adjudicate competing framings the field already argues about,
    rather than re-summarize a literature?

### A framework bridging natural- and social-science evidence (food systems)

- **Ericksen, "Conceptualizing food systems for global environmental change research," Global Environmental Change 2008, 18(1):234–245.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2007.09.002`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378007000714`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** builds a food-systems framework that ties environmental-change drivers to
    **food security, ecosystem services, and social welfare** outcomes — a coupled human–environment
    system, not a resource study (the framing
    [`gec-topic-selection`](../../skills/gec-topic-selection/SKILL.md) rewards).
  - **Self-check:** is your system framed as coupled human–environment, naming the societal outcomes at
    stake, rather than as a single-sector resource analysis?

### Systematic empirical assessment / tracking (descriptive, global)

- **Berrang-Ford, Ford & Paterson, "Are we adapting to climate change?," Global Environmental Change 2011, 21(1):25–33.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2010.09.012`; ScienceDirect `pii/S0959378010000968`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns a vague policy question ("is adaptation actually happening?") into a
    tracked, evidence-based global assessment with an explicit, repeatable method — descriptive work made
    rigorous and policy-relevant ([`gec-research-design`](../../skills/gec-research-design/SKILL.md)).
  - **Self-check:** does your descriptive/tracking design state an explicit, repeatable method and answer
    a question policymakers actually ask, at a stated scale?

  **not GEC**. (Geels *is* in GEC for the 2015 SCP paper listed above — confirm the specific article.)
- **Gibson, Ostrom & Ahn, "The concept of scale and the human dimensions of global change: a survey" (2000)**
  — in *Ecological Economics* 32(2):217–239 (DOI `10.1016/S0921-8009(99)00092-0`), **not GEC**.
- **Howe, Mildenberger, Marlon & Leiserowitz, "Geographic variation in opinions on climate change…" (2015)**
  — in *Nature Climate Change* 5:596–603 (DOI `10.1038/nclimate2583`), **not GEC**.
- **Adger et al., "Are there social limits to adaptation to climate change?" (2009)** — in *Climatic
  Change* 93(3–4):335–354 (DOI `10.1007/s10584-008-9520-z`), **not GEC**.
- **Lambin & Meyfroidt, "Global land use change, economic globalization, and the looming land scarcity" (2011)**
  — in *PNAS* 108(9):3465–3472, **not GEC**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on ScienceDirect's GEC article page (a
`pii/S0959378...` URL with a `10.1016/...gloenvcha` DOI and volume/issue) and update the access-date
header. When in doubt, omit.
