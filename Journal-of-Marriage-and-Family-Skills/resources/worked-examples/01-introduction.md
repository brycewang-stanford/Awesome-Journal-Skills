# Worked Example — Introduction in JMF house style

> **Illustrative and FICTIONAL.** The paper, dataset, and numbers below are invented to
> demonstrate *Journal of Marriage and Family* (JMF) house style. No real study is described.
> JMF style here is derived only from this pack's own skills (`jmf-writing-style`,
> `jmf-theory-and-conceptual-framework`, `jmf-topic-selection`, `jmf-literature-positioning`).
> Confirm all format rules against this pack's `official-source-map.md`.

A JMF introduction must let a family scientist *from another discipline* follow the argument: by
the end, the reader knows the **question, the conceptual framework, the data/design, and why it
matters for families** — argument-first, jargon-defined, in modified APA.

---

## ❌ Before (discipline-insider, "so what" buried)

> Marital quality has been studied extensively (Smith, 2009; Jones, 2014; Lee, 2018). Some studies
> find that financial strain lowers marital satisfaction, while others find no effect. The
> mechanisms remain unclear. Using panel data, we estimate fixed-effects models of the association
> between income volatility and marital satisfaction. We find that income volatility is negatively
> associated with satisfaction (b = −.12, p < .01). We contribute to the literature on marriage and
> money.

What's wrong (per `jmf-writing-style` + `jmf-literature-positioning`):

- **No family-science "so what."** "Contribute to the literature" is not a contribution; what do
  families, researchers, or practitioners now understand that they didn't?
- **Literature *dump*, not a debate.** Three parenthetical citations stand in for an argument; the
  reader can't see which conversation the paper joins or what tension it resolves.
- **Coefficient-first, meaning-last.** "b = −.12" with no substantive family interpretation.
- **No conceptual framework.** Why would volatility (vs. level) of income matter for *couples*? The
  mechanism is asserted to be "unclear" and then left unclear.
- **Design under-described** for an interdisciplinary reader (whose marriages? measured how?).

---

## ✅ After (front-loaded family contribution, framework-driven)

> Most research on money and marriage asks whether couples with *less* income are less satisfied. Yet
> a growing share of households face income that is not just low but **unpredictable** — hours that
> swing week to week, gig earnings, irregular benefits. Whether the *instability* of income, holding
> its level constant, strains marriages is a distinct and largely open question with direct stakes
> for the millions of couples in volatile work.
>
> We draw on the **family stress model** (Conger et al., 1990, as developed for economic pressure) to
> argue that income volatility erodes marital satisfaction through a specific pathway: volatility
> raises *perceived economic pressure* — the felt difficulty of meeting obligations — which increases
> conflict and withdrawal between partners, independent of how much the couple earns on average. This
> reframes "money problems" in marriage from a level problem to a **predictability** problem and
> yields a testable mediation: volatility → economic pressure → satisfaction.
>
> We test this with five waves of the (fictional) *National Couples and Work Study*, a probability
> sample of 3,400 different-sex and same-sex married couples followed 2016–2022, using couple
> fixed-effects models that hold constant stable couple traits and average income, with perceived
> economic pressure measured at each wave. Because both partners report, we model the **dyad**, not
> the individual.
>
> By the end of this introduction the reader knows the question (does income *instability* strain
> marriage?), the framework (family stress model), the design (within-couple, dyadic, five-wave), and
> the stakes (a fast-growing population of couples in unstable work, and a shift in how scholars and
> practitioners should think about financial counseling — targeting predictability, not just amount).

Why it works:

- **Family-science significance is front-loaded** and concrete (couples in volatile work; counseling
  implication) — the interdisciplinary reader never digs for the "so what" (`jmf-writing-style`).
- **A real conceptual framework** (family stress model) generates a *mechanism and a testable
  mediation*, not an "unclear mechanism" (`jmf-theory-and-conceptual-framework`).
- **Joins a specific debate** (level vs. *volatility* of income) and stakes a position
  (`jmf-literature-positioning`) — engaging the conversation, not dumping citations.
- **Dyadic, within-couple design** is named up front and matched to the family-level claim
  (`jmf-research-design`); the couple, not the person, is the unit.
- **Argument-first**: the coefficient lives in the results, not the intro; the intro sells the idea.

## Companion: the JMF structured abstract (~200–225 words)

JMF requires a labeled structured abstract. The same fictional study:

> **Objective.** This study asks whether the *volatility* of household income, independent of its
> level, diminishes marital satisfaction, and whether perceived economic pressure explains the link.
> **Background.** A growing share of couples face unpredictable earnings, yet most money-and-marriage
> research treats income as a level. The family stress model implies that instability itself may
> strain couples by raising felt economic pressure.
> **Method.** Five waves (2016–2022) of a probability sample of 3,400 married couples (different-sex
> and same-sex) were analyzed with couple fixed-effects and dyadic mediation models holding average
> income constant; both partners reported satisfaction and economic pressure.
> **Results.** Within couples, greater income volatility predicted lower marital satisfaction; roughly
> half of the association operated through increased perceived economic pressure. The pattern held for
> both partners and across couple types.
> **Conclusion.** Income *instability*, not only low income, is a family stressor, working through
> perceived economic pressure.
> **Implications.** Financial counseling and policy that stabilize earnings — not only raise them —
> may protect marital quality; researchers should measure income volatility, not just level.
