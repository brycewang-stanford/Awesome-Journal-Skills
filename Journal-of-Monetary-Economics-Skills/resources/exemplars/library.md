# *Journal of Monetary Economics* Exemplars Library (method × topic)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06-08.** Every paper below was checked to confirm it
> actually appeared in the **_Journal of Monetary Economics_** (JME) — the Elsevier field journal for
> monetary theory and policy, business cycles, and macroeconomics (editors S. Borağan Aruoba and Eric
> Swanson; submission via Editorial Manager) — with year and volume/issue, against the journal's
> ScienceDirect article pages, the RePEc/IDEAS series record (`ideas.repec.org/a/eee/moneco/...`), and
> corroborating Google Scholar lookups carrying the journal name. Papers that could not be fully verified
> **as _JME_** were **omitted** — this is deliberately a short, clean list, not a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Sibling-journal guard.** *JME* is **not** *AEJ: Macroeconomics*, the *Journal of Money, Credit and
> Banking*, the *American Economic Review*, the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, the *Journal of Economic
> Theory*, or the *Journal of Political Economy*. Note one near-trap: the **Carnegie-Rochester-NYU
> Conference Series on Public Policy** is a *separate Elsevier series* (it appears as an annual JME-linked
> issue but is cited under its own title) — a paper "in the Carnegie-Rochester series" is **not** cited as
> JME. Several canonical monetary papers — Taylor rules, Gertler–Karadi, sticky-information Phillips
> curve — live in those venues. The [Omitted](#omitted-for-lack-of-full-verification-do-not-attribute-to-jme)
> section records the traps checked and rejected.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only**. It does not
> reproduce or invent IRFs, elasticities, or specific findings — read the original on ScienceDirect /
> RePEc before citing any number. For JME-specific scope, the up-front submission fee, the "up or out"
> first-revision rule, the 40-page cap, and house style (≤100-word abstract that may not begin with "This
> paper" or "We"; author-date references with journal titles spelled in full), see

## By method

### A tractable theoretical device that becomes field infrastructure (monetary theory)

- **Calvo, "Staggered Prices in a Utility-Maximizing Framework," _Journal of Monetary Economics_ 1983, 12(3):383–398.**
  Verified: ScienceDirect `0304-3932(83)90060-0`; RePEc `eee/moneco/v12y1983i3p383-398`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** introduces the random-adjustment ("Calvo") pricing friction that makes
    staggered price-setting analytically tractable in general equilibrium — a *modelling* contribution whose
    payoff is that the entire New Keynesian apparatus could be built on it. The
    [`jme-contribution-framing`](../../skills/jme-contribution-framing/SKILL.md) lesson that a clean,
    reusable device can be the contribution.
  - **Self-check:** if your contribution is a model device, is it *tractable and reusable* enough that other
    work can build on it, not a one-off fix?

### Structural econometric estimation of a forward-looking macro relation (inflation dynamics)

- **Galí & Gertler, "Inflation Dynamics: A Structural Econometric Analysis," _Journal of Monetary Economics_ 1999, 44(2):195–222.**
  Verified: ScienceDirect `S0304393299000239`; RePEc `eee/moneco/v44y1999i2p195-222`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** estimates a New Keynesian Phillips curve by GMM using *marginal cost* rather
    than an output gap, and nests a backward-looking term so the data can say how forward-looking inflation
    is — estimation built to *adjudicate* the forward- vs. backward-looking debate
    ([`jme-data-analysis`](../../skills/jme-data-analysis/SKILL.md): moment-based estimation with the
    identifying assumptions made explicit).
  - **Self-check:** does your structural estimate let the data discriminate between competing models, with
    instruments/moments whose validity you defend?

### SVAR identification of a macro shock under weak theory (monetary policy effects)

- **Uhlig, "What Are the Effects of Monetary Policy on Output? Results from an Agnostic Identification Procedure," _Journal of Monetary Economics_ 2005, 52(2):381–419.**
  Verified: ScienceDirect `S0304393205000073`; RePEc `eee/moneco/v52y2005i2p381-419`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** identifies monetary-policy shocks with *sign restrictions* agreed across
    models rather than disputed zero restrictions, and shows the output response is far less precisely
    pinned down than the recursive-VAR consensus claimed — an identification contribution that *changes the
    inference*, the core [`jme-identification-strategy`](../../skills/jme-identification-strategy/SKILL.md)
    move.
  - **Self-check:** are your identifying restrictions ones the field would *accept ex ante*, and do you show
    how the conclusion moves as you weaken them?

### Ramsey / quantitative optimal-policy analysis in an estimated model (monetary–fiscal policy)

- **Schmitt-Grohé & Uribe, "Optimal Simple and Implementable Monetary and Fiscal Rules," _Journal of Monetary Economics_ 2007, 54(6):1702–1725.**
  Verified: ScienceDirect (JME 54(6)); RePEc `eee/moneco/v54y2007i6p1702-1725`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** computes welfare-ranked *simple, implementable* interest-rate and tax rules in
    a calibrated sticky-price economy, insisting the rules guarantee a unique equilibrium — normative
    quantitative macro with a concrete policy deliverable
    ([`jme-contribution-framing`](../../skills/jme-contribution-framing/SKILL.md): turn a model result into a
    rule a central bank could actually run).
  - **Self-check:** does your optimal-policy exercise deliver an *operational* rule (and check determinacy),
    rather than only an unimplementable Ramsey allocation?

### Quantitative business-cycle modelling matching open-economy/macro moments (business cycles)

- **Schmitt-Grohé & Uribe, "Closing Small Open Economy Models," _Journal of Monetary Economics_ — see note.**
  *(This widely-cited model-comparison paper is verified in the* **_Journal of International Economics_** *2003, 61(1):163–185, not JME — see the JIE library. It is listed here only to flag the cross-field confusion: do not cite it as JME.)*

## Omitted for lack of full verification (do not attribute to _JME_)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these famous "monetary economics" papers were **excluded** after
web-checking — each is real and important, but **not in _JME_** (the sibling-journal traps the brief
warned of):

- **Taylor, "Discretion versus Policy Rules in Practice" (1993)** — verified in the **_Carnegie-Rochester
  Conference Series on Public Policy_** 39:195–214, a *separate Elsevier series*, **not _JME_** proper. The
  Taylor-rule classic, and exactly the near-trap noted in the sibling guard.
- **Gertler & Karadi, "Monetary Policy Surprises, Credit Costs, and Economic Activity" (2015)** — verified in
  **_AEJ: Macroeconomics_** 7(1):44–76, **not _JME_**. The high-frequency-instrument / proxy-VAR landmark.
- **Mankiw & Reis, "Sticky Information versus Sticky Prices…" (2002)** — verified in the **_Quarterly Journal
  of Economics_** 117(4):1295–1328, **not _JME_**.
- **Schmitt-Grohé & Uribe, "Optimal Fiscal and Monetary Policy Under Sticky Prices" (2004)** — verified in the
  **_Journal of Economic Theory_** 114(2):198–230, **not _JME_**. (Their *simple-rules* paper above *is* JME;
  this Ramsey companion is JET — easy to conflate.)
- **Christiano, Eichenbaum & Evans, "Monetary Policy Shocks: What Have We Learned and to What End?" (1999)** —
  verified as a chapter in the **_Handbook of Macroeconomics_**, **not a _JME_** article.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on the journal's ScienceDirect page (or the RePEc
`eee/moneco` series record) with volume/issue — and check it is not actually in the **Carnegie-Rochester**
series — then update the access-date header. When in doubt, **omit**.
