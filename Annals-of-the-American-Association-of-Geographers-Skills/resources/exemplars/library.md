## How to use this library

The Annals is the discipline's **four-area flagship**: each paper below earns its place by making a
result legible and useful across geography, and by defending its method on its own terms (see
[`../../skills/aaag-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aaag-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/aaag-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aaag-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **area × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) makes the geography load-bearing and
(2) defends the design (see
[`../../skills/aaag-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aaag-research-design/SKILL.md)). Run the
self-check before claiming your paper is "Annals-shaped."

## By area × method

### Geographic Methods — spatially varying coefficients (GWR)

- **Harris, Fotheringham & Juggins, "Robust Geographically Weighted Regression: A Technique for
  Quantifying Spatial Relationships Between Freshwater Acidification Critical Loads and Catchment
  Attributes," Annals of the Association of American Geographers 2010, 100(2):286–306.**
  Verified: Taylor & Francis article page (`doi.org/10.1080/00045600903550378`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** advances a method (robust GWR) where the *spatial nonstationarity* is the
    point, and demonstrates it on a real environmental problem — a Methods contribution that improves
    geographic inference rather than applying an off-the-shelf model (see `aaag-research-design`: "take
    space seriously").
  - **Self-check:** does your method advance geographic *inference* (dependence, scale, nonstationarity),
    or is it a generic technique with coordinates attached?

### Geographic Methods — scale and multiscale modeling

- **Fotheringham, Yang & Kang, "Multiscale Geographically Weighted Regression (MGWR)," Annals of the
  American Association of Geographers 2017, 107(6):1247–1265.**
  Verified: Taylor & Francis article page (`doi.org/10.1080/24694452.2017.1352480`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** makes **scale** the explicit object — letting each relationship operate at
    its own spatial scale — which is a portable geographic concept far beyond the example data (see
    `aaag-theory-building`: scale as a load-bearing concept).
  - **Self-check:** is scale a result in your paper (tested, varied), or an unexamined modeling choice?

### Human Geography — place, structuration, time-geography (theory)

- **Pred, "Place as Historically Contingent Process: Structuration and the Time-Geography of Becoming
  Places," Annals of the Association of American Geographers 1984, 74(2):279–297.**
  Verified: Taylor & Francis / Wiley article page (`doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-8306.1984.tb01453.x`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a theory paper that reconceptualizes **place** as process, integrating
    structuration and time-geography — the kind of portable conceptual move that reorders how empirical
    human geographers see their object (see `aaag-theory-building`: place/space-time mode).
  - **Self-check:** does your conceptual move change what *empirical* geographers must now account for,
    not just rename a debate?

### Nature and Society — hazards, vulnerability of people and places

- **Cutter, Mitchell & Scott, "Revealing the Vulnerability of People and Places: A Case Study of
  Georgetown County, South Carolina," Annals of the Association of American Geographers 2000,
  90(4):713–737.**
  Verified: Taylor & Francis article page (`doi.org/10.1111/0004-5608.00219`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** integrates biophysical hazard exposure with social vulnerability in one
    place-based framework — a Nature-Society contribution that theorizes *both* sides and operationalizes
    them spatially (see `aaag-research-design`: nature-society / mixed methods).
  - **Self-check:** does your coupled-systems paper theorize and measure the social *and* the biophysical,
    or assert one as backdrop to the other?

### Nature and Society / climate — drought, agriculture, and vulnerability

- **Liverman, "Drought Impacts in Mexico: Climate, Agriculture, Technology, and Land Tenure in Sonora and
  Puebla," Annals of the Association of American Geographers 1990, 80(1):49–72.**
  Verified: Taylor & Francis / Wiley article page (`doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-8306.1990.tb00003.x`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** links a biophysical hazard (drought) to social structure (technology, land
    tenure) across two regions — a paired-place design that shows vulnerability is produced by
    human-environment relations, not climate alone (see `aaag-theory-building`: environment-society mode).
  - **Self-check:** does your environmental-impact paper explain *why* impacts vary across places through
    a coupled mechanism, or just map where they occurred?

## Omitted for lack of Annals verification (do NOT attribute to the Annals without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these plausible "geography classics" were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not the Annals. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Tobler, "A Computer Movie Simulating Urban Growth in the Detroit Region" (1970)** — verified to be in
  *Economic Geography* 46(sup1):234–240, **not the Annals**. (Source of "Tobler's First Law"; a very
  common misattribution.)
- **Anselin, "Local Indicators of Spatial Association—LISA" (1995)** — verified to be in *Geographical
  Analysis* 27(2):93–115, **not the Annals**.
- **Brunsdon, Fotheringham & Charlton, "Geographically Weighted Regression: A Method for Exploring Spatial
  Nonstationarity" (1996)** — verified to be in *Geographical Analysis* 28(4):281–298, **not the Annals**.
  (Note: *later* GWR papers by Fotheringham and colleagues *are* in the Annals — listed above — so do not
  conflate the original GA paper with the Annals MGWR paper.)
- **Cutter, Boruff & Shirley, "Social Vulnerability to Environmental Hazards" (2003)** — verified to be in
  *Social Science Quarterly* 84(2):242–261, **not the Annals**. (Note: a *different* Cutter paper from
  2000, "Revealing the Vulnerability of People and Places," *is* in the Annals and is listed above.)
- **Hägerstrand, "What About People in Regional Science?" (1970)** — verified to be in *Papers of the
  Regional Science Association* 24:7–21, **not the Annals**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on Taylor & Francis Online
(`tandfonline.com/journals/raag21`) or Wiley with volume/issue/pages and DOI, cross-check a second
source, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
