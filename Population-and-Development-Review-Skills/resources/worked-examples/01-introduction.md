# Worked Example: A *PDR*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the introduction arc *PDR* rewards (from `popdevr-writing-style` and
`popdevr-topic-selection`): **population question → why it matters for development/policy across fields →
data & method → headline quantity with magnitude and interval → what it identifies → contribution to the
population-and-development debate → brief roadmap.** The governing rules from `popdevr-writing-style` are
**front-load the contribution** (by the end of the intro the reader knows the population question, the
development stakes, the data and method, the finding, and why it matters) and write **quantity-first,
meaning-second** (lead with the demographic quantity and its magnitude, then interpret it for
development). PDR also prizes a **lucid, essayistic** register: the intro should read as an argument, not
a results dump. Jargon is defined on first use so a reader from another field can follow.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Does Schooling Open the Demographic-Dividend Window? Fertility
Decline and the Support Ratio in a Mid-Income Country, 1970–2015."* Fictional setting: a single
mid-income country with a recent fertility transition and high-quality census and survey data.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> The relationship between education and fertility has been studied extensively in economics, sociology,
> and demography. Many factors are associated with fertility, including income, schooling, urbanization,
> and family planning. In this paper, we use census and survey data and apply a decomposition together
> with a difference-in-differences design to study how schooling relates to fertility and to the age
> structure. We estimate several models and report the results. Understanding population change is
> important for development and for policymakers. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes
> the data, Section 4 presents the methods, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `popdevr-writing-style` / `popdevr-topic-selection` / `popdevr-theory-building`):**

- **Leads with method** ("we apply a decomposition together with a difference-in-differences design")
  instead of the population-and-development question — the named anti-pattern in `popdevr-writing-style`.
- **No population question and no development stakes on page one.** An economist cannot tell what changed,
  by how much, or why it matters for growth (fails the `popdevr-topic-selection` broad-interest test).
- **No headline quantity with magnitude or uncertainty** — violates quantity-first prose.
- **Development meaning is a throwaway** ("important for development") rather than a specific claim.
- **Catalog prose**, not the sustained essay-argument PDR rewards; over-signposted roadmap doing the work
  the argument should do.

---

## After (*PDR* arc — population question + development stakes + quantity, contribution early)

> **Can mass schooling do more than lower fertility — can it open the demographic-dividend window, the
> period when a falling youth burden lets a country save and invest its way to faster growth?** We show
> that the expansion of female lower-secondary schooling in this mid-income country lowered completed
> fertility by **0.9 births per woman (95% CI 0.7–1.1)** between the 1970 and 2015 birth cohorts, and
> that this decline did **most of the work** — about **62%** — of raising the economic support ratio
> (workers per consumer) into the range that, elsewhere, has coincided with a sustained growth boost.
> *(Population question + development stakes + headline quantity with magnitude and interval, in the
> first breath; "completed fertility" and "support ratio" are defined in place for a non-demographer.)*
>
> Reading this as a dividend, rather than as a mechanical correlation between schooling and births, is
> hard for two reasons. First, schooling and fertility may both respond to a third force — urban labor
> demand — so a raw association need not be causal. Second, a dividend is *conditional*: a favorable age
> structure raises growth only where the larger working-age share is actually employed and educated, so
> the demographic window can open without the economic payoff arriving. *(Why it is hard — the
> identification problem and the conditional-dividend logic, in development-and-population terms.)*
>
> We combine complete-count census birth histories for the 1970–2015 female birth cohorts with a
> staggered rollout of a lower-secondary school-building program across districts, and we decompose the
> change in the economic support ratio into its fertility-driven and mortality-driven components, with
> bootstrap intervals on every component and an event-study check that fertility was not already falling
> faster in soon-to-be-treated districts. *(Data and method in one paragraph; identification and the
> decomposition handled explicitly, per `popdevr-research-design` / `popdevr-data-analysis`.)*
>
> Of the rise in the support ratio, **0.9 births of avoided fertility (62%)** is the dominant channel,
> with declining child mortality contributing the rest; the schooling effect appears only in cohorts
> exposed to the program after age 12, and is **absent where district labor demand stayed flat** — the
> dividend opened demographically but paid off economically only where jobs followed. *(Headline result
> restated with the cohort signature and the conditional boundary — the observable implications from
> `popdevr-theory-building`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that, in this setting, the demographic dividend is **a schooling-driven,
> employment-conditional outcome**, not the automatic consequence of a falling birth rate that a purely
> age-structural account would predict. This reframes the policy lever: investing in girls' schooling
> *and* labor demand together, rather than family planning alone, is what converts a demographic window
> into growth. *(Contribution stated early and against a specific rival reading, with the policy payoff
> PDR expects.)* The decomposition is **portable**: any population scientist can apply it to separate the
> fertility and mortality channels of an age-structure shift — and to ask, in an ageing high-income
> setting, how much of a *closing* dividend is demographic versus institutional. *(Portability — the
> cross-field payoff that travels beyond the case, per `popdevr-theory-building`.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 places the result in the dividend and fertility-transition
> debates; Section 3 presents the school-rollout design and the support-ratio decomposition; Section 4
> reports the cohort and district results and their sensitivity to the labor-demand condition. *(Brief
> roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *PDR* bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Contribution and development stakes front-loaded** — by the end of the first paragraph the reader has
  the population question (fertility decline), the development stakes (the dividend and growth), the data
  and method, the finding, and why it matters across fields (`popdevr-writing-style`).
- **Quantity-first, meaning-second** — the headline (0.9 births; 62% of the support-ratio rise) leads
  with magnitude **and a 95% interval**, then is interpreted for development, rather than "the model
  shows…" (`popdevr-data-analysis`: report uncertainty honestly).
- **Reads across fields** — "completed fertility" and "support ratio" are defined on first use, and the
  portability note speaks to an ageing-society economist, satisfying the `popdevr-topic-selection`
  broad-interest test.
- **Contribution type is explicit** — this is a **decomposition + causal design** that separates what a
  bare age-structural account conflates (a demographic window vs. an economic payoff), the move
  `popdevr-theory-building` calls a mechanism contribution with a boundary.
- **The strongest rival is named and adjudicated** — the automatic-dividend (age-structure) account would
  predict growth wherever fertility fell; the data show the payoff only where labor demand followed,
  satisfying the `popdevr-research-design` adjudication test.
- **Identification is handled, not asserted** — the staggered school rollout, the event-study pre-trend
  check, and the labor-demand condition address confounding by urban labor demand, avoiding the
  `popdevr-data-analysis` anti-pattern of reading a correlation as a development effect.
- **Method is demoted to a tool**, introduced where the design is described, never as the lead (avoids the
  `popdevr-writing-style` "leading with the method" anti-pattern), and the prose argues rather than
  catalogs — the essayistic register PDR rewards.
