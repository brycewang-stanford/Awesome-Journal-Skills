## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **contribution × population-and-development question** is closest to yours, then study
how that paper turns a population process into a contribution **of broad interest across demography and
development** — the *PDR* bar (see
[`../../skills/popdevr-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/popdevr-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/popdevr-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/popdevr-theory-building/SKILL.md), and
[`../../skills/popdevr-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/popdevr-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each,
ask the self-check question before claiming your paper is "*PDR*-shaped." Note how many of these are
**synthetic/conceptual essays** — the form PDR is most known for.

## By contribution type

### Conceptual framework that reorganizes a field (formal / synthetic)

- **Bongaarts, "A Framework for Analyzing the Proximate Determinants of Fertility," _PDR_ 1978, 4(1):105–132.**
  Verified: PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47; JSTOR `10.2307/1972149`; widely indexed as *Population and
  Development Review* 4(1).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reduces the many social determinants of fertility to a small set of
    **proximate determinants** (marriage, contraception, abortion, postpartum infecundability) through
    which everything else must act — a framework that reorganized fertility analysis worldwide. The
    [`popdevr-theory-building`](../../skills/popdevr-theory-building/SKILL.md) synthetic-framework move:
    sharpen a distinction the prior debate ran together, then show it does work.
  - **Self-check:** does your framework *reduce* a sprawling literature to a tractable, predictive core
    with a clear boundary, rather than summarize it?

### Restatement of a grand theory (synthetic essay — the PDR signature)

- **Caldwell, "Toward a Restatement of Demographic Transition Theory," _PDR_ 1976, 2(3/4):321–366.**
  Verified: PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47; JSTOR `10.2307/1971615`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the archetypal **PDR synthetic essay** — it reframes the demographic
    transition around **intergenerational wealth flows**, arguing fertility falls when the net flow
    reverses from children-to-parents to parents-to-children. A model of the
    [`popdevr-writing-style`](../../skills/popdevr-writing-style/SKILL.md) essayistic register and the
    population-and-development bridge.
  - **Self-check:** does your essay *restate* a debate with a new organizing principle and testable
    implications, not merely review it?

### Cultural-economic synthesis of a fertility regime (synthetic essay)

- **Lesthaeghe & Surkyn, "Cultural Dynamics and Economic Theories of Fertility Change," _PDR_ 1988, 14(1):1–45.**
  Verified: PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47; JSTOR `10.2307/1972499`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** integrates **value change** with economic accounts of fertility decline,
    laying groundwork for the second-demographic-transition program — a
    [`popdevr-literature-positioning`](../../skills/popdevr-literature-positioning/SKILL.md) move that
    engages two conversations (culture and economics) at once.
  - **Self-check:** does your paper bridge two field literatures that usually talk past each other, with a
    framework that subsumes both?

### Diffusion / social-interaction account of a transition (empirical synthesis)

- **Bongaarts & Watkins, "Social Interactions and Contemporary Fertility Transitions," _PDR_ 1996, 22(4):639–682.**
  Verified: JSTOR `2137804`; PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47 (*Population and Development Review* 22(4)).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses cross-national fertility data across many developing countries to argue
    that **social interaction and diffusion**, not development thresholds alone, govern the timing and pace
    of fertility decline — the [`popdevr-data-analysis`](../../skills/popdevr-data-analysis/SKILL.md)
    comparative-evidence-plus-mechanism standard, with the development meaning made explicit.
  - **Self-check:** can you connect a cross-country population pattern to a mechanism (here, diffusion)
    rather than read the cross-country gradient as the explanation?

### Synthesis-and-appraisal of a field's theories (migration)

- **Massey, Arango, Hugo, Kouaouci, Pellegrino & Taylor, "Theories of International Migration: A Review and Appraisal," _PDR_ 1993, 19(3):431–466.**
  Verified: JSTOR `2938462`; PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47. (This famous migration synthesis is in **PDR**,
  *not* *Demography* — a clean sibling-guard data point.)
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** organizes the scattered theories of international migration (neoclassical,
    new economics of migration, dual labor market, world systems, network/cumulative causation) into one
    appraisal — the [`popdevr-topic-selection`](../../skills/popdevr-topic-selection/SKILL.md) "frame
    flows as a population-and-development process" move at field scale.
  - **Self-check:** does your review *appraise and integrate* rival theories against evidence, with a
    development framing, rather than list them?

### Two-process framework with a development boundary (proximate determinants, regional)

- **Bongaarts, Frank & Lesthaeghe, "The Proximate Determinants of Fertility in Sub-Saharan Africa," _PDR_ 1984, 10(3):511–537.**
  Verified: JSTOR `1973518`; PDR Cumulative Contents 1–47 (*Population and Development Review* 10(3)).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** applies and adapts the proximate-determinants framework to a regional
    fertility regime, showing **where the general framework's parameters differ** — the
    [`popdevr-theory-building`](../../skills/popdevr-theory-building/SKILL.md) scope-condition discipline:
    state where a framework applies and where it must be re-tuned.
  - **Self-check:** when you port a framework to a new development context, do you report the boundary and
    the re-estimated parameters, not just the average fit?

## Omitted for lack of full verification (do not attribute to _PDR_ without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these strong candidates were **excluded** after web-checking — each
is a real, important paper, but **not in _PDR_** (the sibling-journal traps the brief warned of):

- **Massey & Denton, "Hypersegregation in U.S. Metropolitan Areas" (1989)** — verified in **_Demography_**
  26(3):373–391, **not _PDR_**.
- **Lee & Carter, "Modeling and Forecasting U.S. Mortality" (1992)** — verified in the **_Journal of the
  American Statistical Association_** 87(419):659–671, **not _PDR_**. The canonical mortality-forecast method.
- **Zelinsky, "The Hypothesis of the Mobility Transition" (1971)** — verified in the **_Geographical
  Review_** 61(2):219–249, **not _PDR_**. (Often cited alongside PDR migration work, but published elsewhere.)
- **Lutz, Sanderson & Scherbov, "The Coming Acceleration of Global Population Ageing" (2008)** — verified
  in **_Nature_** 451:716–719, **not _PDR_**. (The authors also publish on ageing in PDR; this specific
  paper is in *Nature*.)
- **Preston, "The Changing Relation between Mortality and Level of Economic Development" (1975)** —
  verified in **_Population Studies_** 29(2), **not _PDR_**. The "Preston curve" classic.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on the PDR Cumulative Contents index or the
journal's Wiley/JSTOR record with volume/issue, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, **omit**.
