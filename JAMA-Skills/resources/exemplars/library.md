## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **design × clinical question** is closest to yours, then study how that
paper meets the JAMA bar: a single pre-specified primary outcome, effect sizes with 95%
CIs, the correct EQUATOR reporting standard, and conclusions bounded to the evidence (see
[`../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md),
and [`../../skills/jama-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-writing-style/SKILL.md)).
For each, ask the self-check question before claiming your manuscript is "JAMA-shaped."

## By design

### Large randomized prevention trial stopped early for harm (RCT)

- **Writing Group for the Women's Health Initiative Investigators, "Risks and Benefits of Estrogen Plus Progestin in Healthy Postmenopausal Women: Principal Results From the Women's Health Initiative Randomized Controlled Trial," JAMA 2002;288(3):321-333.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.288.3.321` (jamanetwork.com / PubMed 12117397).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a primary-prevention RCT (16 608 women) whose data and safety
    monitoring board stopped the trial early because overall risks exceeded benefits — a
    model of pre-specified outcomes, an a priori monitoring rule, and conclusions that
    follow the primary result rather than the hoped-for one. See
    [`../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** is your primary outcome and stopping rule pre-specified, so a null or
    adverse result is reported as plainly as a favorable one?

### Behavioral-intervention RCT, ancillary remission outcome (RCT)

- **Gregg EW, Chen H, Wagenknecht LE, et al (Look AHEAD Research Group), "Association of an Intensive Lifestyle Intervention With Remission of Type 2 Diabetes," JAMA 2012;308(23):2489-2496.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.2012.67929` (jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1486829).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a randomized trial of an intensive lifestyle intervention vs
    diabetes support/education reporting a patient-relevant outcome (diabetes remission) with
    CONSORT discipline — clear randomized comparison, defined outcome, effect estimates with
    CIs. Pairs with the CONSORT row in
    [`../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** is your comparison genuinely randomized and your headline outcome
    clinically meaningful, not a surrogate chosen after the fact?

### Diagnostic-management algorithm, prospective multicenter (diagnostic / management strategy)

- **Christopher Study Investigators (van Belle A, Büller HR, Huisman MV, et al), "Effectiveness of Managing Suspected Pulmonary Embolism Using an Algorithm Combining Clinical Probability, D-Dimer Testing, and Computed Tomography," JAMA 2006;295(2):172-179.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.295.2.172` (PubMed 16403929).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a prospective study of 3306 consecutive patients evaluating a
    dichotomized clinical decision rule + D-dimer + CT, reporting the 3-month venous
    thromboembolism rate to show the strategy is safe — the diagnostic-pathway analogue of
    STARD thinking (consecutive enrollment, pre-defined thresholds, outcomes with risk
    estimates). See
    [`../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-study-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** are patients enrolled consecutively, the decision thresholds pre-defined,
    and the follow-up outcome the thing clinicians actually need to know is safe?

### Consensus definition validated in cohort data (consensus + cohort validation)

- **Singer M, Deutschman CS, Seymour CW, et al, "The Third International Consensus Definitions for Sepsis and Septic Shock (Sepsis-3)," JAMA 2016;315(8):801-810.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.2016.0287` (jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2492881).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a task-force consensus paper that did not stop at expert
    opinion — it interrogated large electronic-health-record cohorts to test the predictive
    validity of the proposed criteria (SOFA, qSOFA), pairing a definitional contribution
    with empirical validation. A model for how JAMA frames a guideline-shaping statement.
  - **Self-check:** does your definitional/criteria claim carry external validation against
    real patient data, not just committee agreement?

### Pairwise systematic review and meta-analysis (evidence synthesis)

- **Zheng SL, Roddick AJ, "Association of Aspirin Use for Primary Prevention With Cardiovascular Events and Bleeding Events: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis," JAMA 2019;321(3):277-287.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.2018.20578` (jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2721178).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a PRISMA-style synthesis of 13 randomized trials (~164 000
    participants) that reports benefit **and** harm side by side (cardiovascular reduction
    against major-bleeding increase) — exactly the balanced, certainty-aware reporting the
    PRISMA row in
    [`../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-reporting-standards/SKILL.md)
    calls for.
  - **Self-check:** does your review report harms with the same rigor as benefits, with a
    reproducible search and a study-selection flow diagram?

### Network meta-analysis comparing multiple treatments (evidence synthesis)

- **Khera R, Murad MH, Chandar AK, et al, "Association of Pharmacological Treatments for Obesity With Weight Loss and Adverse Events: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis," JAMA 2016;315(22):2424-2434.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1001/jama.2016.7602` (PubMed 27299618).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a network meta-analysis comparing five obesity drugs against
    placebo on both weight loss and adverse events — a model for when indirect comparisons
    are needed and how to report comparative effectiveness with uncertainty rather than a
    single winner. See
    [`../../skills/jama-statistics/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jama-statistics/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** if you use a network/indirect comparison, are the assumptions and the
    adverse-event picture reported as carefully as the efficacy ranking?

## Omitted for venue mismatch or lack of confirmation (do not attribute to JAMA without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination and guard against **sibling-venue confusion**, the
following were **excluded** after checking:

- **SPRINT, "A Randomized Trial of Intensive vs Standard Blood-Pressure Control" (2015)** —
  the primary results appeared in the ***New England Journal of Medicine*** (2015;
  373:2103-2116), **not JAMA**. Several SPRINT *secondary analyses* appear in *JAMA
  Cardiology* and *JAMA Network Open*, which are **specialty journals, not JAMA itself**.
  Listed here only as a guardrail.
- **Yang Q, et al, "Added Sugar Intake and Cardiovascular Diseases Mortality Among US Adults"
  (2014)** — published in ***JAMA Internal Medicine*** (a JAMA Network specialty journal),
  **not JAMA**. Excluded to avoid sibling confusion.
- **Maternal-sepsis incidence and SPRINT cognitive/residual-lifespan analyses** surfaced
  during searching but resolved to *JAMA* vs *JAMA Network Open* / *JAMA Cardiology*
  variously; only items confirmed on a `jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/...` page (or a
  `10.1001/jama....` DOI for JAMA proper) were included above.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on
`jamanetwork.com/journals/jama` (the article page must read **JAMA**, not a specialty
journal) with volume/issue, verify the DOI begins `10.1001/jama.` for JAMA proper, and
update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
