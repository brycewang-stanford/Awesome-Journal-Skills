# Worked Example: An ESR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **ESR introduction arc** that the pack's own skills require. ESR is Europe's
**quantitative-sociology flagship**, so the introduction must do four things by the time it ends
(`eursr-writing-style` → "front-load the contribution"; `eursr-topic-selection` → the four-part fit
test):

1. **The question**, stated plainly for a comparative quantitative sociologist.
2. **The argument** — a named **macro–micro mechanism** with a **cross-level hypothesis** that *travels*
   (`eursr-theory-building`'s portability test), not just a list of significant predictors.
3. **The evidence** — what **comparative or longitudinal leverage** adjudicates the leading rival
   account (`eursr-research-design`'s adjudication sentence).
4. **Why it matters across European contexts** — the discipline-level stakes, with **scope conditions**.

ESR favors comparative cross-national and longitudinal designs with rigorous modeling. This example
takes a **multilevel comparative** angle — the combination the skills treat as the central ESR target.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Buffered Risk: How Active Labour-Market Policy Conditions the
Unemployment Scar across 24 European Countries."* Fictional setting: a harmonized panel linking
individual employment histories to country-level activation spending.

---

## Before (national/results-first, buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Unemployment has long been studied in the sociological literature on labour markets. A number of
> studies have examined the consequences of unemployment for later wages, and there is debate about
> whether these effects persist. In this paper, we use panel data from several European countries to
> examine the relationship between past unemployment and subsequent wages. We estimate a series of
> fixed-effects models and find that past unemployment is associated with lower wages, and that this
> association varies across countries. This question has not been studied with this combination of data
> before. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes the
> data, Section 4 presents the models, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Results-first, no mechanism.** "Is associated with lower wages" with cross-country variation noted
  but unexplained is the named anti-pattern in `eursr-theory-building` ("a list of significant
  predictors"). No **macro–micro mechanism** and no **cross-level hypothesis** → fails the
  **portability test**.
- **"Not studied with this data before" as the contribution** — exactly the `eursr-topic-selection`
  anti-pattern ("'not yet studied' as the whole contribution").
- **Comparative leverage wasted.** The country variation is reported but does no theoretical work; ESR
  exists to exploit exactly this variation (`eursr-research-design`).
- **No adjudication.** The design is named ("fixed-effects models") but we are never told what rival
  account it rules out.
- **Contribution buried** and an **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's job
  (`eursr-writing-style`).

---

## After (ESR arc — question + macro–micro mechanism + comparative leverage + stakes, front-loaded)

> **Why does losing a job leave a lasting mark on some workers' wages but not others — and why does the
> size of that mark differ so sharply across Europe?** We argue that the answer lies in *institutional
> buffering*: where active labour-market policy quickly re-attaches the unemployed to work, a spell
> signals less to future employers and disrupts careers less, so the individual **scar** is smaller. The
> scar, in other words, is not a fixed property of unemployment but a **macro–micro** outcome — a
> consequence of how a national policy regime reshapes the individual penalty. *(question + argument in
> the first breath; the concept, "institutional buffering," and the mechanism are named immediately.)*
>
> The claim matters beyond labour-market sociology. Stratification research increasingly asks how welfare
> institutions condition the individual-level reproduction of disadvantage; here we show one such process
> in motion — a country-level policy moderating a within-person penalty — rather than assuming the
> penalty is constant. If the mechanism holds, it reframes a range of "scarring" findings (from illness,
> divorce, or migration) as instances of the *same* buffering logic, legible to sociologists of work,
> the welfare state, and the life course alike. *(why it matters across European contexts — the
> contribution is portable, stated against a specific prior view: scarring as institution-conditioned,
> not fixed.)*
>
> Identifying this is hard because countries with strong activation policy also differ in many other
> ways, and a simple cross-country comparison conflates **buffering** with everything else that varies
> between societies. We therefore exploit **within-person** wage change after a spell (holding the worker
> constant) and ask whether the *size* of that within-person scar varies with country-level activation
> spending — a **cross-level interaction** that a single cross-section cannot identify. The adjudication
> is sharp: if buffering drives our results, the scar should shrink as activation spending rises *net of*
> who becomes unemployed; if instead composition drove it, the gradient would vanish once we compare
> workers to themselves. We find the former. *(evidence and the `eursr-research-design` adjudication
> sentence: what we'd see if the rival were true vs. what we see — and the design that separates them.)*
>
> Our contribution is theoretical as well as empirical: we specify the **scar as an institution-
> conditioned quantity** and state its **scope conditions** — buffering operates where activation
> genuinely speeds re-employment, among prime-age workers, and is weaker where spells are short for other
> reasons. With ~24 countries the macro evidence rests on few clusters, so we keep the country-level claim
> modest and confirm it with few-cluster-robust inference. *(contribution + scope conditions + honest
> macro-N posture — `eursr-theory-building`, `eursr-data-analysis`.)* Beyond scarring, the lesson is that
> **the individual costs of social risk are written partly by national institutions** — a mechanism a
> sociologist studying any state-buffered risk can import.
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. We develop the buffering argument and its cross-level implication, then
> present the panel design and the within-person test that separates buffering from composition, and close
> with scope conditions and what institution-conditioned scarring implies for theories of cumulative
> disadvantage. *(brief roadmap — carries the argument, not a table of contents.)*

## Why the "after" meets the ESR bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded** — by the end of the intro the reader has the **question, the macro–micro
  argument, the comparative leverage, and the stakes** (`eursr-writing-style`: "by the end of the
  introduction the reader knows the question, the mechanism and hypotheses, the comparative/longitudinal
  leverage, and what the paper changes").
- **A mechanism that travels** — "institutional buffering" / the institution-conditioned scar is offered
  for others to import, with explicit **scope conditions**; it passes the `eursr-theory-building`
  **portability test** ("could a sociologist studying a different institution import this?").
- **The cross-level interaction is the headline** — the macro × micro hypothesis (activation × the
  within-person scar) is exactly what `eursr-theory-building` and `eursr-research-design` treat as the
  ESR contribution, and what the comparative panel can identify.
- **Design adjudicates the leading rival** — the buffering-vs-composition **adjudication sentence** is
  written out, and the within-person comparison is matched to it (`eursr-research-design`).
- **Honest macro-N posture** — the ~24-cluster limit is stated and few-cluster-robust inference promised
  (`eursr-data-analysis`), rather than over-claiming a country-level effect.
- **No "not-studied-before" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named
  `eursr-topic-selection` / `eursr-writing-style` anti-patterns are avoided.

> One-sentence hook (fillable, as the skills imply): "We show that **national activation policy shrinks
> the individual wage scar of unemployment** by **buffering re-employment**, using **a cross-level
> interaction in a 24-country panel** to separate buffering from composition."
