# Worked Example: A Lancet-Style Abstract + Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the artifacts a Lancet original-research **Article** opens with, derived from this
pack's own skills:

- the **structured abstract** under the five exact headings **Background / Methods / Findings /
  Interpretation / Funding** (`lancet-abstract`) — "Findings" not "Results", "Interpretation" not
  "Conclusions", with the registration number in Methods and the funder named;
- the **Research in context** panel's three headed parts — *Evidence before this study* (a documented
  systematic search), *Added value of this study*, *Implications of all the available evidence*
  (`lancet-research-in-context`);
- a brief, **CONSORT**-anchored Introduction + Methods opening that promises the flow diagram and
  pre-specified primary analysis (`lancet-reporting`, `lancet-statistics`, `lancet-writing`).

**Illustrative trial (fictional):** *"Early oral rehydration-plus-zinc versus standard oral rehydration
for acute childhood diarrhoea in low-resource settings (the FICTIONAL-ORZ trial): a multicentre,
double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trial."* Fictional setting: 14 hospitals across four
low-income and middle-income countries. **All numbers below are invented.**

---

## Before (a non-Lancet first draft)

> **Abstract.** *Results.* We tested whether adding zinc to oral rehydration helps children with
> diarrhoea. We enrolled children and gave some of them zinc. The zinc group did better (p<0.05). *Conclusions.*
> Zinc is beneficial and should be used. More research is needed.
>
> **Introduction.** Diarrhoea has been studied extensively. Many trials have looked at rehydration. In
> this paper we use a randomised design and a logistic regression model to study zinc supplementation.
> Diarrhoea is an important problem. We report our results below.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Wrong abstract headings** — "Results"/"Conclusions" instead of **Findings**/**Interpretation**, and
  the mandatory **Background / Methods / Funding** sections are missing (`lancet-abstract`).
- **No registration number and no funder** in the abstract (`lancet-abstract`, `lancet-study-design`).
- **No effect size and no 95% CI** — only a bare "p<0.05", which `lancet-statistics` rejects in favour of
  confidence intervals with absolute *and* relative effects.
- **Interpretation ends on "more research is needed"** — a named anti-pattern; it must state the
  practice/policy takeaway calibrated to the design (`lancet-abstract`, `lancet-writing`).
- **No Research in context panel**, so the evidence base rests on "studied extensively" rather than a
  documented systematic search (`lancet-research-in-context`).
- **Introduction leads with method** ("we use a logistic regression model") and is a literature-review
  throat-clear rather than problem → gap → objective (`lancet-writing`).
- **No CONSORT flow / analysis population** promised; ITT is never mentioned (`lancet-reporting`,
  `lancet-statistics`).

## After (Lancet structured abstract — five exact headings)

> **Background** Acute diarrhoea remains a leading cause of death in children younger than 5 years in
> low-resource settings. Whether adding zinc to early oral rehydration shortens illness and reduces
> treatment failure, compared with oral rehydration alone, is uncertain in this population.
>
> **Methods** We did a multicentre, double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trial in 14 hospitals in
> four low-income and middle-income countries. Eligible participants were children aged 6–59 months
> presenting with acute non-bloody diarrhoea and some dehydration. We randomly assigned participants
> (1:1) to oral rehydration solution plus oral zinc or to oral rehydration solution plus matching
> placebo, stratified by site and age band, with allocation concealment and masking of participants,
> caregivers, clinicians, and outcome assessors. The primary outcome was treatment failure by day 7
> (a composite of persistent dehydration, intravenous rehydration, or death) in the intention-to-treat
> population. This trial is registered with ClinicalTrials.gov, NCT00000000 *(fictional)*.
>
> **Findings** Between Jan 1, 2023, and Dec 31, 2024 *(fictional)*, we screened 4210 children and randomly
> assigned 3600 (1800 to zinc, 1800 to placebo). Treatment failure by day 7 occurred in 198 (11·0%) of
> 1800 children in the zinc group versus 306 (17·0%) of 1800 in the placebo group (absolute risk
> difference −6·0 percentage points, 95% CI −8·3 to −3·7; relative risk 0·65, 95% CI 0·55 to 0·77;
> p<0·0001; number needed to treat 17). Median time to resolution of diarrhoea was shorter with zinc
> (key secondary outcome). Serious adverse events occurred in 21 (1·2%) versus 24 (1·3%) children; no
> deaths were attributed to the intervention. *(All numbers fictional.)*
>
> **Interpretation** In children with acute diarrhoea and some dehydration in low-resource settings,
> adding zinc to early oral rehydration reduced day-7 treatment failure by about a third in relative terms
> and 6 percentage points in absolute terms, supporting its use alongside oral rehydration in first-line
> management where the disease burden is greatest.
>
> **Funding** Fictional Global Child Health Foundation.

## After (Research in context panel — three headed parts)

> **Research in context**
>
> **Evidence before this study**
> We searched MEDLINE (via PubMed), Embase, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, and the
> Global Index Medicus for randomised controlled trials published from database inception to Dec 1, 2022,
> using the terms ("zinc") AND ("oral rehydration" OR "diarrhoea" OR "diarrhea") AND ("child*"), with no
> language restriction; the search was last updated on Dec 1, 2022. We included randomised trials in
> children younger than 5 years. Prior trials suggested zinc may shorten diarrhoea duration, but most were
> single-country, varied in dehydration severity at entry, and were judged to provide low-to-moderate
> certainty evidence for treatment failure as an outcome. *(Fictional synthesis.)*
>
> **Added value of this study**
> This multicentre, placebo-controlled trial across four countries provides a pre-specified, adequately
> powered estimate of the effect of adding zinc to early oral rehydration on a day-7 treatment-failure
> composite, reported as both an absolute and a relative effect, in children with some dehydration in
> low-resource settings. *(Fictional.)*
>
> **Implications of all the available evidence**
> Taken with prior trials, these findings support adding zinc to early oral rehydration in first-line
> management of acute childhood diarrhoea in low-resource settings; from a policy perspective they inform
> programmes serving the populations carrying most of the disease burden, and future work should examine
> longer-term outcomes and implementation. *(Fictional; no real policy is invented.)*

---

## After (Introduction — brief, problem → gap → objective)

> Acute diarrhoea is among the leading causes of death in children younger than 5 years, and most of that
> burden falls on low-income and middle-income settings. Oral rehydration is the cornerstone of
> management, yet treatment failure remains common, and whether adding zinc to *early* oral rehydration
> reduces failure in children who already have some dehydration is unresolved. *(problem and gap — the
> systematic search lives in the panel, not in a long literature review.)*
>
> We did a multicentre, double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled trial to test, against a
> pre-specified intention-to-treat primary analysis, whether oral rehydration plus zinc lowers day-7
> treatment failure compared with oral rehydration plus placebo. *(specific objective; the design and
> ITT primary analysis are named up front; participant flow is reported with a CONSORT flow diagram.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the Lancet bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Abstract headings are exact** — Background / Methods / **Findings** / **Interpretation** / Funding,
  not Results/Conclusions (`lancet-abstract`).
- **Registration number in Methods and funder named** in the abstract (`lancet-abstract`,
  `lancet-study-design`).
- **Primary outcome carries an effect size with a 95% CI, absolute *and* relative, with NNT** and an exact
  P value alongside (not instead of) the CI; the ITT analysis population is stated (`lancet-statistics`).
- **Interpretation states the practice implication calibrated to the design** — "supporting its use", not
  "proves", and not "more research is needed" as the whole message (`lancet-abstract`, `lancet-writing`).
- **Research in context panel has all three headed parts**, and *Evidence before this study* documents a
  real systematic search (databases + terms + dates + language) rather than asserting what was known
  (`lancet-research-in-context`).
- **CONSORT discipline is promised** — a flow diagram for the RCT, screened/assigned numbers that
  reconcile with the abstract, and harms reported (`lancet-reporting`).
- **Introduction is brief and objective-led**, with the method demoted to a tool and no literature-review
  throat-clearing (`lancet-writing`).
