# The American Historical Review (the-american-historical-review)

## Journal positioning

The American Historical Review (AHR) is the flagship journal of the American
Historical Association and a generalist venue for the whole discipline of history,
publishing original research of significance across all fields, periods, and world
regions. Its defining expectation is an argument whose interest reaches **beyond the
specialist subfield** — a contribution that historians of other periods and places
will find consequential — grounded in deep primary research and an explicit
historiographical intervention. A meticulous but narrowly specialist study with no
broader stake is a poor fit, however sound. This skill is a **fit / venue-selection /
re-framing** aid. It does not replace the journal's current submission guidelines.
Before submitting, re-check the live AHR author instructions and style guide.

## When to trigger

- The author names AHR for a history manuscript and wants a fit/framing check.
- A deeply researched but specialist study must be re-framed to show its
  discipline-wide significance and historiographical stake.
- The author is choosing between AHR and a period/area-specialist history journal.
- The author needs AHR's argument-significance bar and desk-reject heuristics.

## Scope & topic fit

- Original historical arguments in any field, period, or region, when the stake is
  legible to non-specialists.
- Work that reframes a historiographical debate or opens a new line of inquiry, not
  merely adding a case to a settled one.
- Transnational, comparative, and methodologically innovative history (including the
  history of ideas, the body, the environment, capitalism, empire, and the digital).
- Studies that put a rich archive to conceptual work, rather than reporting findings
  for their own sake.

## Method & evidence bar

- The contribution is an **original, clearly stated argument** with significance
  beyond the subfield; "we know more about X" is not enough — name the intervention.
- Primary-source command must be deep and appropriate to the field; archival, textual,
  or material evidence is read critically, not merely cited.
- The historiographical positioning is explicit: what conversation the piece enters and
  how it changes it.
- Interpretation is controlled by evidence; claims are proportionate to the sources and
  alert to their silences and biases.
- Engagement with relevant scholarship across (not only within) the subfield
  demonstrates the broader stake.

## Structure & house style

- Long-form scholarly article with a sustained argument; re-check current word limits
  and article types (articles, AHR's distinctive features) on the live guide.
- Chicago notes-and-bibliography style with full footnotes; archival citations follow
  the journal's form.
- Double-blind review: anonymize the manuscript (remove self-identifying citations and
  acknowledgements) per current policy.
- Prose is argument-driven and accessible to historians outside the subfield; foreign-
  language sources are quoted and translated per policy.
- Images and figures, where used, require reproduction permissions and meet the
  journal's specifications.

## Official-submission checklist

- Before giving submission-ready advice, read `../../resources/source-basis.md` and
  `../../resources/official-source-map.md`; start from the AHA / OUP anchors, then cite
  the current AHR page you checked.
- Search the live site for "American Historical Review submission guidelines" and follow
  the current version.
- Re-check article types and word limits, the Chicago citation/footnote form, and the
  abstract requirement.
- Confirm anonymization requirements for double-blind review.
- Re-check image/illustration permissions and specifications, and any
  translation/transliteration policy for non-English sources.
- Re-check competing-interest, funding (if applicable), and AI-use disclosure, and any
  open-access terms.
- If the live official instructions conflict with this skill, the official instructions
  win.

## Pre-submission self-check

- [ ] The argument's significance is legible to historians outside the subfield, not only to specialists.
- [ ] The historiographical intervention is named explicitly — what debate it enters and how it changes it.
- [ ] Primary sources are read critically and the evidence supports the claims, including their limits.
- [ ] The manuscript is anonymized for double-blind review per current policy.
- [ ] Chicago footnotes/citations and any required abstract follow the current style guide.
- [ ] Image permissions and translations of non-English sources are handled.

## Common desk-reject triggers

- A sound but narrowly specialist study with no significance beyond the subfield.
- An argument that adds a case to a settled debate without reframing it.
- Thin or uncritical use of primary sources, or claims out of proportion to the evidence.
- No explicit historiographical positioning ("the literature says… and so do I").
- Wrong venue: a piece pitched to one area community that a specialist journal would serve better.

## Re-routing decision

- Society/economy/culture history with a problem-driven argument → `past-and-present`.
- Modern European or transnational focus → `the-journal-of-modern-history`.
- British, medieval, or early-modern archival study → `the-english-historical-review`.
- Early-American / Atlantic-world history → `the-william-and-mary-quarterly`.
- Argument about historical method or epistemology → `history-and-theory`.

```text
[Argument] <the intervention in one line — what debate it changes>

[Re-route suggestion] <if not a fit, a better-matched venue>
```
