# Past & Present (past-and-present)

## Journal positioning

Past & Present, published by Oxford University Press for the Past and Present
Society, is a flagship of social, economic, and cultural history with a defining
**problem-driven ethos**: it favors articles built around a "big question" — a
genuine historical problem whose resolution speaks to processes of social change,
power, and structure — rather than a self-contained narrative or an additive case
study. Its house tradition is comparative, conceptually ambitious, and willing to
range across periods and societies when the question demands it; what unites the
journal is the conviction that a particular episode should illuminate something
broadly significant about how societies work and change. A meticulous local study
that never frames its larger stake is a poor fit, however well-sourced. This skill
is a **fit / venue-selection / re-framing** aid. It does not replace the journal's
current submission guidelines. Before submitting, re-check the live Past & Present
author instructions and style guide.

## When to trigger

- The author names Past & Present for a social, economic, or cultural history
  manuscript and wants a fit/framing check.
- A well-sourced local or specialist study must be re-framed around a larger
  historical problem and its comparative significance.
- The author is choosing between Past & Present and a generalist venue (AHR) or a
  period/area-specialist journal.
- The author needs the journal's problem-driven argument bar and desk-reject
  heuristics.

## Scope & topic fit

- Social history of class, community, labor, popular politics, protest, and the
  structures of everyday power.
- Economic history treated as social process — markets, property, subsistence,
  inequality — when the stake is interpretive rather than purely quantitative.
- Cultural history of belief, ritual, mentalities, and the symbolic order, read for
  what it reveals about social relations.
- Comparative and connective history across regions or periods when the comparison
  drives the argument rather than decorating it.
- Studies of social change — transitions, crises, long-run transformations — that
  recast a received explanatory model.
- Work that puts a rich body of sources to conceptual work on a defined historical
  problem, not findings reported for their own sake.

## Method & evidence bar

- The contribution is an **original argument organized around a real historical
  problem**; name the question and why its answer matters beyond the case.
- Primary-source command is deep and critically handled — archival, quantitative, or
  cultural evidence read against its silences, not merely catalogued.
- Historiographical positioning is explicit: which interpretive debate the piece
  enters and how it revises the prevailing model.
- Interpretation is controlled by evidence; large claims about social change are
  proportioned to what the sources can bear.
- Engagement reaches across subfields, with comparison or wider framing integral to the
  argument and any social-science concepts used with historical discipline, not as ornament.

## Structure & house style

- Long-form scholarly article sustaining a single argument; defer exact word limits
  and article types to the live guide.
- Chicago notes-and-bibliography style with full footnotes; archival and quantitative
  citations follow the journal's form.
- Double-blind review: anonymize the manuscript — remove self-identifying citations
  and acknowledgements — per current policy.
- Prose is argument-led and legible to historians outside the subfield; non-English
  sources are quoted and translated per policy.
- Tables, figures, or images, where used, require permissions and meet the journal's
  specifications.

## Official-submission checklist

- Before giving submission-ready advice, read `../../resources/source-basis.md` and
  `../../resources/official-source-map.md`; start from the OUP / Past and Present
  Society anchors, then cite the current Past & Present page you checked.
- Search the live site for "Past and Present submission guidelines" and follow the
  current version.
- Re-check article types, word limits, the Chicago footnote form, and the abstract
  requirement.
- Confirm anonymization requirements for double-blind review.
- Re-check image/table permissions and specifications, and the translation policy for
  non-English sources.
- Re-check competing-interest, funding (if applicable), and AI-use disclosure, and any
  open-access terms.
- If the live official instructions conflict with this skill, the official instructions
  win.

## Pre-submission self-check

- [ ] The article is built around a stated historical problem, not just a narrative or an additive case.
- [ ] The larger significance — what the case reveals about social change or power — is explicit.
- [ ] Primary sources are read critically and claims are proportioned to the evidence.
- [ ] The historiographical intervention names the model it revises.
- [ ] The manuscript is anonymized for double-blind review per current policy.
- [ ] Chicago footnotes, any abstract, and translations of non-English sources are handled.

## Common desk-reject triggers

- A well-sourced local study with no larger problem or comparative stake.
- Narrative or descriptive history that never frames an interpretive question.
- Thin or uncritical source use, or claims about social change out of proportion to the evidence.
- No explicit historiographical positioning — restating the literature without revising it, or quantitative material reported for its own sake.
- Wrong venue: a piece whose real audience is one area community a specialist journal serves better.

## Re-routing decision

- Discipline-wide significance for a generalist readership → `the-american-historical-review`.
- Modern European or transnational focus with archival depth → `the-journal-of-modern-history`.
- British, medieval, or early-modern empirical study → `the-english-historical-review`.
- Everyday-life, class, gender, or marginalized-group focus with methodological self-consciousness → `journal-of-social-history`.
- Argument about historical method, periodization, or explanation itself → `history-and-theory`.
