## How to use this library

JOP is a **general-interest, methodologically diverse** journal that makes acceptance **contingent on
replicability** (see [`../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md)
and [`../../skills/jop-replication-and-data-policy/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jop-replication-and-data-policy/SKILL.md)).
A paper earns its place by making a **testable** argument whose design a replication analyst could re-run,
legible to readers across subfields (see
[`../../skills/jop-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jop-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/jop-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/jop-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the row
whose **subfield × method** is closest to yours, then run the self-check before claiming your paper is
"JOP-shaped."

## By method

### Historical-persistence observational causal inference (American politics / race)

- **Acharya, Blackwell & Sen, "The Political Legacy of American Slavery," JOP 2016, 78(3):621–641.**
  Verified: University of Chicago Press `10.1086/686631` (journal page lists vol. 78, no. 3, pp. 621–641);
  cross-checked SSRN abstract 2538616.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** links a historical treatment (county slave share in 1860) to present-day
    attitudes and defends the **persistence mechanism** against confounding with an explicit causal
    framework rather than a raw cross-sectional correlation — the
    [`jop-research-design`](../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md) "name the source of variation and
    the rival" move applied to a deep-roots question.
  - **Self-check:** does your persistence claim isolate a plausibly exogenous historical treatment and
    confront the obvious confounds, rather than reading a long-run correlation as a legacy?

### Field experiment in a comparative setting (comparative politics / political communication)

- **Conroy-Krutz & Moehler, "Moderation from Bias: A Field Experiment on Partisan Media in a New Democracy," JOP 2015, 77(2):575–587.**
  Verified: University of Chicago Press `10.1086/680187` (journal page lists vol. 77, no. 2, pp. 575–587).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** randomizes exposure to pro-government, pro-opposition, neutral, or no
    talk-radio in a real transit setting, turning a question about partisan media effects into a clean
    **field experiment** with a manipulation a replication analyst can audit — the experimental-design and
    reproducibility bar in [`jop-research-design`](../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** is your treatment a realistic, well-defined manipulation delivered in the field, with
    randomization and analysis specified so the result can be re-run?

### Regression discontinuity / external validity (American politics / methods)

- **Hainmueller, Hall & Snyder, "Assessing the External Validity of Election RD Estimates: An Investigation of the Incumbency Advantage," JOP 2015, 77(3):707–720.**
  Verified: University of Chicago Press `10.1086/681238` (journal page lists vol. 77, no. 3, pp. 707–720);
  replication on the JOP Dataverse.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** confronts the central limitation of the **close-election RD design** — that
    it identifies an effect only at the 50–50 threshold — and estimates how the incumbency advantage
    behaves away from the cutoff, an honest treatment of *what the estimand is and is not* (the
    [`jop-research-design`](../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md) "state the estimand" discipline).
  - **Self-check:** have you said exactly *where* your design identifies an effect, and probed whether it
    travels beyond that point — rather than over-claiming external validity?

### Formal / game-theoretic model of institutions (comparative politics / authoritarianism)

- **Boix & Svolik, "The Foundations of Limited Authoritarian Government: Institutions, Commitment, and Power-Sharing in Dictatorships," JOP 2013, 75(2):300–316.**
  Verified: University of Chicago Press / prior-publisher DOI `10.1017/S0022381613000029` (journal page
  lists vol. 75, no. 2, pp. 300–316).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent model shows how parties, legislatures, and councils solve a
    **commitment and monitoring problem** between a dictator and his allies — yielding a portable
    explanation for *why authoritarian institutions exist* rather than describing one regime. The
    [`jop-theory-building`](../../skills/jop-theory-building/SKILL.md) formal-mode bar: a mechanism that
    generates comparative statics, not a just-so story.
  - **Self-check:** does your model deliver a generalizable, testable implication about institutional form,
    legible to a comparativist who does not work on your region?

### Formal model with empirical implications (comparative politics / elections)

- **Chernykh & Svolik, "Third-Party Actors and the Success of Democracy: How Electoral Commissions, Courts, and Observers Shape Incentives for Electoral Manipulation and Post-Election Protests," JOP 2015, 77(2):407–420.**
  Verified: University of Chicago Press `10.1086/679598` (journal page lists vol. 77, no. 2, pp. 407–420).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a model of self-enforcing compliance shows that third parties (commissions,
    courts, observers) matter most in **close elections**, where the threat of protest alone cannot deter
    manipulation — a conditional, testable prediction that ties an institutional design question to
    observable election outcomes ([`jop-theory-building`](../../skills/jop-theory-building/SKILL.md) →
    [`jop-research-design`](../../skills/jop-research-design/SKILL.md)).
  - **Self-check:** does your formal result give a *conditional* implication (when the effect should be
    strong vs. weak) that the data can confront, rather than an unconditional claim?

## Omitted for lack of JOP verification (do NOT attribute to JOP without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not JOP. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Acharya, Blackwell & Sen, "Explaining Causal Findings Without Bias: Detecting and Assessing Direct
  Effects" (2016)** — verified in the *American Political Science Review* 110(3):512–529, **not JOP**.
  (The same authors' "Political Legacy of American Slavery" *is* JOP and is listed above — do not conflate
  the methods paper with the slavery paper.)
- **Gerber & Huber, "Partisanship, Political Control, and Economic Assessments" (2010)** — verified in the
  *American Journal of Political Science* 54(1):153–173, **not JOP**.
- **Fearon, "Self-Enforcing Democracy" (2011)** — verified in the *Quarterly Journal of Economics*
  126(4):1661–1708, **not JOP**.
- **Eggers, Folke, Fowler, Hainmueller, Hall & Snyder, "On the Validity of the Regression Discontinuity
  Design … 40,000 Close Races" (2015)** — verified in the *American Journal of Political Science*
  59(1):259–274, **not JOP**. (The related Hainmueller, Hall & Snyder external-validity paper above *is*
  JOP — same RD-on-elections topic, different venue.)
- **Gerber, Green & Larimer, "Social Pressure and Voter Turnout" (2008)** — verified in the *American
  Political Science Review* 102(1):33–48, **not JOP**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on the University of Chicago Press JOP site
(`journals.uchicago.edu/.../10.1086/*` or legacy `10.1017/S0022381*`) with volume/issue/pages, cross-check
the JOP Dataverse where applicable, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
