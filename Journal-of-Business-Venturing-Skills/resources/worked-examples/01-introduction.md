# Worked Example: A JBV-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JBV introduction arc** that the pack's skills jointly prescribe:
**entrepreneurial phenomenon first → why it is a genuine puzzle → the mechanism (multidisciplinary lens)
→ what the study does → what the field now understands about entrepreneurship → contribution stated
explicitly → brief roadmap.** The governing rules from this pack:

- `jbv-writing-style`: **open on the phenomenon, not a gap or a method**; front-load question, mechanism,
  and contribution; write so an economist *and* a psychologist both follow; abstract-style economy.
- `jbv-topic-selection`: entrepreneurship must be the **central contribution, not the empirical setting**;
  frame as a **phenomenon-puzzle, not gap-spotting**; name the disciplinary lens.
- `jbv-theory-development`: a mechanism **specific to the entrepreneurial phenomenon** (not any
  organization), with a named engine (economics / psychology / sociology) and scope conditions.
- `jbv-contribution-framing`: the contribution must **challenge an assumption / resolve a tension**, be
  stated explicitly, and carry boundary conditions.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Quitting on Time: How Founders' Fear of Wasted Effort Shapes When
Nascent Ventures Are Abandoned."* Fictional setting: a fictional multi-wave panel of 900 nascent
entrepreneurs tracked from first founding activity through either first sale or disengagement.

---

## Before (buries the phenomenon — typical first-draft intro)

> The entrepreneurship literature has examined many antecedents of new-venture outcomes. Prior work has
> studied resources, networks, and human capital. However, less attention has been paid to how
> psychological factors relate to venture persistence. In this paper, we use a discrete-time hazard model
> on a panel of nascent entrepreneurs to test the relationship between a sunk-cost measure and the
> probability of venture disengagement, controlling for human capital and industry. Persistence is an
> important outcome for entrepreneurship research and practice. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3
> describes the data, Section 4 presents the method, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Opens on a literature gap and a method**, not on the entrepreneurial phenomenon — the exact
  anti-pattern named in `jbv-writing-style` ("method-first opening that hides the entrepreneurial
  stakes") and `jbv-topic-selection` ("gap-spotting as the sole justification").
- **Gap-spotting** ("less attention has been paid") instead of a phenomenon-puzzle.
- **No entrepreneurial mechanism** — "psychological factors relate to persistence" could describe any
  organization's employees; `jbv-theory-development` calls this "generic-org theory in a startup costume."
- **Contribution is implicit** — the reader must infer the "so what for entrepreneurship theory"
  (`jbv-contribution-framing` anti-pattern: "implicit contribution").
- **Method leads** ("we use a discrete-time hazard model") before the question is even posed.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (JBV arc — phenomenon first, mechanism, explicit contribution)

> **Every founder who keeps going is also choosing not to quit — yet we know far more about why people
> start ventures than about how they decide to stop.** A nascent entrepreneur who has poured a year of
> unpaid evenings into a venture faces a recurring, wrenching judgment: persist in the face of weak
> signals, or disengage and reclaim the time. We study that abandonment decision, and show that founders
> who have invested more *effort* — not more money — quit **later**, even when the venture's prospects are
> objectively no better. *(phenomenon made vivid first; the puzzle and the headline both in the opening
> breath — `jbv-writing-style`.)*
>
> This is genuinely hard to explain. A rational-search account says founders should abandon a venture the
> moment expected returns fall below the value of their next-best use of time, and prior sunk effort
> should be irrelevant to that forward-looking call. *(why it is a real puzzle, stated against a specific
> prior view — not gap-spotting.)*
>
> We argue the entrepreneurial setting itself creates the bias. Founding is an act of **identity
> investment under Knightian uncertainty**: because a nascent venture has no market verdict yet, the
> founder cannot tell a temporary setback from a fatal one, so accumulated personal effort becomes a
> salient — and misleading — signal of the venture's worth. The more of *themselves* founders have put in,
> the more abandoning the venture reads as repudiating their own judgment, and the longer they persist.
> *(a mechanism specific to the entrepreneurial phenomenon, with a named engine that integrates
> psychology — identity, escalation — with economics — uncertainty, search; `jbv-theory-development`.)*
>
> We test this with a fictional multi-wave panel of 900 nascent entrepreneurs tracked from their first
> founding activity until either a first sale or disengagement, with effort and money invested measured
> *before* outcomes are known so that temporal precedence supports the mechanism claim. Founders in the
> top quartile of cumulative effort disengage **about 7 months later** than otherwise-comparable founders,
> and the effect is concentrated where venture quality signals are weakest — exactly where the
> identity-as-signal mechanism predicts it should bite, and absent once an external funder or first
> customer supplies a verdict. *(what the study does and the headline result restated with the mechanism;
> the moderator is an entrepreneurial scope condition, not a generic covariate.)*
>
> **Our contribution is to recast founder persistence as identity defense, not just optimistic
> forecasting.** The field has largely treated persistence as a virtue — grit, passion, commitment — or as
> a forecasting error about returns; we show a distinct, entrepreneurship-specific channel in which the
> *absence of a market verdict* turns sunk effort into an identity signal that delays abandonment, and we
> bound where it holds: it weakens sharply once any external party prices the venture. *(contribution
> stated explicitly, challenging an assumption, with boundary conditions — `jbv-contribution-framing`.)*
> This reframing matters beyond this sample: it implies that **anything supplying an early external
> verdict — an accelerator gate, a first paying customer, a structured kill-criterion — should shorten
> wasteful persistence**, which speaks to investors and ecosystem designers, though the theoretical payoff
> for how entrepreneurs decide to *stop* comes first. *(practical relevance, kept secondary to theory.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the identity-as-signal mechanism and its boundary
> conditions; Section 3 describes the nascent-entrepreneur panel and the hazard design; Section 4 reports
> the persistence results and the verdict-availability moderation. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JBV bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Opens on the entrepreneurial phenomenon, vivid and first** — the founder's abandonment judgment, not
  a gap or the hazard model (`jbv-writing-style`: "opening makes the entrepreneurial phenomenon vivid").
- **Phenomenon-puzzle, not gap-spotting** — the rational-search benchmark makes the finding *surprising*,
  satisfying `jbv-topic-selection`'s "framed as a phenomenon-puzzle, not a literature gap."
- **Entrepreneurship is the contribution, not the setting** — the mechanism (no market verdict →
  effort-as-identity-signal) is *specific to nascent venturing* and would not read identically for an
  incumbent's project, clearing `jbv-theory-development`'s "generic-org theory in a startup costume" test.
- **Multidisciplinary mechanism, signposted** — psychology (identity, escalation) is integrated with
  economics (Knightian uncertainty, search) so both audiences follow (`jbv-writing-style`,
  `jbv-theory-development`).
- **Contribution explicit, with an assumption challenged and boundary conditions** — persistence recast
  from virtue/forecasting to identity defense, bounded to the no-verdict regime
  (`jbv-contribution-framing`).
- **Temporal precedence and a phenomenon-appropriate moderator** are previewed, anticipating the design
  demands of `jbv-methods` and `jbv-data-analysis` (selection into founding, survivorship, hazard timing).
- **Method is demoted to a tool**, named only where the design is described, never as the lead.
- **Practice kept secondary to theory** — the investor/ecosystem implication appears after the
  theoretical contribution, as `jbv-contribution-framing` requires.
