# Worked Example: A *Psychological Bulletin* Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the introduction arc *Psychological Bulletin* rewards for a **meta-analytic
synthesis** (not a primary study, and not freestanding theory — that belongs in *Psychological Review*):
**the open question the field cannot resolve → why a synthesis can settle it → the pooled effect with
its interval and heterogeneity → the moderator that explains the disagreement → an honest bias check →
the integrative contribution, stated early → brief roadmap.** *Bulletin*'s first gate
([`psychbull-topic-selection`](../../skills/psychbull-topic-selection/SKILL.md)) is **synthesis fit**:
there must be a locatable body of comparable studies worth pooling, and the contribution is **integrating
prior studies**, not reporting new data. The craft from
[`psychbull-writing-style`](../../skills/psychbull-writing-style/SKILL.md) is to write the introduction
as an **evaluative argument** — the open question and what a synthesis resolves — not a study-by-study
recap, while the methods satisfy **MARS/PRISMA** item by item.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Does Brief Mindfulness Training Reduce Test Anxiety? A Random-
Effects Meta-Analysis of 96 Controlled Studies, With Moderators and Bias Diagnostics."* Fictional
setting: a PRISMA-documented synthesis of controlled trials of short mindfulness interventions on
self-reported test anxiety.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Mindfulness has been studied extensively, and many studies have examined its effects on anxiety. The
> literature is large and somewhat mixed, with some studies finding effects and others not. In this
> review, we conducted a meta-analysis of studies on mindfulness training and test anxiety. We searched
> several databases, screened the results, coded the studies, and computed effect sizes. We then ran a
> random-effects model and examined moderators. Understanding whether mindfulness reduces anxiety is
> important for students and educators. The remainder of this article describes our search and coding
> methods, presents the meta-analytic results, and discusses their implications and limitations.

**What's wrong (against `psychbull-topic-selection` / `psychbull-writing-style` / `psychbull-moderators-and-bias`):**

- **No question, no pooled answer, no effect size on page one.** A reader cannot tell what the synthesis
  found or how big or how heterogeneous it is (`psychbull-writing-style`: lead with the contribution).
- **Leads with method** ("we searched databases, coded studies, ran a random-effects model") instead of
  the open question a synthesis can settle (`psychbull-topic-selection`).
- **"Somewhat mixed" is not a synthesis question.** It does not say *what the disagreement is* or *which
  moderator might explain it* — the heart of a *Bulletin* contribution
  (`psychbull-moderators-and-bias`).
- **No heterogeneity, no prediction interval, no bias check signaled** — exactly the reporting a
  meta-analysis lives or dies on (`psychbull-meta-analysis-methods`,
  `psychbull-moderators-and-bias`).
- **No statement that this adds beyond prior reviews,** so the fit gate is unmet
  (`psychbull-topic-selection`: justify a new synthesis).
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (*Bulletin* arc — question + pooled effect + moderator + bias check, contribution early)

> **Does brief mindfulness training actually reduce test anxiety, and if the effect is real, what makes
> it larger in some studies than others?** Across 96 controlled studies (*k* = 96; *N* = 11,420), brief
> mindfulness training reduced self-reported test anxiety relative to control, with a **random-effects
> pooled effect of *g* = 0.34, 95% CI [0.26, 0.42]**, but with **substantial heterogeneity** (*I*² = 71%;
> 95% prediction interval [−0.10, 0.78]) — meaning the *average* effect is small-to-moderate while the
> *true* effect in a new setting could plausibly be near zero. *(Open question + pooled effect with its
> CI + the heterogeneity and prediction interval, in the first breath — `psychbull-meta-analysis-methods`.)*
>
> The literature is not merely "mixed"; it is **systematically mixed in a way prior narrative reviews
> could not adjudicate**: trials with active control conditions report much smaller effects than those
> with waitlist controls, and the field has disagreed about whether the benefit is specific to
> mindfulness or a generic expectancy effect. The unresolved question is therefore not "does it work?"
> but **"how much of the apparent benefit survives a proper control, and for whom?"** *(The disagreement
> named precisely, with the candidate moderator — `psychbull-topic-selection`,
> `psychbull-moderators-and-bias`.)*
>
> A meta-analysis can settle this because enough comparable controlled trials now exist to **estimate the
> effect, model its heterogeneity, and test control type as a moderator** — which no single trial and no
> narrative review can do. *(Why a synthesis, and why now — the `psychbull-topic-selection` fit gate.)*
>
> We assembled the studies with a **PRISMA-documented search** of five databases plus grey literature and
> trial registries, with a transparent records flow (identified → screened → eligible → included) and
> double coding with reliability reported, so an independent team could recover the same pool
> (`psychbull-literature-search-strategy`, `psychbull-inclusion-and-coding`). Effects were pooled with a
> **random-effects model** (REML), with dependent effect sizes handled by robust variance estimation, and
> moderators tested by meta-regression (`psychbull-meta-analysis-methods`). *(Search and model in one
> paragraph, MARS/PRISMA-compliant — method as warrant, not as lead.)*
>
> **Control type was the decisive moderator:** the pooled effect was *g* = 0.21, 95% CI [0.12, 0.30]
> against active controls but *g* = 0.52 against waitlist controls, a difference that accounts for a large
> share of the heterogeneity. Crucially, the effect **survived a bias check**: a funnel-asymmetry test and
> a selection-model adjustment shifted the active-control estimate only modestly (adjusted *g* = 0.18),
> so the benefit is **small but not an artifact of publication bias**. *(The moderator that explains the
> disagreement, plus an honest small-study/bias diagnostic — `psychbull-moderators-and-bias`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to **reconcile a contested literature**: brief mindfulness training reduces test
> anxiety, but the headline benefit is roughly halved once expectancy is controlled, and the residual
> effect is real yet modest. This reframes the debate from "does it work?" to **"it works, weakly, beyond
> expectancy — and the strong claims rest on weak controls."** *(Integrative contribution stated early,
> relative to the prior disagreement — `psychbull-theory-integration`.)* For practice and for future
> trials, the actionable implication is that **studies without active controls overstate the effect**, a
> design lesson that travels to other brief-intervention literatures. *(General payoff beyond the
> specific effect.)*
>
> The review proceeds as follows: the Method reports the PRISMA search, eligibility, coding, and model;
> the Results report the pooled effect, heterogeneity, the control-type moderator, and bias diagnostics;
> the Discussion states what the synthesis does and does not license. *(Brief roadmap — no
> over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Psychological Bulletin* bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **It is a synthesis, framed as one** — the contribution is integrating prior controlled trials and
  resolving their disagreement, not new data and not freestanding theory (the
  `psychbull-topic-selection` scope gate; pure theory would go to *Psychological Review*).
- **Pooled effect leads, with heterogeneity** — *g* = 0.34, 95% CI [0.26, 0.42], *I*² = 71%, and a
  **prediction interval**, so the reader sees both the average and the spread of true effects
  (`psychbull-meta-analysis-methods`).
- **A moderator explains the disagreement** — control type halves the effect and absorbs much of the
  heterogeneity, converting "mixed findings" into an answered question
  (`psychbull-moderators-and-bias`).
- **Bias is confronted, not deferred** — a funnel-asymmetry test and a selection-model adjustment are
  reported up front, pre-empting the "it's just publication bias" rebuttal
  (`psychbull-moderators-and-bias`, `psychbull-open-science-and-transparency`).
- **The search is reproducible** — a PRISMA flow over multiple databases plus grey literature, with
  double coding and reliability, so the pool could be re-recovered
  (`psychbull-literature-search-strategy`, `psychbull-inclusion-and-coding`).
- **Contribution is front-loaded and integrative** — the reframing ("works, weakly, beyond expectancy")
  appears early and is tied to the prior debate (`psychbull-theory-integration`,
  `psychbull-writing-style`), and method is a warrant rather than the lead.
