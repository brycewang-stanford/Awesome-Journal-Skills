credibly identified* labor result carry a *general-interest-within-labor* lesson — the JOLE bar (see

## By method

### Equilibrium search theory of discrimination (labor theory)

- **Black, "Discrimination in an Equilibrium Search Model," JOLE 1995, 13(2):309–333.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu` JOLE Vol 13, No 2; RePEc `ucp:jlabec/v13y1995i2p309-33`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent search model shows that even when only *some* employers
    dislike hiring minority workers, search frictions let that distaste depress minority wages
    market-wide — a clean theoretical payoff that reorganizes how to read taste-based vs. statistical
    discrimination evidence (`jole-contribution-framing` "theory advance"; `jole-topic-selection`
    discrimination scope).
  - **Self-check:** does your theory deliver a labor insight a labor economist outside your subfield
    takes away, rather than a model whose contribution is the math?

### Local-labor-market / skill-cell design for immigration (applied micro)

- **Card, "Immigrant Inflows, Native Outflows, and the Local Labor Market Impacts of Higher Immigration," JOLE 2001, 19(1):22–64.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/209979` (JOLE Vol 19, No 1); RePEc `ucp:jlabec/v19y2001i1p22-64`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** divides immigrants and natives into skill groups within cities and studies
    skill-group-specific outcomes — a design that confronts the central selection problem in the
    immigration debate head-on, and whose lesson (how much, and for whom, immigration moves local labor
    markets) is first-order across labor economics.
  - **Self-check:** does your design isolate variation that rules out the sorting/selection story a
    referee raises first (here, immigrants choosing booming cities)?

### Measurement-error correction in a supply-shock design (applied micro / econometrics)

- **Aydemir & Borjas, "Attenuation Bias in Measuring the Wage Impact of Immigration," JOLE 2011, 29(1):69–113.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/656360` (JOLE Vol 29, No 1); RePEc
  `ucp:jlabec/v29y2010i1p69-113` (online-ahead dated 2010; print Vol 29(1), 2011).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** shows that sampling error in the measured immigrant share attenuates
    estimated wage effects, and that correcting it materially changes the headline — a methods
    contribution that disciplines an entire empirical literature rather than estimating one more
    coefficient (`jole-contribution-framing` "new fact / better identification").
  - **Self-check:** is the measurement problem you solve one that biases a *whole class* of existing
    estimates, not just your own?

### Structural model of college choice + earnings (human capital)

- **Strayer, "The Returns to School Quality: College Choice and Earnings," JOLE 2002, 20(3):475–503.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/339674` (JOLE Vol 20, No 3); RePEc `ucp:jlabec/v20y2002i3p475-503`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** specifies how high-school quality works *through* college choice as well as
    directly on wages, separating two channels a reduced-form return would blur — a model that pays off
    in a sharper interpretation of "returns to school quality."
  - **Self-check:** does your structure decompose a return into channels a labor economist actually wants
    separated, rather than adding parameters with no interpretive payoff?

### Multiple instruments for a family-economics parameter (IV / quasi-experiment)

- **Angrist, Lavy & Schlosser, "Multiple Experiments for the Causal Link between the Quantity and Quality of Children," JOLE 2010, 28(4):773–824.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/653830` (JOLE Vol 28, No 4); RePEc `ucp:jlabec/v28y2010i4p773-824`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** brings several independent instruments (twins, sibling sex composition) to
    one classic question — the quantity–quality trade-off — and shows the causal link is weak once
    confounders are stripped out; a model of triangulating IVs rather than leaning on one.
  - **Self-check:** can you bring more than one source of quasi-random variation to bear, so the answer
    does not hinge on a single instrument's exclusion restriction (`jole-identification-strategy` IV
    branch)?

### Decomposition with linked historical administrative data (discrimination / human capital)

- **Carruthers & Wanamaker, "Separate and Unequal in the Labor Market: Human Capital and the Jim Crow Wage Gap," JOLE 2017, 35(3):655–696.**
  Verified: `journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/690944` (JOLE Vol 35, No 3); RePEc `ucp:jlabec/doi10.1086-690944`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses newly assembled linked data to decompose a large racial wage gap into
    human-capital and residual components, disciplining the measurement so the decomposition carries a
    credible causal reading — novel data answering an old, broad labor question.
  - **Self-check:** do your data let you separate human-capital differences from a residual
    "discrimination" component cleanly enough that a skeptic accepts the decomposition?

## Omitted for sibling-confusion or lack of full verification (do not attribute to JOLE without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking — several are famous
labor papers that are *not* in JOLE, which is exactly the trap this file guards against:

- **Card & Krueger, "Minimum Wages and Employment" (New Jersey/Pennsylvania, 1994)** — verified to be in
  the *American Economic Review* 84(4), **not JOLE**.
- **Abowd, Kramarz & Margolis, "High Wage Workers and High Wage Firms" (1999)** — verified to be in
  *Econometrica* 67(2):251–333, **not JOLE** (the canonical AKM paper).
- **Goldin & Rouse, "Orchestrating Impartiality" (2000)** — verified to be in the *American Economic
  Review* 90(4):715–741, **not JOLE**.
- **Neumark, Bank & Van Nort, "Sex Discrimination in Restaurant Hiring: An Audit Study" (1996)** — in the
  *QJE* 111(3):915–941, **not JOLE**.
- **Bertrand & Hallock, "The Gender Gap in Top Corporate Jobs" (2001)** — in the *ILR Review* 55(1):3–21,
  **not JOLE** (a sibling labor outlet).
- **Oreopoulos, "Why Do Skilled Immigrants Struggle in the Labor Market?" (2011)** — in *AEJ: Economic
  Policy* 3(4):148–171, **not JOLE**.
- **Card, "Using Regional Variation in Wages to Measure the Effects of the Federal Minimum Wage" (1992)** —
  in the *ILR Review* 46(1):22–37, **not JOLE**.
- **"The Effect of Unemployment Insurance on Unemployment Duration," JOLE 1987, 5(3):386–390** — appears
  in JOLE but is a **short note (pp. 386–390)** whose authorship was not cleanly confirmed by web
  search; omitted from the flagship list rather than risk a misattribution.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `journals.uchicago.edu` (JOLE article page
with volume/issue) or the `ucp:jlabec` RePEc handle, and update the access-date header. When in doubt —
especially with the sibling journals above — **omit**.
