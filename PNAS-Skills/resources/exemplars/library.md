## How to use this library

PNAS spans **three divisions — Biological, Physical, and Social Sciences** — and prizes work that a
reader from *another* division still finds significant (the cross-division test in
[`../../skills/pnas-fit/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pnas-fit/SKILL.md)). Pick the row whose **field × method**
is closest to yours, then study how that paper carries a *general* lesson to a broad scientific audience
and how its front matter would support the **Significance Statement + abstract** discipline in
[`../../skills/pnas-significance/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pnas-significance/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/pnas-abstract/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pnas-abstract/SKILL.md). For each, ask the self-check
before claiming your paper is "PNAS-shaped."

## By field × method

### Genomics method paper — high-throughput data → a general tool (Biological Sciences)

- **Eisen, Spellman, Brown & Botstein, "Cluster analysis and display of genome-wide expression patterns," PNAS 1998, 95(25):14863–14868.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.95.25.14863** (pnas.org; PMID 9843981; PMC56171).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a simple, general analytical method (hierarchical clustering + the
    now-ubiquitous heat-map display) introduced on microarray data — a method everyone could reuse, which
    is a classic PNAS "broadly significant tool" contribution rather than a single-system finding.
  - **Self-check:** is your method general enough that a reader in a *different* subfield could adopt it,
    not just admire your result?

### Genomics / functional interpretation — knowledge-based statistics (Biological Sciences)

- **Subramanian, Tamayo, Mootha, et al., "Gene set enrichment analysis: A knowledge-based approach for interpreting genome-wide expression profiles," PNAS 2005, 102(43):15545–15550.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.0506580102** (pnas.org; PMID 16199517).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** reframes expression analysis around *gene sets* rather than single genes,
    turning a statistical idea into a method (GSEA) adopted across biology — broad significance via a
    reusable inferential framework.
  - **Self-check:** does your contribution change how a whole community analyzes its data, not just
    report one more analysis?

### Computational biophysics — simulation as a window on mechanism (Biological / Physical Sciences)

- **Karplus & Kuriyan, "Molecular dynamics and protein function," PNAS 2005, 102(19):6679–6685.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.0408930102** (pnas.org; PMID 15870208).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a synthesis showing how molecular-dynamics simulation links protein motion
    to function — written so chemists, structural biologists, and physicists all see why it matters; a
    model of a cross-division "perspective + evidence" PNAS article.
  - **Self-check:** would a reader from the *neighboring* discipline (here: physics ↔ biology) grasp the
    payoff, satisfying the `pnas-fit` cross-division test?

### Network science — an algorithm that travels across domains (Physical Sciences)

- **Girvan & Newman, "Community structure in social and biological networks," PNAS 2002, 99(12):7821–7826.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.122653799** (pnas.org; widely indexed).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** one method (edge-betweenness community detection) demonstrated on **both
    social and biological** networks in a single paper — the breadth is *built into* the result, exactly
    the cross-division significance PNAS rewards.
  - **Self-check:** does your method demonstrably work in more than one domain, so the lesson is general
    rather than system-specific?

### Quantitative social/physical science — scaling laws of human systems (Physical / Social Sciences)

- **Bettencourt, Lobo, Helbing, Kühnert & West, "Growth, innovation, scaling, and the pace of life in cities," PNAS 2007, 104(17):7301–7306.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.0610172104** (pnas.org).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** brings a physics-style scaling framework to urban data, producing a
    quantitative, predictive claim about cities that economists, physicists, and urban planners all care
    about — a textbook PNAS interdisciplinary result.
  - **Self-check:** does your framework yield a *quantitative, general* regularity (not a one-city
    description) that crosses field boundaries?

### Behavioral / well-being economics — large survey, sharp distinction (Social Sciences)

- **Kahneman & Deaton, "High income improves evaluation of life but not emotional well-being," PNAS 2010, 107(38):16489–16493.**
  DOI **10.1073/pnas.1011492107** (pnas.org; widely indexed).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a single, clean conceptual distinction (life *evaluation* vs. emotional
    *well-being*) tested on a very large survey — a plain, broadly significant social-science result whose
    "so what" is legible to any reader, ideal for a strong Significance Statement.
  - **Self-check:** can your central distinction, and why it matters, be stated in one plain sentence for
    the Significance Statement?

## Omitted for lack of PNAS verification (do NOT attribute to PNAS — sibling-venue confusion)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following frequently-misremembered candidates were **excluded**
after checking; each is a real, important paper — just **not in PNAS**:

- **Anfinsen, "Principles that Govern the Folding of Protein Chains" (1973)** — published in ***Science***,
  181(4096):223–230, DOI 10.1126/science.181.4096.223. A `10.1126/science…` DOI is **not** PNAS.
- **Rosenberg et al., "Genetic Structure of Human Populations" (2002)** — published in ***Science***,
  298(5602):2381–2385, DOI 10.1126/science.1078311 — **not** PNAS (related serial-founder follow-ups by
  the same group *do* appear in PNAS, but this specific paper does not).
- **Pritchard, Stephens & Donnelly, "Inference of population structure using multilocus genotype data"
  (2000)** — published in ***Genetics***, 155(2):945–959 — **not** PNAS.
- **Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir & Zhao, "Poverty impedes cognitive function" (2013)** — published in
  ***Science***, 341(6149):976–980, DOI 10.1126/science.1238041 — **not** PNAS. Listed here only as a
  guardrail because it is often grouped with PNAS behavioral-science work.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm a **`10.1073/pnas…` DOI on pnas.org** (with
volume/issue/pages) and update the access-date header. If the DOI prefix is `10.1126` (Science) or
`10.1038` (Nature/Nature Communications) or the venue is *Cell* or *PLOS ONE*, it does **not** belong
here. **When in doubt, omit.**
