# Worked Example: A PNAS Significance Statement + Abstract (before → after)

PNAS is unusual among general-science venues: every research article carries **two distinct front-matter
artifacts** — a mandatory **≤120-word Significance Statement** written for an educated non-specialist
(answers *"so what, and who cares?"*), and a **~250-word single-paragraph Abstract** written for
scientists (answers *"what did you do and find?"*). They must not read the same. This example shows a
typical first-draft pair rewritten to clear the PNAS bar, drawn entirely from this pack's own skill
files.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"A soil bacterium reroutes its central metabolism to survive
weeks of complete drying."* Fictional setting: a lab study of a single drought-tolerant soil microbe and
the metabolic switch it uses to enter and exit dormancy.

The genre rules being demonstrated:

- **Significance Statement** (`pnas-significance`): ≤120 words; gap → plain-language advance → why it
  matters → optional concrete application; no jargon, no acronyms, no citations; genuinely broader and
  plainer than the abstract.
- **Abstract** (`pnas-abstract`): ~250 words; one paragraph, no subheadings/citations/figure callouts;
  five moves (context → gap → approach → quantified result → implication); ≥1 quantified result with
  uncertainty; distinct in register from the Significance Statement.
- **Fit** (`pnas-fit`): a cross-division reader from another PNAS division must say "I understand why
  this matters" — and the broad implication must be *demonstrated*, not asserted.

---

## Before (the two artifacts are nearly identical, and both are too technical)

**Significance Statement (draft — 121 words, reads like a compressed abstract):**

> Here we report that the soil bacterium exhibits a previously uncharacterized metabolic flux
> redistribution under desiccation stress. Using 13C metabolic flux analysis (13C-MFA) and RNA-seq, we
> demonstrate downregulation of glycolytic flux and concomitant upregulation of the pentose phosphate
> pathway (PPP), elevating NADPH/NADP+ ratios by 4.2-fold. Knockout of the gene *zwf* abolished
> desiccation tolerance. These findings elucidate the metabolic basis of anhydrobiosis and have
> important implications for understanding microbial stress physiology, with potential downstream
> applications in biotechnology, synthetic biology, and the engineering of stress-tolerant strains for a
> range of industrial and environmental contexts that may benefit society in numerous important ways.

**Abstract (draft):**

> Here we report that the soil bacterium exhibits a metabolic flux redistribution under desiccation. We
> performed 13C-MFA and RNA-seq. Glycolytic flux decreased and PPP flux increased, raising NADPH. *zwf*
> knockout abolished tolerance. This elucidates anhydrobiosis with implications for biotechnology.

**What's wrong (against `pnas-significance` / `pnas-abstract` / `pnas-fit`):**

- **The Significance Statement is a condensed abstract** — same content, same register. The skill's
  single most-flagged failure: it must be genuinely broader and plainer, not the abstract minus words.
- **Jargon and undefined acronyms** in the statement (`13C-MFA`, `PPP`, `NADPH/NADP+`, *zwf*) — a bright
  undergraduate in another field cannot follow it (`pnas-significance` undergraduate-rewrite drill).
- **Method-led** ("Using 13C metabolic flux analysis…") instead of problem-led.
- **Over length** — 121 words, past the ≤120 ceiling; and **over-claiming** — "benefit society in
  numerous important ways" is asserted, not demonstrated (a `pnas-fit` fatal trigger).
- **The abstract is too short and under-quantified** — it states directions ("decreased", "increased")
  but no magnitude with uncertainty; the skill requires ≥1 quantified result (magnitude + unit + CI/P).
- **The abstract ends on the implication asserted, not earned**, and opens with method, not stake.

---

## After — Significance Statement (plain, ≤120 words, distinct from the abstract)

> *(118 words. No acronyms, no gene names as such, no citations — the four moves: gap → plain advance →
> why it matters → concrete application.)*
>
> Microbes in soils worldwide routinely dry out completely and then revive when rain returns, but how a
> cell stays alive for weeks with almost no water has been unclear. We found that one common soil
> bacterium survives drying by quietly rewiring how it burns sugar: instead of extracting energy the
> usual way, it shifts to a route that builds up a protective chemical the cell needs to repair itself.
> Switching off the single gene that controls this route left the bacterium unable to survive drying.
> The result shows that surviving extreme dehydration can hinge on one metabolic switch — a clue for
> predicting how soil life, and the carbon it cycles, will respond to longer droughts.

---

## After — Abstract (~250 words, five moves, quantified, scientist register)

> *(243 words. Single paragraph; context → gap → approach → quantified results → implication; acronyms
> defined on first use; quantified headline with uncertainty.)*
>
> Soil microbial communities are repeatedly subjected to complete desiccation and rehydration, cycles
> that govern how much carbon soils store or release, yet the cellular mechanisms that allow individual
> cells to survive weeks without water remain poorly resolved. Whether desiccation tolerance reflects a
> specific, regulated metabolic program — rather than generic stress damage — has been difficult to
> establish. Here we combined carbon-13 metabolic flux analysis with transcriptomics in a drought-
> tolerant soil bacterium to ask how central carbon metabolism is reorganized as cells enter dormancy.
> As water was withdrawn, flux through glycolysis fell while flux through the pentose phosphate pathway
> rose, increasing the intracellular ratio of reduced to oxidized nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide
> phosphate (NADPH/NADP+) by 4.2-fold (95% CI 3.6–4.8). This redirection supplied the reducing power
> used to regenerate antioxidant and repair systems during drying. Deleting glucose-6-phosphate
> dehydrogenase (the enzyme that commits carbon to the pentose phosphate pathway) lowered survival after
> a 14-day desiccation from 71% to 4% (P < 0.001) without impairing growth under hydrated conditions,
> identifying this single branch point as necessary for tolerance. Re-wetting reversed the flux shift
> within hours, indicating a rapidly reversible program rather than irreversible damage. These results
> show that survival of extreme dehydration in this organism is controlled by a discrete, reversible
> rerouting of central metabolism, suggesting that the capacity of soil communities to withstand
> intensifying drought may depend on a small number of conserved metabolic control points.

---

## Why the "after" pair meets the PNAS bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Two distinct artifacts, not one written twice** — the Significance Statement is plain and answers
  "why does a non-specialist care?"; the abstract is technical and answers "what did you do and find?"
  (`pnas-abstract` / `pnas-significance`: "if the two read the same, the Significance Statement is
  wrong").
- **Significance Statement passes the undergraduate test** — every term a second-year student in another
  field would not know is gone: *zwf* becomes "the single gene that controls this route", the pentose
  phosphate pathway becomes "a route that builds up a protective chemical", and NADPH is described by
  what it *does* (`pnas-significance` undergraduate-rewrite drill).
- **Within the cap** — 118 words ≤ 120; the abstract is 243 ≤ ~250, a single unstructured paragraph with
  no citations or figure callouts (`pnas-writing` order; `pnas-abstract` hard constraints).
- **Quantified headline with uncertainty** — "4.2-fold (95% CI 3.6–4.8)" and "71% to 4% (P < 0.001)",
  not "significantly increased" (`pnas-abstract`: magnitude + unit + uncertainty).
- **Problem-led, not method-led** — both artifacts open on the stake (carbon cycling, surviving drought),
  with the technique demoted to the approach sentence (`pnas-abstract` anti-pattern: "do not open with
  method or organism").
- **Broad significance demonstrated, not asserted** — the cross-division implication (drought, soil
  carbon) follows directly from the result, and the over-claim "benefit society in numerous important
  ways" was cut; nothing is promised the data cannot back (`pnas-fit` fatal weak-fit triggers).
- **Cross-division reader test passes** — a climate scientist or ecologist reading this metabolism paper
  can state why it matters (soil carbon under drought), satisfying the `pnas-fit` general-significance
  test for a paper spanning the Biological and, by implication, environmental readership.
