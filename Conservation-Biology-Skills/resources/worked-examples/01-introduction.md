# Worked Example: A *Conservation Biology* Abstract + Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the house style the skills describe: an **abstract (≤ 300 words)** that states the
conservation problem, approach, and finding; and an **introduction** that, by its end, has told a broad
conservation reader **the problem, the question, the approach, and why it matters for conserving
biological diversity** — with the contribution **front-loaded** and the so-what made **actionable and
transferable**, not local-only (`conbio-writing-style`, `conbio-conservation-relevance-and-implications`,
`conbio-topic-selection`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Restored hedgerows recover farmland-bird occupancy within five
years across a fragmented agricultural region."* Fictional setting: a fictional agricultural region where
land managers can replant field-edge hedgerows; we (fictionally) tracked occupancy of a guild of
farmland birds before and after restoration. **Every figure is invented for teaching.**

---

## Before (system-insider draft — sound, but the conservation relevance is buried)

> **Abstract.** Hedgerows are a feature of agricultural landscapes. We surveyed birds in our study
> region over several years using point counts and fitted occupancy models that account for imperfect
> detection. We compared sites with and without restored hedgerows. We found differences between site
> types. Detection probability varied with observer and season. We discuss our results in the context of
> the local avifauna and suggest further study.
>
> **Introduction.** The relationship between agricultural landscape structure and birds has been studied
> extensively. Many authors have examined hedgerows, field margins, and habitat fragmentation. In this
> paper we use single-season and multi-season occupancy models, with a Bayesian implementation, to
> analyze point-count data collected in our region. Farmland biodiversity is an important topic. We
> describe the study area, the survey protocol, and the modeling framework, and then present results.

**What's wrong (against the skills):**

- **Abstract hides the finding** — it says "differences between site types" with no direction, magnitude,
  or conservation point. (`conbio-writing-style`: the abstract must state the finding.)
- **Leads with method** ("we use occupancy models… a Bayesian implementation") instead of the
  conservation problem and question — the named anti-pattern in `conbio-writing-style`.
- **No conservation relevance by the end of the intro.** A generalist cannot tell what decision this
  informs. (`conbio-conservation-relevance-and-implications`: don't make the reader dig for the so-what.)
- **Local-only framing** ("in the context of the local avifauna") with no transferable lesson — fails the
  `conbio-topic-selection` novelty-plus-transferability test.
- **Throat-clearing** ("has been studied extensively," "is an important topic") and an over-described
  roadmap doing the argument's work.

---

## After (Conservation Biology arc — problem → question → approach → conservation payoff, front-loaded)

> **Abstract (fictional; 188 words — under the 300-word cap).** Hedgerow loss is a leading driver of
> farmland-bird declines, yet managers rarely know how quickly restoration recovers populations, or
> whether a recovery measured at one farm transfers to a fragmented region. We asked how fast restored
> hedgerows recover farmland-bird occupancy and whether the recovery rate depends on how isolated a
> restored site is. Across 60 fictional farms — half with hedgerows restored in staggered years, half
> unrestored — we fitted multi-season occupancy models that account for imperfect detection, and related
> the recovery trajectory to landscape isolation. Restored hedgerows raised guild occupancy from 0.34 to
> 0.61 within five years, with most of the gain in years two to four; recovery was roughly twice as fast
> at well-connected sites as at isolated ones. The result supports a specific decision: where the goal is
> rapid bird recovery on a fixed restoration budget, prioritizing field edges that link existing habitat
> over equally degraded but isolated edges should accelerate recovery. The recovery-versus-isolation
> relationship, not the regional total, is what transfers: it gives managers elsewhere a rule for
> *sequencing* hedgerow restoration. *(problem → question → approach → finding → conservation payoff.)*
>
> **Introduction (closing arc).** Hedgerow removal has accompanied agricultural intensification across
> many regions, and farmland-bird guilds have declined alongside it — making field-edge habitat a target
> for restoration spending. *(the conservation problem, stated first.)* But managers face a question the
> existing evidence answers poorly: **how quickly does restoring a hedgerow actually recover bird
> occupancy, and does the answer depend on where in the landscape the hedgerow sits?** Recovery rate, not
> just eventual presence, is what a budget-constrained restoration program needs to know. *(the question,
> in conservation terms.)*
>
> Answering this credibly is hard because farms that restore hedgerows often differ from those that don't
> — in soil, ownership, or pre-existing bird abundance — so a one-time comparison conflates restoration
> with the conditions that made restoration likely. We address this with restorations that happened in
> **staggered years** and with before-and-after occupancy modeling that accounts for imperfect detection,
> comparing each restored farm to not-yet-restored farms. *(the approach, demoted to a tool and tied to
> the inference problem, per `conbio-writing-style`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to turn a static "hedgerows help birds" into a **recovery trajectory** and to show
> it **bends with landscape connectivity** — converting a familiar correlation into a decision rule a
> manager can act on. *(contribution front-loaded.)* The transferable lesson is not our region's bird
> total but the **recovery-versus-isolation relationship**: where rapid recovery is the goal, sequence
> restoration to connect existing habitat first. This reframes hedgerow restoration as a problem of
> **where and in what order**, not merely whether — a lesson that travels to any fragmented system where
> restoration budgets are spent edge by edge. *(transferable, actionable so-what — the journal's applied
> mission.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Conservation Biology* bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Conservation problem stated first; relevance clear by the end of the intro** — the reader knows the
  decision at stake (how to sequence a fixed restoration budget) without digging
  (`conbio-writing-style`: front-load the conservation contribution).
- **Abstract states problem, approach, and finding, with direction and magnitude**, and is **under 300
  words** (`conbio-writing-style`: the ≤ 300-word abstract that does not hide the finding).
- **Actionability sentence is completable honestly:** *"Because we found connectivity speeds recovery, a
  manager should sequence restoration to connect existing habitat first (under conditions of a fixed
  budget and rapid-recovery goal), with the confidence a staggered before/after design supports."*
  (`conbio-conservation-relevance-and-implications` actionability test.)
- **Implications are proportional to evidence** — a sequencing recommendation, not a mandate; the
  transferable claim is the **mechanism/decision rule**, not the regional number
  (`conbio-conservation-relevance-and-implications`: make it transferable; don't over-claim).
- **Novelty + transferability** — it advances a static result into a recovery trajectory others can reuse,
  clearing the `conbio-topic-selection` fit test (not a local "first record").
- **Method demoted to a tool** — occupancy modeling appears only where the inference problem is
  introduced, never as the lead (avoids the "leading with the method" anti-pattern).
