## By method × topic

### Population/extinction modeling — delayed extinction from habitat loss (extinction debt)

- **Cowlishaw, G. (1999). "Predicting the Pattern of Decline of African Primate Diversity: an Extinction
  Debt from Historical Deforestation." *Conservation Biology* 13(5):1183–1193.**
  Verified: `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1046/j.1523-1739.1999.98433.x`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses a species–area relationship to convert *historical* deforestation
    into a forecast of primate extinctions still "owed" — a model that turns a past land-use fact into a
    forward-looking conservation warning that transfers to other forest-loss systems.
  - **Self-check:** does your model deliver a transferable *prediction a manager can act on ahead of the
    loss*, not just a description of the system as it is now?

### Modeling critique / theory — which species habitat destruction endangers

- **McCarthy, M.A., Lindenmayer, D.B., & Drechsler, M. (1997). "Extinction Debts and Risks Faced by
  Abundant Species." *Conservation Biology* 11(1):221–226.**
  Verified: `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1046/j.1523-1739.1997.95381.x`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** interrogates a prior model's assumptions (when do good competitors / abundant
    species face the *greatest* extinction risk?) and shows the conclusion hinges on coexistence and
    spatial assumptions — a model of advancing a debate rather than reporting a new field result.
  - **Self-check:** does your contribution sharpen or correct a model the field already argues over, with
    the conservation stakes of getting it wrong made explicit?

### Evidence-based / science-to-practice argument (Essay-style)

- **Pullin, A.S., & Knight, T.M. (2001). "Effectiveness in Conservation Practice: Pointers from Medicine
  and Public Health." *Conservation Biology* 15(1):50–54.**
  Verified: `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1523-1739.2001.99499.x`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an early, influential call to base conservation on systematic evidence,
    importing the evidence-based-medicine model — a forward-looking argument grounded in evidence, the
    shape of a strong Essay / Conservation Practice and Policy piece (`conbio-topic-selection` article-type
    choice).
  - **Self-check:** does your argument propose a *change in how conservation is practiced* that travels
    well beyond any single case?

### Social-science design — choice experiment on conservation behavior

- **Nielsen, M.R., Jacobsen, J.B., & Thorsen, B.J. (2014). "Factors Determining the Choice of Hunting and
  Trading Bushmeat in the Kilombero Valley, Tanzania." *Conservation Biology* 28(2):382–391.**
  Verified: `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/cobi.12197`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses a stated-preference choice experiment with bushmeat-trade actors to
    identify what would induce them to switch livelihoods — social-science method matched to a
    conservation decision (where to target alternative-livelihood incentives), and a clean example of the
    journal's social dimension (`conbio-study-design`).
  - **Self-check:** is your social-science design pointed at a decision (what intervention, for whom), and
    does it name the tradeoffs honestly?

### Spatial prioritization — refining where conservation acts

- **"Refining Biodiversity Conservation Priorities." *Conservation Biology* (2005), 19(6).**
  Verified (title, journal, year, DOI/article page): `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1523-1739.2005.00307.x`.
  - **Note on attribution:** the title, journal, year, and DOI are web-verified; the **author list was not
    pinned down by search at access time**, so no author names are asserted here — confirm authorship on
    the Wiley article page before citing.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** argues that coarse hotspot boundaries are too blunt for on-the-ground
    decisions and pushes priority-setting down to actionable, realistically sized localities — a
    transferable decision rule, not a one-region map.
  - **Self-check:** does your prioritization output a decision at the scale managers actually act on?

## Omitted / guardrail — do NOT attribute these to *Conservation Biology* without re-checking

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following frequently-cited candidates were **excluded** after
checking, because they are **not** in the *Conservation Biology* journal (the exact sibling-confusion the
header warns about):

- **Soulé, M.E. (1985). "What is Conservation Biology?"** — verified to be in ***BioScience*** 35:727–734,
  **not** the *Conservation Biology* journal (the journal launched in 1987). Listed here only as a
  guardrail.
- **Margules, C.R., & Pressey, R.L. (2000). "Systematic conservation planning."** — verified to be in
  ***Nature*** 405:243–253, **not** *Conservation Biology*. A foundational planning paper, but wrong
  venue for this library.
- **Brashares, J.S., Arcese, P., et al. (2004). "Bushmeat hunting, wildlife declines and fish supply in
  West Africa."** — verified to be in ***Science*** 306:1180–1183, **not** *Conservation Biology*.
- Any hit with a **`10.1111/conl…`** (Conservation Letters) or **`10.1111/csp2…`** (Conservation Science
  and Practice) DOI from the same Wiley platform — these are **sibling journals**, not *Conservation
  Biology*, and were filtered out during verification.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com` with a
`10.1111/cobi…`, `10.1111/j.1523-1739…`, or `10.1046/j.1523-1739…` DOI, and update the access-date
header. When in doubt, omit.
