# Worked Example: A JEP-Style Opening (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JEP opening arc** from
[`jep-narrative-arc`](../../skills/jep-narrative-arc/SKILL.md):
**hook (puzzle/fact/example/debate) → why it matters now → the tension → preview of the synthesis and the
takeaways**, written in the accessible JEP voice
([`jep-writing-style`](../../skills/jep-writing-style/SKILL.md)).

Two JEP house rules drive the rewrite and distinguish it from a research-journal introduction:

- JEP is the AEA's **open-access, non-technical synthesis** journal, sister to the **Journal of Economic
  Literature** (JEL — long, technical surveys of record) and to the **AER** (primary research). A JEP piece
  must be **readable by ~90% of AEA members**, explaining ideas through **economic intuition rather than
  notation** ([`jep-topic-selection`](../../skills/jep-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
  [`jep-accessibility-and-translation`](../../skills/jep-accessibility-and-translation/SKILL.md)).
- JEP communicates evidence through **plain magnitudes, ranges, and honest caveats — never equations,
  coefficient tables, or significance asterisks** in the body
  ([`jep-evidence-without-equations`](../../skills/jep-evidence-without-equations/SKILL.md)).

**Illustrative article (fictional):** *"Did Remote Work Reshape the City? What a Decade of Evidence Tells
Us."* Fictional synthesis of an invented literature on how remote work changed where people live and what
they earn, written for a broad AEA audience. Every magnitude is invented.

---

## Before (a working-paper intro pasted into a "perspectives" piece)

> The literature on agglomeration economies and urban wage premia is extensive. In this paper we revisit
> the spatial equilibrium framework of the canonical model and estimate the effect of remote-work adoption
> on the urban wage gradient. Using a shift-share instrument that exploits pre-period industry exposure to
> teleworkability, we estimate β in a two-way fixed-effects specification and find a coefficient of −0.142
> (s.e. 0.038), significant at the 1% level (***). The estimate is robust to the inclusion of MSA-by-year
> fixed effects and to the Borusyak–Hull exogeneity correction. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3
> presents the empirical strategy, Section 4 reports results, and Section 5 concludes.

**What is wrong (against `jep-narrative-arc` / `jep-accessibility-and-translation` /
`jep-evidence-without-equations` / `jep-writing-style`):**

- **Opens with "the literature… is extensive"** — the JEP anti-pattern. There is no hook, no puzzle, and
  no reason a non-urban economist should keep reading.
- **Leads with machinery** ("shift-share instrument," "two-way fixed effects," "β," "Borusyak–Hull") — this
  reads as an **AER** submission, not a JEP synthesis. The notation and estimator names are exactly what
  JEP asks authors to translate away.
- **Reports a raw coefficient with asterisks** ("−0.142 (s.e. 0.038)… (***)") — JEP communicates evidence
  through plain magnitudes and ranges, never coefficients and stars.
- **No synthesis and no takeaways** — it estimates one number; it does not distill what a *decade* of work
  has taught, which is the JEP job.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing work the argument should do.
- **It's a single new result** — that belongs in a research journal; a JEP piece would *weigh many studies*.

---

## After (JEP arc — hook → why now → tension → preview of synthesis + takeaways)

> **In 2019, a software engineer and a similar worker in sales lived in the same expensive city for the same
> reason: that was where the jobs were. Five years later, one of them files taxes from a town three hours
> away — and earns nearly the same paycheck.** That small change, multiplied across millions of workers, is
> quietly rewriting one of the oldest regularities in economics: the wage premium for living in a big,
> productive city. *(hook — a concrete, vivid example any economist feels.)*
>
> Why does this matter now? For a century, cities paid more because density made workers more productive,
> and that premium shaped where people lived, what housing cost, and how regions diverged. If remote work
> loosens the link between *where you live* and *what you earn*, it touches labor markets, housing,
> regional inequality, and public finance at once. A decade of new evidence has accumulated fast enough that
> we can now say something — carefully — about how much has actually changed. *(stakes + "why now," in plain
> language a non-specialist reads in one breath;
> [`jep-topic-selection`](../../skills/jep-topic-selection/SKILL.md).)*
>
> The tension is that the early claims ran ahead of the evidence. Some argued the city wage premium would
> collapse; others, that density would reassert itself once the novelty faded. **Both camps pointed to real
> data and reached opposite conclusions** — which usually means the truth depends on details the slogans
> skip. *(tension — there is something genuinely at issue;
> [`jep-balance-and-objectivity`](../../skills/jep-balance-and-objectivity/SKILL.md).)*
>
> This article synthesizes what we have learned. The short version: the urban wage premium has **narrowed,
> but not vanished**. Across a dozen studies in several countries, the pay gap between big-city and smaller-
> market workers in remote-friendly jobs has shrunk by **roughly a quarter** since 2019 — a real change,
> **about the size of a typical college-wage-premium swing over a decade**, but far from the collapse some
> predicted. The narrowing is concentrated in jobs that can genuinely be done from anywhere, and it **fades
> for work that still rewards being in the room**. *(preview of the synthesis with a plain magnitude, a
> familiar benchmark, and a caveat — no equation, no asterisk;
> [`jep-evidence-without-equations`](../../skills/jep-evidence-without-equations/SKILL.md).)*
>
> Three takeaways organize what follows. First, **geography still pays, but less, and unevenly** across
> occupations. Second, the mechanism is **selection as much as treatment** — who moves matters as much as
> what moving does — which is why naive before-and-after comparisons mislead. Third, the open question that
> should worry us is **distributional**: the workers gaining location freedom are not the ones who most need
> cheaper housing. *(3 takeaways = the spine;
> [`jep-narrative-arc`](../../skills/jep-narrative-arc/SKILL.md).)* The rest of the article weighs the
> evidence behind each, and flags where it is still thin. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JEP bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Opens with a hook, not the literature** — a vivid two-worker example replaces "the literature is
  extensive," satisfying [`jep-narrative-arc`](../../skills/jep-narrative-arc/SKILL.md) ("opens with a
  hook… not 'the literature on…'").
- **Accessible to ~90% of AEA members** — no "shift-share," no "β," no "two-way fixed effects"; the
  identification worry appears in words ("selection as much as treatment," "naive before-and-after
  comparisons mislead"), per
  [`jep-accessibility-and-translation`](../../skills/jep-accessibility-and-translation/SKILL.md).
- **Evidence as plain magnitude + benchmark + caveat** — "shrunk by roughly a quarter… about the size of a
  typical college-wage-premium swing… far from the collapse some predicted," with no coefficient and no
  asterisk, exactly the
  [`jep-evidence-without-equations`](../../skills/jep-evidence-without-equations/SKILL.md) bar (magnitude in
  feelable units, anchored to a benchmark, body of evidence, honest caveat).
- **Synthesis with a tension and takeaways, not one result** — it weighs "a dozen studies," states what
  both camps got right, and lands three repeatable takeaways — the JEP job, not the AER job
  ([`jep-topic-selection`](../../skills/jep-topic-selection/SKILL.md)).
- **Balanced, not advocacy** — both predictions are steelmanned, the verdict ("narrowed but not vanished")
  follows the weight of evidence, and the hardest open question (distributional) is flagged rather than
  buried ([`jep-balance-and-objectivity`](../../skills/jep-balance-and-objectivity/SKILL.md)).
- **Sibling check passes:** this is **not** a single identified estimate (AER) and **not** an exhaustive
  technical survey with full method coverage (JEL) — it is an accessible, balanced synthesis for a broad
  audience, which is JEP's identity.
