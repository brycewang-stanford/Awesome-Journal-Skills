# Worked Example: A JPSP-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the introduction arc JPSP rewards: **psychological question → the theoretical tension
the field cannot resolve → a precise theoretical account (the mechanism) → the multi-study package that
tests it, study by study → the key effect with an effect size and interval → the theoretical
contribution, stated early → brief roadmap of the package.** JPSP's **first gate is theoretical
innovation** ([`jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses`](../../skills/jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses/SKILL.md)): a tidy
set of studies with a thin idea will not clear it. The governing craft from
[`jpsp-writing-style`](../../skills/jpsp-writing-style/SKILL.md) is a **rich, theory-driven narrative
that still fits the section's capped introduction + discussion** (ASC ≤ 3,500 words; IRGP ≤ 5,000 words —
verify on the live section page), and from
[`jpsp-study-design`](../../skills/jpsp-study-design/SKILL.md) the package must **converge**: each study
adds an inference the previous one could not.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When Sharing a Setback Builds Closeness: Vulnerable Disclosure,
Perceived Responsiveness, and the Repair of Threatened Bonds"* — a fictional five-study package
(Interpersonal Relations and Group Processes, IRGP). Fictional setting: experiments plus a daily-diary
and a dyadic study of disclosure in close relationships.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Self-disclosure has been studied extensively in social and personality psychology, and many studies
> link disclosure to relationship outcomes such as intimacy and satisfaction. Various theories have been
> proposed about how people share information with relationship partners, and prior work has examined
> both positive and negative disclosures. In the present research, we conducted five studies in which
> participants disclosed events to a partner and we measured closeness. We used experiments, a diary
> study, and a dyadic study, and we ran several analyses. We predicted that disclosing setbacks would
> affect closeness. Understanding disclosure is important for relationships and well-being. Study 1
> tests our first hypothesis, Study 2 tests the next, and so on; the General Discussion integrates the
> findings.

**What's wrong (against `jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses` / `jpsp-literature-positioning` / `jpsp-writing-style`):**

- **No theoretical innovation on page one.** There is no idea — no mechanism, no tension the field cannot
  resolve — only a topic and a method list; this fails JPSP's first gate
  (`jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses`).
- **Leads with "we conducted five studies" (method), not the psychological question** — the anti-pattern
  `jpsp-writing-style` names.
- **Gap stated as vagueness** ("has been studied extensively," "affect closeness") rather than a precise
  puzzle or conflicting predictions the section's readers would recognize (`jpsp-literature-positioning`).
- **No headline effect, no effect size, no interval** — the reader cannot tell what was found or how big
  it is (`jpsp-data-analysis`).
- **The package looks stapled,** not convergent: nothing says what each study adds that the others cannot
  (`jpsp-study-design`).
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (JPSP arc — question + theory + converging package + effect, contribution early)

> **When people share a personal setback with a close partner, does it pull the relationship apart or
> draw it closer — and what determines which?** We propose that disclosing a *vulnerability* functions
> as a relationship test: it puts the discloser at risk, so the partner's response becomes diagnostic of
> the bond, and a **responsive** reaction repairs closeness precisely *because* the discloser was
> exposed. Across five studies, vulnerable disclosure increased felt closeness when partners responded
> responsively but eroded it when they did not, and **perceived partner responsiveness mediated the
> effect** (Study 3 indirect effect *ab* = 0.24, 95% CI [0.13, 0.36]). *(Psychological question + the
> theoretical account and its mechanism + the headline effect with an interval, in the first breath.)*
>
> Whether sharing a setback helps or harms a bond is genuinely unresolved: one tradition holds that
> negative disclosure burdens partners and depresses satisfaction, while another holds that openness
> deepens intimacy — yet neither specifies *the condition that decides between them*. The unanswered
> question is not "does disclosure matter?" but **"why does the same act of sharing build closeness in
> some couples and corrode it in others?"** *(The tension named precisely, as two competing predictions
> the field has not reconciled — `jpsp-literature-positioning`.)*
>
> Our account makes a **diagnostic prediction a rival cannot**: if vulnerability is what makes the
> partner's response informative, then responsiveness should matter **more** after high-vulnerability
> disclosures than after routine ones — an interaction the "disclosure is uniformly burdensome" and
> "disclosure is uniformly good" views do not predict. *(A hypothesis that distinguishes the account from
> its alternatives — the `jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses` diagnosticity rule.)*
>
> We test this with a **converging five-study package** (`jpsp-study-design`): two preregistered
> experiments manipulate disclosure vulnerability and partner responsiveness to establish causal
> direction (Studies 1–2); a mediation experiment isolates **perceived responsiveness** as the mechanism
> (Study 3); a 14-day dyadic daily-diary study shows the effect unfolds within couples in everyday life,
> with actor and partner effects (Study 4); and a final experiment rules out the salient alternative that
> mere **disclosure intimacy**, not vulnerability, drives the effect (Study 5). Each study supplies an
> inference the others cannot — causal control, mechanism, ecological validity, and discriminant
> evidence — so no single study carries the claim alone. *(The package, with each study's distinct role —
> `jpsp-study-design` convergence.)*
>
> Pooling the experiments, responsive reactions to high-vulnerability disclosures raised closeness
> relative to unresponsive reactions (*d* = 0.46, 95% CI [0.29, 0.63]), and the predicted
> vulnerability × responsiveness interaction held in both experiments and in the within-person diary
> data. *(Key effect restated with magnitude, interval, and the signature interaction —
> `jpsp-data-analysis`: report uncertainty, not bare significance.)*
>
> Our contribution is theoretical: we reframe negative self-disclosure not as a burden or a benefit but
> as a **diagnostic risk** whose relational consequence is determined by perceived responsiveness — a
> mechanism that **integrates the two conflicting literatures** under one account and specifies the
> moderator that decides between them. *(Contribution stated early and tied to a specific theoretical
> advance — the `jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses` "novel connection / integrative mechanism" move.)* The
> account is **portable** to other risky relational acts — asking for help, confessing a transgression —
> wherever exposure makes a partner's response diagnostic. *(Generalization that travels beyond the
> paradigm — `jpsp-topic-selection` general-interest test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds by building the package: Studies 1–2 establish the causal interaction, Study 3
> isolates the mechanism, Study 4 tests it in couples' daily lives, and Study 5 rules out the disclosure-
> intimacy alternative; the General Discussion states scope and boundary conditions. *(Brief roadmap —
> the package's logic, not a section-by-section recap.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the JPSP bar

Mapped to the pack's skill checklists:

- **Theoretical innovation leads** — the first paragraph states a *mechanism* (vulnerability makes the
  partner's response diagnostic) that **integrates two conflicting literatures**, clearing JPSP's first
  gate (`jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses`: integrative mechanism / novel connection).
- **The tension is precise, not generic** — two competing predictions the field has not reconciled, with
  the decisive moderator named (`jpsp-literature-positioning`: a real puzzle, not "X is understudied").
- **The package converges** — each study has a distinct, stated role (causation → mechanism → ecological
  validity → discriminant test), so no single study can break the story
  (`jpsp-study-design` convergence).
- **The key effect is reported with magnitude and interval** — *d* = 0.46, 95% CI [0.29, 0.63] and the
  mediation *ab* with its CI, not bare *p* values (`jpsp-data-analysis`).
- **A diagnostic hypothesis rules out rivals** — the vulnerability × responsiveness interaction is one a
  competing account does *not* predict, and Study 5 pre-empts the disclosure-intimacy alternative
  (`jpsp-theory-and-hypotheses` diagnosticity; `jpsp-study-design` alternative-explanation study).
- **Rich but disciplined** — the narrative is theory-driven yet written tightly enough to fit the IRGP
  intro + discussion cap, with detail pushed to embedded exhibits (`jpsp-writing-style`).
- **Method is demoted to a tool** — "five studies" appears only where the package is described, never as
  the lead (avoids the `jpsp-writing-style` "leading with method" anti-pattern).
