# Worked Example: A CA-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **CA Major-Article introduction bar** from `curranthro-writing-style`,
`curranthro-topic-selection`, and `curranthro-theory-building`: **by the end of the introduction the
reader knows the question, the argument, the evidence, the author's position in the field, why it matters
to anthropology *broadly*, and why it is a claim worth debating** — because under the **CA✩ Treatment**
this paper will be published *with* signed Comments and the author's Reply. The arc is **question →
all-fields significance → ethnographic setting & access (with positionality) → the grounded finding and
its mechanism → the portable, commentary-worthy concept → brief roadmap.**

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Counting the Dead Twice: Ledger-Keeping and the Making of Ancestral
Debt in a Highland Trading Confederacy."* Fictional setting: a highland market network where households
keep parallel ledgers of the living and of the dead. (Subfield: sociocultural anthropology; mode:
ethnographic fieldwork — participant observation + ledger analysis. Chosen because record-keeping, value,
and ancestry are stakes that reach exchange theory, the archaeology of accounting/notation, and the
linguistics of enumeration — and bold enough that commentators across subfields would argue about it.)

---

## Before (subfield-insider, descriptive, no commentary-worthy claim — typical first draft)

> The anthropology of money and accounting has grown in recent years. Many scholars have studied
> household record-keeping in various societies. In this paper, based on fourteen months of fieldwork, I
> describe how households in a highland trading confederacy keep ledgers, including ledgers concerning
> deceased kin. I conducted participant observation and examined ledgers. I find that ledger-keeping is
> important to households and helps them organize obligations. Section 2 reviews the literature on
> accounting, Section 3 describes my methods, Section 4 describes the ledgers, Section 5 discusses my
> findings, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `curranthro-writing-style` / `curranthro-topic-selection`):**

- **Subfield-insider framing.** It speaks only to economic-anthropology specialists; an archaeologist of
  notation, a linguist of number, or a bioanthropologist is given no reason to care. CA's bar is
  **all-fields significance** — a named anti-pattern in `curranthro-topic-selection`.
- **Description, not an agenda-setting intervention.** "Ledger-keeping is important" is not a
  contribution; nothing here a scholar in another subfield could *import* (`curranthro-theory-building`'s
  portability test fails), and nothing a commentator would bother to debate (fails the CA✩ test).
- **No positionality.** We learn nothing about the author's relationship to the households or how access
  shaped what was seen (`curranthro-theory-building`: reflexivity).
- **No ethics in view.** Ledgers of the dead are sensitive; consent and anonymization are invisible
  (`curranthro-transparency-and-data`).
- **Over-signposted six-section roadmap** doing the work the argument should do, and padding the word
  count CA caps (`curranthro-writing-style`: references and endnotes count toward the limit).

---

## After (CA arc — all-fields significance + reflexive argument + commentary-worthy concept)

> **When a highland household enters a death into its ledger, is it closing an account — or opening a new
> creditor?** I argue that double ledger-keeping does not merely record obligations; it *converts the dead
> into ongoing economic agents*, so that ancestors continue to lend, charge interest, and be owed long
> after burial. *(question + the portable, debatable claim in the first breath — this is about how
> accounting produces ancestry, not a description of bookkeeping.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond the anthropology of finance: notation and the recording of value sit at the
> center of anthropology's oldest questions — reciprocity and the gift in social theory, the archaeology
> of tallies and proto-writing, and the linguistics of how quantity and the dead are *named*. If a
> bookkeeping practice can keep the dead economically alive, it speaks to how recording technologies
> remake kinship and time across human life. *(all-fields significance, framed so an archaeologist of
> notation and a linguistic anthropologist of enumeration both see the stake — `curranthro-topic-selection`
> all-fields test; and bold enough to invite Comment.)*
>
> I came to the ledgers not as an auditor but as a debtor: to be trusted, I was enrolled into a
> household's accounts, and my own entry — and what it would have cost me to default to an ancestor —
> taught me the practice from inside. My account is shaped by that position. *(positionality made part of
> the argument's credibility — `curranthro-theory-building` reflexivity, disarming a predictable Comment.)*
>
> Over fourteen months of participant observation and close ledger analysis — with consent renewed as the
> project changed, and with all households and the confederacy given pseudonyms because records of the
> dead can expose the living to claims and harm — I traced how an entry reclassifies a deceased kinsperson
> from mourned relative into standing creditor. *(setting, design, and ethics in one breath; consent and
> anonymization stated, per `curranthro-research-design` and `curranthro-transparency-and-data`.)*
>
> The mechanism is **not** simply memory or pious obligation, the usual explanations: households describe,
> and enact, a *transfer of economic personhood* — the language of mourning gives way to the language of
> accounts at the moment of entry, after which a missed offering is booked as arrears, not grief. *(the
> finding restated with its mechanism and a ruled-out rival reading — the `curranthro-research-design`
> adjudication move, anticipating the first Comment.)*
>
> My contribution is a portable concept I call **posthumous crediting**: the process by which a recording
> practice extends economic agency past death, turning ancestors into participants in a living ledger.
> This reframes household accounting — long treated as a tool of memory — as a *kinship-and-credit
> technology*, and offers a concept an archaeologist of tallies or a linguist of number could carry into
> their own material. I expect commentators to press whether "crediting" over-economizes mourning; I take
> that debate up directly. *(contribution stated early as a *portable, debatable* argument — the
> `curranthro-theory-building` portability + commentary stress test.)*
>
> The article proceeds in three movements: the ledger and the moment of entry; the language and ritual
> through which the dead are credited; and the scope conditions under which records make ancestors agents.
> *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the CA bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **All-fields significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names stakes a theorist of
  exchange, an archaeologist of notation, and a linguistic anthropologist of number all recognize
  (`curranthro-topic-selection`: "a scholar in a different subfield should see why it matters").
- **A portable, commentary-worthy concept, not a description** — "posthumous crediting" is offered for
  others to use and is bold enough to debate, passing the `curranthro-theory-building` portability +
  commentary stress test; the finding is attached to a mechanism with scope conditions.
- **Reflexivity is visible** — the author's position as a debtor is part of the evidence
  (`curranthro-theory-building`: "name how your position bears on the interpretation").
- **Ethics is woven in** — ongoing consent and pseudonymization appear in the introduction because records
  of the dead can endanger the living (`curranthro-transparency-and-data`).
- **The strongest rival reading is adjudicated** — "not simply memory or pious obligation… instead a
  transfer of economic personhood" (the `curranthro-research-design` adjudication sentence), pre-empting
  the obvious Comment.
- **Built for the CA✩ Treatment** — the introduction names the debate it expects ("whether 'crediting'
  over-economizes mourning") and promises to engage it, exactly what a Major Article anchoring published
  Comments + a Reply should do (`curranthro-review-process`).
- **Reads across subfields and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and glossed, the roadmap is three
  movements not six sections, and the framing is legible to a generalist within CA's Major-Article word
  limit (`curranthro-writing-style`: 6,000–10,000 words incl. references and endnotes).
