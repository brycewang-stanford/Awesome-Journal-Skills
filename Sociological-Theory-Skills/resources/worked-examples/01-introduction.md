# Worked Example: An ST-Style Theory Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **ST introduction arc** from `soctheory-writing-style`:
**theoretical problem (the tension) → the tradition it lives in → what existing theory cannot do →
the move this paper makes → the contribution (the new way of seeing), stated early** — for a
purely *conceptual* contribution. The deliverable is **concepts + a mechanism + numbered
propositions**, never hypotheses-with-data. The vocabulary rule
(`soctheory-writing-style`, `soctheory-theory-construction`) is absolute: **propositions, not
hypotheses; mechanisms, not findings; illustrations, not tests; no "H1," no "supported," no
estimation, ever.**

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Provisional Belonging: How Membership Survives the Loss of
Its Criteria."* Fictional conceptual setting: groups whose formal entry criteria have dissolved
yet whose boundary persists (the example is invented to carry the argument; it asserts nothing
about any real group, organization, or policy).

---

## Before (reads as a literature review / essay — typical first-draft intro)

> The concept of group boundaries has been studied extensively in cultural sociology and
> social-identity research. Many scholars have examined how groups draw symbolic boundaries and
> maintain membership. Lamont and others distinguished moral, cultural, and socioeconomic
> boundaries, and later work has added refinements. In this paper, we hypothesize that groups
> facing the erosion of their entry criteria will engage in boundary-maintenance behaviors, and
> we propose a model in which criterion-erosion moderates the boundary–cohesion relationship.
> Group boundaries are an important topic. This paper reviews the literature and integrates
> several streams. Section 2 reviews boundary theory, Section 3 develops our model, Section 4
> discusses implications, and Section 5 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `soctheory-writing-style` / `soctheory-theory-construction` /
`soctheory-contribution-framing`):**

- **Opens with a definition and a literature survey** ("has been studied extensively"), not the
  theoretical *tension* — a named anti-pattern in `soctheory-writing-style` (dictionary/survey opening).
- **Uses hypothesis-and-data vocabulary** ("we hypothesize," "moderates the … relationship").
  That is ASR/AJS register; it reads as **wrong-journal** to ST editors
  (`soctheory-theory-construction`: never "H1," never moderation-to-be-tested).
- **No theoretical problem on page one.** A reader cannot tell what existing theory *cannot* see.
- **Contribution is "we integrate streams"** — surveying/organizing a literature is *not* a
  theoretical contribution at ST (`soctheory-contribution-framing` sizing table).
- **No mechanism** — the social process that would make any relationship hold is absent
  (`soctheory-theory-construction` build order, step 3).
- **No positioned move** — it "draws on" boundary theory without saying whether it extends,
  challenges, reconstructs, or synthesizes (`soctheory-literature-positioning`).

---

## After (ST arc — problem → tradition → gap → move → contribution early)

> Membership is theorized everywhere as the *application of a criterion*: a group admits those
> who meet a standard — a credential, a belief, a shared practice — and the boundary is the line
> the criterion draws. Yet some groups keep a hard boundary **after the criterion that once
> defined them has dissolved**: no one can any longer say what the entry test *is*, and still
> insiders and outsiders are sorted with confidence. Existing theory has no vocabulary for this:
> it can model how criteria draw boundaries and how boundaries shift when criteria change, but
> not how a boundary **outlives its own criterion**. *(the theoretical problem — a tension current
> theory cannot resolve — in the first breath.)*
>
> This problem lives in the conversation on symbolic boundaries and group membership, where a
> boundary is theorized as the trace of a classificatory criterion that members share. That
> framing makes the criterion logically prior to the boundary, so when a criterion erodes the
> field expects the boundary to blur. *(the tradition, and the specific commitment that creates
> the blind spot.)*
>
> We **challenge and extend** that tradition. We argue that a boundary can become **detached from
> its criterion** and run on a different basis, and we build the theory that specifies how. We
> define two concepts — **provisional belonging** (membership held on the strength of an
> unsettled, no-longer-specifiable criterion) and **criterial drift** (the process by which the
> ground of a boundary migrates from a shared standard to the ongoing practice of sorting
> itself) — and we specify the mechanism that links them: once a criterion is unrecoverable,
> each act of *treating* someone as inside or outside re-enacts the boundary, so the boundary is
> reproduced by its own application rather than by the lost standard. *(the move: positioned
> against a named tradition, with new concepts + the mechanism, not a data plan.)*
>
> From this we derive propositions. **P1:** the more unrecoverable a group's defining criterion,
> the more its boundary is sustained by criterial drift, because each act of sorting becomes the
> boundary's only remaining ground. **P2:** provisional belonging is stable only while the
> criterion remains *unsettled*; if members converge on an explicit replacement criterion, drift
> ceases and the boundary reverts to ordinary criterial maintenance. *(propositions stated as
> relationships between concepts, each with a one-line mechanism — never an empirical magnitude.)*
>
> Our contribution is to **respecify the boundary–criterion relation**: before this paper,
> boundary theory treated the criterion as prior and the boundary as its trace; after it, theory
> can see boundaries that have **outlived their criteria and are reproduced by their own
> enactment**, and can state the condition under which that detachment ends. This differs from the
> nearest prior view — boundary-as-trace-of-criterion — not by adding a contingency but by
> changing what *grounds* a boundary in the theory. *(contribution framed before → after, against
> a named prior view, per `soctheory-contribution-framing`.)* The reframing opens implications for
> any conversation in which a social form persists after its original basis is gone — institutions,
> rituals, and reputations among them. *(implication for theory — reasoned, not empirical advice.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. We first define the two concepts and their assumptions, then
> specify the drift mechanism and derive the propositions, and finally state the boundary
> conditions and implications for theory. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the ST bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Opens with the theoretical problem, not a definition or survey** — the tension ("a boundary
  that outlives its criterion") is on page one (`soctheory-writing-style`: problem first; no
  dictionary opening).
- **Positioned move is explicit** — "We challenge and extend" the symbolic-boundaries tradition,
  not "we draw on" it (`soctheory-literature-positioning`).
- **Concepts are defined on first use and labeled consistently** — *provisional belonging* and
  *criterial drift* each get a one-sentence definition and are reused verbatim
  (`soctheory-theory-construction` concept template).
- **The mechanism is explicit** — "each act of sorting re-enacts the boundary" is the engine,
  stated before the propositions (`soctheory-theory-construction` build order, step 3).
- **Propositions, not hypotheses** — P1–P2 are relationships between concepts, each earned by a
  preceding argument and carrying a mechanism; no "H1," no magnitudes, no "supported"
  (`soctheory-theory-construction` proposition discipline; `soctheory-writing-style`
  no-hypothesis-vocabulary rule).
- **Contribution stated early and as before → after, against a named prior view**
  ("boundary-as-trace-of-criterion"), and explicitly **not** a relabel or an added moderator
  (`soctheory-contribution-framing`).
- **No methods/results/data** anywhere — the reasoning *is* the contribution; the implication is
  a reasoned theoretical consequence, not proven advice (`soctheory-writing-style`).
- **A boundary condition is theorized, not apologized for** — P2 bounds the theory (provisional
  belonging is stable only while the criterion is unsettled), which is itself part of the
  contribution (`soctheory-boundary-conditions`).
