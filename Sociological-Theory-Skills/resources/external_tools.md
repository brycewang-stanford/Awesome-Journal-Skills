# External Tools & Resources for ST Theory-Construction

**Sociological Theory** (ST) publishes **conceptual, theory-construction work only**. There is
**no estimation, no hypothesis test, and no results section**; data, if used, is illustrative.
So this resource list is deliberately different from an empirical journal's: it centers on tools
for *finding the theoretical conversation*, *managing a foundational literature*, *drawing
conceptual figures*, and *checking the logic of an argument* — not on statistical software or
data vendors.

> Volatile specifics (editorial team, exact length/abstract limits, processing fee, portal
> requirements) change. Always verify submission-stage requirements against the official ST /
> SAGE author pages before submission (检索于 2026-06；以官网为准).

## 1. Locating the theoretical conversation

| Tool | Use |
|------|-----|
| Web of Science / Scopus | Citation-trail mapping: who cites the concept you want to extend, and where the conversation has stalled |
| Google Scholar | "Cited by" forward search to find the most recent theoretical critiques of a concept |
| Sociological Theory + ASR/AJS archives | The home conversation — ST theory almost always engages an existing ST/ASR/AJS stream |
| Connected Papers / Litmaps | Visual citation graphs to see the structure of a theoretical tradition and spot the gap |
| Annual Review of Sociology | Authoritative state-of-the-field summaries; a good review tells you where a theory is exhausted vs. open (and is itself a review venue, not a theory venue) |

## 2. Foundational theory traditions (durable, not volatile)

These are the perennial bases sociological theory builds on or against. Knowing **which
conversation you are in** matters more than any single citation.

- Classical theory: Marx, Weber, Durkheim, Simmel — read in service of a move, not name-dropped
- Field theory: Bourdieu (field, capital, habitus)
- Pragmatism / cultural-cognitive: habit, problem-situations, dual-process models of culture
- Social mechanisms / analytical sociology: mechanism-based explanation, micro-macro links
- Cultural sociology: meaning, classification, performance, the strong program
- Systems theory: Luhmann (communication, differentiation, autopoiesis)
- Relational sociology: transactions over substances, ties, processes
- Metatheory / philosophy of social science: warrant, interpretation, abduction, realism vs. interpretivism

ST follows the **ASA Style Guide** (author-date in-text; full reference list; 7th edition, June
2022 — verify current). Configure your manager to ASA author-date output and verify against the
current ASA Style Guide. **References and footnotes count toward ST's inclusive length cap**, so
prune from the first draft.

## 4. Conceptual figures (the only "figures" ST uses)

ST figures carry *theoretical* work: mechanism diagrams, 2x2 / nxm typologies, process models,
concept maps, recursive/feedback loops. They are NOT data plots.

| Tool | Use |
|------|-----|
| Microsoft PowerPoint / Keynote | Most common for mechanism diagrams and process models; export to high-resolution image |
| draw.io (diagrams.net) | Free; clean orthogonal connectors for multi-stage process models |
| Lucidchart | Collaborative diagramming; good for typologies and 2x2 matrices |
| OmniGraffle (macOS) | Precise control for publication-quality conceptual models |
| TikZ / LaTeX | For authors who draft in LaTeX; precise typologies and process diagrams |

Design rule: every box must be a defined concept, every arrow a stated mechanism. A figure that
cannot be read off the propositions is decoration, not theory (see
[`../skills/soctheory-conceptual-exhibits/SKILL.md`](../skills/soctheory-conceptual-exhibits/SKILL.md)).

## 5. Argument-logic aids

Theory papers are evaluated on logical soundness. These help you stress-test the argument before
reviewers do.

- Argument-mapping tools (e.g., Rationale, MindMup, or a simple premise → premise → conclusion
  outline) to expose hidden assumptions
- Toulmin model (claim / grounds / warrant / backing / rebuttal) as a per-proposition checklist
- Counterfactual / hard-case worksheets: for each proposition, name a case where it would NOT
  hold and check whether a boundary condition covers it
- Abduction logs: write the "surprising observation → best theoretical explanation" chain in
  prose before formalizing the proposition (cf. Timmermans & Tavory, ST 2012)

## 8. Cautions

1. **No empirical test, ever.** If you find yourself wanting a "sample," "measure," or
   "estimate," the project belongs at ASR/AJS, not ST. ST builds the theory those journals later
   examine; data here is illustrative only.
2. **Engage the home conversation.** Verify you have read the recent ST/ASR/AJS pieces on your
   concept; reviewers are usually drawn from exactly that tradition.
3. **Verify the venue of every cited paper.** Several canonical theory papers were published in
   ASR or AJS, not ST — a misattribution costs credibility (see
   [`exemplars/library.md`](exemplars/library.md)).
4. **Verify limits and the fee.** Length, abstract, formatting limits and the processing fee can
   change — confirm on the current author page before you submit.
5. **Do not submit while under review elsewhere.** The ASA treats simultaneous submission as
   unethical.
