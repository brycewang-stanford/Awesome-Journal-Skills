# Worked Example: An AERJ-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **AERJ introduction arc** that the skills above ask for. AERJ is the **flagship
journal of the American Educational Research Association**, read across the *whole field* of education
research and demanding of **both quantitative and qualitative** work. So the introduction must do five
things, in plain language a researcher from another subfield can follow:

1. **Question of broad significance** — not a one-district description (`aerj-topic-selection`).
2. **Field-wide stake** — who beyond the niche cares, and the policy/practice stake
   (`aerj-topic-selection`, `aerj-literature-positioning`).
3. **A conceptual framework that does work** — the frame motivates the design, not decoration
   (`aerj-theory-and-framework`).
4. **A credible design defended on its own terms** — nesting acknowledged, the strongest rival named
   (`aerj-research-design`).
5. **Front-loaded contribution** for a general education-research audience, in **APA 7th** style
   (`aerj-writing-style`).

It also states a **dominant education-research lens** at the outset. AERJ is now an integrated,
field-wide journal, so the framing should signal fit across the whole readership while making clear
whether the paper primarily speaks to policy/institutional, teaching/learning, developmental, or
cross-cutting debates (`aerj-topic-selection`).

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Whose Counselor? Caseload Reductions, Counselor–Student Match,
and College-Going in a Statewide Reform."* Fictional setting: an invented state that lowered the
maximum high-school counselor caseload, phased in across districts in different years. **Dominant lens:
policy/institutional resource allocation** (a system-level organization question), with attention to
**equity** and **context** as AERJ expects.

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> School counselors have long been studied in the education literature, and many scholars have written
> about their roles. In this paper, we use a difference-in-differences design with a multilevel model to
> study a policy that reduced counselor caseloads. We assemble administrative data on students and
> schools and estimate the effect of the reform on college enrollment. Counseling is an important topic
> for schools and policymakers. We also explore whether effects differ by student subgroup. Section 2
> reviews the literature, Section 3 presents our conceptual framework, Section 4 describes the data and
> methods, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against the skills):**

- **Leads with method** ("we use a difference-in-differences design with a multilevel model") instead of
  the question — the anti-pattern named in `aerj-topic-selection` and `aerj-writing-style`.
- **No question and no contribution on page one.** A reader from another subfield cannot tell what was
  found or why the field should care (`aerj-writing-style`: front-load the contribution).
- **No broad significance.** "Important topic for schools" is throat-clearing, not a field-wide stake
  (`aerj-topic-selection`: who *beyond the niche* cares).
- **Framework is a section number, not a frame.** Nothing here does conceptual work
  (`aerj-theory-and-framework`: a "theory" that never reappears is an anti-pattern).
- **Design asserted, not defended.** Nesting is named as a model choice but no rival explanation is ruled
  out (`aerj-research-design`: the adjudication sentence is missing).
- **Equity as an afterthought** ("explore whether effects differ by subgroup") rather than a framed
  question (`aerj-topic-selection`, `aerj-theory-and-framework`: who, where, under what conditions).
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do (`aerj-writing-style`).

## After (AERJ arc — broad question, framework that works, design defended, contribution early)

> **Does giving students more access to a school counselor change who goes to college — and does it
> narrow gaps, or widen them?** We show that a statewide reduction in counselor caseloads raised
> four-year college enrollment by 2.1 percentage points on average, but that the gain was concentrated
> among students who were newly matched to a counselor who shared their first-generation status — a
> pattern that *closed* part of the first-generation enrollment gap rather than lifting all students
> equally. *(Question of broad significance + headline finding + the equity stake, in the first breath;
> APA-style numbers.)*
>
> The stakes reach past school counseling. Counseling caseloads are one of the few staffing levers a
> state can pull quickly, and the field has long debated whether *more adult contact* or *better-matched
> adult contact* is what converts opportunity into outcomes — a question about how schools as
> organizations turn resources into learning and life chances that matters to anyone studying staffing,
> equity, or the organization of schooling. *(Field-wide stake; the cross-subfield framing
> `aerj-literature-positioning` asks for — speaking past the counseling niche to organizational and
> equity literatures.)*
>
> We read the reform through a **resource-and-relationships** framework: a counselor is not an
> interchangeable unit of "dosage" but a relational resource whose value depends on the *match* between
> counselor and student (here, shared first-generation experience). The framework does real work — it
> predicts that lowering caseloads should help most where it produces a *better-matched* relationship,
> not merely *more* contact, and it tells us to estimate match-specific effects rather than a single
> average. *(A framework that motivates the design, per `aerj-theory-and-framework`; equity assumptions
> made explicit.)*
>
> Identifying this is hard because districts that cut caseloads may differ from those that did not in
> exactly the ways that drive college-going. We exploit that the reform phased in **district by district
> across different years** for budget-cycle reasons unrelated to local college-going trends, comparing
> each district to not-yet-treated districts with a heterogeneity-robust staggered design, and we verify
> no differential pre-trends. Because students are nested in schools and districts, we model the nesting
> explicitly and cluster inference at the district level. *(Design defended on its own terms; nesting
> acknowledged — the default in `aerj-research-design`.)*
>
> The strongest rival is that better-resourced districts adopted earlier *and* improved college advising
> in other ways at the same time. **If that rival were true rather than our account, the gains would
> appear system-wide and before the caseload change; instead they appear only after a student is matched
> to a same-background counselor, and not in unmatched classrooms in the same schools.** *(The
> adjudication sentence `aerj-research-design` requires — the rival is ruled out, not waved away.)*
>
> This paper makes three contributions. Conceptually, it reframes counselor access from a *dosage*
> question into a *match* question, and shows the reframing changes what we conclude about an equity
> reform. Empirically, it provides the first heterogeneity-robust estimates of a statewide caseload
> reform on college-going, with effects identified off match rather than average contact. For policy and
> practice, it implies that *how* counseling capacity is allocated — not only *how much* of it there is —
> determines whether a popular reform narrows or merely shifts opportunity gaps. *(Contribution
> front-loaded — conceptual, empirical, and policy/practice — as `aerj-writing-style` and
> `aerj-topic-selection` require.)*

## Why the "after" clears the AERJ bar (skill-by-skill)

| AERJ demand | Skill | Where the "after" satisfies it |
|---|---|---|
| Broad significance, not one-district description | `aerj-topic-selection` | First two paragraphs: the dosage-vs-match debate and the equity stake reach the whole field, not just counseling. |
| Dominant lens, framed not by method | `aerj-topic-selection` | A system-level resource-allocation question -> **policy/institutional lens**; the method (multilevel DiD) does not decide the AERJ fit. |
| Field-wide positioning, cross-subfield | `aerj-literature-positioning` | Connects counseling to organizational staffing and equity literatures — the "so what for the field" sentence. |
| Framework that does work | `aerj-theory-and-framework` | The resource-and-relationships frame *generates* the match-specific estimand and the design choice. |
| Design defended; rival ruled out; nesting modeled | `aerj-research-design` | Staggered heterogeneity-robust design, explicit nesting/clustering, and the adjudication sentence. |
| Front-loaded contribution, APA, reads for the field | `aerj-writing-style` | Question + finding + stake in paragraph one; contribution stated explicitly; plain language; APA-style numerals. |

> **Note on numbers and policy:** every figure, the framework name, and the reform are **invented for
> teaching**. When you adapt this arc, the framework, estimand, and rival must come from *your* study;
> see the matching skills above, and benchmark design positioning against real AERJ articles before
> importing journal-process claims.
