# Worked Example: A Mind-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **Mind introduction arc** drawn from this pack's own skill files:
**state a sharp, deniable thesis → locate the live debate and the target view → sketch the argument
(premises → conclusion) → flag the load-bearing premise → anticipate the strongest objection → give a
short roadmap** — all within reach of a broad philosophical readership, with the rule from
[`mind-structure-and-exposition`](../../skills/mind-structure-and-exposition/SKILL.md) that **a Mind
reader should know your claim by the end of page one**.

There are no data, statistics, or empirical results here: at Mind, as
[`mind-thesis-and-argument`](../../skills/mind-thesis-and-argument/SKILL.md) puts it, **the paper *is*
the argument**.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Promising Without Intending: Why the Communicative Act, Not the
Inner State, Binds."* Fictional thesis: that the obligation created by a promise is grounded in the
public communicative act, not in any prior intention to perform.

---

## Before (buries the thesis — typical first-draft intro)

> The nature of promising has been discussed extensively in the philosophical literature. Many writers
> have considered the relationship between promises, intentions, and obligations, and the topic remains
> of great interest. In this paper I will explore some questions about what happens when someone makes a
> promise. I will look at several views and consider various cases, and I will suggest that the standard
> picture may face certain difficulties. The discussion will draw on work in the philosophy of language
> and in ethics. Section 2 surveys the literature on promising. Section 3 introduces my framework.
> Section 4 discusses objections. Section 5 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **No thesis on page one.** "I will suggest that the standard picture may face certain difficulties"
  is not a deniable claim — it is a promise to have one later. Violates
  [`mind-thesis-and-argument`](../../skills/mind-thesis-and-argument/SKILL.md) ("State the thesis. One
  sentence, in the strongest defensible form. It must be **deniable**.").
- **No argument is sketched.** The reader cannot see which premises are meant to yield which conclusion.
- **A "literature dump," not a live debate.** "Discussed extensively… remains of great interest" names
  no disagreement and no target view — the anti-pattern flagged by
  [`mind-literature-positioning`](../../skills/mind-literature-positioning/SKILL.md).
- **No nearest competitor placed,** so a referee's first question ("isn't this just the standard
  expressivist line?") is unanswered.
- **Throat-clearing and hedging** ("may face certain difficulties") — the
  [`mind-writing-style`](../../skills/mind-writing-style/SKILL.md) anti-pattern.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do — flagged by
  [`mind-structure-and-exposition`](../../skills/mind-structure-and-exposition/SKILL.md).

---

## After (Mind arc — deniable thesis, the debate, the argument, the objection, a short roadmap)

> **A promise binds in virtue of what it communicates, not in virtue of any intention to perform that
> lies behind it.** I argue that an agent who sincerely utters "I promise to φ" incurs the obligation to
> φ even if, at the moment of utterance, she forms no intention to φ at all — and that this is not a
> defect in the promise but a feature of what promising *is*. *(The thesis, in one deniable sentence,
> stated first: a competent peer can reject it, and many will.)*
>
> The debate I enter is over the **ground** of promissory obligation. On the **intention-first** view —
> the target of this paper — a promise obligates because, and only because, it expresses a genuine
> commitment of the will; the utterance is evidence of a binding inner state, and where that state is
> absent the "promise" is at best defective and at worst void. *(The live disagreement and the
> strongest version of the opposing view, stated in its own terms, as
> [`mind-literature-positioning`](../../skills/mind-literature-positioning/SKILL.md) requires — not a
> strawman.)*
>
> My argument is short. **(P1)** A sincere, uptaken utterance of "I promise to φ" is a public
> communicative act that invites the hearer to rely on the speaker's φ-ing. **(P2)** When such reliance
> is invited and the invitation is accepted, the speaker incurs an obligation not to defeat it. **(P3)**
> Whether reliance has been invited and accepted is fixed by the communicative act and its uptake, not
> by any inner intention concealed behind it. **(C)** Therefore the obligation is grounded in the
> communicative act, not in the intention. *(The premises and the conclusion are visible at once, as
> [`mind-thesis-and-argument`](../../skills/mind-thesis-and-argument/SKILL.md) demands; this is an
> inference the reader can check, not a result to be reported.)*
>
> The **load-bearing premise is (P3)**, and I defend it hardest in §3: that what binds is the public
> act, not the private state behind it. The **nearest competing view** holds that (P3) is false because
> an insincere promise — one made with no intention to perform — is merely a successful *deception*, not
> a *promise*, and so generates no obligation. *(The closest competitor is placed early, pre-empting
> "isn't this just the standard line?")*
>
> I take that objection to be the strongest, and I meet it head-on: the insincere promisor is bound
> *precisely because* her hearer cannot, and need not, peer behind the utterance — to deny this is to
> make every obligation hostage to a private state no one can check, which would unravel the practice of
> promising altogether. *(The strongest objection is named in the opponent's voice and answered, per
> [`mind-objections-and-replies`](../../skills/mind-objections-and-replies/SKILL.md) — not quarantined
> for a later section.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 fixes the target intention-first view and the case that
> motivates it; Section 3 defends (P1)–(P3), with (P3) the focus; Section 4 answers the deception
> objection and two others. *(A short roadmap — no over-signposting, per
> [`mind-structure-and-exposition`](../../skills/mind-structure-and-exposition/SKILL.md).)*

## Why the "after" meets the Mind bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Deniable thesis, stated first** — the opening sentence is a claim a competent peer can reject (and
  the intention-first theorist will), satisfying
  [`mind-thesis-and-argument`](../../skills/mind-thesis-and-argument/SKILL.md)'s deniability test and
  [`mind-structure-and-exposition`](../../skills/mind-structure-and-exposition/SKILL.md)'s "thesis by
  the end of page one."
- **A live debate with a steelmanned target** — the intention-first view is stated in its **strongest**
  form before being opposed, as [`mind-literature-positioning`](../../skills/mind-literature-positioning/SKILL.md)
  requires; the nearest competitor (the deception reply) is placed, not hidden.
- **The argument is explicit and checkable** — numbered premises (P1)–(P3) → conclusion (C), the form
  Mind accepts (deductive, defending the premises), with the **load-bearing premise (P3) named** so the
  reader knows where the weight falls — straight from
  [`mind-thesis-and-argument`](../../skills/mind-thesis-and-argument/SKILL.md).
- **The strongest objection is anticipated and answered in the intro**, in the opponent's voice, per
  [`mind-objections-and-replies`](../../skills/mind-objections-and-replies/SKILL.md) — "anticipating and
  answering the best objection is much of the contribution."
- **Accessible, argument-first prose** — short declarative sentences lead with the claim then the
  reason; no formalism is left unglossed, meeting
  [`mind-writing-style`](../../skills/mind-writing-style/SKILL.md).
- **Scope is calibrated** — the thesis claims exactly what the argument earns (the *ground* of the
  obligation), not the broader and indefensible "intentions never matter to promising."
