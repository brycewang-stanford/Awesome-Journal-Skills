# Worked Example: Reframing an Introduction for Perspectives on Psychological Science

**This is a fictional example. It does not describe a real paper, dataset, or finding.** It illustrates
how an introduction is rewritten into the PoPS voice — broad, provocative, accessible, with the claim
stated up front. (Numbers are invented for illustration.)

## Before (reads like a single-study report)

> In Experiment 1 (N = 142) we tested whether ego depletion impairs subsequent self-control on a Stroop
> task. We found a significant effect (d = 0.31, p = .04). In Experiment 2 we replicated this with a
> different manipulation. These results contribute to the self-control literature and suggest that
> willpower is a limited resource. We discuss implications and call for more research.

## Diagnosis

- **Wrong venue signal.** This is a two-study empirical report — it belongs at *Psychological Science*,
  not PoPS. PoPS does not publish single empirical studies.
- **No superordinate message.** The take-home is one effect, not a claim *about the field*.
- **No organizing spine.** Nothing reframes the area; it reads as paper-then-paper.
- **Annotated-bibliography risk.** "Contributes to the literature" and "call for more research" are the
  exact phrases a PoPS reviewer treats as empty.
- **Buried claim, no provocation.** The reader cannot tell, by sentence three, what to argue about.

## After (a PoPS perspective opening)

> For two decades the field treated self-control as a single depletable resource — a framing that
> launched hundreds of studies and a popular metaphor. We argue this framing is the wrong unit of
> analysis, and that the replication turbulence around "ego depletion" is a symptom of it rather than an
> anomaly. Synthesizing across the cognitive, motivational, and developmental literatures, we propose a
> **process taxonomy** that sorts self-control phenomena by the mechanism each task actually engages
> (attention allocation, value computation, or strategic deployment). The taxonomy reconciles a decade
> of contradictory effect sizes — studies disagree because they sit in different cells, not because the
> effect is real-or-fake — and it predicts where the field should *and should not* expect depletion-like
> patterns. We close with three discriminating tests and the open-data resources the field now needs to
> run them.

## Why the "after" fits PoPS

- **Superordinate claim, stated first:** the framing itself is wrong — a claim about the field, not one effect.
- **Cross-area integration:** cognitive + motivational + developmental, the breadth PoPS expects.
- **An organizing spine:** the process taxonomy is the contribution; it *reconciles* contradictions.
- **Provocative + forward-looking:** it reframes a popular construct and ends with discriminating tests, not "more research is needed."
- **Calibrated, not a hot take:** the reframe is offered as an argument with evidence and testable predictions.
