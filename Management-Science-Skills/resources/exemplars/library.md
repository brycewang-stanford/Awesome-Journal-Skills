# Management Science Exemplars Library (method × Department)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06-07.** Every paper below was checked against INFORMS
> PubsOnline (`pubsonline.informs.org/journal/mnsc` / DOI prefix `10.1287/mnsc.*`) to confirm it actually
> appeared in ***Management Science*** specifically — *not* a sister or look-alike venue. Papers without
> full confirmation as *Management Science* were **omitted** and several are listed below as
> explicit guardrails. It is deliberately a short, clean list (6 verified) rather than a long, uncertain
> one.
>
> **Sibling-confusion warning.** *Management Science* (INFORMS) is **not** *Operations Research*, *M&SOM*
> (Manufacturing & Service Operations Management), *POM* (Production and Operations Management),
> *Organization Science*, *Marketing Science*, or *Information Systems Research* — all distinct INFORMS or
> sibling journals with their own DOIs. Several landmark papers people *assume* are in Management Science
> are actually in other journals; see the omissions section.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design / contribution positioning only**. It
> does **not** reproduce or invent any coefficient, theorem statement, or finding — read the original on
> INFORMS PubsOnline before citing any result or number. For venue-specific policy, scope, and house
> style, see [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × Department** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a
*rigorous* result deliver a *decision-relevant insight that travels across Departments* — the Management
Science bar (see [`../../skills/mgsci-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/mgsci-topic-selection/SKILL.md)
and [`../../skills/mgsci-contribution-framing/SKILL.md`](../../skills/mgsci-contribution-framing/SKILL.md)).
For each, ask the self-check before claiming your paper is "Management Science-shaped." The list is built
to span the journal's **bimethodological, multi-department** range: analytical OM, optimization,
empirical IS, behavioral decision analysis, marketing science, and business strategy.

## By method × Department

### Analytical / stochastic operations — multi-echelon inventory (Operations Management)

- **Clark, A. J. & Scarf, H., "Optimal Policies for a Multi-Echelon Inventory Problem," *Management
  Science* 1960, 6(4):475–490.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.6.4.475` (`pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/mnsc.6.4.475`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a clean analytical result (the echelon base-stock structure) that founded
    multi-echelon inventory theory — formal optimization whose managerial reading (how to set stock at
    each stage of a serial supply chain) is immediate and general. The archetypal Management Science
    analytical-OM paper.
  - **Self-check:** does your model deliver a *policy structure* a manager can act on, not just an
    existence theorem?

### Diffusion model bridging theory and data (Marketing / new-product management)

- **Bass, F. M., "A New Product Growth for Model Consumer Durables," *Management Science* 1969,
  15(5):215–227.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.1040.0264` (50th-anniversary reprint of the 1969 original;
  `pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.1040.0264`). INFORMS members voted it a Top-10 most-influential
  Management Science paper.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** one compact model (innovation + imitation) that both *explains* the S-curve
    of adoption and *fits* eleven consumer durables — a mechanism with a decision lever (forecasting and
    timing new-product launches) that travels far beyond its original products.
  - **Self-check:** can your mechanism be stated simply *and* confronted with data, with a managerial use
    that generalizes past the specific application?

### Methodology with a managerial reading — efficiency measurement (Optimization & Decision Analytics)

- **Banker, R. D., Charnes, A. & Cooper, W. W., "Some Models for Estimating Technical and Scale
  Inefficiencies in Data Envelopment Analysis," *Management Science* 1984, 30(9):1078–1092.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.30.9.1078` (`pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/mnsc.30.9.1078`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a methodological advance (the BCC variable-returns-to-scale DEA model) that
    nonetheless answers a decision question every manager asks — *which units are efficient, and is the
    inefficiency technical or scale?* Shows Management Science publishes method when the managerial payoff
    is explicit (contrast a pure-algorithm paper that belongs in Operations Research).
  - **Self-check:** does your method come attached to a decision a manager would act on, or is the
    contribution the algorithm alone (then aim at Operations Research)?

### Empirical firm-level study (Information Systems)

- **Brynjolfsson, E. & Hitt, L., "Paradox Lost? Firm-Level Evidence on the Returns to Information Systems
  Spending," *Management Science* 1996, 42(4):541–558.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.42.4.541` (`pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.42.4.541`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** firm-level data are brought to bear on a broad, contested question (does IT
    spending actually pay off?), and the empirical answer *changes the decision* about investing in
    information systems — an empirical IS paper whose lesson reaches strategy and economics readers.
  - **Self-check:** does your empirics adjudicate a question managers and other Departments already argue
    about, with effect sizes (not just significance) that imply a decision?

### Behavioral / cognitive perspective on managerial risk (Behavioral Economics & Decision Analysis)

- **Kahneman, D. & Lovallo, D., "Timid Choices and Bold Forecasts: A Cognitive Perspective on Risk
  Taking," *Management Science* 1993, 39(1):17–31.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.39.1.17` (`pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.39.1.17`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a mechanism stated in plain words (narrow framing + the inside view produce
    simultaneously timid choices and over-optimistic forecasts) with a direct managerial implication for
    how firms should aggregate risky decisions — a behavioral contribution that travels to strategy,
    finance, and project management.
  - **Self-check:** is your behavioral claim a *named mechanism* with a decision implication, not a
    repackaged correlation?

### Conceptual / strategy under complexity (Business Strategy)

- **Schoemaker, P. J. H., "Strategy, Complexity, and Economic Rent," *Management Science* 1990,
  36(10):1178–1192.**
  Verified: DOI `10.1287/mnsc.36.10.1178` (`pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.36.10.1178`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** links decision complexity to the durability of competitive rents — a
    strategy contribution that a Management Science reader outside strategy (decision analysis, economics)
    can still use, illustrating the cross-department reach the journal prizes.
  - **Self-check:** does your strategy argument carry an insight a non-strategy reader still finds
    informative, rather than speaking only to one stream?

## Omitted for lack of *Management Science* confirmation (do not attribute to Management Science)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following well-known papers were **excluded** after web checking —
each is a real landmark but in a *different* venue. They are listed only as guardrails against the
sibling-confusion trap:

- **Black, F. & Scholes, M., "The Pricing of Options and Corporate Liabilities" (1973)** — *Journal of
  Political Economy* 81(3):637–654, **not Management Science**.
- **Cox, J., Ross, S. & Rubinstein, M., "Option Pricing: A Simplified Approach" (1979)** — *Journal of
  Financial Economics* 7(3):229–263, **not Management Science**.
- **Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D., "Advances in Prospect Theory: Cumulative Representation of Uncertainty"
  (1992)** — *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty* 5(4):297–323, **not Management Science**. (Kahneman &
  Lovallo 1993, above, *is* genuinely Management Science — do not conflate them.)
- **Cohen, M., March, J. & Olsen, J., "A Garbage Can Model of Organizational Choice" (1972)** —
  *Administrative Science Quarterly* 17(1):1–25, **not Management Science** (and ASQ ≠ INFORMS's
  *Organization Science*).
- **Davis, F. D., "Perceived Usefulness, Perceived Ease of Use, and User Acceptance of Information
  Technology" (1989)** — *MIS Quarterly* 13(3):319–340, **not Management Science**.
- **Lippman, S. & Rumelt, R., "Uncertain Imitability …" (1982)** — *Bell Journal of Economics* 13(2),
  **not Management Science**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `pubsonline.informs.org/journal/mnsc` (with a
`10.1287/mnsc.*` DOI and a volume/issue) — **not** an `opre.` (Operations Research), `msom.`, `mksc.`
(Marketing Science), `orsc.` (Organization Science), or `isre.` DOI — and update the access-date header.
When in doubt, omit.
