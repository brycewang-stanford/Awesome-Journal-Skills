# Worked Example: A *Language*-Style Introduction (before → after)

*Language* is read across every subfield, so the introduction must, by the time it ends, state the
**empirical generalization theory-neutrally**, give the **analysis and its key prediction**, ground it in
**checkable evidence** (numbered, Leipzig-glossed examples), and name the **rival account it
adjudicates** — all legible to a linguist outside the subfield (`lang-writing-style`: front-load the
claim; `lang-theory-building`: state predictions that adjudicate frameworks; `lang-topic-selection`:
cross-subfield stakes). A glossed pattern that is not raised to a general claim is the kind of
**descriptive data dump** the journal returns before review.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When Objects Trigger Agreement: Differential Object Agreement and
the Locality of Case in Tsérian."* Fictional setting: in Tsérian, the verb agrees with some direct
objects but not others.

---

## Before (subfield-insider, buries the claim — typical first-draft intro)

> Object agreement has been widely studied in many languages. In this paper I present new data from
> Tsérian, a language that has not been described in the agreement literature. Tsérian has an
> interesting agreement system in which the verb sometimes agrees with the object. I show that object
> agreement occurs with some objects and not others. I analyze this within a Minimalist framework using
> Agree. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 gives background on Tsérian, Section 3 presents
> the data, Section 4 gives the analysis, and Section 5 concludes.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Descriptive framing.** "Tsérian has an interesting system" tells a semanticist or a typologist
  nothing about why to read on (`lang-topic-selection`: cross-subfield significance).
- **No generalization, no prediction.** "Sometimes agrees with the object" is the pattern un-analyzed;
  no theory-neutral statement of *what conditions* agreement (`lang-theory-building`).
- **"Has not been described" as the contribution** — the `lang-topic-selection` anti-pattern, and a
  desk-return risk.
- **Framework-internal.** It assumes Minimalism and never engages the rival (information-structure)
  account a functionalist reviewer will raise (`lang-literature-positioning`: parochialism).
- **No data in the intro.** A *Language* reader expects a glossed example to see the pattern
  (`lang-tables-figures`).
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's job (`lang-writing-style`).

---

## After (*Language* arc — generalization + analysis + prediction + glossed evidence + stakes)

> **When does a verb agree with its object?** Across languages, object agreement is often *differential*:
> it targets some objects and skips others. Tsérian offers an unusually clean case. The verb agrees with
> a **specific** object (1a) but not a **non-specific** one (1b), even when word order, case, and
> animacy are held constant:
>
> ```
> (1)  a.  Maré  ré-tʃi-lá        ké-woni.
>          Mare  3SG.A-3SG.O-see  DEF-child
>          'Mare saw the (specific) child.'
>      b.  Maré  ré-lá            woni.
>          Mare  3SG.A-see        child
>          'Mare saw a (non-specific) child.'
> ```
>
> *(The generalization is stated theory-neutrally and shown, not asserted: agreement tracks specificity
> with everything else controlled — legible to any linguist before any formalism.)*
>
> This matters beyond Tsérian because two general theories predict differently here. On a **case-driven**
> account, object agreement reflects a structural Case relation and should track the object's syntactic
> position; on an **information-structure** account, it reflects the object's discourse status and should
> track specificity/topicality independent of position. Tsérian **adjudicates**: because specific and
> non-specific objects occupy the *same* position (2), only the information-structure account predicts
> the split we see — but, we argue, it is a *grammaticalized* specificity feature checked in the syntax,
> not a live discourse effect. *(The stakes are cross-subfield — syntax and its interface with semantics
> and information structure — and the rival frameworks and the deciding prediction are named up front,
> per `lang-theory-building` and `lang-literature-positioning`.)*
>
> Our evidence is an elicited paradigm from eight Tsérian speakers plus a 40,000-word annotated text
> corpus; every relevant token is glossed and sourced, and the corpus lets us check that the elicited
> split holds in spontaneous use. *(Design and evidence in one paragraph, with the checkability
> `lang-research-design` and `lang-data-and-transparency` require.)*
>
> We show that object agreement in Tsérian is controlled by a syntactic **[+specific]** feature: it
> appears if and only if the object bears that feature, it is blocked under the same conditions that
> block agreement elsewhere in the language (a locality effect), and — the key prediction — it is
> **insensitive to discourse topicality** once specificity is fixed. A live information-structure account
> predicts the opposite; the data favor grammaticalized specificity. *(Headline finding, restated with
> the observable implication that separates the two theories.)*
>
> The contribution is general: Tsérian shows that **differential object agreement can be driven by a
> grammaticalized semantic feature rather than by case or by live discourse status**, and it supplies a
> diagnostic — the topicality-insensitivity test — that a linguist working on any differential-agreement
> or differential-object-marking system can apply. *(Portable claim + a diagnostic that travels — the
> *Language* payoff.)*
>
> We develop the specificity analysis and its predictions, then present the elicited paradigm and the
> corpus test that separates specificity from topicality, and close with the diagnostic's application to
> two other languages. *(Brief roadmap that carries the argument, not a table of contents.)*

## Why the "after" meets the *Language* bar

- **Generalization stated theory-neutrally and shown with a glossed example** — a syntactician,
  semanticist, or typologist sees the pattern before any formalism (`lang-writing-style`;
  `lang-tables-figures`: numbered, Leipzig-glossed, aligned, sourced).
- **Analysis with an adjudicating prediction** — the topicality-insensitivity test decides between
  case-driven and information-structure accounts (`lang-theory-building`: rung 3).
- **Framework-fluent, not parochial** — it engages the rival account fairly rather than assuming one
  formalism (`lang-literature-positioning`).
- **Checkable evidence** — elicited paradigm + annotated corpus, glossed and sourced
  (`lang-research-design`, `lang-data-and-transparency`).
- **Cross-subfield stakes and a portable diagnostic** — the claim and the test travel beyond Tsérian
  (`lang-topic-selection`).
- **No "never-described-X" contribution and no over-signposted roadmap** — the named anti-patterns are
  avoided.

> One-sentence hook (fillable): "We show that **object agreement in Tsérian tracks a grammaticalized
> [+specific] feature, not case or discourse topicality**, using **an elicited paradigm and a corpus
> topicality test** to separate the two theories — and we give a diagnostic other differential-agreement
> systems can use."
