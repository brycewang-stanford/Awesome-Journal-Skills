# Language Exemplars Library (subfield × contribution type)

> **Coarse-citation policy (zero hallucination).** Each work below is a well-known article the compiler
> is confident appeared in **Language, the journal of the Linguistic Society of America** — cited by
> **author, year, and volume** only. Exact page ranges and issue numbers are **deliberately omitted or
> approximate**; confirm them on JSTOR, Project MUSE (pre-2026), or Cambridge Core (2026+) before citing
> any specifics. This file gives **design/positioning guidance only** — it reproduces no findings and no
> numbers. Read the original before citing anything precise.
>
> **Sibling-journal guard.** *Language* is **not** *Word*, *Natural Language & Linguistic Theory*
> (*NLLT*), *Linguistic Inquiry*, *Journal of Linguistics*, *Phonology*, *Journal of Semantics*, or
> *Language Variation and Change*, and several works people *assume* are "in *Language*" are actually in
> a sibling venue or a book. The [Omitted](#omitted-verify-before-attributing-to-language) section
> records the traps.

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **subfield × contribution** is closest to yours, then study how that paper raises a
pattern into a **theoretically grounded, cross-subfield claim** — the *Language* bar (see
[`../../skills/lang-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/lang-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and

## By subfield

### Foundational / theory of the discipline
- **Bloomfield, "A Set of Postulates for the Science of Language," Language, 1926 (vol. 2).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns methodological groundwork into a general framework the field could
    build on — a claim about how linguistics itself should be done, legible to every subfield.
  - **Self-check:** does your paper state a generalization others can adopt, not just a local result?

- **Sapir, "Sound Patterns in Language," Language, 1925 (vol. 1).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** argues that sound systems are *patterned* and psychologically real, not
    mere phonetics — an early statement of the phoneme's theoretical status. Analysis over description.
  - **Self-check:** does your phonetic observation deliver a claim about representation?

### Phonology / morphology
- **Zwicky & Pullum, "Cliticization vs. Inflection: English n't," Language, 1983 (vol. 59).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses a small English fact to draw a **general diagnostic distinction**
    (clitic vs. affix) that travels far beyond English — the model of a Research-Report-scale point with
    outsized theoretical reach.
  - **Self-check:** does your morphological case yield a portable diagnostic, not a language-specific list?

- **Bybee & Slobin, "Rules and Schemas in the Development and Use of the English Past Tense," Language, 1982 (vol. 58).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** ties usage and acquisition evidence to a claim about **rules vs. schemas**,
    adjudicating between rival models of morphological knowledge — evidence marshaled to decide a theory.
  - **Self-check:** does your data adjudicate between competing accounts, or just illustrate one?

### Syntax
- **Fillmore, Kay & O'Connor, "Regularity and Idiomaticity in Grammatical Constructions: The Case of *let alone*," Language, 1988 (vol. 64).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a founding **Construction Grammar** statement — one construction argues
    that the lexicon–syntax boundary must be rethought, a claim that reshaped a general question.
  - **Self-check:** does your construction bear on grammatical architecture, not just its own idiosyncrasy?

- **Bresnan & Mchombo, "Topic, Pronoun, and Agreement in Chichewa," Language, 1987 (vol. 63).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses richly glossed Bantu data to argue a general point about
    **agreement vs. pronominal incorporation**, framed so non-Bantuist syntacticians can use it.
  - **Self-check:** are your glossed data raised to a claim a typologist or theorist outside the language can carry?

### Semantics / typology
- **Kay & McDaniel, "The Linguistic Significance of the Meanings of Basic Color Terms," Language, 1978 (vol. 54).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** links cross-linguistic semantic data to a general model of category
    structure — semantics with typological reach and a cognitive claim.
  - **Self-check:** does your meaning analysis connect to a general question about composition or categories?

- **Dryer, "The Greenbergian Word Order Correlations," Language, 1992 (vol. 68).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** rebuilds word-order typology on a large, **areally controlled** sample and
    revises the received generalizations — a methodological and substantive contribution at once.
  - **Self-check:** does your typological claim guard against genealogical/areal non-independence?

### Sociolinguistics / variation
- **Labov, "Contraction, Deletion, and Inherent Variability of the English Copula," Language, 1969 (vol. 45).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** shows that variation is **structured**, not free, and builds a formal model
    of it — moving variationist data into the theory of grammar.
  - **Self-check:** does your variable connect to a mechanism/model, not just a correlation?

### Psycholinguistics / foundations
- **Chomsky, "Review of B. F. Skinner's *Verbal Behavior*," Language, 1959 (vol. 35).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a review that became a field-defining argument about the inadequacy of
    behaviorist accounts of language — the paradigm of a claim reshaping the whole discipline.
  - **Self-check:** does your critique teach the field a general lesson, not just fault one target?

### Discourse / structural method
- **Harris, "Discourse Analysis," Language, 1952 (vol. 28).**
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** extends distributional method beyond the sentence, opening a general
    program — method introduced as a portable tool, not applied for its own sake.
  - **Self-check:** does your method generalize, or does it only fit your one dataset?

## Omitted (verify before attributing to Language)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these commonly-misattributed works are **excluded** — each is in a
sibling venue or a book, **not** the journal *Language*:

- **Greenberg, "Some Universals of Grammar…" (1963)** — in the volume *Universals of Language* (MIT
  Press), **not** the journal *Language*.
- **Hockett, "Two Models of Grammatical Description" (1954)** — in *Word*, **not** *Language*.
- **Labov, "The Social Motivation of a Sound Change" (1963)** — in *Word*, **not** *Language*.
- **Weinreich, Labov & Herzog, "Empirical Foundations for a Theory of Language Change" (1968)** — in the
  volume *Directions for Historical Linguistics*, **not** the journal.
- **Prince & Smolensky, *Optimality Theory* (1993)** — a technical report / book, **not** a *Language*
  article.

Before adding any work to this library, confirm it in *Language* (JSTOR / Project MUSE for pre-2026,
Cambridge Core for 2026+), fix the coarse citation, and watch the *Word* / *NLLT* / *Linguistic Inquiry*
/ book-chapter traps. **When in doubt, omit.**
