# Topic Selection & Fit (lang-topic-selection)

*Language* is the LSA's **generalist flagship**, covering phonology, syntax, semantics, morphology,
historical and typological work, sociolinguistics, psycholinguistics, and computational linguistics.
The bar is not "new data from language X" — it is **"a theoretically grounded claim, carried by
rigorous evidence, that a linguist outside my subfield should care about."** A description with no
analytic payoff, or a demonstration that runs only inside one formalism, is off-fit and often
desk-returned. Use this skill to pressure-test fit before you invest.

## When to trigger

- Choosing among projects or framings for a *Language* submission
- A colleague said the paper is "descriptive," "narrow," or "only for [framework] people"
- Deciding between a **General Research Article** and a shorter **Research Report**
- Weighing *Language* against a subfield venue (e.g., *Phonology*, *NLLT*, *Journal of Semantics*,
  *Language Variation and Change*)

## The Language fit test

A strong *Language* paper usually clears all four:

1. **Theoretical stakes.** It changes how linguists *analyze* something — an interface, a typological
   generalization, a processing mechanism — not just what one language happens to do.
2. **Rigorous, transparent evidence.** Glossed primary data, a corpus, an experiment, or historical
   sources documented well enough that a reader could check the pattern (see `lang-data-and-transparency`).
3. **Cross-subfield significance.** A phonologist and a syntactician can both see why it matters —
   because it bears on a shared question about grammar, meaning, variation, or change.
4. **Framework-fluent, not parochial.** The claim engages rival analyses; it does not assume one
   formalism and ignore the alternatives (see `lang-theory-building`).

## Language vs. a subfield venue (frame to the right journal)

| | *Language* (LSA) | Subfield venue |
|--|------------------|----------------|
| Audience | **the whole discipline** | specialists in one area |
| Premium on | **general theoretical import + broad legibility** | depth within the subfield's assumptions |
| Framework stance | **engage across frameworks** | often assumes the house framework |
| If your paper is | a claim that reshapes a general question | a fine-grained result for insiders |

> Sending framework-internal work to *Language* invites a "why not [subfield journal]?" reviewer note.
> Send it here when the payoff travels beyond the framework and the subfield.

## Framing by subfield (write past your home area)

| Home subfield | Reach the discipline by… |
|---------------|--------------------------|
| Phonology / phonetics | state the general claim about representations or the phonetics–phonology interface, not just the alternation |
| Syntax / morphology | show what the pattern reveals about architecture (e.g., locality, the lexicon–syntax boundary), not one derivation |
| Semantics / pragmatics | tie the analysis to a general question about meaning composition or inference |
| Sociolinguistics / variation | connect the variable to a mechanism of change or a general model of variation |
| Historical / typological | extract the portable generalization, not a single-language chronology |
| Psycho- / computational | link the model or experiment to a claim about grammar or processing the field can carry |

## Fit-screen decision table (what to do with each verdict)

| Signal from the four-part test | Action |
|--------------------------------|--------|
| All four clear; claim travels | proceed to `lang-theory-building` |
| Rigorous data but thin stakes | climb from description to analysis before submitting |
| Framework-internal result | broaden the engagement, or send to the subfield venue |
| Pure description of one language | reframe around a general claim, or it is off-fit |

## Calibration (Language appetite, hedged)

Orienting contrasts, not rankings; confirm scope on the current author pages. *Language* is unusual in
asking a phonetician's paper to be readable by a semanticist: breadth of readership is the constraint
that shapes framing. It rewards **new empirical territory raised to a general claim** — a
well-documented pattern from an under-described language is welcome *when* it is a case of something.
Illustrative: fieldwork on an evidential system framed as "language Y has four evidentials" reads as
descriptive; reframed as "the evidential–tense interaction adjudicates between two theories of how
evidentiality composes," it speaks to semantics broadly and flips the verdict to strong fit.

## Anti-patterns

- A description of one language with no theoretical claim (a classic desk return)
- A result that holds only inside one framework, with rivals ignored
- "First study of X in language Y" as the whole contribution
- Defaulting to *Language* for prestige when a subfield venue fits the contribution better
- A quantitative pattern with no model and no shared data behind it

## Output format

```
【Question】one sentence
【Theoretical claim】the general point a linguist outside my subfield can use
【Evidence base】glossed data / corpus / experiment / historical sources
【Cross-subfield significance】who beyond my area cares, and why
【Framework stance】which rivals it engages
【Type】General Research Article / Research Report / other section
【Fit verdict】strong / needs reframing / off-fit / better at a subfield venue
【Next】lang-theory-building
```
