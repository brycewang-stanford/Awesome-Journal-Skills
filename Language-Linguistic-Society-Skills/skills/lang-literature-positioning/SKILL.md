# Literature Positioning (lang-literature-positioning)

At *Language* the literature section proves the paper is **in dialogue with current linguistics** —
across frameworks, not only inside your own. Because the journal is generalist and its editors recruit
**double-anonymous** reviewers from across subfields, your positioning is the signal they read to place
the paper and to match referees. A section that engages only your framework's in-group tells the editor
this belongs at a subfield venue. This skill maps the conversation and locates your contribution in it.

## When to trigger

- Drafting or restructuring the literature/positioning section
- A reader said the paper "ignores the obvious competing analysis" or "reinvents a known result"
- Writing a **Discussion Note** or **Perspectives Commentary** that must engage a specific published
  claim precisely and fairly
- Deciding which debates to foreground for a general linguistics readership

## How to position for Language

1. **Name the question, not the construction.** State the live theoretical debate the paper joins
   (e.g., "is this alternation phonological or morphological?") in terms a linguist outside your area
   recognizes — not just your subfield's internal dispute.
2. **Locate the gap as a tension, not a hole.** *Language* rewards papers that adjudicate or reframe a
   real disagreement, not ones that fill an empty descriptive cell ("no one has looked at X").
3. **Engage the strongest rival account, fairly.** Cite the best version of the analysis you challenge;
   cross-framework reviewers will know it and will punish a straw man.
4. **Set up the payoff.** Positioning should hand off cleanly to `lang-theory-building`: the tension
   you name is the prediction you will adjudicate.

## Cross-framework positioning (Language-distinctive)

*Language* prizes engagement **across** traditions. If your account is generative, still engage the
functionalist or usage-based reading of the same data (and vice versa) where it bears on the claim. The
goal is not to referee a framework war but to show your analysis wins on the **data and predictions**,
whatever lens the reader brings.

## Discussion Note / Commentary positioning

If you are writing a **Discussion Note** on a published *Language* article or a **Perspectives
Commentary**:

- Represent the **target claim faithfully** — quote or paraphrase it exactly before critiquing.
- Make the critique **substantive and general**: it should teach the field something, not score a
  point on notation.
- Anticipate the **author's response/Rejoinder** — *Language* publishes both in the Perspectives
  format; a fair critique survives the reply.

## Positioning move table (gap-as-tension, Language-style)

| Referee signal | The Language-specific fix |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| "Reinvents a known result." | locate the live debate and the strongest prior analysis |
| "Straw-mans the rival framework." | engage the best version of the account you challenge |
| "Subfield-only literature." | reframe the tension for the whole discipline |
| "Missing the foundational reference." | situate against and extend the standard account |

Because editors infer area and method from the blinded manuscript to recruit cross-subfield referees, a
narrow literature map invites a poor reviewer match. Illustrative: a paper framed as "OT has not
modeled this tone sandhi" reads as gap-filling; reframed as a tension between representational and
computational accounts of the sandhi that the author's data adjudicate, it engages a debate the whole
phonology community — and interested syntacticians — recognize.

## Anti-patterns

- A subfield-only literature wall a cross-framework reviewer cannot follow
- Straw-manning the rival account (double-anonymous reviewers from that tradition will notice)
- "No one has studied X" instead of a genuine theoretical tension
- Citing only your own framework's in-group for coverage
- A Discussion Note that misreads or over-reads the target paper's actual claim

## Positioning pass for Language

Treat this skill as an executable review pass, not a prose hint. First lock the empirical
generalization, the evidence base, the analysis's predictions, and the theoretical payoff; then judge
whether the manuscript answers the venue's real reader: linguists across subfields who value grounded
analysis, transparent evidence, and fair engagement across frameworks.

- **Do the pass:** build a three-column map — incumbent debate, unresolved tension, this manuscript's
  delta; include one rival framework whose omission would make a referee doubt the fit.
- **Return a ledger:** give `claim / evidence / risk / manuscript location` rows so the next agent can
  edit rather than rediscover the issue.
- **Sibling guard:** compare against *NLLT* and *Syntax* (syntax), *Phonology* (phonology), *Journal of
  Semantics* / *Natural Language Semantics* (semantics), *Diachronica* (historical), *Language
  Variation and Change* (variation); if a sibling owns the contribution, recommend re-routing before
  polishing.
- **Stop condition:** do not give submission-ready advice until `resources/official-source-map.md` has
  been checked for volatile rules and the manuscript has one concrete fix for the largest venue risk.

## Output format

```
【Question】the live linguistic debate the paper joins
【Key prior work】the strongest analyses engaged (across frameworks, fairly stated)
【Tension】the unresolved problem (not an empty descriptive cell)
【Our move】how the contribution adjudicates/reframes it → theory-building
【If Note/Commentary】the target claim + the general lesson the critique teaches
【Next】lang-research-design
```
