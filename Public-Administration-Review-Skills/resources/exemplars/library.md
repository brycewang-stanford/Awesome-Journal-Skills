## How to use this library

PAR is public administration's **practice-bridging flagship**: each paper below earns its place by
making a result legible to the *whole field* and to reflective practitioners (see
[`../../skills/pubar-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pubar-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/pubar-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pubar-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the
row whose **subfield × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) states field-wide
significance, (2) defends its design on its own terms (see
[`../../skills/pubar-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pubar-research-design/SKILL.md)), and (3)
carries a credible practitioner implication. Run the self-check before claiming your paper is
"PAR-shaped."

## By method

### Foundational concept + measurement (public service motivation)

- **Perry & Wise, "The Motivational Bases of Public Service," PAR 1990, 50(3):367–373.**
  Verified: JSTOR + PAR/Wiley indexing; ERIC EJ406473.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** introduces and formalizes **public service motivation (PSM)** — one
    portable construct that travels across HR, leadership, and behavior research and directly informs
    how managers recruit and motivate public servants (see `pubar-theory-building`: portability + the
    managerial lever).
  - **Self-check:** does your concept give the field a *named, measurable* construct another subfield
    can use, with a clear implication for practice?

### Survey / perception comparison (red tape, public management)

- **Bozeman & Feeney, "Stakeholder Red Tape: Comparing Perceptions of Public Managers and Their Private Consultants," PAR 2009, 69(4):710–726.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library article page.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** sharpens the **red tape** construct by comparing how different
    stakeholders perceive the same rules — a measurement-and-design advance with stakes for how the
    field studies burden and how managers diagnose it.
  - **Self-check:** does your study clarify a contested construct rather than re-measure it the same way?

### Survey experiment (representative bureaucracy, coproduction)

- **Riccucci, Van Ryzin & Li, "Representative Bureaucracy and the Willingness to Coproduce: An Experimental Study," PAR 2016, 76(1):121–130.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library (DOI 10.1111/puar.12401) + JSTOR.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** brings a clean **survey experiment** to a macro claim (representative
    bureaucracy) by manipulating the apparent gender composition of officials and measuring citizens'
    willingness to coproduce — micro-foundations a practitioner can act on (who staffs a program shapes
    citizen cooperation). Pairs with the experiment guidance in
    [`../../skills/pubar-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/pubar-research-design/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** if your claim is about institutions, can you isolate the individual-level mechanism
    experimentally and state the managerial implication?

### Field / behavioral experiment (nudges, public employees)

- **Bellé & Cantarelli, "Nudging Public Employees Through Descriptive Social Norms in Healthcare Organizations," PAR 2021 (DOI 10.1111/puar.13353).**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library article page.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** applies a **behavioral/experimental** design to a concrete managerial
    lever (descriptive social-norm messaging) inside public organizations — exactly the
    research-meets-practice profile PAR rewards, with a directly actionable Evidence-for-Practice takeaway.
  - **Self-check:** does your experiment test an intervention a manager could actually deploy?

### Meta-analysis / synthesis (red tape, performance, employee outcomes)

- **George, Pandey, Steijn, Decramer & Audenaert, "Red Tape, Organizational Performance, and Employee Outcomes: Meta-analysis, Meta-regression, and Research Agenda," PAR 2021, 81(4):638–651.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library (DOI 10.1111/puar.13327).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** consolidates decades of red-tape findings into a **meta-analytic**
    synthesis with a research agenda — a field-level contribution that also tells managers what the
    accumulated evidence does and does not support.
  - **Self-check:** does your synthesis resolve a contested regularity for the whole field, not just
    catalog studies?

### Conceptual / theory development (red tape, behavioral process)

- **Davis, Stazyk, Kline & Green, "'Feeling out' the rules: A psychological process theory of red tape," PAR 2024 (DOI 10.1111/puar.13750).**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library article page.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** advances **theory** (a psychological-process account of how rules become
    experienced as red tape) — the kind of contribution PAR's *Conceptualizing Public Administration*
    type rewards, reframing what empirical scholars must measure (see `pubar-theory-building`:
    conceptual mode).
  - **Self-check:** does your conceptual move change what empirical scholars in the field can see or
    must now account for, not just rename a debate?

## Omitted for lack of PAR verification (do NOT attribute to PAR without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** after web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not PAR. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Moynihan & Pandey, "The Big Question for Performance Management: Why Do Managers Use Performance
  Information?" (2010)** — verified to be in **JPART** 20(4):849–866, **not PAR**. A very common
  cross-journal misattribution within public administration.
- **Ansell & Gash, "Collaborative Governance in Theory and Practice" (2008)** — verified to be in
  **JPART** 18(4):543–571, **not PAR**.
- **Herd & Moynihan, "Administrative Burden: Learning, Psychological, and Compliance Costs in
  Citizen-State Interactions" (2015)** — verified to be in **JPART** 25(1):43–69, **not PAR**. (Their
  2018 *Administrative Burden* is a Russell Sage **book**, not a PAR article.)
- **Meier, Wrinkle & Polinard, "Bureaucracy and Organizational Performance: Causality Arguments About
  Public Schools" (2000)** — verified to be in the *American Journal of Political Science* 44(3):590–602,
  **not PAR**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on Wiley Online Library
(`onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/15406210`) with volume/issue/pages or DOI, cross-check an indexing
record, and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
