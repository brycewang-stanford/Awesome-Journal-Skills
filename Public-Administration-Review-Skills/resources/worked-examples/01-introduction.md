# Worked Example: A PAR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **PAR introduction bar** from `pubar-writing-style` and `pubar-topic-selection`:
**by the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the argument, the evidence, why it
matters to public administration *broadly*, and what a public manager should do about it.** The funnel
is **question → field-wide significance → why it is hard to identify → setting & design → headline
finding with the mechanism → contribution (portable argument) → Evidence for Practice → brief
roadmap**, with PAR's distinctive demand that the contribution **bridge research and practice** and the
design be *defended on its own terms*.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Cutting the Form, Not the Mission: How a Procurement-Simplification
Mandate Reshapes Frontline Discretion."* Fictional setting: a set of state agencies that adopted a
red-tape-reduction mandate in **different years**, creating staggered within-state variation. (Subfield:
public management / administrative burden; method: staggered difference-in-differences. Chosen because
red tape and frontline discretion are field-wide stakes — see the exemplars in
[`../exemplars/library.md`](../exemplars/library.md).)

---

## Before (niche-insider, no practitioner stake — typical first draft)

> The literature on red tape is extensive. Scholars have long debated its effects, and many studies
> examine its correlates across agencies. In this paper, we use a staggered difference-in-differences
> design with the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator to study the effects of a
> procurement-simplification mandate on processing in state agencies where the mandate was adopted at
> different times. We assemble an agency-level panel and estimate event-study specifications. Red tape
> is an important topic in public administration. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section 3 describes
> the data, Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, Section 5 reports results, Section 6 discusses
> robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `pubar-writing-style` / `pubar-topic-selection`):**

- **Niche-insider framing.** It speaks only to red-tape specialists; a budgeting, HR, or governance
  scholar is given no reason to care, and **no practitioner stake** is stated — PAR's whole bar is
  field-wide significance *plus* practice relevance.
- **Leads with method** ("we use a staggered DID design with the Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator") instead
  of the question and argument — the `pubar-writing-style` anti-pattern of burying the contribution.
- **No argument and no finding on page one.** The reader does not learn what was found, the mechanism,
  or the managerial implication. "Red tape is important" is throat-clearing.
- **No Evidence for Practice.** Nothing a public manager could act on — the PAR signature is missing.
- **Over-signposted seven-section roadmap** padding the word count PAR caps at ~8,000 (which **counts
  references and endnotes**).

---

## After (PAR arc — field significance + finding + practitioner so-what, stated early)

> **Does cutting procurement red tape free frontline staff to use professional judgment — or does it
> simply move the paperwork somewhere else?** We show that when state agencies adopt a
> procurement-simplification mandate, frontline processing time falls by roughly **22%** *and* the
> share of decisions resolved by frontline discretion rather than escalated upward rises sharply —
> without any measurable increase in improper-payment rates. *(question + field-wide significance +
> headline finding in the first breath — this is a claim about discretion and administrative burden,
> not just a processing number.)*
>
> Why this matters beyond procurement: a central question across public administration — from
> administrative-burden theory to street-level bureaucracy to performance management — is whether rules
> meant to ensure accountability instead displace the professional judgment that makes public services
> work. If a feasible administrative reform restores discretion without sacrificing control, it speaks
> directly to whether burden is a fixed cost of accountability or a **design choice** managers can
> change. *(the field-level stake, framed for an HR, budgeting, or governance scholar alike —
> `pubar-topic-selection` significance test.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because agencies do not adopt the mandate at random: an agency may
> simplify procurement precisely when a reform-minded leadership is already loosening internal controls,
> so a simple before-after comparison would confound the mandate with the broader managerial shift that
> prompted it. *(why it is hard — the identification problem stated plainly, per `pubar-research-design`.)*
>
> We exploit a set of state agencies that adopted the mandate in **different years** for legislative-
> calendar reasons unrelated to local processing trends. The staggered timing lets us compare each
> agency to not-yet-adopting agencies using a heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences estimator,
> and we verify no differential pre-trends in processing or discretion before adoption. *(setting &
> design in one paragraph; the modern staggered estimator is named only as a tool, not as the lead.)*
>
> Processing time falls by **22%** and frontline-resolved decisions rise by **9 percentage points**
> after adoption — never before — while improper-payment rates are flat. The mechanism is **not** simply
> fewer steps: the newly simplified rule reallocates *authority* to the frontline, and it is that
> reallocation, not the step count, that moves outcomes. *(headline finding restated with the mechanism
> and a ruled-out rival — the `pubar-research-design` adjudication move.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that **administrative burden is not a fixed price of accountability but a
> design parameter that managers set**: the same control objective can be met with a rule that leaves
> discretion at the frontline. This reframes red-tape reduction — often treated as mere efficiency
> trimming — as a **discretion-allocation** decision with consequences for how public services are
> delivered. *(contribution stated early and as a *portable* argument: a budgeting scholar studying
> controls, an HR scholar studying autonomy, and an implementation scholar can all use it —
> `pubar-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> **Evidence for Practice.** *(the PAR signature, stated honestly and tied to the confirmatory estimates:)*
> - Simplifying procurement rules can cut frontline processing time substantially without raising
>   improper-payment rates — control and speed are not strictly in tension.
> - The gains come from **reallocating decision authority to the frontline**, not from cutting steps
>   alone; managers should target *who decides*, not just *how many forms*.
> - Agencies considering similar reforms can expect benefits to appear only after adoption and to grow
>   over time, so early post-reform evaluations may understate the effect.
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the discretion-allocation argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 presents the design and the main results; Section 4 examines the mechanism and
> scope conditions. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the PAR bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Field-wide significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names the stake
  (administrative burden, street-level discretion, accountability-vs-judgment) so an HR, budgeting, or
  governance scholar sees why it matters (`pubar-topic-selection`).
- **Practitioner so-what is built in** — the **Evidence for Practice** block gives 3 honest, specific
  takeaways tied to confirmatory estimates, not boilerplate (`pubar-writing-style`,
  `pubar-theory-building` dual test). This is the PAR signature that distinguishes it from JPART.
- **Argument-first, contribution early** — the claim ("burden is a design parameter, not a fixed price")
  is stated plainly and early (`pubar-writing-style`: "front-load the contribution").
- **Portable argument, not a bare finding** — framed so another PA subfield can import it, passing the
  `pubar-theory-building` portability test; attached to a mechanism with scope conditions.
- **Identification problem named before the design**, and the **single strongest rival is adjudicated**
  ("the mechanism is *not* simply fewer steps… instead…") — the `pubar-research-design` adjudication
  sentence, with no-differential-pre-trends stated plainly.
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the staggered, heterogeneity-robust estimator appears only where the
  design is discussed (avoids the `pubar-writing-style` "leading with the estimator" anti-pattern; the
  estimator choice follows the modern-staggered-DiD guidance referenced in [`../code/`](../code/)).
- **Reads past the niche and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and defined, the roadmap is three
  sections not seven, and the framing fits PAR's ≤ 8,000-word limit (which counts references/endnotes).
