## By tradition

### Identity theory — the self anchored in social structure (programmatic address)

- **Burke, Peter J. "Identities and Social Structure: The 2003 Cooley-Mead Award Address," *Social
  Psychology Quarterly* 2004, 67(1):5–15.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/019027250406700103` (DOI 10.1177/019027250406700103).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the canonical statement that identity processes (verification, the
    self-as-meaning) are *embedded in* social structure rather than free-floating — the cleanest articulation
    of the structure–individual link that defines SPQ.
  - **Self-check:** is your self/identity outcome *anchored in* social structure or position, or is it a
    free-floating individual trait (which would drift toward JPSP)?

### Social structure & personality + identity — proximate structure shapes identity processes (survey)

- **Merolla, David M., Richard T. Serpe, Sheldon Stryker & P. Wesley Schultz. "Structural Precursors to
  Identity Processes: The Role of Proximate Social Structures," *Social Psychology Quarterly* 2012,
  75(2):149–172.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0190272511436352` (DOI 10.1177/0190272511436352); issue
  confirmed via the SPQ Volume 75, Number 2 table of contents.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** survey-based test showing that *proximate social structures* feed identity
    salience and commitment — structural variables are theorized causes, not nuisance controls, exactly as
    `spq-research-design` requires for the SSP tradition.
  - **Self-check:** are your structural variables measured and theorized as causes of the identity process,
    not just adjustment controls?

### Group processes / status — expectation states made observable (experiment + measurement)

- **Kalkhoff, Will, David Melamed, Josh Pollock, Brennan Miller, Jon Overton & Matthew Pfeiffer. "Cracking
  the Black Box: Capturing the Role of Expectation States in Status Processes," *Social Psychology
  Quarterly* 2020, 83(1):26–48.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0190272519868988` (DOI 10.1177/0190272519868988).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** opens the "black box" of expectation states — the unobserved mechanism in
    status-characteristics theory — with measurement, turning a theorized cognitive-social process into
    something captured rather than assumed. Pairs with the experimental section of `spq-research-design`.
  - **Self-check:** does your design *capture* the social-psychological mechanism, not just its downstream
    behavioral trace?

### Group processes / status — how hierarchies secure deference (theory + experiment)

- **Ridgeway, Cecilia L. & Sandra Nakagawa. "Is Deference the Price of Being Seen as Reasonable? How Status
  Hierarchies Incentivize Acceptance of Low Status," *Social Psychology Quarterly* 2017, 80(2):132–152.**
  Verified on SAGE (DOI 10.1177/0190272517695213): *Social Psychology Quarterly* 80(2):132–152, 2017.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a named mechanism — implicit status rules trade "the dignity of being seen as
    reasonable" for deference — links a macro feature (the status order) to an individual disposition
    (acceptance of low status). Textbook structure–individual reasoning.
  - **Self-check:** can you name the social-psychological mechanism that converts a structural position into
    an individual response, in one sentence?

### Affect / group processes — formalizing interaction dynamics (formal model)

- **Heise, David R. "Modeling Interactions in Small Groups," *Social Psychology Quarterly* 2013,
  76(1):52–72.**
  Verified: `journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0190272512467654` (DOI 10.1177/0190272512467654).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a formal program (affect control lineage) that derives testable predictions
    about who acts on whom in groups from cultural sentiments — the SPQ-style formal paper that states the
    substantive puzzle before the math, per `spq-theory-building`.
  - **Self-check:** does your formal model deliver a non-obvious prediction a reader can test, with
    assumptions kept transparent and motivated?

### Affect control — cognition of events as a social-psychological process (measurement / experiment)

- **Rogers, Kimberly B. "Event Likelihood Judgments Revisited," *Social Psychology Quarterly* 2021, 84(2):177–188.**
  Verified on SAGE (DOI 10.1177/0190272521997065): *Social Psychology Quarterly* 84(2):177–188, 2021.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** treats how people judge the likelihood of social events as governed by
    culturally shared affective meanings — an individual-cognition outcome kept *social* by grounding it in
    shared sentiments, which is what keeps it SPQ rather than psychology.
  - **Self-check:** is your cognitive outcome tied to *socially shared* meanings/structure, or is it a purely
    intra-individual effect (which would fit Psychological Science)?

## Omitted for lack of full SPQ verification (do not attribute to SPQ without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking:

- **Stress-process / mastery work (Pearlin, Schieman, and colleagues)** — the strong candidates surfaced by
  search (e.g., mastery as mediator; "Control in the Face of Uncertainty") verified to the *Journal of
  Health and Social Behavior* (JHSB) and *Journal of Aging and Health*, **not SPQ**. SSP mental-health work
  is split across JHSB and SPQ; confirm the venue on SAGE before citing as SPQ. Listed here only as a
  guardrail.
- **Affect control theory "double special issue in honor of David R. Heise" framing articles** — some
  related pieces returned by search sit in *American Behavioral Scientist* (DOI prefix `10.1177/0002764...`),
  **not SPQ**; only confirm the specific SPQ articles, not the cross-journal cluster.
Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `journals.sagepub.com` (the SPQ article page
with DOI and year) — and explicitly rule out the sibling journals listed in the header (JPSP, Psychological
Science, ASR, Social Forces, *Social Psychology*) — then update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
