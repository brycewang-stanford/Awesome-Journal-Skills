# Worked Example: An AMJ-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **AMJ introduction arc** that recurs across this pack's front-end skills:
**phenomenon hook → the conversation and its tension → research question → theoretical approach &
study → intended contribution** — on the first 1–2 pages (`amj-writing-style` "Structure to enforce";
`amj-literature-positioning` "Front-end structure"). AMJ rewards a paper that *tells a story*: a
compelling hook anchored in a real managerial phenomenon, a clear *theoretical* contribution, and a
motivating move that is a **tension, boundary, or problematization — not gap-spotting**.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When Helping the Newcomer Hurts the Helper: Status Threat and the
Withdrawal of Peer Onboarding Support."* Fictional setting: a quantitative field study of experienced
employees assigned as peer mentors to new hires across the branches of one services firm.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Onboarding has been studied extensively in the human resource management and organizational behavior
> literatures. Many scholars have examined socialization tactics, and prior work has shown that peer
> support helps newcomers adjust. However, no study has examined how peer mentors themselves respond
> when a newcomer they support performs well. In this paper, we use hierarchical linear modeling on
> multi-wave survey data from 214 mentor–newcomer dyads to test whether newcomer performance is related
> to mentor helping behavior. We hypothesize that newcomer performance is negatively related to
> subsequent mentor helping. Onboarding is an important issue for organizations. The remainder of the
> paper reviews the literature, describes our methods, presents results, and discusses implications.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Gap-spotting as motivation** — "no study has examined" is named an anti-pattern in
  `amj-literature-positioning` and `amj-topic-selection`; an empty cell is not a theoretical reason the
  question matters.
- **No phenomenon hook** — opens on a literature, not on something happening in the world
  (`amj-writing-style`: introduction should *hook the phenomenon* first).
- **Bald prediction, no mechanism** — "negatively related to subsequent mentor helping" gives a sign but
  no *why*, the `amj-theory-development` anti-pattern.
- **Buried lede / no contribution on page 1** — the theoretical "so what" never appears; `amj-writing-style`
  and `amj-contribution-framing` require the contribution visible within the first page.
- **Method-first framing and throat-clearing** — "we use hierarchical linear modeling…," "is an important
  issue" — passive fog and vague stakes.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (AMJ arc — hook → conversation & tension → question → approach → contribution)

> Organizations increasingly ask their best employees to onboard new hires, assuming the arrangement is
> win–win: newcomers adjust faster and mentors build leadership skills. Yet managers at one services
> firm noticed a puzzle — some of their strongest mentors quietly pulled back exactly when their
> newcomers began to excel. *(phenomenon hook — a concrete, managerially recognizable tension in the
> world, per `amj-writing-style`.)*
>
> This puzzle sits at the intersection of two conversations that predict opposite things. The
> socialization literature treats peer helping as a stable, prosocial behavior that benefits mentor and
> newcomer alike. Social-comparison and status theory, by contrast, hold that a fast-rising peer can
> become a **status threat** to those nearby. When the person you are helping starts to outshine you,
> these literatures disagree about what you do next. *(names the conversation and the tension — a
> `amj-literature-positioning` motivating move, not a gap.)*
>
> We therefore ask: **when, and why, does a newcomer's strong performance lead an experienced mentor to
> withdraw helping rather than sustain it?** We argue that a high-performing newcomer activates
> upward social comparison in the mentor, which generates **status threat**; status threat, in turn,
> motivates the mentor to protect standing by **withdrawing the very support that helped the newcomer
> rise**. Crucially, we theorize a **boundary condition**: this effect should weaken — and may reverse —
> where mentoring is a formally rewarded part of the mentor's role, because there the mentor's status is
> tied *to* the newcomer's success rather than threatened by it. *(research question as a why/when
> statement, with an explicit mechanism chain and a theorized boundary — `amj-theory-development`.)*
>
> We test this account in a multi-wave field study of mentor–newcomer dyads across the branches of one
> services firm, measuring newcomer performance, mentor status threat, and mentor helping over time, and
> using the firm's variation in whether mentoring is formally rewarded to test the boundary condition.
> *(theoretical approach and study, in one paragraph — design demoted to a tool, not the lead.)*
>
> We contribute to the socialization literature by **problematizing its assumption that peer helping is
> unconditionally prosocial**, showing instead that helping is contingent on the helper's own status
> position — a *boundary condition* the literature has overlooked. By identifying **status threat** as
> the mechanism, we also bridge socialization and social-comparison theory, advancing our understanding
> of *why* support can erode at the moment it appears to be working. *(intended contribution stated on
> page 1, naming the contribution types — problematization + boundary + mechanism/bridge — per
> `amj-contribution-framing`, and aligned to what the discussion will deliver.)* For practice, the study
> warns that pairing strong newcomers with strong peers can backfire unless mentoring is built into how
> mentors themselves are evaluated. *(practical relevance — an AMJ acceptance criterion — grounded in the
> phenomenon, not boilerplate.)*

## Why the "after" meets the AMJ bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Phenomenon hook first, contribution on page 1** — the intro opens on a recognizable managerial puzzle
  and states the theoretical "so what" before the methods, satisfying `amj-writing-style`
  ("Contribution is clear within the first page") and the front-end structure in
  `amj-literature-positioning`.
- **Motivation is a tension + problematization, not a gap** — two theories predict opposite things, and
  the paper challenges the "helping is unconditionally prosocial" assumption — two of the three
  legitimate motivating moves in `amj-literature-positioning`; the gap-spotting language is gone.
- **Explicit mechanism and a theorized boundary** — antecedent (newcomer performance) → mechanism (status
  threat via upward comparison) → outcome (helping withdrawal), with a moderator that has a *reason*
  (mentoring is rewarded), exactly the chain `amj-theory-development` requires; written as an a-priori
  why/when claim, guarding against HARKing.
- **Named contribution type(s)** — problematization, boundary condition, and a mechanism that bridges two
  literatures, stated in the form `amj-contribution-framing` asks for, and promised here so the
  discussion can deliver the *same* contribution (intro ↔ discussion consistency).
- **Practical relevance is real, not bolted on** — a specific, findings-grounded implication (how to pair
  and evaluate mentors), meeting AMJ's third acceptance criterion without displacing the theoretical one.
- **Active voice, demoted method, no over-signposting** — "We argue…," "We test…"; the estimator is a
  tool mentioned where the design is, and the roadmap paragraph is cut.
