> actually appeared in *The Academy of Management Journal* (AMJ), with year and volume/issue. Candidates
> that turned out to be in a **sibling venue** — *Academy of Management Review* (AMR, theory-only),
> *Administrative Science Quarterly*, *Organization Science*, *Journal of Management*, *Strategic

> Methods* — were **omitted** and are logged in the guardrail section. It is deliberately a short, clean
> list (6 verified) rather than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **AMJ is empirical-only.** Its mission is to publish *empirical* research that tests, extends, or
> builds management theory (see [`../../skills/amj-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amj-topic-selection/SKILL.md)).
> Purely conceptual / theory pieces belong to **AMR**, not AMJ, and are not listed here.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design and framing positioning only**. It does
> not reproduce or invent coefficients, effect sizes, or specific findings — read the original before
> citing any number. For AMJ-specific policy, scope, and a "do-not-misattribute" list, see
> [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × theme** is closest to yours, then study how that paper turns a real
organizational phenomenon into an explicit *theoretical* contribution with practical relevance — the AMJ
bar (see [`../../skills/amj-contribution-framing/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amj-contribution-framing/SKILL.md)
and [`../../skills/amj-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/amj-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each, ask
the self-check question before claiming your paper is "AMJ-shaped." All are empirical, consistent with
AMJ's mission.

## By method

### Theory-building from cases — methodological exemplar (qualitative)

- **Eisenhardt & Graebner, "Theory Building from Cases: Opportunities and Challenges," AMJ 2007, 50(1):25–32.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal article page, DOI `10.5465/amj.2007.24160888`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** the canonical AMJ statement of how rich case data become crisp constructs
    and propositions — the reference `amj-theory-development` points to for grounded, theory-building
    (rather than hypothetico-deductive) work. Use it as the template for justifying a qualitative AMJ design.
  - **Self-check:** does your inductive study build *theory* (constructs + propositions) from the data,
    rather than report an interesting case with no theoretical yield?

### Inductive field case of an organizational process (qualitative)

- **Dutton & Dukerich, "Keeping an Eye on the Mirror: Image and Identity in Organizational Adaptation," AMJ 1991, 34(3):517–554.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal, 34(3):517–554.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a single-organization, longitudinal field study that turns a concrete
    phenomenon (how an agency responded to a public problem over time) into a durable theoretical account
    of organizational image and identity — the AMJ "phenomenon → theory" arc done inductively.
  - **Self-check:** does your case yield a *process theory* a reader from another division can carry to
    new settings, not just a narrative of one organization?

### Multi-study field + experiment on bias (mixed quantitative)

- **Hekman, Aquino, Owens, Mitchell, Schilpzand & Leavitt, "An Examination of Whether and How Racial and Gender Biases Influence Customer Satisfaction," AMJ 2010, 53(2):238–264.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal, 53(2):238–264 (JSTOR `25684320`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** combines a field study with controlled studies so the field correlation and
    the causal mechanism reinforce each other — a model of using *multiple methods* to make a single
    contribution credible, the kind of triangulation `amj-methods` rewards.
  - **Self-check:** do your studies *converge*, with the experiment isolating the mechanism the field data
    can only suggest?

### Experiments + field test of a counterintuitive effect (quantitative, micro/OB)

- **Hekman, Johnson, Foo & Yang, "Does Diversity-Valuing Behavior Result in Diminished Performance Ratings for Non-White and Female Leaders?," AMJ 2017, 60(2):771–797.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal, DOI `10.5465/amj.2014.0538`, 60(2):771–797.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a *surprising, theory-driven* question (those who promote diversity may be
    penalized, depending on who they are) tested across experimental and field data — exactly the
    tension/boundary motivating move `amj-topic-selection` and `amj-literature-positioning` ask for.
  - **Self-check:** is your effect *counterintuitive given a rival prediction*, and do you establish the
    boundary condition (for whom it holds or reverses) theoretically, not just empirically?

### Meta-analytic test of a mediated system (quantitative synthesis, HR/strategy)

- **Jiang, Lepak, Hu & Baer, "How Does Human Resource Management Influence Organizational Outcomes? A Meta-Analytic Investigation of Mediating Mechanisms," AMJ 2012, 55(6):1264–1294.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal, DOI `10.5465/amj.2011.0088`, 55(6):1264–1294.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses meta-analysis not just to estimate an average effect but to adjudicate
    *mediating mechanisms* (how HR systems reach outcomes) — meta-analysis in service of theory, the bar
    AMJ sets for synthesis papers. AMJ explicitly welcomes meta-analytic methods.
  - **Self-check:** does your synthesis test a *theorized mechanism or boundary*, rather than catalog a
    correlation everyone already accepts?

### Archival event study / quasi-experiment (quantitative, macro/strategy)

- **Flammer, "Corporate Social Responsibility and Shareholder Reaction: The Environmental Awareness of Investors," AMJ 2013, 56(3):758–781.**
  Verified: Academy of Management Journal, 56(3):758–781 (JSTOR `43589942`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an event study on archival market data converts a strategy-and-society
    question into a credible quasi-experimental design (market reaction around environmental news),
    pairing naturally with this pack's [`../code/`](../code/) event-study / DiD skeleton.
  - **Self-check:** does your archival design exploit a sharply timed, plausibly exogenous event so the
    estimate is causal, not a correlation a referee can reattribute to selection?

  *Journal of Applied Psychology* 93:48–58, **not AMJ**.

  Discontinuity Approach" (2015)** — verified to be in *Management Science* 61:2549–2568, **not AMJ**
  (distinct from her 2013 AMJ event-study paper listed above).
- **Gulati, "Network Location and Learning …" (1999)** — verified to be in the *Strategic Management
  Journal* 20(5):397–420, **not AMJ**.
- **Bartunek & Rynes, "Academics and Practitioners Are Alike and Unlike" (2014)** — verified to be in the
  *Journal of Management* 40(5):1181–1201, **not AMJ**.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on the Academy of Management Journal article page
(`journals.aom.org/doi/...`, an AMJ DOI of the form `10.5465/amj.*`) with volume/issue, confirm it is
*empirical* (not an AMR-style theory piece), and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
