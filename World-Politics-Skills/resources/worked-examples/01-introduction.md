# Worked Example: A *World Politics*-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **World Politics introduction arc** required by `wp-writing-style`:
**puzzle that travels → why it is hard / what the field gets wrong → argument & mechanism → cases &
evidence → contribution stated by the end of the intro → scope conditions → brief roadmap** — with the
journal's defining rule that the introduction must **front-load the contribution and show why the
argument matters *beyond your cases*** to a comparative-politics **and** IR reader who works on other
countries. *World Politics* is **theory-forward**: a finding is not a contribution until it is attached
to portable theory (mechanism + scope conditions), per `wp-theory-building`.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"When Patronage Outlives the Patron: Party Machines and
Authoritarian Succession."* Fictional comparison: a theoretically motivated set of single-party regimes
that did and did not survive the death of a founding leader.

---

## Before (single-case insider intro — buries the contribution, never travels)

> The literature on authoritarian regimes is vast. Scholars have long debated why some autocracies are
> durable. In this article we study the case of Country X, using process tracing and an original
> archive of party-congress records, to examine what happened to its ruling party after the founder
> died. We employ a most-similar-systems comparison and qualitative content analysis. Country X is an
> important and understudied case. We find that the party continued to function. Section 2 reviews the
> literature on authoritarianism, Section 3 describes our methods, Section 4 presents the historical
> narrative, Section 5 discusses, and Section 6 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `wp-writing-style` / `wp-topic-selection` / `wp-theory-building`):**

- **Single-case-insider framing** that never says why the argument **travels** — the named anti-pattern
  in `wp-writing-style` ("a single-case-insider intro that never states why the argument travels") and
  an off-fit signal in `wp-topic-selection` ("doesn't travel").
- **Leads with method** (process tracing, most-similar-systems) instead of the puzzle and argument.
- **No portable theory:** no mechanism, no scope conditions — `wp-theory-building` calls this
  "atheoretical / just a single-case story."
- **Contribution buried** ("we find that the party continued to function") rather than stated by the end
  of the introduction — violates the journal's front-load rule.
- **Undefined case-specific terms** assume area expertise; `wp-writing-style` requires defining
  country/region-specific institutions for a cross-case audience.
- **Over-signposted roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.

---

## After (World Politics arc — travels, theory-forward, contribution front-loaded)

> **Why do some authoritarian ruling parties survive the death of the leader who built them, while
> others fracture within months?** We argue that succession survival turns not on the founder's charisma
> but on whether the party has become a **self-enforcing distributor of patronage** — an organization
> whose mid-level cadres expect more from cooperation than from defection regardless of who sits at the
> top. Where patronage flows are routed through the party rather than through the person, the leader's
> death removes a figurehead but not the equilibrium; where they run through the patron personally, his
> death destroys the very thing holding cadres in line. *(puzzle that travels + argument + mechanism, in
> the first breath.)*
>
> Getting this right is hard because the regimes that institutionalize patronage are also the ones with
> stronger states and richer economies, so durability and institutionalization are confounded by the
> conditions that produced both — a comparison of survivors and non-survivors risks crediting the party
> for what prosperity did. *(why it is hard — the inferential threat a referee raises first.)*
>
> We test the argument on a theoretically motivated set of single-party regimes that faced a founder's
> death, chosen to **hold economic level and external threat roughly constant** while varying whether
> patronage was party-routed or person-routed. By "party-routed patronage" we mean appointments,
> rents, and promotions allocated through formal party organs (a politburo, a cadre department) rather
> than at the leader's personal discretion — an institutional distinction we define and code explicitly
> so it can be applied to regimes outside our set. *(cases & variation; case-specific terms defined for a
> cross-case reader, per `wp-writing-style`.)*
>
> Across the comparison, party-routed regimes weather succession and person-routed regimes do not, and
> the within-case evidence shows the predicted mechanism operating: in surviving regimes, cadres who
> stood to lose patronage placement coordinated to keep the party intact, while in fracturing regimes the
> same cadres raced to capture personal ties to rival successors. *(evidence mapped to observable
> implications — and to what we should *not* see if a rival story were true.)*
>
> Our contribution is to relocate the source of authoritarian durability **from the leader to the
> organization of patronage**, against the dominant view that personalist regimes are fragile *because*
> they are personalist: we show the fragility is not personalism as such but **person-routing of
> distribution**, a feature some nominally institutionalized parties also share. *(contribution stated by
> the end of the intro, relative to a specific prior view — the World Politics front-load rule.)* The
> argument is scoped to single-party and dominant-party regimes with a living founder-successor problem;
> it is **not** a claim about military juntas or monarchies, where succession runs on different rules.
> *(scope conditions — portability without overclaiming, per `wp-theory-building`.)* For a scholar of any
> region, the testable implication travels: **the survival of a personalist regime should track how much
> of its patronage is routed through the party, not how charismatic its founder was.**
>
> The article proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the patronage-routing argument and its observable
> implications; Section 3 presents the comparison and coding; Section 4 traces the mechanism through the
> succession episodes. *(brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the World Politics bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Contribution stated by the end of the introduction** — the reader knows the question, the argument,
  the evidence, and *why it matters beyond the cases* before Section 2 (`wp-writing-style`: "front-load
  the contribution").
- **It travels** — the claim is framed as a portable proposition about patronage-routing applicable to
  single-party regimes anywhere, not "new to Country X" (`wp-topic-selection` travels test).
- **Theory-forward** — an explicit **mechanism** (self-enforcing patronage equilibrium) and explicit
  **scope conditions** (single-/dominant-party regimes, not juntas or monarchies) turn case evidence into
  an argument the field can carry (`wp-theory-building`).
- **Reads across cases** — the case-specific construct ("party-routed patronage") is *defined and coded*
  so a non-area reader can apply it (`wp-writing-style`: define case-specific terms; state scope).
- **Identification threat named** before the design — durability/institutionalization confounding is
  surfaced and the comparison is built to hold it constant.
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the comparison and coding appear where the design is discussed, never as
  the lead (avoids the "leading with method" anti-pattern shared across the skills).
- **Fits the format discipline** — this is intro prose only; balance/coding tables and full case
  narratives belong in the body and the **online supplement (≤ 15 pages)**, and **notes + references
  count toward the ≤ 12,500-word limit** (`wp-writing-style`). Keep the ≤ **150-word abstract** stating
  question, approach, and findings.
