## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **method × question** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a result
**travel across cases** and **advance a theoretical debate** — the *World Politics* bar (see
[`../../skills/wp-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/wp-topic-selection/SKILL.md),
[`../../skills/wp-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/wp-theory-building/SKILL.md), and
[`../../skills/wp-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/wp-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each, ask the
self-check question before claiming your paper is "World Politics–shaped." Note the spread of *kinds* of
work: formal-strategic theory, conceptual typology, cross-national statistics, comparative-historical
analysis, a **review essay** (the journal publishes these), and a methodological article — *World
Politics* is theory-forward and method-pluralist.

## By method

### Formal / strategic IR theory (the security dilemma)

- **Jervis, "Cooperation Under the Security Dilemma," *World Politics* 1978, 30(2): 167–214.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/abs/cooperation-under-the-security-dilemma/C8907431CCEFEFE762BFCA32F091C526`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a transparent strategic logic (offense–defense balance and the
    distinguishability of offensive vs. defensive postures) explains when anarchy forces conflict and when
    it permits cooperation — a small set of assumptions that reorganized IR theory and travels to any
    dyad. The model-as-argument move `wp-theory-building` asks of formal work.
  - **Self-check:** does your strategic argument yield a **comparative static** a reader can carry to other
    cases, not just rationalize one episode?

### Conceptual typology / theory of democratic stability (comparative)

- **Lijphart, "Consociational Democracy," *World Politics* 1969, 21(2): 207–225.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/abs/consociational-democracy/BB47BF2A5A4EBAE341FD3FA4E262410F`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** introduces a concept (elite accommodation in fragmented societies) that
    explains democratic stability *against* the prevailing expectation that deep cleavages doom democracy —
    a portable type, not a description of one polity, launching a decades-long research program.
  - **Self-check:** is your concept defined so it travels across cases without **concept stretching**
    (`wp-theory-building`), and does it overturn a default expectation?

### Cross-national statistical political economy (resource curse)

- **Ross, "Does Oil Hinder Democracy?," *World Politics* 2001, 53(3): 325–361.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/abs/does-oil-hinder-democracy/67665D8D240C8F43CD4A2DCB35894071`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a cross-national statistical test adjudicates a broad debate — whether oil
    wealth is causally bad for democracy — and unpacks *mechanisms* (rentier, repression, modernization)
    rather than reporting one correlation. Pairs naturally with the quantitative skeleton in
    [`../code/`](../code/).
  - **Self-check:** does your cross-national design **adjudicate a debate the field already argues about**
    and separate competing mechanisms, not estimate a parameter no one disputes?

### Comparative-historical analysis of regime durability (qualitative-comparative)

- **Lachapelle, Levitsky, Way & Casey, "Social Revolution and Authoritarian Durability," *World Politics*
  2020, 72(4): 557–600.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/abs/social-revolution-and-authoritarian-durability/B62A931E63978E8B8466225EC123D2A9`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a cross-regional comparison argues that violent social revolution forges
    cohesive parties and coercive apparatuses, explaining why revolutionary regimes are unusually
    long-lived — a mechanism with explicit scope conditions that travels across continents.
  - **Self-check:** does your comparative-historical claim specify **who does what under what conditions**
    and **where it does not hold**, so it is a case *of* something general?

### Review essay that synthesizes a literature (World Politics publishes these)

- **Schedler-adjacent field synthesis — "The Era of Electoral Authoritarianism," *World Politics* 2012,
  64(1): 161–198.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/abs/era-of-electoral-authoritarianism/207FD78FC7E5A3A9B2C6BD7FC1194559`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a **review essay** that takes stock of work on electoral authoritarianism
    and pushes the field toward synthesis and sharper theory — exactly the review-article role
    `wp-topic-selection` describes (analyze a related body of work *and* advance how the field should
    proceed). Confirm current commissioning norms before pitching one (待核实 / TO VERIFY on commissioning).
  - **Self-check:** does your review **reorganize a literature and set its agenda**, rather than annotate
    it? (And is it commissioned / cleared with the editors?)

### Methodological article (concepts & inference in comparative-historical work)

- **Capoccia & Kelemen, "The Study of Critical Junctures: Theory, Narrative, and Counterfactuals in
  Historical Institutionalism," *World Politics* 2007, 59(3): 341–369.**
  Verified: `cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics/article/study-of-critical-junctures-theory-narrative-and-counterfactuals-in-historical-institutionalism/BAAE0860F1F641357C29C9AC72A54758`
  (and the Vol. 59 No. 3, April 2007 front-matter table of contents on Cambridge Core).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** sharpens a *concept* (critical junctures) and the *inferential tools*
    (narrative, counterfactuals) the field uses — *World Politics* rewards methodological pieces when they
    discipline how comparativists reason, not just demonstrate a technique.
  - **Self-check:** does your methods contribution change how the field **reasons about cases**, with a
    payoff a substantive comparativist would adopt?

## Omitted for sibling-confusion or lack of full verification (do not attribute to *World Politics*)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking — several are
landmark comparative/IR pieces that live in *sibling* venues and are routinely mis-cited to *World
Politics*:

- **Fearon, "Rationalist Explanations for War" (1995)** — verified to be in *International Organization*
  49(3), **not World Politics**. Guardrail entry.
- **Acemoglu & Robinson, "Why Did the West Extend the Franchise?" (2000)** — *Quarterly Journal of
  Economics* 115(4), **not World Politics**. Guardrail entry.
- **Pierson, "Increasing Returns, Path Dependence, and the Study of Politics" (2000)** — *American
  Political Science Review* 94(2), **not World Politics**. Guardrail entry (its themes overlap heavily
  with the Capoccia & Kelemen piece, which *is* World Politics).
- **Lipset & Rokkan, "Cleavage Structures, Party Systems, and Voter Alignments" (1967)** — a chapter in an
  edited Free Press volume, **not a World Politics article**, despite a similarly titled PDF surfacing
  under the journal in search; omitted pending a clean article-page confirmation.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on
`cambridge.org/core/journals/world-politics` (an article page carrying the volume/issue) and update the
access-date header. If web verification is unavailable, add the row as **待核实 / TO VERIFY** with **no
attribution** until checked. **When in doubt, omit.**
