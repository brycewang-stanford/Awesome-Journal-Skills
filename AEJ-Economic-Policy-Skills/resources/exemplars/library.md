# AEJ: Economic Policy Exemplars Library (by policy area)

> **Verified via web search, access date 2026-06.** Every paper below was checked to confirm it
> actually appeared in the ***American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*** (AEJ: Policy, the American
> Economic Association's quarterly policy journal) and carries a **`10.1257/pol…` DOI stem** — the marker
> that confirms AEJ: Policy specifically (the `pol.` infix is the AEA tell for this journal). Year,
> volume(issue), and pages were read off the AEA article pages (`aeaweb.org/articles?id=…`). Papers that
> could not be fully verified as AEJ: Policy were **omitted** — this is deliberately a short, clean list
> rather than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Sibling-confusion guard (do not misattribute).** AEJ: Policy is **not** any of these, which share the
> `10.1257/` AEA prefix or the subject area but use a different infix:
> *AER* (`10.1257/aer…`), *AEJ: Applied Economics* (`10.1257/app…`), *AEJ: Macroeconomics*
> (`10.1257/mac…`), *AEJ: Microeconomics* (`10.1257/mic…`), *Journal of Economic Literature*
> (`10.1257/jel…`), *Journal of Economic Perspectives* (`10.1257/jep…`). Only the **`10.1257/pol`** stem
> confirms AEJ: Policy. The omissions section below records papers excluded on exactly this ground — the
> well-known EITC-knowledge paper (AER) and the EITC-information experiment (AEJ: Applied) are **not**
> AEJ: Policy. This pack is also distinct from *J. Public Economics* and *J. Health Economics*.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only**. It does not
> reproduce or invent estimates, elasticities, or specific findings — read the original before citing any
> number. For AEJ: Policy-specific scope and house rules, see
> [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **policy area × design** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a
*credibly identified* estimate answer a *broad-interest policy question* with a *welfare / cost-benefit /
distributional* reading — the AEJ: Policy bar (see
[`../../skills/aejpol-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/aejpol-writing-style/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-writing-style/SKILL.md)). For each, ask
the self-check before claiming your paper is "AEJ: Policy-shaped." Remember the AEA house rule: report
**standard errors / confidence intervals, never significance asterisks**
([`aejpol-tables-figures`](../../skills/aejpol-tables-figures/SKILL.md)).

## By policy area

### Public finance & taxation (bunching / behavioral response to tax schedules)

- **Saez, "Do Taxpayers Bunch at Kink Points?," AEJ: Economic Policy 2010, 2(3):180–212.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.2.3.180` (DOI `10.1257/pol.2.3.180`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns a sharp behavioral prediction — taxpayers should bunch at kinks in
    the tax schedule — into an identification strategy and a structural elasticity that feeds optimal-tax
    reasoning. The design (bunching at the EITC kink) and the policy object (the taxable-income elasticity)
    reinforce each other, the AEJ: Policy sweet spot. Routes to
    [`../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md) (bunching)
    and [`../../skills/aejpol-theory-model/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-theory-model/SKILL.md)
    (elasticity → welfare).
  - **Self-check:** does your design recover the elasticity the optimal-policy formula actually needs, and
    do you defend the counterfactual density?

### Environmental & energy (carbon tax / emissions, synthetic-control evaluation)

- **Andersson, "Carbon Taxes and CO2 Emissions: Sweden as a Case Study," AEJ: Economic Policy 2019, 11(4):1–30.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20170144` (DOI `10.1257/pol.20170144`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** evaluates a real, broad-interest climate-policy lever (a carbon tax) with a
    transparent counterfactual (synthetic control), delivering a magnitude policymakers can act on. A
    credibly identified policy effect with a clear cost-effectiveness reading.
  - **Self-check:** is your policy lever a real instrument with a defended counterfactual, and is the
    effect stated in policy-actionable units?

### Health (public insurance, provider/coverage response)

- **Buchmueller, Miller & Vujicic, "How Do Providers Respond to Changes in Public Health Insurance Coverage? Evidence from Adult Medicaid Dental Benefits," AEJ: Economic Policy 2016, 8(4):70–102.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20150004` (DOI `10.1257/pol.20150004`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses cross-state variation in Medicaid dental coverage to identify the
    supply-side (provider) response to a coverage policy — a policy-relevant general-equilibrium margin
    that a demand-only study would miss. Connects to
    [`../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md)
    (policy variation across jurisdictions).
  - **Self-check:** does your design capture the policy-relevant margin (here, supply response), not only
    the mechanical first effect?

### Education (financial aid, long-run RD/eligibility design)

- **Bettinger, Gurantz, Kawano, Sacerdote & Stevens, "The Long-Run Impacts of Financial Aid: Evidence from California's Cal Grant," AEJ: Economic Policy 2019, 11(1):64–94.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20170466` (DOI `10.1257/pol.20170466`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** exploits eligibility thresholds in a large aid program to identify long-run
    outcomes (degree completion, earnings), then reads them against the program's cost — the cost-benefit
    framing AEJ: Policy rewards. Pairs with
    [`../../skills/aejpol-theory-model/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-theory-model/SKILL.md)
    (effects → cost-effectiveness).
  - **Self-check:** do you trace the policy's effect to a welfare-relevant long-run outcome and compare it
    to program cost, not just an intermediate one?

### Labor & social insurance (unemployment-insurance generosity)

- **Boone, Dube, Goodman & Kaplan, "Unemployment Insurance Generosity and Aggregate Employment," AEJ: Economic Policy 2021, 13(2):58–99.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20160613` (DOI `10.1257/pol.20160613`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses border-county and cross-state variation in UI generosity to estimate an
    aggregate-employment effect — directly the parameter the macro-policy debate over UI turns on. A
    design built to answer the contested policy question, not a tangential one. Routes to
    [`../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md`](../../skills/aejpol-identification/SKILL.md)
    (cross-border DID) and [`../code/stata/03_did_modern.do`](../code/stata/03_did_modern.do).
  - **Self-check:** does your estimand match the policy parameter under debate (here, the *aggregate*
    effect), and is the cross-border design's confound addressed?

### Regulation & antitrust (enforcement, long-run innovation effects)

- **Watzinger, Fackler, Nagler & Schnitzer, "How Antitrust Enforcement Can Spur Innovation: Bell Labs and the 1956 Consent Decree," AEJ: Economic Policy 2020, 12(4):328–359.**
  Verified: `aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/pol.20190086` (DOI `10.1257/pol.20190086`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** treats a specific regulatory action (a consent decree compelling patent
    licensing) as the policy lever and identifies its downstream innovation effect, with a clear lesson for
    how enforcement design shapes innovation. A regulation paper whose contribution is a transferable
    policy mechanism, not a single firm history.
  - **Self-check:** is your regulatory action a clean lever with an identified downstream effect and a
    lesson that travels beyond the one case?

## Omitted for lack of full verification, or to avoid sibling-confusion (do not attribute to AEJ: Policy without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following were **excluded** after checking:

- **Chetty, Friedman & Saez, "Using Differences in Knowledge across Neighborhoods to Uncover the Impacts of
  the EITC on Earnings" (2013)** — a natural AEJ: Policy-area candidate, but web search confirms it is in
  the ***American Economic Review*** (103(7):2683–2721, DOI `10.1257/aer.103.7.2683`), **not** AEJ: Policy.
  Listed only as a sibling-confusion guardrail: the `aer` infix, not `pol`, is the tell.
- **Chetty & Saez, "Teaching the Tax Code: Earnings Responses to an Experiment with EITC Recipients"
  (2013)** — published in ***AEJ: Applied Economics*** (5(1):1–31, DOI `10.1257/app.5.1.1`), **not**
  AEJ: Policy. The `app` infix is the tell.
- Other frequently-cited tax/health/labor policy papers surfaced in search were left off because their
  **exact AEJ: Policy volume/issue/pages** could not be confirmed to the same standard in one pass; add
  them only after reading the AEA article page and confirming the `10.1257/pol` DOI.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `aeaweb.org/articles?id=…` (article page with
volume/issue/pages) **and** that the DOI begins `10.1257/pol`, then update the access-date header. When in
doubt, omit. Shared-hub method guidance:
[`../../../shared-resources/empirical-methods/reviewer-objection-checklist.md`](../../../shared-resources/empirical-methods/reviewer-objection-checklist.md).
