# External Tools & Resources for Art Bulletin–Style Art History

Resources useful when researching, illustrating, and preparing an *Art Bulletin* submission. The Art
Bulletin is a **humanities** journal: a submission turns on an **original art-historical argument**,
**close visual / formal analysis** of objects, and **documentary, archival, and provenance**
evidence — **not** datasets, statistics, or replication. The defining production task is **working
with images**: securing **reproduction rights and permissions**, paying photography costs, and
supplying **high-resolution** files. Check licenses and current access terms before use, and verify
any Art Bulletin–specific policy in [`official-source-map.md`](official-source-map.md).

## 1. Image sources & digital repositories

| Source | Provider | Typical use |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| Wikimedia Commons | Wikimedia | Public-domain reproductions; always re-verify the rights status |
| Open-access museum collections (Met, Rijksmuseum, NGA, Getty, Cleveland, Smithsonian Open Access) | Museums | High-resolution, often rights-cleared public-domain images |
| Europeana | EU cultural heritage | Aggregated European collections with per-item rights labels |
| Artstor (in JSTOR) / ITHAKA | ITHAKA | Teaching/research images (licensing differs from publication rights) |
| IIIF manifests / Mirador / Universal Viewer | IIIF community | Deep-zoom comparison of high-resolution images across institutions |
| Image rights & licensing agencies (Art Resource, Bridgeman Images, Scala, ARS) | Commercial | Licensing reproductions and clearing artist-estate rights |

> A museum or repository providing a **viewing** copy is not the same as holding **reproduction
> rights for publication**. Clear rights with the **copyright holder** (artist/estate or rights
> agency), and obtain a **publication-quality file** separately.

## 2. Permissions, rights & fair use

- **The author secures and pays for permissions and photography** (CAA policy). Budget time and money
  early; clearance can take months for living artists, estates, and some museums.
- **Public domain vs. in copyright**: a work of art can be public domain while a *specific photograph*
  of it carries its own rights claim — clear the **reproduction** you will actually print.
- **Fair use**: the **College Art Association Code of Best Practices in Fair Use for the Visual Arts**
  is the field-standard reference; it is the **author's responsibility** to determine whether fair use
  applies. CMOS **chapter 13** covers rights and permissions.
- **Tracking**: keep a permissions log per figure (rights holder, contact, fee, license scope —
  world rights / print + electronic, edition, term — date granted, and the wording of the required
  **credit line**).

## 3. Reproduction quality & image preparation

- **Resolution**: supply press-quality files (commonly **300 dpi at print size**, or higher for
  detail crops); confirm current specs with the editorial/production office at acceptance.
- **Formats**: TIFF (lossless) for photographs and details; vector (EPS/PDF) for plans, diagrams, and
  line drawings. At *submission* all illustrations go in a **single Word file ≤ 10 MB, max 20 images**;
  high-resolution files are supplied **on acceptance**.
- **Tools**: Adobe Photoshop / Affinity Photo / GIMP for tonal and color correction and detail crops;
  Adobe Illustrator / Inkscape for plans, maps, and diagrams; ensure faithful color and avoid
  over-sharpening or manipulation that misrepresents the object.

## 4. Sources, archives & provenance evidence

- **Archives & primary documents**: institutional and museum archives, inventories, account books,
  wills, sale and auction catalogues, correspondence; finding aids and digitized collections.
- **Provenance**: catalogues raisonnés, collection and exhibition histories, dealer and auction
  records (e.g., Getty Provenance Index), and ownership chains — essential for attribution and for
  flagging spoliation / restitution concerns.
- **Technical art history**: conservation and scientific reports (X-radiography, infrared
  reflectography, pigment and dendrochronology analysis) where the material object carries the
  argument.

## 5. Writing, citation & reference management

- **Style**: **The Chicago Manual of Style** (notes / endnotes, **not** author-date), per the CAA
  Publications Style Guide; citations follow **CMOS chapter 14**. Keep one consistent style.
- **Reference managers**: Zotero, EndNote, or BibTeX/BibLaTeX with a Chicago **notes** style; Zotero
  is well suited to images, museum objects, and archival items.
- **Manuscript**: Microsoft Word (12-pt, double-spaced), **endnotes not footnotes**; prepare a
  **double-blind** manuscript (no author-identifying text or notes) plus a separate cover sheet.

## 6. Process & portal

| Item | Note |
|------|------|
| Submission | **Electronic only** — e-mail (`the.art.bulletin@collegeart.org`) or large-file transfer; **Microsoft Word, not PDF** |
| Owner / publisher | **College Art Association (CAA)** / **Taylor & Francis (Routledge)** |
| Review model | **Double-blind** — anonymize text and notes |
| Article length | **Up to 16,000 words including endnotes** (verify) |
| Abstract / bio | Abstract **≤ 100 words**; biographical statement **≤ 50 words** (verify) |
| Illustrations | **Max 20 images** in a single Word file **≤ 10 MB** at submission; high-res supplied on acceptance |
| Permissions | **Author** secures and pays for rights and photography; fair use per CAA code |
| Open access | Hybrid (T&F **Open Select**); optional **APC** after acceptance (verify) |

## 7. Cautions

1. **Verify volatile specifics** (editor, exact caps, illustration/file limits, Chicago edition,
   fee/APC, fair-use wording) on the official CAA / Taylor & Francis pages — they change; unverified
   items are marked 待核实.
2. **Clear rights to the actual reproduction you will print** — public-domain object ≠ rights-free
   photograph; start clearance early because it is slow and the cost is the author's.
3. **Anonymize properly** — The Art Bulletin is double-blind; strip author-identifying text and notes,
   and submit Word (not PDF) so the office can anonymize.
4. **Images carry the argument** — supply legible figures and exact figure references; the visual
   evidence, not a dataset, is what reviewers scrutinize.
5. **Cite to Chicago notes** — endnotes, full credit lines, and consistent note style; not author-date.
