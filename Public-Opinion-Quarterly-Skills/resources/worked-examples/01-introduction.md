# Worked Example: A POQ-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the POQ introduction discipline from `poq-writing-style` and
`poq-survey-design-and-measurement`: **front-load the contribution** (opinion theory, current opinion,
*or* survey validity), **name the methodological move plainly** in **Total Survey Error (TSE)** terms,
**define acronyms on first use**, and make the headline result survive the **artifact-vs-effect test**
(*"if this were a survey artifact rather than a real opinion signal, the data would look like ___;
instead they look like ___"*). The contribution must be legible to **both** substantive opinion
scholars **and** survey methodologists.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Does the Response Scale Make the Trend? A Split-Ballot Test of a
Reported Shift in Public Confidence."* Fictional setting: a recurring national survey whose headline
"confidence" item switched from a 4-point to a 5-point (middle-offering) scale between waves, after
which a sharp apparent rise in "confidence" was reported.

---

## Before (buries the contribution — typical first-draft intro)

> Public confidence in institutions has been studied extensively. Many scholars have noted that survey
> measurement can be sensitive to design. In this paper, we field a survey experiment and run logistic
> regressions with weights to study confidence. We randomize question features and analyze the results.
> Understanding public confidence is important for democracy. Section 2 reviews the literature, Section
> 3 describes the data, Section 4 presents the method, Section 5 reports results, and Section 6
> concludes.

**What's wrong (against `poq-writing-style` / `poq-topic-selection` / `poq-survey-design-and-measurement`):**

- **Never states which kind of contribution this is** — opinion theory, current opinion, or survey
  validity? A POQ reader cannot tell whether the payoff is substantive or methodological (the named
  anti-pattern in `poq-writing-style` and the fit test in `poq-topic-selection`).
- **No TSE framing.** The methodological move (which error source is at issue — here *measurement*, via
  response-scale wording) is glossed, not named. POQ readers are survey scientists.
- **Acronyms / design undefined**; no question wording, no population, no response-rate plan signposted
  — nothing that previews the AAPOR **Appendix A: Disclosure Elements**.
- **No headline number, no artifact-vs-effect contrast** that would distinguish a measurement artifact
  from a real opinion shift.
- **Throat-clearing** ("has been studied extensively," "is important for democracy") and an
  **over-signposted roadmap** doing the argument's work.

---

## After (POQ arc — contribution front-loaded, TSE-named, artifact-vs-effect explicit)

> **Was the recent jump in measured public confidence a real change of opinion, or an artifact of a
> changed response scale?** We show it was largely the latter: adding an explicit middle option to the
> confidence item raises the share expressing "confidence" by **8.0 percentage points** in a randomized
> split-ballot — a *measurement* effect, not an *opinion* movement. *(Contribution stated in the first
> breath, and it is clearly a contribution to **survey validity**, with the headline number and units.)*
>
> This matters because the item's scale changed between two waves of a widely cited tracking survey,
> exactly when its confidence figure rose — so the reported "trend" confounds a genuine attitude shift
> with a **question-wording / response-option change**, the measurement link in the **Total Survey
> Error (TSE)** chain. Distinguishing the two is hard observationally, because in the field the scale
> change and any real opinion change occur together. *(Why it is hard — the identification problem,
> named in TSE terms.)*
>
> We resolve it with a **split-ballot experiment**: respondents are randomly assigned the original
> 4-point scale or the 5-point (middle-offering) scale, holding the question stem, mode, and field
> period fixed, so any difference is attributable to the response option alone. The target population,
> exact wording of both ballots, **AAPOR-defined response rate (RR3)**, sample sizes and precision, and
> the design effect from weighting and clustering are reported in **Appendix A: Disclosure Elements**.
> *(Design and disclosure, named plainly — `poq-survey-design-and-measurement`.)*
>
> The middle-offering scale shifts **8.0 points** of the distribution into the "confident" range
> (off a base of 47%), concentrated among low-information respondents — the satisficing-prone group a
> middle option most affects. *(Headline result restated, with the mechanism and scope condition.)*
> **Artifact-vs-effect test:** if a *real* rise in confidence were driving the wave-over-wave jump,
> the increase would appear under *both* scales and across information levels; instead it appears
> **only** under the 5-point ballot and **only** among low-information respondents — the signature of a
> measurement artifact, not an opinion change. *(The POQ-specific test, made explicit.)*
>
> Our contribution is to survey validity: a reported confidence "trend" is **partly manufactured by an
> instrument change**, and we quantify how much. *(Contribution stated relative to a specific prior
> reading of the data.)* The broad lesson travels beyond this item — **any tracking series that alters
> response options mid-stream risks reporting a measurement artifact as an attitude change**, so trend
> claims should be benchmarked against a fixed-instrument bridge or a split-ballot before they are read
> as opinion movement. *(Lesson legible to both opinion scholars and methodologists.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 describes the survey and the split-ballot design; Section 3
> reports the scale effect and its moderation by information; Section 4 discusses implications for
> reading the published trend. *(Brief roadmap — no over-signposting.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the POQ bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Contribution front-loaded and typed** — the reader knows by the first sentence that this is a
  **survey-validity** contribution, with the headline number (8.0 pp) and units immediately
  (`poq-writing-style`: front-load the contribution; `poq-topic-selection` fit test #1).
- **TSE move named plainly** — the error source (**measurement**, via response options) is stated, not
  glossed; the design isolates it (`poq-survey-design-and-measurement`: walk the TSE chain).
- **Acronyms defined on first use** (TSE, AAPOR, RR3) and **AAPOR disclosure previewed** — Appendix A
  with wording, population, response rate per AAPOR Standard Definitions, sample size/precision, and
  design effect (`poq-transparency-and-data-policy`).
- **Artifact-vs-effect test passed explicitly** — the one-sentence contrast (*both scales vs. only the
  5-point ballot*) is the POQ-specific test from `poq-survey-design-and-measurement` and the
  measurement-validity test from `poq-theory-and-hypotheses`.
- **Hypothesis is directional and falsifiable**, with the null an opinion-change account would imply —
  no HARKing; the design is the kind one would preregister (`poq-theory-and-hypotheses`).
- **Reaches both audiences** — a communication scholar and a survey methodologist can both follow the
  claim and the stakes; the broad lesson generalizes to any mid-stream instrument change
  (`poq-writing-style`: readable by both).
- **Sized to type** — this crisp, single-contribution result is a natural **Research Note** (< 3,000
  words of text and notes) rather than an Article (`poq-topic-selection` type choice;
  `poq-writing-style` caps).
