> Academic's *Public Opinion Quarterly* article pages (`academic.oup.com/poq/...`) to confirm it
> actually appeared in **Public Opinion Quarterly** (Oxford University Press, sponsored by AAPOR), with
> year and volume(issue):page. Candidates that could not be fully verified **as POQ** were **omitted** —
> this is deliberately a short, clean list rather than a long, uncertain one.
>
> **Use principle (zero hallucination):** this file gives **design positioning only**. It does not
> reproduce or invent response rates, coefficients, or specific findings — read the original on Oxford
> Academic before citing any number. For POQ-specific policy, scope, and a "do-not-misattribute" list,
> see [`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

> **Sibling-confusion guard.** POQ is **not** the *American Journal of Political Science*,
> *Political Analysis*, *Journalism & Mass Communication Quarterly*, the
> *International Journal of Public Opinion Research*, or *Survey Methodology*. Several survey-methods
> classics live in those venues (or in books / *Applied Cognitive Psychology* / *Journal of the Royal
> Statistical Society*); do **not** attribute them to POQ. See the omissions section below.

## How to use this library

Pick the row whose **topic × method** is closest to yours, then study how that paper makes a *credible*
result carry a **public-opinion or survey-validity** lesson — the POQ bar (see
[`../../skills/poq-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/poq-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/poq-survey-design-and-measurement/SKILL.md`](../../skills/poq-survey-design-and-measurement/SKILL.md)).
For each, ask the self-check before claiming your paper is "POQ-shaped." Every paper is framed in
**Total Survey Error (TSE)** terms — note *which* error source each one isolates.

## By method

### Split-ballot question-order / context effect (measurement — survey experiment)

- **Schuman, Presser & Ludwig, "Context Effects on Survey Responses to Questions About Abortion," POQ 1981, 45(2):216–223.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article-abstract/45/2/216/1927150`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a clean split-ballot showing that the **order** in which a general and a
    specific abortion item appear changes the marginal — the questionnaire context *is* the
    identification, isolating a **measurement** (question-order) effect from any real attitude. The
    template for "the trend was the instrument, not the opinion."
  - **Self-check:** does your design randomize the wording/order feature so the difference is
    attributable to measurement alone, not to a substantive shift?

### Split-ballot response-option effect (measurement — survey experiment)

- **Presser & Schuman, "The Measurement of a Middle Position in Attitude Surveys," POQ 1980, 44(1):70–85.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article-abstract/44/1/70/1870969`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** five split-ballot experiments quantify how **offering vs. omitting a
    middle alternative** moves the distribution — a response-option (measurement) effect, pre-specified
    and replicated. Directly parallels the fictional worked example in
    [`../worked-examples/01-introduction.md`](../worked-examples/01-introduction.md).
  - **Self-check:** have you held the stem, mode, and field period fixed so only the response options
    vary, and pre-specified the estimand?

### Mode effects & social-desirability bias (mode — randomized mode experiment)

- **Kreuter, Presser & Tourangeau, "Social Desirability Bias in CATI, IVR, and Web Surveys: The Effects of Mode and Question Sensitivity," POQ 2008, 72(5):847–865.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article/72/5/847/1833162`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a randomized mode comparison validated against records shows
    self-administered modes elicit fewer socially desirable answers on sensitive items — a model of
    isolating a **mode** effect (and distinguishing it from a substantive change) with a record-check
    benchmark.
  - **Self-check:** in your mixed-mode design, can you quantify and adjust for mode, rather than
    conflate a mode artifact with opinion change?

### Nonresponse bias vs. response rate (nonresponse — research synthesis / meta-analysis)

- **Groves & Peytcheva, "The Impact of Nonresponse Rates on Nonresponse Bias: A Meta-Analysis," POQ 2008, 72(2):167–189.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article/72/2/167/1920564`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** synthesizing 59 methodological studies, it shows the **response rate is a
    weak predictor of nonresponse bias** — reframing how the field should report and reason about
    **nonresponse**, the canonical "assess the bias, not just the rate" reference.
  - **Self-check:** do you assess nonresponse *bias* (with auxiliary/benchmark data), not merely report
    an AAPOR response rate as if it certified representativeness?

### Communication / misinformation and belief (opinion-formation — observational + experimental)

- **"All the President's Lies: Repeated False Claims and Public Opinion," POQ 2023, 87(3):764 (issue article).**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article/87/3/764/7275174`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** links repeated elite falsehoods to public belief, with the effect
    conditional on partisanship — a contemporary contribution to **opinion formation / communication**
    that still takes measurement and the partisan-moderation null seriously.
  - **Self-check:** is your communication mechanism made observable and tested against the rival
    (here, partisan) account, with hypotheses stated before the test?

### Current public opinion over time (trend — descriptive synthesis)

- **Norrander & Wilcox, "Trends in Abortion Attitudes: From Roe to Dobbs," POQ 2023, 87(2):427–458.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/poq/article-abstract/87/2/427/7159704`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a disciplined read of **current public opinion** across decades and
    states — the model for a "Polls in Context"-style argument about *why a movement matters now* and
    *how the polls support the read*, with attention to label and conditional-item wording.
  - **Self-check:** does your trend rest on comparable, fixed instruments across waves (so a scale or
    wording change is not misread as opinion movement — see the worked example)?

## Omitted for lack of full POQ verification (do not attribute to POQ without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following frequently-cited survey/opinion works were **excluded**
after checking — several are canonical but live in **sibling** venues, exactly the misattribution this
file guards against:

- **Zaller & Feldman, "A Simple Theory of the Survey Response" (1992)** — this is in the
  *American Journal of Political Science* (36(3)), **not POQ**. Listed here only as a guardrail.
- **Krosnick, satisficing / response-order theory (1991)** — the foundational statement appeared in
  *Applied Cognitive Psychology* (5(3)), **not POQ**; POQ papers *apply* it. Web search could not
  confirm a POQ placement of the originating article, so it is omitted.
- **Schuman & Presser, *Questions and Answers in Attitude Surveys* (1981)** — a **book** (Academic
  Press), not a POQ article; its component split-ballot studies that *did* appear in POQ (e.g., the two
  listed above) are cited individually instead.
- General **"effect of the question" review work** surfacing in search (e.g., in the
  *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society, Series A*) — **not POQ**; omitted.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `academic.oup.com/poq` (article page with
volume/issue) and update the access-date header. When the venue is in doubt — especially for survey-
methods classics that may sit in *Survey Methodology*, *IJPOR*, *Political Analysis*, *AJPS*, or
*JMCQ* — **omit**.
