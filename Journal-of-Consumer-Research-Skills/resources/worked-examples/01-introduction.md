# Worked Example: A JCR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **JCR introduction arc** assembled from this pack's skills:
**consumption phenomenon → conceptual stakes (which theory changes) → the psychological process →
what we learn → contribution, front-loaded → brief preview of the study chain** — written for JCR's
**interdisciplinary** readership (psychology, anthropology, sociology, economics, marketing), with the
**consumer insight leading and the method demoted to a tool** (`jcr-writing-style`).

JCR's dominant tradition is **theory-driven behavioral experimentation** that isolates a psychological
**process** (mediator) and its **boundary conditions** (`jcr-theory-development`, `jcr-methods`), so the
example below is an experiments paper. The same arc — phenomenon, conceptual move, what we learn —
applies to an interpretive / CCT paper, with a grounded conceptual framework in place of an a priori
process.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Borrowed Time: How Renting Rather Than Owning a Product Weakens
the Self–Object Bond and Lowers Care."* Fictional setting: lab and online experiments comparing how
consumers treat products they **rent** versus **own**.

---

## Before (buries the insight — typical first-draft intro)

> The sharing economy has grown rapidly and has been studied extensively across marketing and
> management. In this paper, we run five experiments with manipulation checks and mediation analyses to
> examine ownership versus rental. Using a 2 (ownership: own vs. rent) × 2 design with a measured
> mediator and PROCESS Model 4, we test whether ownership status affects product care. Access-based
> consumption is an important and growing topic for managers. Study 1 establishes the effect, Study 2
> tests mediation, Study 3 adds a moderator, Study 4 replicates with a different product, and Study 5
> rules out an alternative explanation. Our results have implications for firms in the rental market.

**What's wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Leads with method and design** ("we run five experiments… PROCESS Model 4") instead of the
  consumption insight — the named anti-pattern in `jcr-writing-style` ("consumer insight precedes
  method") and `jcr-theory-development` ("effect in search of a process").
- **No conceptual contribution.** It never says *which theory of the consumer changes*; per
  `jcr-topic-selection` and `jcr-contribution-framing` a robust effect alone is "a curiosity, not a
  contribution."
- **Managerial framing substitutes for a theory move** ("important topic for managers,"
  "implications for firms") — flagged by `jcr-contribution-framing` as "overclaiming managerial
  relevance in place of a consumer-theory advance" and by `jcr-topic-selection` as a JMR/JM signal,
  not JCR.
- **Subfield shorthand** (a bare "PROCESS Model 4," "2 × 2") excludes JCR's interdisciplinary readers
  (`jcr-writing-style`: define field terms; an experimentalist and a CCT scholar should both follow).
- **Process is missing.** The mediator is never named, so there is no mechanism — the core JCR
  contribution (`jcr-theory-development`).
- **A study-by-study list** does the work the conceptual argument should do.

---

## After (JCR arc — consumer insight + conceptual move, contribution front-loaded)

> **When people rent a product instead of owning it, do they care for it less — and if so, why?** We
> show that consumers treat rented products more carelessly than identical owned products not because
> renting is anonymous or temporary per se, but because **ownership extends the self into the object**,
> and a rented object is never incorporated into the self to defend. *(consumption phenomenon + the
> conceptual stakes, in the first breath — legible to any reader.)*
>
> This matters because the consumer's relationship to possessions is a foundational construct in
> consumer research: objects we own become part of who we are, and we protect them as we protect
> ourselves. Access-based consumption — renting, subscribing, sharing — is now a routine way people
> obtain products, yet it severs the very ownership link that this account assumes. **Does the
> self–object bond, and the care it motivates, survive when consumers gain access without owning?**
> *(why it matters conceptually — names the theoretical conversation the paper enters.)*
>
> We argue it does not, and we specify the **process**. Owning a product triggers psychological
> incorporation of the object into the **extended self**; that incorporation, not the product's value or
> the length of possession, is what motivates care. Renting grants use without incorporation, so the
> protective motive never engages. This predicts a precise **boundary condition**: anything that makes a
> rented object feel self-relevant — personalizing it, anticipating long-term access — should restore
> care, whereas anything that deepens an owned object's link to the self should heighten it. *(the
> psychological process (mediator) and the moderation that tests it — `jcr-theory-development`.)*
>
> Across five experiments, renters report and exhibit lower product care than owners of the same item;
> **self–object incorporation mediates** the effect; and the gap **closes when renters personalize the
> object or expect prolonged access** — the moderation the process predicts — while a temporariness
> account and a monetary-stake account do not explain the pattern. *(what we find, stated as the study
> chain effect → process → boundary, with rivals named — `jcr-methods`, `jcr-theory-development`.)*
>
> Our contribution is to **deepen** the theory of the extended self by specifying the consumer behavior
> it governs and the condition under which it lapses: incorporation, not legal ownership or monetary
> stake, is the active ingredient, so access-based consumption **predictably weakens the care that
> ownership motivates** unless self-relevance is restored by other means. *(contribution stated early,
> as a named move — deepen — against a specific prior account; `jcr-contribution-framing`.)* For
> consumers and the markets serving them, this reframes the rise of renting and subscribing not as a
> neutral change in how products are obtained but as a change in **how much of the self consumers invest
> in what they use** — with consequences for stewardship of shared and rented goods. *(consumer
> relevance, in accessible language — a preview of the required Consumer Relevance and Contribution
> Statement.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. We first establish the rent–own care gap and its incorporation
> mechanism, then test the self-relevance boundary, then rule out temporariness and monetary-stake
> accounts. *(brief preview of the study chain — no study-by-study over-listing.)*

## Why the "after" meets the JCR bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Consumer insight precedes method; contribution front-loaded** — the phenomenon (renters care less)
  and the conceptual stakes open the paper; the design ("five experiments," mediation) is demoted to one
  later clause (`jcr-writing-style`: "front-load the contribution… lead with the phenomenon and the
  insight rather than the method").
- **A named conceptual move against a target theory** — the contribution is stated as **deepen** the
  *extended-self* account, specifying its active ingredient and its boundary, not "we document a new
  effect" (`jcr-contribution-framing`: advance / deepen / repudiate, name the theory).
- **The process is the contribution** — the mediator (self–object incorporation) and a
  **moderation-of-process** boundary (personalization / expected access) are stated a priori and mapped
  to the study chain effect → process → boundary (`jcr-theory-development`, `jcr-methods`).
- **Rival accounts named up front** — temporariness and monetary stake are flagged as alternatives the
  design rules out, pre-empting the reviewer's first objection (`jcr-theory-development`: "name the
  rival processes… and design to rule them out").
- **Interdisciplinary legibility** — the argument rests on a construct (the extended self) drawn from
  the psychology/anthropology of possessions and is stated without subfield shorthand, so a behavioral
  experimentalist and a CCT scholar both follow it (`jcr-writing-style`, `jcr-topic-selection`).
- **Consumer relevance, not managerial tactics** — the closing frames stakes for consumers and the
  meaning of access-based consumption, not firm profit, avoiding the "overclaiming managerial relevance"
  anti-pattern (`jcr-contribution-framing`) and the JMR/JM venue signal (`jcr-topic-selection`).
- **Fit test passes** — the one-sentence question ("does renting weaken the care ownership motivates,
  and why?") is a genuine consumer-behavior question with a conceptual move and a base discipline doing
  real work — the `jcr-topic-selection` fit gate.
