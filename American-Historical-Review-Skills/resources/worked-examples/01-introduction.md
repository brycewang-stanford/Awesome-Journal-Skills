# Worked Example: An AHR-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **AHR introduction arc** drawn from this pack's own skills: **open with the
stakes, not the background → place the historiographical intervention early → state a contestable
thesis earned from primary sources → name the strongest counter-reading → close the opening on
significance for historians beyond the subfield.** The AHR publishes **arguments grounded in primary
sources that intervene in a live historiographical conversation** — not hypotheses, datasets, or
regressions. A history introduction succeeds when a historian of a *different* place and period can see,
on the first page, what is being revised and why it matters.

**Illustrative article (fictional):** *"The Ledger and the Lamp: Guild Record-Keeping and the Making of
Urban Trust in a Sixteenth-Century Port City."* Fictional setting: a mid-sized European port whose
merchant and artisan guilds left several decades of internal account books, dispute records, and
admission registers in a (fictional) municipal archive.

---

## Before (buries the argument — typical first-draft intro)

> The history of guilds has been studied extensively by many historians. Guilds were important
> institutions in the economic and social life of early modern cities, and a great deal has been written
> about their regulations, their apprenticeships, and their decline. This article examines the records
> of the guilds of one port city in the sixteenth century. Drawing on account books, dispute records,
> and admission registers, it describes how the guilds kept their records and what these records
> contained. Relatively little has been written about this particular city. The first section provides
> background on the city and its economy. The second section describes the guilds. The third section
> discusses their records. The conclusion offers some final thoughts.

**What is wrong (against this pack's skills):**

- **Leads with background, not stakes** — opens on "guilds were important" rather than a question or an
  argument. (`ahr-structure-and-exposition`: "Open with the stakes, not the background";
  `ahr-writing-style`: "Lead with the idea.")
- **No contestable thesis** — it promises to "describe how the guilds kept their records," which no
  historian could dispute. That is description, not argument. (`ahr-argument-development`: "A thesis no
  one could dispute (description in argument's clothing).")
- **"Little has been written" as the contribution** — antiquarian novelty, not a historiographical
  intervention. (`ahr-historiography-positioning`: "'First to study' framing when the real contribution
  is a reinterpretation"; `ahr-topic-selection`: the named antiquarian anti-pattern.)
- **No significance beyond the subfield** — a historian of another region or century is given no reason
  to read on. (`ahr-topic-selection`: the significance-beyond-the-subfield test.)
- **Sources unread** — the records are inventoried, not *criticized*; no provenance, genre, or silence.
  (`ahr-sources-and-archives`: read along *and against* the grain.)
- **Over-signposted, time-only roadmap** doing the work the argument should do.
  (`ahr-structure-and-exposition`: "Section headings that mark only time, not the argument's progress.")

---

## After (AHR arc — stakes + intervention + contestable thesis, significance early)

> In the winter accounts of one port city's mercers' guild, a recurring marginal note records that a
> member's word had been "taken on the book" — a debt or a promise entered without surety, on the
> strength of the ledger alone. **That small phrase, repeated across decades of guild records, lets us
> watch trust between strangers being manufactured by an institution rather than presumed among kin or
> neighbors — and it suggests that the credit economy of the early modern city was built less on
> personal reputation than on the mundane, bureaucratic act of being written down.** *(stakes and
> contestable thesis in the opening breath — an argument, not a description.)*
>
> Historians have long explained early modern commercial trust as a function of **personal reputation,
> kinship, and face-to-face community** — the merchant known to his peers, vouched for by his name.
> *(names the live conversation and the reigning account — `ahr-historiography-positioning`.)* This
> article argues that, in a city of strangers and newcomers, that account is incomplete: the guilds'
> own record-keeping — admission registers, dispute books, and running accounts — did not merely
> *document* trust but actively *produced* it, converting an unknown newcomer into a creditworthy
> member through inscription. *(states the intervention precisely: a **reframing**, not "no one has
> studied this.")*
>
> The claim rests on a body of guild records surviving for several decades of the sixteenth century:
> internal account books, the registers recording admissions and fees, and the books in which the guild
> adjudicated disputes among its members. *(the primary-source basis stated plainly —
> `ahr-sources-and-archives`.)* These are not transparent windows onto commercial life; they are
> documents the guild made about itself, for its own governance, and they fall silent precisely where
> the unenrolled — women traders, casual laborers, outsiders — did business beyond the book. Read with
> that silence in view, they show the institution *deciding whom its writing would vouch for.* *(source
> criticism — provenance, genre, and the archive's silences — built into the argument, not deferred.)*
>
> A skeptical reader will object that the records register a trust that already existed face-to-face,
> and merely wrote it down. *(names the strongest counter-reading —
> `ahr-argument-development`.)* But the dispute books repeatedly show the guild enforcing obligations
> between members who, by their own testimony, had never met outside the hall — trust that existed
> *because* of the book, not before it. Where the older account would predict reliance on a shared name,
> the records show reliance on a shared register. *(answers the rival by accounting for its evidence,
> not ignoring it.)*
>
> The stakes reach beyond one guild in one port. If commercial trust could be **institutionally
> manufactured through routine record-keeping**, then the familiar story of a transition from
> personal, reputational credit to impersonal, bureaucratic finance needs its chronology and its
> mechanism rethought: the impersonal was already at work inside the most "traditional" of corporate
> bodies. *(significance for historians of other places and periods — the AHR generalist bar;
> `ahr-topic-selection`.)* For historians of the state, of capitalism, and of literacy and the document
> alike, the humble guild ledger becomes a site where modern, paper-based trust was assembled long
> before the institutions usually credited with it.
>
> The article proceeds by following that act of inscription: first into the admission register, where
> membership was made; then into the account books, where credit was extended on the book; and finally
> into the dispute records, where the writing was tested and enforced. *(brief roadmap that tracks the
> argument's moves, not merely the passage of time.)*

## Why the "after" meets the AHR bar

Mapped to this pack's skill checklists:

- **Opens with the stakes, not the background** — the entry point is a single telling phrase from a
  document, which immediately states the question and the thesis (`ahr-structure-and-exposition`:
  "A vivid entry point … that quickly states the question, the argument, and why it matters";
  `ahr-writing-style`: "Lead with the idea").
- **States a contestable thesis** — "trust was manufactured by record-keeping, not presumed among kin"
  is a claim a competent historian could dispute, which is exactly the test in
  `ahr-argument-development` ("a sentence a competent historian could disagree with").
- **Places the historiographical intervention early and names its type** — the reigning
  reputation/kinship account is named, and the move is identified as a **reframing**, satisfying both
  specialists and generalists (`ahr-historiography-positioning` intervention table; two-audiences rule).
- **Earns the claim from criticized primary sources** — the records are introduced *and* read against
  the grain for genre and silence, never treated as a transparent window
  (`ahr-sources-and-archives`: provenance / bias / silences / representativeness).
- **Answers the strongest counter-reading** — the "it was already there face-to-face" objection is
  named and met with the dispute-book evidence, not dodged (`ahr-argument-development`).
- **Carries significance beyond the subfield** — the closing reframes a broad narrative (personal →
  impersonal credit) for historians of the state, capitalism, and literacy, clearing the AHR
  generalist test (`ahr-topic-selection`).
- **Narrative and analysis move together** — the roadmap follows the act of inscription through the
  records, so chronology serves the claim rather than replacing it
  (`ahr-structure-and-exposition`: interleave narrative and analysis).
