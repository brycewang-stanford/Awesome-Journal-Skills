## How to use this library

The AHR is the discipline's **generalist flagship across all periods and places**, and it rewards work
that makes a *local, source-grounded* finding carry a *general* historiographical lesson (see
[`../../skills/ahr-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ahr-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/ahr-historiography-positioning/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ahr-historiography-positioning/SKILL.md)).
Pick the row whose **field × method** is closest to yours, then study how that piece names a
conversation, stakes an intervention, and reaches historians beyond its subfield. For each, ask the
self-check question before claiming your own piece is "AHR-shaped."

These are landmark essays, forums, and addresses chosen because their interventions are unusually
legible. They are not a template for length or apparatus — confirm current article norms in
[`../official-source-map.md`](../official-source-map.md).

## By field / method

### Gender history — a category that reorganized the field (theory + historiographical intervention)

- **Joan W. Scott, "Gender: A Useful Category of Historical Analysis," AHR 1986, 91(5):1053–1075.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article-abstract/91/5/1053/155166`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a single conceptual move — treating *gender* as an analytic category
    rather than a synonym for women — reframed how historians of every field read power and difference.
    The canonical "small intervention, field-wide consequence" AHR essay.
  - **Self-check:** does your contribution offer a *category or reading* others can carry into their own
    periods, not just a finding local to your archive?

### Cultural history / the historian's craft — a presidential address as argument

- **Natalie Zemon Davis, "History's Two Bodies," AHR 1988, 93(1):1–30.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article-abstract/93/1/1/84948`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an AHA presidential address that argues *about* the discipline through
    concrete historical figures — modeling how to make a reflexive, methodological claim land for
    historians of every field rather than as abstract theory.
  - **Self-check:** when you reflect on method, do you ground it in specific historical material so a
    generalist sees the stakes, rather than arguing in the abstract?

### Microhistory & source criticism — defending a reading of the evidence

- **Natalie Zemon Davis, "'On the Lame'," AHR 1988, 93(3):572–603** (AHR Forum, *The Return of Martin
  Guerre*). Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article/93/3/572/57937`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a model of **source criticism under challenge** — Davis answers a critic
    by showing how she read fragmentary, ambiguous records, making the *interpretation of evidence*
    itself the argument. Pairs directly with
    [`../../skills/ahr-sources-and-archives/SKILL.md`](../../skills/ahr-sources-and-archives/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** can you defend your reading of a difficult source — its provenance, silences, and
    what it can and cannot bear — against the skeptical reading a referee will raise first?

### European political history — reframing a master narrative (historiographical reframing)

- **Lynn Hunt, "The World We Have Gained: The Future of the French Revolution," AHR 2003, 108(1):1–19.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article/108/1/1/22525`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** takes an exhaustively studied event and argues for a *new frame* for it,
    showing that an AHR contribution can be the reorganization of an existing historiography rather than
    a newly excavated archive (`ahr-historiography-positioning`: the **reframing** intervention type).
  - **Self-check:** if your subject is well-trodden, are you offering a genuinely different frame —
    not just one more case — that other historians can adopt?

### Transnational / global history — interrogating a method (AHR Conversation)

- **AHR Conversation, "On Transnational History," AHR 2006, 111(5):1441–1464.**
  Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article-abstract/111/5/1441/10247`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** a multi-scholar AHR Conversation that pressure-tests an emerging
    approach — useful as a map of how the discipline debates *scale and method*, and a guide to framing
    your own work as transnational without making "transnational" a mere buzzword.
  - **Self-check:** if you cross national or regional boundaries, can you say what the transnational
    *scale* lets you see that a national frame cannot?

### Global history / theory of periodization — adjudicating a contested concept (AHR Roundtable)

- **Dipesh Chakrabarty, "The Muddle of Modernity," AHR 2011, 116(3):663–675** (AHR Roundtable:
  Historians and the Question of "Modernity"). Verified: `academic.oup.com/ahr/article-abstract/116/3/663/41808`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** intervenes in how historians use a load-bearing but slippery category
    ("modernity"), modeling an argument that travels across regions and periods — the global, all-period
    reach the AHR prizes (`ahr-topic-selection`: significance beyond the subfield).
  - **Self-check:** if you lean on a big category (modernity, the state, capitalism, the secular), do
    you interrogate it rather than assume it — and does that interrogation matter outside your field?

## Omitted for lack of full verification, or to avoid sibling-journal confusion (do not attribute to AHR without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-fabrication, these candidates were **excluded** after checking:

- **William Cronon, "The Trouble with Wilderness; Or, Getting Back to the Wrong Nature" (1996)** —
  verified to be in **Environmental History** 1(1):7–28 (also Oxford Academic), **not the AHR**. A
  classic environmental-history essay, but a different journal; listed here only as a guardrail.
- **Frederick Cooper & Ann Laura Stoler, "Between Metropole and Colony: Rethinking a Research Agenda"** —
  a **book chapter** in *Tensions of Empire* (University of California Press, 1997), **not an AHR
  article**. Frequently cited alongside AHR colonial-history work, so flagged to prevent misattribution.
- **Julia Adeney Thomas, "History and Biology in the Anthropocene" (2014, AHR 119(5))** — *was*
  located on `academic.oup.com/ahr`, but is held back from the main list only because the diverse
  field/method slots above were already filled by fully cross-checked entries; verify the page
  directly before adding it.

Famous essays historians sometimes misremember as AHR but which belong to **sibling journals** — the
*Journal of American History*, *Past & Present*, the *Journal of Modern History*, *American Quarterly*,
or *History and Theory* — were not included; confirm the masthead before citing any of them as AHR.

Before adding any new piece to this library, confirm it on `academic.oup.com/ahr` (an article page with
volume/issue) and update the access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
