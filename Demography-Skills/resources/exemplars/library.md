> by Springer through 2020, by **Duke University Press** from 2021) — with year and volume/issue, against
> the journal's own article pages (`read.dukeupress.edu/demography/...`), its pre-2021 Springer record

## By method

### Multidimensional measurement of a population process (formal / measurement)

- **Massey & Denton, "Hypersegregation in U.S. Metropolitan Areas: Black and Hispanic Segregation Along Five Dimensions," _Demography_ 1989, 26(3):373–391.**
  Verified: `read.dukeupress.edu/demography/article/26/3/373/...` (also Springer `10.2307/2061599`; RePEc `spr/demogr/v26y1989i3p373-391`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** replaces a single segregation index with **five conceptually distinct
    dimensions**, then defines "hypersegregation" as being extreme on several at once — a measurement
    advance that reorganized how the field operationalizes residential separation. The
    [`demog-theory-building`](../../skills/demog-theory-building/SKILL.md) measurement-contribution move:
    separate what prior work conflated.
  - **Self-check:** does your measure *distinguish* components that earlier single indices ran together,
    with a substantive population payoff?

### Event-history with endogenous selection (timing / family demography)

- **Lillard, Brien & Waite, "Premarital Cohabitation and Subsequent Marital Dissolution: A Matter of Self-Selection?," _Demography_ 1995, 32(4):437–457.**
  Verified: Springer `link.springer.com/article/10.2307/2061690`; PubMed PMID 8829976; RePEc `spr/demogr`.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** models the **hazard of marital disruption** while treating the decision to
    cohabit as endogenous — correlated unobserved heterogeneity across two linked processes — so the
    apparent "cohabitation effect" is tested against a self-selection rival. A model of the
    [`demog-research-design`](../../skills/demog-research-design/SKILL.md) adjudication test in an
    event-history frame.
  - **Self-check:** does your survival/event-history design confront the selection story (who enters the
    state) rather than read a raw hazard contrast as the effect?

### Multistate / increment-decrement life table (mortality & aging)

- **Manton & Land, "Active Life Expectancy Estimates for the U.S. Elderly Population: A Multidimensional Continuous-Mixture Model of Functional Change Applied to Completed Cohorts, 1982–1996," _Demography_ 2000, 37(3):253–265.**
  Verified: `read.dukeupress.edu/demography/article/37/3/253/...` (Springer `10.2307/2648040`; RePEc `spr/demogr`).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** an **increment-decrement (multistate) life table** lets disability states
    and mortality evolve jointly, yielding active-life-expectancy estimates on completed cohorts — the
    [`demog-research-design`](../../skills/demog-research-design/SKILL.md) multistate design done with
    explicit transition rates and exposure.
  - **Self-check:** do your states, transitions, and base exposures make the derived life-table quantity
    (here, active life expectancy) reproducible and interpretable?

### Survival analysis of a mortality differential (health & mortality)

- **Hummer, Rogers, Nam & Ellison, "Religious Involvement and U.S. Adult Mortality," _Demography_ 1999, 36(2):273–285.**
  Verified: Springer `link.springer.com/article/10.2307/2648114`; PubMed PMID 10332617; Google Scholar lookup (journal *Demography*, pp. 273–285, DOI 10.2307/2648114).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** translates a hazard-model differential into a **life-expectancy gap at age
    20** and decomposes how much is health selection vs. social/behavioral pathway — uncertainty and
    mechanism reported as [`demog-data-analysis`](../../skills/demog-data-analysis/SKILL.md) expects, not
    a bare coefficient.
  - **Self-check:** can you express your mortality contrast as a demographic quantity (a difference in
    e₀ / e₂₀) and say what share is selection vs. mechanism?

### Trajectory analysis of intentions over the life course (fertility)

- **Hayford, "The Evolution of Fertility Expectations Over the Life Course," _Demography_ 2009, 46(4):765–783.**
  Verified: `read.dukeupress.edu/demography/article/46/4/765/...` (Springer `10.1353/dem.0.0073`; PMC2831352).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses **group-based trajectory analysis** to describe how women's expected
    fertility evolves across the whole childbearing span — a measurement/description contribution that
    distinguishes heterogeneous paths a single mean would hide
    ([`demog-theory-building`](../../skills/demog-theory-building/SKILL.md) observable-implications logic).
  - **Self-check:** does your design recover *distinct subgroup trajectories* with population meaning,
    rather than report an average that averages away the phenomenon?

### Determinants model linking flows to development outcomes (migration)

- **Durand, Kandel, Parrado & Massey, "International Migration and Development in Mexican Communities," _Demography_ 1996, 33(2):249–264.**
  Verified: `read.dukeupress.edu/demography/article-abstract/33/2/249/...` (Springer `10.2307/2061875`; PubMed PMID 8827168).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** models remittance/savings decisions with predictors at **individual,
    household, community, and macro levels**, framing migration as a component of community change rather
    than only an individual outcome — the [`demog-topic-selection`](../../skills/demog-topic-selection/SKILL.md)
    "frame flows as population redistribution" move.
  - **Self-check:** is your migration question pitched as a population-change process (with the right
    nesting of levels), legible to a demographer outside migration?

## Omitted for lack of full verification (do not attribute to _Demography_ without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, these strong candidates were **excluded** after web-checking — each
is a real, important paper, but **not in _Demography_** (the sibling-journal traps the brief warned of):

- **Preston, "The Changing Relation between Mortality and Level of Economic Development" (1975)** —
  verified to be in **_Population Studies_** 29(2), **not _Demography_**. The "Preston curve" classic.
- **Bongaarts, "A Framework for Analyzing the Proximate Determinants of Fertility" (1978)** — verified in
  **_Population and Development Review_** 4(1):105–132, **not _Demography_**.
- **Bongaarts & Feeney, "On the Quantum and Tempo of Fertility" (1998)** — verified in **_Population and
  Development Review_** 24(2):271–291, **not _Demography_**. (The tempo/quantum idea underlies this pack's
  fictional worked example, but the source paper is PDR.)
- **Massey, Arango, Hugo, Kouaouci, Pellegrino & Taylor, "Theories of International Migration: A Review
  and Appraisal" (1993)** — verified in **_Population and Development Review_** 19(3):431–466, **not
  _Demography_**.
- **Lee & Carter, "Modeling and Forecasting U.S. Mortality" (1992)** — verified in the **_Journal of the
  American Statistical Association_** 87(419):659–671, **not _Demography_**. The canonical mortality-
  forecasting method.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on `read.dukeupress.edu/demography` (or the
pre-2021 Springer `link.springer.com/.../demogr` record) with volume/issue, and update the access-date
header. When in doubt, **omit**.
