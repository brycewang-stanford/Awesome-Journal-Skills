# Worked Example: A Nature Geoscience Abstract + Opening (before → after)

This demonstrates the **Nature Geoscience opening arc** from `ngeo-results-framing` —
**Earth-system context → open question/controversy → "Here we show" (the advance with number and
uncertainty) → mechanism → why it matters broadly** — together with the `ngeo-scope-fit` two-part gate
(**broad Earth-system advance AND cross-disciplinary interest**, grounded in quantitative evidence), the
`ngeo-writing-style` rule that the opening must be **accessible to non-specialists**, and the hard
`ngeo-length-management` limits (main text ~3,000 words; abstract ~200 words, unreferenced — **verify the
current limits on the author pages**).

**Illustrative study (fictional):** *"Basal meltwater controls the pace of ice-stream acceleration."*
Fictional result: a measured link between subglacial water pressure and ice-stream speed-up that survives
in an ice-sheet model.

---

## Before (buries the result; reads like a field report; fails the gate on the page)

> **Abstract.** In this study we present a series of observations collected during a field campaign on a
> West Antarctic ice stream. Instruments were deployed over two seasons and a range of variables were
> recorded. We also ran a numerical model under various configurations. Our results may suggest that
> subglacial hydrology could possibly play some role in the observed velocity changes, which may be of
> interest for projections of future ice-sheet behaviour.
>
> **Opening.** The study of ice streams has a long history, and many groups have investigated their
> dynamics over the past decades [1–18]. West Antarctica is an important region. Subglacial hydrology has
> become a topic of interest. In order to explore these questions, we instrumented a site and ran a model,
> which we describe below. Section 2 describes the field site, Section 3 the instruments, and Section 4 the
> model setup.

**What's wrong (against the Nature Geoscience skills):**

- **Leads with method and campaign logistics**, not the advance — the `ngeo-results-framing` anti-pattern
  ("an abstract that is a fieldwork summary rather than a result statement").
- **No "Here we show," no number, no uncertainty** anywhere in the abstract — fails
  `ngeo-writing-style` ("every quantity with its uncertainty") and the house abstract convention.
- **Hedging stack** ("may suggest ... could possibly play some role") — the claim is not calibrated to
  evidence; a named `ngeo-writing-style` anti-pattern.
- **Model not grounded** — "ran a model under various configurations" with no observational anchor invites
  the classic `ngeo-methods` desk-reject.
- **Breadth not argued** — an editor cannot tell why this clears the `ngeo-scope-fit` gate or why a
  non-glaciologist should read it; no prior belief changed, no first-order question named.
- **Regional-setting tour + numbered-section roadmap** burn the scarce word budget `ngeo-length-management`
  needs; a Nature Article rarely uses numbered sections.
- **Jargon-first** ("subglacial hydrology," "velocity changes") with no non-specialist gloss.

---

## After (Nature arc — "Here we show," grounded, calibrated, accessible, length-aware)

> **Abstract.** How fast marine ice sheets can lose mass depends on how quickly their ice streams
> accelerate, yet what paces that acceleration has been unclear. Here we show, from two years of
> subglacial observations on a West Antarctic ice stream combined with a hydrologically coupled
> ice-sheet model, that basal water pressure sets the speed-up rate: a **0.4 ± 0.1 MPa** rise in water
> pressure preceded a **35 ± 8%** velocity increase within days, and the model reproduces this response
> only when subglacial drainage is included. The result identifies basal meltwater, rather than ocean
> forcing alone, as a first-order control on how abruptly ice streams speed up, and implies that
> projections omitting subglacial hydrology may underestimate the pace of West Antarctic mass loss.
> *(context → open question → "Here we show" with numbers + uncertainty → mechanism → broad implication;
> accessible, finding-first, model grounded in observations.)*
>
> **Opening.** The Antarctic Ice Sheet loses most of its mass through fast-flowing ice streams, so how
> quickly those streams can accelerate sets an upper bound on near-term sea-level rise. *(Earth-system
> context, one sentence any geoscientist grasps.)* Ocean-driven thinning is known to trigger
> acceleration, but what controls its *pace* — why some streams speed up over days and others over years —
> has remained unresolved, in part because the base of an ice stream is so hard to observe directly.
> *(the open question / controversy, framed as a scientific problem, not a regional description.)* Here we
> show that the pressure of water at the ice-stream bed sets the acceleration rate: across two field
> seasons, each rise in basal water pressure was followed within days by a proportional velocity increase,
> and an ice-sheet model reproduces the timing only when subglacial drainage is represented. *(what this
> study shows — the advance, with the mechanism, within the first paragraph.)* Because basal meltwater is
> itself sensitive to warming, this control links atmospheric and oceanic change to the abruptness of
> ice-stream flow, and it matters for every projection of West Antarctic mass loss that currently omits
> subglacial hydrology. *(why it matters broadly — names the out-of-subfield stakes and the prior belief
> it changes.)*

---

## Why the "after" meets the Nature Geoscience bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **One advance, "Here we show," finding first** — "basal water pressure sets the ice-stream
  acceleration rate"; the title would encode it and the abstract leads with it, satisfying
  `ngeo-results-framing` ("abstract uses 'Here we show' and leads with the finding + uncertainty").
- **Opening executes the four moves** — context → gap → advance → broad significance — and reaches the
  result inside the first paragraph, not after a literature or regional tour.
- **Clears the two-part `ngeo-scope-fit` gate explicitly:** the *advance* is a newly identified first-order
  control, and the *broad interest* names out-of-subfield stakes (sea-level projection, atmosphere–ocean
  coupling) plus the prior belief changed ("ocean forcing alone"). The neighbour and displacement tests
  pass.
- **Grounded with quantified uncertainty** — every headline number carries its error ("0.4 ± 0.1 MPa",
  "35 ± 8%"), and the model is explicitly validated against the observations, meeting `ngeo-methods`
  (model anchored to data) and `ngeo-writing-style` (uncertainty on every quantity).
- **Accessible to non-specialists** — "ice streams," "basal water pressure," and "subglacial drainage" are
  each glossed in plain terms before any jargon compounds, meeting the `ngeo-writing-style` broad-audience
  requirement.
- **Length-aware** — no regional tour, no instrument dump, no numbered-section roadmap; that reclaimed
  budget is what `ngeo-length-management` needs to keep the main text under ~3,000 words. The instrument
  detail, drainage-model configuration, and full uncertainty budget would go to online **Methods**, and
  extended time series to Supplementary Information, per `ngeo-methods` and `ngeo-supplementary`.
