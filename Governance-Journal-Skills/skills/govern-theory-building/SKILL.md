# Theory Building (govern-theory-building)

A *Governance* contribution is judged by what it adds to how we understand **governing** — the state,
institutions, the policy process, bureaucracy, governance networks, accountability. The argument must be
sharp enough to test (`govern-research-design`) and **portable** enough to matter beyond one case. This
skill builds the argument and its concepts; it does not run the empirics.

## When to trigger

- Stating the core argument, mechanism, and scope conditions
- A reviewer said the theory is "ad hoc," "just a description," or "won't travel"
- Defining or refining a concept for comparative use
- Specifying what the theory predicts that rivals do not

## Build a portable argument

1. **Name the mechanism, not just the correlation.** State *why* governing produces the outcome — the
   causal logic (delegation incentives, blame avoidance, institutional layering, policy feedback,
   network coordination). A mechanism is what lets the claim travel.
2. **State scope conditions explicitly.** Where does the argument hold — which regime types, levels of
   government, policy domains, capacity levels? Scope conditions are a feature, not a hedge; they make
   portability honest.
3. **Specify observable implications in advance.** What should we see in the cases/countries if the
   argument is right — and what would we see instead if a rival institutional account were right?
4. **Connect to a theory others can use.** Tie the contribution to an existing family (historical
   institutionalism, principal-agent, the regulatory state, multilevel governance) so the field can
   build on it — even when you revise that family.

## Concept formation for comparative work (avoid concept stretching)

Comparative governance lives or dies on concepts. Apply Sartori-style discipline:

- **Define by attributes, then by cases.** Fix the defining attributes (intension) before deciding which
  cases fall under the concept (extension); do not stretch a concept to fit a convenient case.
- **Mind the ladder of abstraction.** To travel to more cases, drop attributes (move up); to gain
  precision, add them (move down). State where on the ladder your concept sits.
- **Establish equivalence across cases.** "Accountability," "agency," "reform," "capacity" do not mean
  the same thing in every system — show functional/contextual equivalence before you compare.
- **Avoid the degreeism / dichotomy trap.** Be explicit about whether the concept is categorical or
  graded, and justify the choice for the comparison at hand.

## Contribution types that land at Governance

- A **new or revised mechanism** for a governing phenomenon, with scope conditions.
- A **reconceptualization** that resolves a measurement or classification problem comparatively.
- A **theory portability claim** — an account known in one context shown to (not) travel, and why.
- A **scope-condition correction** — a leading theory holds *only* under conditions the field missed.

## The strongest-rival test (Governance-specific)

For the **single strongest rival institutional explanation**, write one sentence: *"If the rival
account of governing were right rather than mine, we would observe ___ across cases; my argument
instead implies ___."* If you cannot write it, the theory is not yet a testable contribution — it is a
description waiting for a mechanism.

## Anti-patterns

- A "framework" that organizes cases but predicts nothing
- Concept stretching: relabeling a familiar concept to claim novelty, or applying it where attributes don't hold
- Scope conditions left implicit, so the claim silently over-generalizes
- A mechanism that cannot distinguish your argument from the leading institutional rival
- Theory and case so fused that the argument cannot travel to a second country

## Output format

```
【Core claim】one sentence
【Mechanism】why governing produces the outcome
【Scope conditions】where it holds (regimes / levels / domains / capacity)
【Key concept(s)】definition + position on ladder of abstraction + equivalence note
【Rival ruled out】the strongest-rival sentence
【Observable implications】what we should/should not see across cases
【Next】govern-research-design
```
