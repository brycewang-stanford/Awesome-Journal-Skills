# Writing Style (govern-writing-style)

A *Governance* paper must be readable by a comparativist, a public-administration scholar, and a policy
researcher at once — and it must persuade them the finding teaches something about **how the state
governs**, not just what happened in one administration. The recurring rejection note at this journal is
"competent but parochial." This skill is about reaching that comparative/international readership and
respecting the format — not about generating claims.

## When to trigger

- Drafting the introduction, framing the contribution, or doing the final polish
- Over the word cap and needing to cut without losing the argument
- Writing the **~150-word abstract**
- Aligning citations, headings, and format to the journal/Wiley guidelines before submission

## Reach the comparative/international readership

1. **Front-load the contribution.** By the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the
   argument, the evidence, and why it matters for understanding governing across cases. Do not make a
   reader dig for the "so what."
2. **Comparative framing, not single-country parochialism.** Even a single-case paper must say what the
   case is a case *of* and what the claim should predict elsewhere. Name the general mechanism
   (delegation, blame avoidance, layering, policy feedback, bureaucratic autonomy) so a reader in
   another country or domain can use it.
3. **Define jargon on first use.** A comparativist should follow a public-administration paper and vice
   versa; spell out acronyms and country-specific institutional terms (do not assume everyone knows what
   a "non-departmental public body" or a "Länder" arrangement is).
4. **Argument-first prose.** Lead with claims; use evidence to support them. Avoid "the data show…"
   without saying what they show and why it matters for the theory of the state.
5. **Signpost.** Clear section structure so a reader can navigate the argument across cases.

## Format to the journal's guidelines

- **Citations**: author-date style is typical for the field; confirm the exact style on the live Wiley/
  journal guidelines page and keep it consistent (manage with Zotero/BibTeX). References are excluded
  from the word count. (待核实 — verify the precise reference style on the current author guidelines.)
- **Title page**: separate, non-anonymous file with the word count; corresponding author gives an
  **ORCID iD** per Wiley norms (待核实).
- **Anonymize**: *Governance* is **double-blind** — no author names, affiliations, or acknowledgments
  in the manuscript, no obvious self-references ("as we showed in…"), and strip identifying file
  metadata (see `govern-submission`).
- **Abstract**: **~150 words**, stating the question, the comparative approach, and the finding.

## Fit the word cap (≤ 9,000 words excluding citations/bibliography; 检索于 2026-06；以官网为准)

- Treat **9,000 words excluding citations/bibliography** as the working cap (a third-party listing cites
  3,000–5,000; treat the official 9,000 as binding, 待核实).
- Move balance tables, full specifications, and extended robustness to **supplementary material**.
- Cut throat-clearing and literature dumps; engage the debate, not every paper (see
  `govern-literature-positioning`).
- Prefer one decisive comparative figure to three redundant tables (see `govern-tables-figures`).
- Do not pad a sharp comparative point into a sprawling multi-country, multi-outcome design.

## Checklist

- [ ] Contribution (question + argument + evidence + so-what) stated by the end of the introduction
- [ ] The case is framed as a case *of* something general; the mechanism is named and portable
- [ ] Acronyms spelled out; country-specific institutional terms defined on first use
- [ ] Abstract ~150 words, stating question + comparative approach + finding
- [ ] Manuscript ≤ 9,000 words excluding citations/bibliography; supplement holds the overflow
- [ ] Author-date citations consistent per the live guidelines; references excluded from the count
- [ ] Double-blind clean (no names/affiliations/acknowledgments/self-references); title page separate

## Anti-patterns

- A single-country administrative narrative that never states what the case is a case *of*
- Burying the contribution in the middle of the paper
- An abstract that hides the finding or drifts well past ~150 words
- Unexplained country-specific institutional jargon that locks out non-area readers
- Mixed citation styles; acknowledgments or self-references that break double-blind anonymity
- Letting the manuscript creep past 9,000 words with material that belongs in the supplement
