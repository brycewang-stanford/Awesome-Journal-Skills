# Worked Example: A Governance-Style Introduction (before → after)

This demonstrates the **Governance introduction bar** from `govern-writing-style` and
`govern-topic-selection`: **by the end of the introduction the reader knows the question, the argument,
the evidence, and why it matters for comparative policy, administration, and institutions** — not just
for one country or one agency. The funnel here is **question → comparative / institutional significance
→ why it is hard to identify → setting & design → headline finding with the mechanism → portable
contribution across cases → brief roadmap**, with *Governance*'s distinctive demand that the
contribution **travel across cases and systems** and that the design be **defended on its own terms**.

**Illustrative paper (fictional):** *"Answering to Whom? How a One-Stop-Shop Service Reform Reshapes
Bureaucratic Responsiveness Across Adopting States."* Fictional setting: a set of comparable
parliamentary democracies that rolled out a mandatory **one-stop-shop** front-office reform of their
central administrative agencies in **different years**, creating staggered cross-national variation in
when frontline bureaucracies were reorganized. (Theme: administrative / institutional reform; method:
staggered difference-in-differences across countries. Chosen because the responsiveness of the
administrative state is a comparative-governance stake — see the Lapuente & Van de Walle and the He
et al. exemplars in [`../exemplars/library.md`](../exemplars/library.md).)

---

## Before (insider, single-country, buries the contribution — typical first draft)

> The literature on administrative reform is vast. Scholars have long debated New Public Management and
> its effects on public organizations, and many studies examine service-delivery reforms in single
> countries. In this paper, we use a staggered difference-in-differences design with the Callaway and
> Sant'Anna (2021) estimator to study the effects of a one-stop-shop reform on bureaucratic
> responsiveness in several countries where the reform was adopted in different years. We assemble an
> agency-level panel and estimate event-study specifications. Administrative responsiveness is an
> important topic in public administration. Section 2 reviews the literature on NPM, Section 3 describes
> our data, Section 4 presents the empirical strategy, Section 5 reports results, Section 6 discusses
> robustness, and Section 7 concludes.

**What's wrong (against `govern-writing-style` / `govern-topic-selection`):**

- **Insider, no comparative stake.** It speaks only to NPM specialists; a scholar of regulatory
  governance, the policy process, or comparative institutions is given no reason to care, and the
  cross-national leverage is buried — a named anti-pattern in `govern-topic-selection`
  ("single-case-only / no comparative significance").
- **Leads with method** ("we use a staggered DID design with the Callaway–Sant'Anna estimator") instead
  of the question and argument — the `govern-writing-style` anti-pattern of burying the contribution.
- **No argument and no finding on page one.** The reader does not learn what was found, the mechanism, or
  the institutional stake. "Responsiveness is an important topic" is throat-clearing with vague stakes.
- **No portable contribution.** Nothing a scholar of another governance topic could *import* across
  cases (`govern-theory-building`'s portability test fails).
- **Reads like a literature review, then an over-signposted seven-section roadmap** — and *Governance*
  treats stand-alone reviews as out of scope and counts toward the ~9,000-word cap.

---

## After (Governance arc — comparative significance + argument + finding, contribution stated early)

> **When the administrative state reorganizes its front office, does it become more responsive to
> citizens — or merely more responsive to the politicians who ordered the reform?** We show that when
> central agencies adopt a mandatory one-stop-shop service model, measured responsiveness to citizen
> requests rises by roughly **30%**, but the gain concentrates in agencies whose performance is
> *publicly reported*, and is near zero where it is not — so the reform shifts *whose* signals
> bureaucracies answer, not just how fast they answer. *(question + comparative significance + headline
> finding in the first breath — this is a claim about accountability and the organization of the state,
> not just a service-speed number.)*
>
> Why this matters across comparative governance: a recurring question — from theories of bureaucratic
> accountability to the design of regulatory agencies to the study of governance networks — is whether
> administrative reforms make the state answer to the *public* or to its *principals*. If the same
> structural reform produces responsiveness only when paired with public reporting, then responsiveness
> is a function of the **accountability architecture** a reform is embedded in, not of the front-office
> structure alone. That speaks to any scholar designing or evaluating institutional reform across cases.
> *(the comparative / institutional stake, framed for a regulation scholar and a policy-process scholar
> alike — `govern-topic-selection` comparative-significance test.)*
>
> Identifying this cleanly is hard because countries do not adopt the reform at random: a government may
> reorganize its agencies precisely when a reform-minded coalition is already pushing transparency, so a
> simple before-after comparison would confound the front-office reform with the accountability climate
> that produced it. *(why it is hard — the identification problem stated plainly, per
> `govern-research-design`.)*
>
> We exploit a set of comparable parliamentary democracies that mandated the one-stop-shop model in
> **different years**, for administrative-calendar reasons unrelated to local responsiveness trends. The
> staggered timing lets us compare each adopter to not-yet-adopting countries using a
> heterogeneity-robust difference-in-differences estimator, and we verify no differential pre-trends in
> responsiveness before adoption. *(setting & design in one paragraph; the modern staggered estimator is
> named only as a tool, not as the lead.)*
>
> Adoption raises responsiveness by **9 percentage points** where agency performance is publicly
> reported, against **1 point** where it is not, and the gap opens only after adoption — never before.
> The mechanism is **not** simply faster processing from streamlined intake: the responsiveness gain
> tracks the *visibility* of agency performance to citizens and oversight bodies, consistent with a
> reputational-accountability channel rather than a pure efficiency channel. *(headline finding restated
> with the mechanism and a ruled-out rival — the `govern-research-design` adjudication move.)*
>
> Our contribution is to show that **administrative-structure reforms are responsiveness-neutral until an
> accountability channel makes performance visible**: the same reorganization that looks like a
> service-delivery upgrade is, functionally, an accountability instrument whose effect depends on who can
> see the results. This reframes a familiar NPM-style reform — long evaluated as an efficiency measure —
> as a **conditional accountability mechanism** whose payoff a comparativist can predict from the
> reporting regime. *(contribution stated early and as a *portable* argument: a scholar of regulatory
> agencies, of governance networks, and of the policy process can all carry it to their own cases —
> `govern-theory-building` portability test.)*
>
> The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the conditional-accountability argument and its
> observable implications; Section 3 presents the cross-national design and the responsiveness results;
> Section 4 examines the visibility mechanism and scope conditions across adopters. *(brief roadmap — no
> over-signposting.)*

## Why the "after" meets the Governance bar

Mapped to the skill checklists:

- **Comparative significance is stated, not assumed** — the second paragraph names the cross-case stake
  (accountability architecture, regulatory-agency design, governance networks) so a scholar outside NPM
  sees why it matters (`govern-topic-selection`: "a scholar working on a different governance question,
  in a different country, should see why it matters").
- **Argument-first, contribution early** — the claim ("administrative-structure reforms are
  responsiveness-neutral until an accountability channel makes performance visible") is stated plainly
  and early, not buried in the middle (`govern-writing-style`: "front-load the contribution").
- **Portable argument, not a bare finding** — the contribution is framed so another governance subfield
  and other cases can import it, passing the `govern-theory-building` portability test; the finding is
  attached to a mechanism with scope conditions, not left as "we estimate an effect."
- **Identification problem named before the design**, and the **single strongest rival is adjudicated**
  ("the mechanism is *not* simply faster processing… instead…") — the `govern-research-design`
  adjudication sentence, with no-differential-pre-trends stated plainly.
- **Method demoted to a tool** — the staggered, heterogeneity-robust estimator appears only where the
  design is discussed, never as the lead (avoids the `govern-writing-style` "leading with the estimator"
  anti-pattern; the estimator choice follows the modern-staggered-DiD guidance referenced in
  [`../code/`](../code/)).
- **Reads as comparative governance and respects the cap** — jargon is minimal and defined, the framing
  is legible to a comparative policy/administration audience, the roadmap is three sections not seven,
  and there is no stand-alone literature review (`govern-writing-style`: ≤ 9,000 words excluding
  citations/bibliography; corporate governance and pure reviews are out of scope).
