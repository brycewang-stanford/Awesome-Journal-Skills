## How to use this library

*Governance* is the **comparative venue for policy, administration, and institutions**: each paper below
earns its place by making a result about one agency, reform, or country legible and useful **across cases
and systems** (see
[`../../skills/govern-topic-selection/SKILL.md`](../../skills/govern-topic-selection/SKILL.md) and
[`../../skills/govern-theory-building/SKILL.md`](../../skills/govern-theory-building/SKILL.md)). Pick the
row whose **theme × method** is closest to yours, then study how it (1) states comparative/institutional
significance, and (2) defends its design on its own methodological terms (see
[`../../skills/govern-research-design/SKILL.md`](../../skills/govern-research-design/SKILL.md)). Run the
self-check before claiming your paper is "Governance-shaped."

## By theme × method

### Regulatory governance / bureaucratic reputation (single-agency theory + cases)

- **Maor, "Organizational Reputation and Jurisdictional Claims: The Case of the U.S. Food and Drug Administration," Governance 2010, 23(1):133–159.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/j.1468-0491.2009.01470.x` (journal = *Governance*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** turns a single regulator's behavior into a portable proposition about
    *when* agencies assert authority over novel technologies, anchored in a reputational mechanism that
    travels to other regulators and sectors. The agency case earns its keep by yielding a comparative
    static, not a description (see `govern-theory-building`: "say what the case is a case *of*").
  - **Self-check:** does your agency study deliver a *named, portable mechanism* a scholar of a different
    regulator or country can import, rather than an account specific to your one agency?

### Transparency / norm diffusion (international governance institution)

- **David-Barrett & Okamura, "Norm Diffusion and Reputation: The Rise of the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative," Governance 2016, 29(2):227–246.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/gove.12163` (journal = *Governance*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** explains the spread of a transparency institution by casting it as a
    *reputational intermediary*, linking micro incentives (reformers signaling, donors rewarding) to a
    macro diffusion pattern — a model of how a governance institution travels across countries. Pairs
    with the positioning guidance in
    [`../../skills/govern-literature-positioning/SKILL.md`](../../skills/govern-literature-positioning/SKILL.md).
  - **Self-check:** if your finding is about an institution's spread, can you show the actor-level
    incentives that must hold for the cross-national pattern to emerge?

### Administrative reform / New Public Management (comparative, cross-national)

- **Lapuente & Van de Walle, "The effects of new public management on the quality of public services," Governance 2020, 33(3):461–475.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/gove.12502` (journal = *Governance*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** takes a discipline-defining reform agenda (NPM) and reframes the question
    comparatively — *under what conditions* NPM helps or harms service quality — so the contribution
    speaks to administrative reform everywhere, not to one country's experience. A model
    introduction-to-a-special-collection that states the comparative stake plainly.
  - **Self-check:** does your reform study specify the *conditions* under which the effect holds, so a
    comparativist can predict the result in a new case?

### Policy process / policy experimentation (configurational, QCA method)

- **He, Fan & Su, "Unpack the black box of pilot sampling in policy experimentation: A qualitative comparative analysis of China's public hospital reform," Governance 2024, 37(3):803–824.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/gove.12804` (journal = *Governance*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** uses **qualitative comparative analysis (QCA)** plus illustrative cases to
    identify configurational *pathways* of pilot selection — a transparent configurational design whose
    calibration and pathways are the contribution, legible to policy-process scholars beyond the Chinese
    case (see `govern-research-design`: configurational designs live or die on transparent calibration).
  - **Self-check:** for a configurational claim, do you report calibration, the truth table, consistency
    and coverage, and a robustness check — and is the *set of pathways* the portable result?

### Comparative institutions / delegation & accountability (cross-country, panel)

- **Bodea & Garriga, "Central bank independence in Latin America: Politicization and de-delegation," Governance 2023, 36(1):59–80.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/gove.12706` (journal = *Governance*).
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** studies *de-delegation* of monetary authority comparatively, connecting a
    measurable institutional design (central-bank independence) to political preferences across countries
    — a comparative-institutions paper whose framework a scholar of any independent agency can reuse.
  - **Self-check:** does your institutional-design study link a measurable design feature to a political
    mechanism that recurs across your case set, rather than narrating one country's reform?

### Bureaucratic politics / agenda translation (qualitative, archival + interviews)

- **Gilad, Alon-Barkat & Weiss, "Bureaucratic politics and the translation of movement agendas," Governance 2019, 32(待核实):369–385.**
  Verified: Wiley Online Library DOI `10.1111/gove.12383` (journal = *Governance*; year, volume, and page
  range confirmed). **Issue number not confirmed verbatim — 待核实**; confirm the issue on Wiley Online
  Library before citing.
  - **Why it is an exemplar:** opens the "black box" of how bureaucracies translate social-movement
    demands into policy *between* agenda-setting and legislation, using archival work and elite
    interviews across many ministries — a qualitative design whose mechanism (bureaucratic translation)
    is portable to other states and movements.
  - **Self-check:** does your qualitative study isolate a *stage* or *mechanism* others have black-boxed,
    and present the evidentiary logic (not just a narrative) so the mechanism travels?

## Omitted for lack of Governance verification (do NOT attribute to Governance without re-checking)

To keep the list zero-hallucination, the following plausible candidates were **excluded** because web
search placed them in a *sibling* journal, not *Governance*. They are listed only as guardrails:

- **Provan & Kenis, "Modes of Network Governance: Structure, Management, and Effectiveness" (2008)** —
  verified to be in the *Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory* (**JPART**) 18(2):229–252,
  **not Governance**. A very common misattribution because the title contains "governance."
- **Pavón Mediano, "Agencies' formal independence and credible commitment in the Latin American
  regulatory state" (2020)** — verified to be in **Regulation & Governance** (the Wiley sibling)
  `10.1111/rego.12187`, **not Governance**. Do not conflate *Governance* (`gove`) with *Regulation &
  Governance* (`rego`).
- **Overman et al., "A Multidimensional Reputation Barometer for Public Agencies" (2020)** — verified to
  be in *Public Administration Review* (**PAR**) `10.1111/puar.13158`, **not Governance**. Note: a
  *different* reputation literature (Maor 2010) *is* in *Governance* and is listed above — do not
  conflate the venues.
- **McPhilemy, "Central banks under stress: Reputation, accountability and regulatory coherence"
  (2019)** — verified to be in *Public Administration* `10.1111/padm.12606`, **not Governance**. Note: a
  *different* central-bank paper (Bodea & Garriga 2023) *is* in *Governance* and is listed above.
- **Pollitt & Bouckaert, *Public Management Reform: A Comparative Analysis* (2011/2017)** — an **Oxford
  University Press book**, not a *Governance* article. Listed because it surfaces constantly in
  "administrative reform + Governance" searches.

Before adding any new paper to this library, confirm it on **Wiley Online Library**
(`onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/14680491`) with volume/issue/pages, check that the DOI prefix is
**`gove`** (or the legacy `j.1468-0491`) and **not** `rego` / `puar` / `padm` / `jpam`, and update the
access-date header. When in doubt, omit.
